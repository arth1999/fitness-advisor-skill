# Chapter 7: Functional Assessment -- Posture, Movement, Core, Balance, and Flexibility

## Purpose of Functional Assessment
Functional assessments establish a baseline of a client's movement quality. They identify muscle imbalances, postural deviations, and movement compensations that must be corrected before applying external load. Assessments should be sequenced from static (posture) to dynamic (movement) to loaded.

## Postural Assessment
Static postural assessment observes the client standing in a relaxed, normal stance from anterior, lateral, and posterior views. The trainer uses a plumb line or grid as a reference.

### Common Postural Deviations
- **Kyphosis-Lordosis**: Increased thoracic kyphosis + increased lumbar lordosis + anterior pelvic tilt. Tight hip flexors and lumbar extensors; weak abdominals and hip extensors.
- **Flat Back**: Reduced lumbar lordosis, posterior pelvic tilt. Tight hamstrings and abdominals; weak hip flexors.
- **Sway Back**: Posterior pelvic tilt + anterior shift of pelvis, thoracic kyphosis. Tight hamstrings and upper abdominals; weak hip flexors and lower abdominals.
- **Forward Head**: Head positioned anterior to the plumb line. Tight cervical extensors and upper trapezius; weak deep cervical flexors.
- **Upper Crossed Syndrome**: Tight upper trapezius, levator scapulae, and pectorals; weak deep cervical flexors and lower trapezius/rhomboids.
- **Lower Crossed Syndrome**: Tight hip flexors and lumbar extensors; weak abdominals and gluteals.

## Movement Assessment
Dynamic movement assessments reveal how the body moves during functional tasks.

### Bend-and-Lift Screen (Squat Assessment)
Observe from anterior and lateral views: heels rising, knee valgus, excessive forward lean, lumbar rounding or hyperextension, asymmetrical weight shift. Identify hip-dominant vs. quadriceps-dominant patterns.

### Hurdle Step Screen (Single-Leg Stance)
Assesses bilateral stability and mobility during a step-over motion. Identifies stance-leg stability issues and stepping-leg mobility restrictions.

### Shoulder Push/Pull Screens
Assess shoulder girdle stability during pushing and pulling motions. Watch for scapular winging, elevation, or asymmetry.

### Thomas Test (Hip Flexor/Length Assessment)
Client sits at end of a table, holds one knee to chest, lies back. The opposite leg position reveals: hip flexor tightness (thigh raises off table), rectus femoris tightness (knee extends), or IT band tightness (leg abducts).

### Apley's Scratch Test (Shoulder Mobility)
Client reaches one arm over shoulder and the other up the back trying to touch fingers. Assesses combined shoulder mobility (flexion + abduction + external rotation on top; extension + adduction + internal rotation on bottom).

## Core Function Assessment
McGill's Torso Muscular Endurance Test Battery evaluates core stability through three timed isometric holds:

1. **Trunk Flexor Endurance Test**: Client holds 60-degree torso angle from floor. Assesses anterior core endurance.
2. **Trunk Lateral Endurance Test (Side Bridge)**: Client holds side bridge position with body in straight line. Tests lateral core on each side.
3. **Trunk Extensor Endurance Test**: Client holds horizontal torso position off table edge. Assesses posterior core endurance.

**Interpretation Ratios** (McGill, 2007):
- Flexion:Extension ratio < 1.0
- Right Side Bridge:Left Side Bridge ratio within 1.0 +/- 0.05
- Side Bridge (either side):Extension ratio < 0.75

Muscular endurance is a better predictor of back health than strength or ROM.

## Balance Assessment
- **Stork-Stand Test**: Single-leg stance with eyes open and closed. Timed.
- **Sharpened Romberg Test**: Tandem stance (heel-to-toe), eyes open and closed.
- **Crane Stance Test (functional reach)**: Forward reach distance from stable base.

## Flexibility Assessment
- **Sit-and-Reach Test**: Assesses hamstring and low back flexibility.
- **Straight-Leg Raise**: Assesses hamstring length passively.
- **Shoulder Flexibility Tests**: Using goniometer or visual estimation.

## Key Principle
Correct identified deficits in stability, mobility, and movement patterns (Phase 1-2 of ACE IFT) before progressing clients to loaded exercises (Phase 3).
