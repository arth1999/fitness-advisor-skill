# Chapter 4: Behavior Change and Health Psychology

## The Transtheoretical Model (TTM) of Behavior Change
The TTM describes how individuals progress through five stages when modifying health behaviors. A trainer must identify the client's current stage and apply stage-appropriate strategies.

### Stage 1: Precontemplation
The person is not considering change in the next six months. They may be unaware of the need to change or demoralized by past failures.
- **Strategy**: Provide information about risks of inactivity and benefits of exercise. Help them become aware. Do not push for action.

### Stage 2: Contemplation
The person intends to change within six months but is ambivalent. They weigh pros and cons.
- **Strategy**: Reinforce the pros of exercise. Address perceived barriers. Help build confidence that change is possible.

### Stage 3: Preparation
The person intends to take action within the next 30 days and has made some small steps.
- **Strategy**: Help set realistic goals. Create a concrete action plan. Address any remaining barriers. Provide encouragement.

### Stage 4: Action
The person has been regularly active for less than six months. This is the highest risk stage for relapse.
- **Strategy**: Provide positive feedback. Help build self-efficacy through mastery experiences. Teach relapse prevention. Reinforce consistency.

### Stage 5: Maintenance
The person has been regularly active for six months or more. The new behavior has become a habit.
- **Strategy**: Help maintain motivation through variety and new challenges. Develop independence and self-regulation skills. Plan for lapses.

## Key Behavioral Constructs

### Self-Determination Theory (SDT)
Three basic psychological needs drive intrinsic motivation:
1. **Autonomy**: The need to feel in control of one's own behavior
2. **Competence**: The need to feel capable and effective
3. **Relatedness**: The need to feel connected to others

### Self-Efficacy (Bandura)
The confidence to perform a specific behavior in a specific context. Built through:
- Mastery experiences (most powerful)
- Vicarious experience (modeling)
- Verbal persuasion
- Interpretation of physiological states

### Decisional Balance
Weighing perceived pros against perceived cons of changing. Change occurs when pros outweigh cons.

## Relapse Prevention
- Identify high-risk situations (stress, holidays, travel, illness, work demands)
- Develop specific coping strategies for each situation
- Use "cognitive restructuring" to reframe lapses as learning opportunities, not failures
- A single missed workout does not mean failure -- prevent the "abstinence violation effect"
- Plan for planned breaks (vacations) with modified exercise approaches

## Behavioral Strategies for Trainers
1. Create positive first experiences -- the initial sessions determine long-term adherence
2. Set process goals (completing workouts) alongside outcome goals (weight loss)
3. Use motivational interviewing: express empathy, develop discrepancy, roll with resistance, support self-efficacy
4. Provide autonomy-supportive coaching: offer choices in exercise selection
5. Build self-efficacy by ensuring early success experiences
6. Use social support and group dynamics
7. Help clients move from extrinsic to intrinsic motivation
8. Celebrate small wins consistently
