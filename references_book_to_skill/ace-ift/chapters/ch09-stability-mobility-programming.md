# Chapter 9: Stability, Mobility, and Movement Program Design

## ACE IFT Phase 1: Stability and Mobility Training

### Objective
Correct muscle imbalances, improve postural stability, restore joint mobility, and establish proper motor control patterns. This phase addresses the proximal-to-distal training principle: stabilize the core and proximal joints before training distal mobility and movement.

### Key Concepts
- **Proximal stability for distal mobility**: The lumbopelvic-hip complex must be stable before the extremities can move efficiently.
- **Motor control before load**: Clients must demonstrate proper activation patterns before adding resistance.
- **Address mobility restrictions**: Joint limitations (e.g., ankle dorsiflexion < 15 degrees) must be resolved before teaching loaded movement patterns.

### Core Stability Training
The core is defined as the lumbopelvic-hip complex including the abdominals, spinal extensors, hip musculature, and diaphragm. It serves as the body's center of gravity and origin of all movement.

**Core Training Progression**:
1. **Core activation**: Teach isolated activation of transverse abdominis and multifidus (drawing-in maneuver, abdominal bracing).
2. **Static stability**: Planks, side bridges, bird dogs, dead bug -- holding neutral spine against gravity.
3. **Dynamic stability**: Movement of extremities while maintaining core position (paloff press, cable chops/lifts with stable stance).
4. **Integrated stability**: Core engagement during full-body movements.

### Flexibility Training
Types of flexibility training:
- **Static stretching**: Hold at mild discomfort 15-60 seconds. Most appropriate post-workout or in dedicated flexibility sessions. Pre-exercise static stretching may reduce maximal strength/power output.
- **Dynamic/Active range of motion**: Controlled movement through full ROM. Most appropriate pre-workout and during warm-up.
- **Self-myofascial release (foam rolling)**: Apply pressure with foam roller to tender areas, 30-60 seconds per site. Reduces muscle tension and perceived soreness.

### Balance Training Progression
1. **Static balance**: Single-leg stance on stable surface
2. **Dynamic balance**: Single-leg stance on unstable surface, reaching movements
3. **Reactive balance**: Catching/responding to perturbations

## ACE IFT Phase 2: Movement Training

### The Five Primary Movement Patterns
All human movement and ADLs can be decomposed into five primal patterns:
1. **Bend-and-Lift (Squat/Hip Hinge)**: Picking up objects from the ground
2. **Single-Leg Movement**: Walking, lunging, stair climbing
3. **Pushing**: Opening doors, pushing objects (vertical and horizontal planes)
4. **Pulling**: Closing doors, rowing objects (vertical and horizontal planes)
5. **Rotational (Spiral) Movement**: Turning, twisting, throwing

### Teaching Methodology
- **Part-to-whole**: Teach components separately, then integrate
- **Hip-dominant vs. Quadriceps-dominant**: Emphasize posterior weight shift to activate gluteals and protect knees during bend-and-lift
- **Lower-body kinetic chain**: Foot pronation leads to tibial internal rotation, femoral internal rotation, and knee valgus -- countered by strong hip abductors/external rotators
- **Q-angle considerations**: Wider angle in females increases ACL injury risk; gluteal strength is protective

### Bend-and-Lift Progression
1. Hip hinge with dowel (three-point contact: head, thoracic spine, sacrum)
2. Lower-body alignment drills (knee tracking with resistance band)
3. Body-weight squat to "number 4" position (tibia parallel to torso)
4. Front squat pattern, back squat pattern, overhead squat pattern

### Single-Leg Progression
1. Single-leg balance
2. Half-kneeling lunge to stand
3. Multi-directional lunges (forward, lateral, rotational)
4. Step-ups, single-leg deadlift variations

### Push/Pull/Rotation
Teach proper scapulohumeral rhythm and thoracolumbar dissociation. Ensure mobility in thoracic spine (rotation) is sufficient before loading rotational patterns.

### Phase 2 Exit Criteria
Before progressing to Phase 3 (Load Training), clients must demonstrate:
- Proper technique in all five movement patterns with body weight
- Core stability through full range of each movement
- Control of center of gravity
- Control of movement speed (eccentric emphasis)
