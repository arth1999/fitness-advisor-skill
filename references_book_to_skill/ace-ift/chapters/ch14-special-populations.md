# Chapter 14: Exercise and Special Populations

## Guiding Principles
All clients with medical conditions or special needs must have medical clearance before beginning an exercise program. A trainer's role with special-population clients is to design programs that follow physician guidelines and published, condition-specific exercise recommendations. Trainers do NOT treat conditions; they facilitate safe exercise within established parameters.

## Cardiovascular Conditions

### Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)
- Exercise is a key component of cardiac rehabilitation.
- Monitor for signs of angina, dyspnea, or excessive fatigue.
- Avoid heavy isometric work and Valsalva maneuver.
- Begin with low-intensity, gradually progressive exercise.
- Use RPE rather than HR for intensity monitoring if on beta-blockers.

### Hypertension
- Avoid heavy resistance training with isometric holds.
- Monitor BP before, during, and after exercise.
- Emphasize aerobic exercise (30-60 min, most days) at moderate intensity.
- Resistance training at moderate loads (avoid failure).
- Avoid exercises with the head below the heart.

## Respiratory Conditions

### Asthma
- Ensure adequate warm-up (10-15 minutes).
- Exercise-induced asthma: have rescue inhaler available.
- Interval training may be better tolerated than continuous.
- Avoid triggers (cold air, allergens, high pollen).
- Swimming and indoor activities often better tolerated.

### COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease)
- Include upper-body exercises to reduce ventilatory demand of daily activities.
- Use interval training with longer rest periods.
- Monitor oxygen saturation if available.
- Pursed-lip breathing technique helps manage dyspnea.

## Metabolic Conditions

### Diabetes (Type 1 and Type 2)
- **Type 1**: Monitor blood glucose before, during (if prolonged), and after exercise. Avoid exercise if glucose > 250 mg/dL with ketones. Have fast-acting carbohydrates available.
- **Type 2**: Exercise improves insulin sensitivity for 24-72 hours. Combine aerobic and resistance training. Monitor for hypoglycemia with certain medications.
- Foot care: Inspect feet daily; proper footwear essential due to neuropathy risk.
- Resistance training at moderate intensity (50-80% 1-RM).

### Obesity (BMI >= 30 kg/m2)
- Initial focus on consistency and enjoyment, not intensity.
- Low-impact cardiovascular exercise to protect joints.
- Gradual progression; even 10-minute bouts are beneficial.
- Resistance training: 40-50% 1-RM initially, progress slowly.
- Address balance and postural stability.

### Metabolic Syndrome
Cluster of: elevated waist circumference, elevated triglycerides, reduced HDL, elevated BP, elevated fasting glucose. At least three components for diagnosis.
- Exercise is first-line treatment. Combine aerobic (>=150 min/week) and resistance training.
- Even modest weight loss (5-10%) significantly improves all components.

## Musculoskeletal Conditions

### Low-Back Pain (LBP)
- Avoid bed rest; exercise is beneficial for most LBP.
- Core stabilization exercises are essential (McGill protocol).
- Avoid exercises that exacerbate pain.
- Neutral spine training during all movements.
- Hip mobility work to reduce compensatory lumbar motion.

### Arthritis (Osteoarthritis and Rheumatoid Arthritis)
- Exercise during periods of minimal pain and inflammation.
- Adequate warm-up (10+ minutes) and gradual cool-down.
- ROM exercises daily. Resistance training 2-3 days/week at low-moderate intensity.
- Avoid high-impact activities. Pool-based exercise is excellent.
- Post-exercise soreness should resolve within 2 hours; if longer, reduce intensity next session.

### Osteoporosis and Osteopenia
- Weight-bearing aerobic activities (walking, stair climbing, dancing).
- Resistance training: 2-3 days/week, moderate intensity.
- Avoid spinal flexion exercises (crunches, toe touches) and high-impact activities in diagnosed osteoporosis.
- Balance training to prevent falls.
- Emphasize postural muscles and spinal extension.

## Special Age Groups

### Youth (Prepubescent and Adolescent)
- Focus on skill development, coordination, and movement literacy.
- Avoid maximal lifts before skeletal maturity (Tanner stage 5).
- Emphasize variety and enjoyment.
- Children are not miniature adults -- thermoregulation differs; they fatigue and recover faster.

### Older Adults
- Four priorities: (1) Avoid cardiovascular risk, (2) Avoid orthopedic risk, (3) Preserve muscle mass, (4) Respect slower adaptation.
- Limit to 1-2 challenging sessions per week; fill rest with light activity.
- Combine cardio with resistance training; balance training is essential.
- Sarcopenia: Age-related muscle loss; resistance training is the primary countermeasure.

## Prenatal and Postpartum
- First trimester: Avoid overheating, maintain hydration.
- Second trimester onward: Avoid supine exercises (compresses vena cava).
- Avoid contact sports, scuba diving, and activities with fall risk.
- Monitor for diastasis recti.
- Postpartum: Gradual return. Core rehabilitation before high-intensity activity.

## Key Practice Guidelines
1. ALWAYS obtain medical clearance for clients with known conditions.
2. Communicate with clients' physicians (with written consent).
3. Understand common medications and their effects on exercise response.
4. Maintain detailed records including all communications with healthcare providers.
5. Know when to refer to a specialist -- never exceed your scope.
