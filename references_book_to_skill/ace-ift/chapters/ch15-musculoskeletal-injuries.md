# Chapter 15: Common Musculoskeletal Injuries and Their Impact on Exercise

## Tissue Types and Injuries
Understanding the relationship between tissue type, healing, and exercise programming is essential for safe post-injury training.

- **Strain**: Injury to muscle or tendon (musculotendinous unit). Graded I (mild) through III (complete rupture).
- **Sprain**: Injury to ligament. Graded I (stretched) through III (complete tear).
- **Tendinitis**: Acute inflammation of a tendon.
- **Tendinosis**: Chronic degeneration of a tendon without inflammation. More common in overuse injuries.
- **Bursitis**: Inflammation of a bursa (fluid-filled sac reducing friction between tissues).

## Phases of Tissue Healing
1. **Inflammatory Phase (0-6 days)**: Swelling, redness, heat, pain. Goal: Protect tissue, control inflammation (RICE/PRICE).
2. **Proliferative Phase (3-21 days)**: New tissue formation (collagen deposition). Tissue is weak and easily re-injured. Gentle, protected movement begins.
3. **Remodeling Phase (Day 14 to months)**: Collagen reorganizes along lines of stress. Progressive loading and functional movement are essential stimuli for proper remodeling.

## Common Injuries and Exercise Modifications

### Rotator Cuff Injuries
One or more of the four rotator cuff tendons (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis) are affected. The supraspinatus is most commonly injured.
- **Avoid**: Overhead pressing, lateral raises above 90 degrees, behind-the-neck movements.
- **Include**: Scapular stabilization, external rotation strengthening, low-level band work.

### Shoulder Impingement
Compression of rotator cuff tendons and/or bursa under the acromion.
- **Avoid**: Repetitive overhead movements, narrow-grip pressing.
- **Include**: Scapular retraction exercises, posterior capsule stretching, rotator cuff strengthening.

### Low-Back Pain / Disc Pathology
Herniated or bulging intervertebral discs.
- **Disc herniation direction matters**: Posterior/lateral herniation -- extension exercises often helpful (McKenzie approach). Avoid spinal flexion under load.
- **Include**: Core stabilization (McGill protocol), hip mobility, neutral spine training.
- **Avoid**: Spinal flexion exercises, unsupported forward bending, heavy axial loading.

### Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (Runner's Knee)
Anterior knee pain related to tracking dysfunction of the patella.
- **Contributing factors**: Weak VMO, tight IT band, weak hip abductors/external rotators, foot overpronation.
- **Include**: Hip and knee strengthening (closed-chain), IT band flexibility, foot orthotics if indicated.
- **Avoid**: Deep squatting, excessive downhill running, prolonged sitting with flexed knees.

### Iliotibial Band Syndrome (ITBS)
Pain on lateral aspect of knee from IT band friction over lateral femoral condyle.
- **Include**: IT band stretching and foam rolling, hip abductor strengthening.
- **Avoid**: Running on cambered surfaces, excessive downhill running, worn shoes.

### Ankle Sprains
Most commonly inversion sprain (lateral ankle ligaments). 
- **Rehabilitation focus**: Proprioception training (wobble board, single-leg balance), peroneal strengthening.
- **Graduated return**: Protective taping/bracing, progressive loading, sport-specific drills.

### Achilles Tendinopathy
- **Include**: Eccentric heel drops (Alfredson protocol), calf flexibility, foot intrinsic strengthening.
- **Avoid**: Aggressive calf stretching in acute phase, hill running.

### Plantar Fasciitis
- **Include**: Plantar fascia and calf stretching, foot intrinsic strengthening, night splints.
- **Avoid**: Barefoot walking on hard surfaces, flat unsupportive footwear.

### Shin Splints (Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome)
- **Contributing factors**: Training errors (too much too soon), hard surfaces, improper footwear, foot overpronation.
- **Management**: Relative rest, activity modification (cross-training), gradual return to impact.

## Trainer's Role in Injury Management
1. **Prevention**: Proper program design, appropriate progression, screening for risk factors, teaching correct technique.
2. **Recognition**: Know when to refer -- persistent pain, swelling, giving way, loss of function, pain at rest or at night.
3. **Modification**: Cross-training strategies to maintain fitness while tissues heal. Modify exercises within the client's pain-free range.
4. **Communication**: With client consent, coordinate with the treating healthcare provider to ensure the exercise program complements rehabilitation.

## Cross-Training for Injury
When an injury prevents a specific activity, substitute with:
- **Running injury**: Cycling, swimming, deep-water running, elliptical.
- **Upper-body injury**: Focus on lower-body and core training.
- **Lower-body injury**: Focus on upper-body and core training; seated cardio options.

## Key Principle
Trainers do not treat, diagnose, or rehabilitate injuries. They facilitate safe exercise within the boundaries set by the treating medical professional and the trainer's scope of practice. Any injury beyond a mild strain or sprain, or any pain lasting more than 1-2 weeks, warrants medical referral.
