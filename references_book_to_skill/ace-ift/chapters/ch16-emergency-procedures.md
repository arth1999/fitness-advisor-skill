# Chapter 16: Emergency Procedures

## Emergency Preparedness
Every fitness facility and independent trainer must have a written emergency response plan. The plan must be reviewed regularly and practiced through drills. Key elements include:

- Designated emergency coordinator and chain of command
- Emergency equipment location (AED, first aid kit, phone)
- Evacuation routes and meeting points
- Emergency contact numbers posted
- Incident report forms

## CPR and AED
ACE requires all certified personal trainers to maintain current CPR certification with AED training. Online-only courses are NOT accepted; the course must include a hands-on, in-person skills session. AEDs should be easily accessible and staff should know their location. Survival rates from sudden cardiac arrest decrease by ~10% per minute without defibrillation.

## Common Medical Emergencies

### Choking
- Partial obstruction: Victim can cough, speak, or breathe. Encourage coughing.
- Complete obstruction: Victim cannot cough, speak, or breathe. Universal choking sign (hands to throat).
  - Conscious adult: Heimlich maneuver (abdominal thrusts).
  - Unconscious: Begin CPR. Before rescue breaths, check for and remove visible object.
  - Pregnant or obese: Chest thrusts instead of abdominal thrusts.

### Asthma Attack
- Assist client into comfortable position (usually sitting upright).
- Help administer rescue inhaler if available.
- Call EMS if no improvement, rescue inhaler not available, or severe distress.

### Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident)
Use FAST assessment:
- **F**ace: Ask person to smile. Is one side drooping?
- **A**rms: Ask person to raise both arms. Does one drift down?
- **S**peech: Ask person to repeat a simple phrase. Is speech slurred or strange?
- **T**ime: Call EMS immediately if any signs present. Note time symptoms began.

### Heart Attack (Myocardial Infarction)
Signs and symptoms: Chest pain/discomfort (pressure, squeezing, fullness), pain radiating to arm(s), shoulder, neck, jaw, or back; shortness of breath; cold sweat; nausea; lightheadedness.
- Call EMS immediately.
- Have person rest in comfortable position (semi-sitting often best).
- If not allergic or contraindicated, one adult aspirin may be given.
- If person becomes unresponsive, begin CPR and use AED.

### Seizures
- Clear area of dangerous objects. Do NOT restrain the person.
- Do NOT put anything in the mouth.
- Protect the head with soft padding.
- After seizure: Place in recovery position (on side). Check airway.
- Call EMS if: Seizure lasts > 5 minutes, person does not regain consciousness, another seizure begins, person is injured, this is first seizure, person is pregnant or has diabetes.

### Diabetic Emergencies
- **Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar)**: Sweating, shaking, confusion, irritability, rapid heartbeat. If conscious and able to swallow, give sugar (juice, glucose tablets, candy). Improves within 10-15 minutes.
- **Hyperglycemia (high blood sugar)**: Gradual onset, fruity breath, frequent urination, thirst, confusion in severe cases.
- If unsure whether hyper- or hypoglycemia, give sugar. Hypoglycemia is the more immediate danger and sugar will not significantly worsen acute hyperglycemia.
- Call EMS if person loses consciousness or does not improve within 15 minutes.

### Heat-Related Emergencies
- **Heat cramps**: Painful muscle spasms. Rest in cool place, hydrate, gentle stretching.
- **Heat exhaustion**: Heavy sweating, weakness, cold/pale/clammy skin, weak pulse, possible fainting. Move to cool place, loosen clothing, apply cool wet cloths, sip water.
- **Heat stroke (MEDICAL EMERGENCY)**: Body temp > 104F (40C), hot/dry skin (no sweating), rapid strong pulse, possible unconsciousness. Call EMS immediately. Move to cooler environment, cool rapidly with whatever available.

### Shock (Hypoperfusion)
Life-threatening condition where circulatory system fails to deliver adequate oxygen to tissues.
- Signs: Pale, cool, clammy skin; rapid weak pulse; rapid shallow breathing; altered mental status; nausea.
- Actions: Call EMS. Lay person flat, elevate legs ~12 inches (unless head/spine/neck injury suspected). Keep warm. Do NOT give food or drink.

## Spinal and Neck Injuries
Suspect spinal injury with: any significant trauma (falls, collisions), neck or back pain, numbness/tingling in extremities, altered consciousness, obvious deformity. Do NOT move the person. Stabilize head and neck manually. Call EMS.

## Musculoskeletal Injuries -- Acute Management
For acute sprains, strains, or contusions, follow PRICE:
- **P**rotect: Prevent further injury.
- **R**estrict activity/Rest: Avoid movements that cause pain.
- **I**ce: 15-20 minutes every 1-2 hours for first 24-72 hours.
- **C**ompression: Elastic bandage to limit swelling.
- **E**levate: Above heart level when possible.

## Incident Documentation
After any emergency requiring intervention, complete a detailed incident report including: date/time, location, description of incident, persons involved, actions taken, outcome, witnesses, and recommendations to prevent recurrence. Incident reports serve legal, quality-improvement, and risk-management purposes.
