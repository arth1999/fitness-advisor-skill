# Chapter 12: Water and Electrolytes

## Core Idea
Water comprises ~60% of adult body weight and is distributed between intracellular (2/3) and extracellular (1/3) compartments. Sodium, potassium, and chloride are the primary electrolytes determining fluid distribution, osmotic pressure, and acid-base balance. Water and sodium balance are inextricably linked — sodium concentration in the ECF determines ECF volume.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Body Water Compartmentalization (70-kg man)**:
  - Total body water: 42 L (60% body weight).
  - Intracellular fluid (ICF): 28 L (67% of TBW). Major cation: K+ (156 mEq/L). Major anion: phosphate + protein.
  - Extracellular fluid (ECF): 14 L (33% of TBW). Interstitial: 11 L; Plasma/Intravascular: 3 L. Major cation: Na+ (142-145 mEq/L). Major anion: Cl- (103-116 mEq/L).
  - When to use: Predicting fluid shifts — hyponatremia causes cellular swelling (water moves into cells); hypernatremia causes cellular shrinkage.

- **Starling's Forces for Capillary Fluid Exchange**:
  - Filtration pressure = (Ppl + Πisf) - (Πpl + Pisf)
  - Ppl = capillary hydrostatic pressure (~25 mmHg arteriolar end)
  - Πpl = plasma colloidal osmotic pressure (~28 mmHg, from proteins)
  - Net filtration at arteriolar end (~8 mmHg); net reabsorption at venular end.
  - When to use: Understanding edema formation — hypoalbuminemia reduces Πpl, causing net filtration and peripheral edema.

- **Water Balance via ADH and Thirst**:
  - ↑ ECF osmolarity (or ↓ blood volume/pressure) → hypothalamic osmoreceptors → posterior pituitary releases ADH (vasopressin) → renal collecting duct water reabsorption ↑ (aquaporin-2 insertion).
  - Simultaneous thirst stimulation → water intake.
  - When to use: Explaining fluid retention in dehydration or dilutional states.

## Key Concepts
- **Sodium (Na+)**: Primary ECF cation; determines ECF volume and osmolarity. AI: 1500 mg (19-50 yr). Functions: nerve impulse transmission, muscle contraction, nutrient cotransport (glucose, amino acids). Excess linked to hypertension.
- **Potassium (K+)**: Primary ICF cation; maintains intracellular osmolarity. AI: 4700 mg. Functions: nerve impulse transmission, muscle contraction (especially cardiac), acid-base balance. High Na+:K+ ratio associated with hypertension.
- **Chloride (Cl-)**: Primary ECF anion; component of gastric HCl; involved in acid-base balance (chloride shift in RBCs). AI: 2300 mg.
- **Acid-Base Buffer Systems**: Bicarbonate (HCO3-/H2CO3, primary ECF buffer), Phosphate (HPO42-/H2PO4-, primary ICF buffer), Protein (hemoglobin, plasma proteins). Respiratory regulation (CO2 exhalation via ventilation rate) + Renal regulation (H+ secretion, HCO3- reabsorption/generation).
- **Water AI**: 2.7 L/day (F), 3.7 L/day (M) — includes water from beverages (~75-80%) and foods (~20-25%). Metabolic water contributes ~200-300 mL/day.

## Mental Models
- **The Na+-K+ ATPase pump as the fundamental battery**: Maintains high intracellular K+ and high extracellular Na+ at the cost of ATP; creates the electrochemical gradient driving secondary active transport (glucose, amino acids).
- **Water follows sodium**: In most body compartments, water movement is passive and follows osmotic gradients created primarily by sodium distribution.
- **The kidney as the master regulator**: Filters plasma ~60 times/day; precisely adjusts water and electrolyte reabsorption/excretion in response to hormonal signals (ADH, aldosterone, ANP).

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating water needs as a fixed number**: Water requirements vary dramatically with environment, activity level, and metabolic rate — the AI is a population-level guideline, not an individual prescription.
- **Assuming all salt sensitivity is equal**: ~50% of hypertensive individuals are salt-sensitive; ~25% of normotensive individuals are salt-sensitive. Sodium restriction is most effective in salt-sensitive subgroups.
- **Overlooking potassium for blood pressure**: Increasing potassium intake can be as effective as reducing sodium for blood pressure management in some individuals.

## Key Takeaways
1. The DASH diet (rich in K+, Ca2+, Mg2+ from fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy) lowers blood pressure as effectively as single-drug therapy for mild hypertension.
2. Hyponatremia (Na+ <135 mEq/L) is the most common electrolyte disorder in hospitalized patients and can be life-threatening (cerebral edema).
3. Sweat contains 20-80 mEq Na+/L — heavy sweating during prolonged exercise can cause significant sodium losses requiring replacement beyond just water.
4. Acid-base disorders are classified by primary cause: respiratory (CO2) vs. metabolic (HCO3-); the body compensates using the opposite system.
5. Water absorption is passive and coupled to solute (especially sodium) absorption — oral rehydration solutions leverage this with glucose + sodium.
