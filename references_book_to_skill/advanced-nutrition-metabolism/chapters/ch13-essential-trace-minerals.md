# Chapter 13: Essential Trace and Ultratrace Minerals

## Core Idea
Trace minerals (required <100 mg/day) and ultratrace minerals (<1 mg/day) serve as obligatory cofactors in metalloenzymes, oxygen transport proteins, and hormone systems. Their essentiality is established through deficiency syndromes. Despite tiny quantities in the body (milligrams to a few grams), their absence produces severe metabolic dysfunction.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Iron Absorption Regulation via Hepcidin-Ferroportin Axis**:
  - Hepcidin (liver peptide hormone) binds and internalizes ferroportin (the only known cellular iron exporter on enterocytes, macrophages, hepatocytes).
  - ↑ Hepcidin (high iron stores, inflammation) → ↓ ferroportin → ↓ iron absorption + ↓ iron release from stores.
  - ↓ Hepcidin (iron deficiency, hypoxia, erythropoiesis demand) → ↑ ferroportin → ↑ iron absorption + ↑ iron mobilization.
  - When to use: Understanding anemia of chronic disease (high hepcidin from inflammation blocks iron release despite adequate stores).

- **Heme vs. Nonheme Iron Absorption**:
  - Heme iron (meat, fish, poultry): Absorbed intact via HCP1 transporter; ~25% absorption; unaffected by dietary inhibitors.
  - Nonheme iron (plants, supplements): Requires reduction (Fe3+ → Fe2+) by duodenal cytochrome b reductase; transported by DMT1; ~2-20% absorption; strongly affected by enhancers (vitamin C, meat factor) and inhibitors (phytates, polyphenols, calcium, oxalates).
  - When to use: Counseling vegetarians about iron status — they require ~1.8× the iron RDA due to lower bioavailability.

## Key Concepts
- **Iron**: Body content 2-4 g; ~65% in hemoglobin. Functions: O2 transport (hemoglobin, myoglobin), electron transfer (cytochromes), antioxidant (catalase), thyroid hormone synthesis. RDA: 8 mg (M), 18 mg (F, reproductive age). Deficiency = microcytic hypochromic anemia, fatigue, impaired immunity. Toxicity = hemochromatosis (hereditary), organ damage from free radical production. UL: 45 mg.
- **Zinc**: Body content 1.5-3.0 g. Cofactor for >300 enzymes (DNA/RNA polymerase, carbonic anhydrase, alcohol dehydrogenase, superoxide dismutase). Functions: growth, immune function, wound healing, taste acuity, sexual maturation. RDA: 11 mg (M), 8 mg (F). Deficiency = growth retardation, hypogonadism, dermatitis, impaired wound healing. UL: 40 mg.
- **Copper**: Body content 50-150 mg. Cofactor for: cytochrome c oxidase (ETC), lysyl oxidase (collagen/elastin cross-linking), dopamine β-monooxygenase, ceruloplasmin (iron oxidation for transport), superoxide dismutase. RDA: 900 μg. Deficiency = anemia (impaired iron mobilization), neutropenia, bone/vascular abnormalities. UL: 10,000 μg.
- **Selenium**: Body content ~20 mg. Component of selenoproteins (as selenocysteine — the 21st amino acid): glutathione peroxidases (antioxidant), thioredoxin reductases, iodothyronine deiodinases (T4 → T3 conversion). RDA: 55 μg. Deficiency = Keshan disease (cardiomyopathy), Kashin-Beck disease (osteoarthropathy). UL: 400 μg.
- **Iodine**: Body content 15-20 mg. Sole function: thyroid hormone synthesis (T3, T4) — regulates metabolic rate, growth, development. RDA: 150 μg. Deficiency = goiter, cretinism (fetal deficiency), hypothyroidism. UL: 1100 μg.
- **Chromium**: Body content 4-6 mg. Proposed role: potentiates insulin signaling (mechanism unclear). AI: 35 μg (M), 25 μg (F). Deficiency (rare) = glucose intolerance. No UL established.
- **Manganese**: Body content 10-20 mg. Cofactor for: glycosyltransferases, arginase, pyruvate carboxylase, Mn-SOD. AI: 2.3 mg (M), 1.8 mg (F). Deficiency rare; toxicity (inhalation) = Parkinson-like symptoms. UL: 11 mg.
- **Molybdenum**: Body content ~2 mg. Cofactor for: xanthine oxidase, aldehyde oxidase, sulfite oxidase. RDA: 45 μg. Deficiency extremely rare. UL: 2000 μg.

## Mental Models
- **The hepcidin thermostat**: Body iron is regulated at absorption, not excretion — hepcidin is the thermostat controlling dietary iron uptake; there is no regulated excretion pathway for iron.
- **Mineral antagonism network**: Zn and Cu compete for absorption (high Zn → Cu deficiency); Fe and Zn compete; Ca inhibits Fe, Zn absorption. Multi-mineral supplementation must account for these interactions.
- **Selenocysteine as the 21st amino acid**: Unlike other mineral cofactors that bind to proteins, selenium is cotranslationally incorporated into selenoproteins via a unique UGA codon recoding mechanism.

## Anti-patterns
- **Supplementing iron without confirming deficiency**: Iron overload (especially in males and postmenopausal females) promotes oxidative stress; screen ferritin before supplementing.
- **Treating zinc as a minor immune booster**: While zinc is essential for immune function, high-dose zinc supplementation impairs copper absorption and can cause neutropenia.
- **Assuming all iodine deficiency is dietary**: Goitrogens (thiocyanates in cassava, brassica vegetables; perchlorate) can cause iodine deficiency even with adequate dietary intake.

## Key Takeaways
1. Vitamin C enhances nonheme iron absorption 2-3 fold — consume vitamin C-rich foods with iron-rich plant meals.
2. Zinc absorption is homeostatically regulated — absorption increases during deficiency and decreases during excess.
3. Selenium content of foods depends on soil selenium concentration — Brazil nuts are the richest dietary source (1 nut can provide >100% RDA).
4. Iodized salt is the primary public health strategy for iodine deficiency prevention; sea salt and kosher salt are typically not iodized.
5. Wilson's disease (copper accumulation) and Menkes disease (copper deficiency) illustrate the critical importance of copper transporters ATP7A and ATP7B.
