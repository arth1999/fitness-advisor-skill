# Chapter 3: Carbohydrates

## Core Idea
Carbohydrates supply over half of dietary energy. Their metabolism centers on glucose: its digestion from starch, absorption via specific transporters, and intracellular fate through glycolysis, the TCA cycle, oxidative phosphorylation, glycogenesis, glycogenolysis, the pentose phosphate pathway, and gluconeogenesis — all tightly regulated by insulin and glucagon.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Carbohydrate Classification Hierarchy**: Simple (monosaccharides, disaccharides) vs. Complex (oligosaccharides 3-10 units, polysaccharides >10 units).
  - When to use: Categorizing dietary carbohydrate sources and predicting their digestive requirements.

- **Glucose Transporter System (GLUT 1-5 + SGLT1)**:
  - SGLT1: Na+-dependent active transport of glucose/galactose into enterocytes (apical).
  - GLUT2: Facilitated diffusion of glucose, galactose, fructose across basolateral membrane.
  - GLUT4: Insulin-dependent transporter on muscle and adipose tissue.
  - When to use: Predicting which tissues take up glucose under fed vs. fasting conditions.

- **Glycolysis 10-Step Pathway**: Glucose → Glucose-6-P → Fructose-6-P → Fructose-1,6-bisP → 2 Glyceraldehyde-3-P → 2 Pyruvate. Net yield: 2 ATP + 2 NADH.
  - Key regulatory enzymes: Hexokinase/glucokinase, Phosphofructokinase-1 (rate-limiting, inhibited by citrate, activated by AMP), Pyruvate kinase.

- **Glycemic Index and Glycemic Load Framework**: GI = blood glucose response relative to reference; GL = GI × grams carbohydrate per serving / 100.
  - When to use: Predicting postprandial glucose excursions and insulin demand.

## Key Concepts
- **Anomeric carbon**: The carbonyl carbon in monosaccharides; forms α or β configurations in cyclic structures. α-amylase only cleaves α(1-4) bonds — not β(1-4) cellulose bonds.
- **Glycogen**: Highly branched glucose polymer (α(1-4) + α(1-6) bonds); stored in liver (~100 g) and muscle (~400 g); muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase so cannot export free glucose.
- **TCA Cycle**: Acetyl-CoA + Oxaloacetate → Citrate → ... → Oxaloacetate; produces NADH, FADH2, GTP; amphibolic — both catabolic and anabolic.
- **Oxidative Phosphorylation**: Electron transport chain (Complexes I-IV) creates proton gradient; ATP synthase produces ~2.5 ATP per NADH, ~1.5 ATP per FADH2.
- **Gluconeogenesis**: Synthesis of glucose from lactate, glycerol, and glucogenic amino acids; essentially reverse glycolysis with bypass reactions at three irreversible steps.
- **Cori Cycle**: Muscle lactate → blood → liver → glucose (gluconeogenesis) → blood → muscle.
- **Pentose Phosphate Pathway**: Produces NADPH (for fatty acid synthesis) and ribose-5-phosphate (for nucleotide synthesis).

## Mental Models
- **The glucose trap**: Phosphorylation (glucose → glucose-6-phosphate) traps glucose inside cells because phosphorylated sugars cannot cross membranes.
- **Metabolic branch points**: Glucose-6-phosphate can go to glycolysis (energy), glycogenesis (storage), or pentose phosphate pathway (NADPH/ribose) — the choice depends on cellular energy state.
- **Feed-forward activation**: Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate activates pyruvate kinase, ensuring efficient glycolytic flux.

## Anti-patterns
- **Confusing α and β glycosidic bonds**: α-amylase digests only α(1-4) bonds in starch; β(1-4) bonds in cellulose and lactose require different enzymes (cellulase absent in humans, lactase present on brush border).
- **Assuming fructose metabolism = glucose metabolism**: Fructose bypasses phosphofructokinase regulation, enters glycolysis at the triose phosphate level, and can drive unrestrained lipogenesis.

## Key Takeaways
1. Phosphofructokinase-1 is the single most important regulatory enzyme in glycolysis — activated by AMP, inhibited by citrate and ATP.
2. Insulin stimulates GLUT4 translocation to cell membranes in muscle and adipose tissue — this is the mechanism of postprandial glucose clearance.
3. The liver uses glucokinase (high Km, low affinity) so it only phosphorylates glucose when concentrations are elevated post-meal.
4. Muscle glycogen cannot contribute directly to blood glucose because muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase.
5. The Cori cycle and alanine-glucose cycle recycle muscle glycolytic products back to hepatic glucose.
6. NADPH from the pentose phosphate pathway is essential for fatty acid synthesis and maintaining reduced glutathione.
7. Type 2 diabetes involves impaired GLUT4 translocation and insulin resistance.
