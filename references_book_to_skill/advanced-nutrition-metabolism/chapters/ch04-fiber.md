# Chapter 4: Fiber

## Core Idea
Fiber is not a single compound but a heterogeneous group of nondigestible carbohydrates and lignin with distinct physicochemical properties — solubility, viscosity, and fermentability — that determine their physiological effects and health benefits. The 2015 Dietary Guidelines labeled fiber a "nutrient of public health concern."

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Fiber Classification by Definition**: Dietary fiber (intact and intrinsic in plants) vs. Functional fiber (isolated/extracted/manufactured nondigestible carbohydrates with proven physiological benefits) vs. Total fiber (dietary + functional).
  - When to use: Reading food labels and formulating dietary recommendations.

- **Three-Property Framework for Fiber Effects**:
  - **Solubility**: Water-soluble fibers (pectins, β-glucans, gums, fructans, psyllium) vs. insoluble (cellulose, lignin, some hemicelluloses).
  - **Viscosity/Gel Formation**: Viscous fibers (β-glucans, pectins, guar gum, psyllium) slow gastric emptying, delay nutrient absorption, lower serum cholesterol, and moderate blood glucose.
  - **Fermentability**: Fermentable fibers (pectins, β-glucans, gums, fructans, resistant starch) produce short-chain fatty acids (acetate, propionate, butyrate) via colonic bacteria.
  - When to use: Selecting the right fiber for a specific health goal (e.g., cholesterol-lowering requires viscous, gel-forming fiber).

## Key Concepts
- **Cellulose**: Linear β(1-4) glucose polymer; water-insoluble; non-fermentable; promotes laxation via mechanical stimulation.
- **Hemicellulose**: Heterogeneous polysaccharides with hexose and pentose backbones; variable solubility and fermentability depending on side-chain sugars.
- **Pectins**: α(1-4)-linked D-galacturonic acid backbone; water-soluble; forms viscous gels; almost completely fermented.
- **β-Glucans**: Glucose homopolymers with mixed β(1-4) and β(1-3) linkages (oats, barley); FDA-approved health claim for cholesterol reduction at ≥3 g/day.
- **Fructans (inulin, oligofructose)**: β(2-1)-linked fructose chains; water-soluble; highly fermentable; function as prebiotics promoting bifidobacteria growth.
- **Resistant Starch (RS1-RS4)**: RS1 (physically inaccessible), RS2 (granular, e.g., raw banana), RS3 (retrograded after cooking/cooling), RS4 (chemically modified).
- **Psyllium**: Mucilage with ~70-80% soluble polysaccharides; FDA-approved health claim at 7 g viscous fiber/day for LDL reduction.
- **Short-Chain Fatty Acids (SCFAs)**: Acetate, propionate, butyrate — fermentation products; butyrate is the primary fuel for colonocytes.

## Mental Models
- **Fiber property → Physiological effect mapping**: Viscous → delayed gastric emptying, cholesterol binding; Fermentable → SCFA production, prebiotic effect; Insoluble/non-fermentable → fecal bulking, reduced transit time.
- **SCFAs as metabolic signals**: Not just energy for colonocytes — SCFAs act as signaling molecules regulating GLP-1, PYY secretion and influencing appetite.

## Anti-patterns
- **Treating all fiber as interchangeable for health claims**: Only specific fibers at specific doses have FDA-approved health claims (β-glucan 3 g/day, psyllium 7 g/day for cholesterol).
- **Ignoring water intake with fiber supplements**: Psyllium-containing products require adequate fluid to prevent choking and esophageal obstruction.

## Key Takeaways
1. Viscous, gel-forming fibers (β-glucans, psyllium, pectin) lower serum LDL cholesterol by binding bile acids and reducing reabsorption.
2. Fermentable fibers produce SCFAs, with butyrate serving as the preferred fuel for colonocytes and exhibiting anti-carcinogenic properties.
3. Resistant starch type 3 forms when starchy foods are cooked then cooled (potatoes, rice, pasta) — a practical dietary strategy.
4. The AI for total fiber is 38 g/day (men) and 25 g/day (women) for adults 19-50 years; average U.S. intake is well below this.
5. Fructans (inulin, FOS) function as prebiotics, selectively stimulating bifidobacteria — found in chicory, asparagus, onions, garlic, artichokes.
