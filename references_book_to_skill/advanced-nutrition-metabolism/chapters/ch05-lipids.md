# Chapter 5: Lipids

## Core Idea
Lipids are defined by their solubility in organic solvents, not by a common structure. This chapter covers fatty acids, triacylglycerols, phospholipids, sphingolipids, sterols, and lipoproteins — their digestion requires bile emulsification, their absorption requires chylomicron formation, and their metabolism integrates with carbohydrate and protein pathways through acetyl-CoA.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Fatty Acid Nomenclature Systems**:
  - Delta (Δ) system: counts from carboxyl end (e.g., 18:2 Δ9,12 for linoleic acid).
  - Omega (ω or n) system: counts from methyl end (e.g., 18:2 n-6).
  - When to use: Δ system for biochemical pathway analysis; n-system for nutritional classification (n-3 vs. n-6 families).

- **Lipoprotein Metabolism Cascade**:
  - Chylomicrons (dietary fat transport) → Chylomicron remnants → Liver.
  - VLDL (hepatic fat export) → IDL → LDL (cholesterol delivery to tissues).
  - HDL (reverse cholesterol transport, returning cholesterol to liver).
  - When to use: Understanding cardiovascular risk from lipid profiles — high LDL promotes atherosclerosis; high HDL is protective.

- **Essential Fatty Acid Hierarchy**: Only linoleic acid (18:2 n-6) and α-linolenic acid (18:3 n-3) are essential — humans lack Δ12 and Δ15 desaturases.
  - n-6 pathway: Linoleic → γ-linolenic → arachidonic acid (20:4 n-6) — precursor of pro-inflammatory eicosanoids.
  - n-3 pathway: α-linolenic → EPA (20:5 n-3) → DHA (22:6 n-3) — precursors of anti-inflammatory eicosanoids.

## Key Concepts
- **Triacylglycerols (TAGs)**: Glycerol + 3 fatty acids; comprise ~95% of dietary fat and the majority of adipose tissue stores.
- **Phospholipids**: Glycerol backbone with fatty acids at sn-1 and sn-2, phosphate + polar head group at sn-3; form cell membrane bilayers; phosphatidylcholine is most abundant.
- **Cholesterol**: Sterol with rigid planar ring structure; component of cell membranes (modulates fluidity); precursor of bile acids, steroid hormones, vitamin D.
- **Phytosterols**: Plant sterols that compete with cholesterol for intestinal absorption, reducing serum cholesterol.
- **CETP (Cholesteryl Ester Transfer Protein)**: Transfers cholesteryl esters from HDL to VLDL/LDL in exchange for TAGs.
- **Brown fat thermogenesis**: UCP-1 (thermogenin) uncouples oxidative phosphorylation, generating heat instead of ATP.
- **Ethanol metabolism**: Alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) pathway (cytosol, primary at moderate intake) and MEOS (microsomal ethanol oxidizing system, induced at high intake); both produce acetaldehyde.

## Mental Models
- **Amphipathic assembly**: Lipids with polar heads and nonpolar tails spontaneously organize into bilayers, micelles, and lipoprotein surfaces — structure follows polarity.
- **The acetyl-CoA crossroads**: Fatty acids degrade to acetyl-CoA via β-oxidation; acetyl-CoA cannot be converted to glucose in humans (irreversible pyruvate dehydrogenase reaction).
- **Lipid raft concept**: Cholesterol and sphingolipids create ordered membrane microdomains that concentrate signaling proteins.

## Anti-patterns
- **Confusing dietary cholesterol with serum cholesterol**: Saturated and trans fatty acids have a greater impact on serum LDL cholesterol than dietary cholesterol itself for most people.
- **Assuming all n-6 fatty acids are pro-inflammatory**: While arachidonic acid produces pro-inflammatory eicosanoids, linoleic acid itself has neutral or beneficial metabolic effects.
- **Equating fat-free with healthy**: Fat-free products often contain added sugars; dietary fat is needed for fat-soluble vitamin absorption.

## Key Takeaways
1. Trans fatty acids from partial hydrogenation raise LDL and lower HDL — the worst lipid profile effect of any fatty acid class.
2. EPA and DHA from marine sources are more potent anti-inflammatory agents than α-linolenic acid from plants.
3. Cholesterol synthesis is regulated at HMG-CoA reductase — the target of statin drugs.
4. Phosphatidylinositol-4,5-bisphosphate hydrolysis produces two second messengers (IP3 and DAG) in the insulin signaling cascade.
5. Moderate ethanol consumption (~1 drink/day women, ~2 men) may increase HDL; chronic excess causes fatty liver, alcoholic hepatitis, and cirrhosis.
6. The n-6:n-3 ratio in ancestral diets was approximately 1:1; current Western diets are >10:1, favoring a pro-inflammatory state.
