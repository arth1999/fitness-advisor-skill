# Chapter 7: Integration & Regulation of Metabolism and the Impact of Exercise

## Core Idea
Metabolic pathways do not operate in isolation. Pyruvate and acetyl-CoA are the critical junctions connecting carbohydrate, lipid, and protein metabolism. The fed-fast cycle (fed → postabsorptive → fasting → starvation) and exercise intensity determine fuel selection across tissues, coordinated by insulin, glucagon, epinephrine, cortisol, and growth hormone.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Fed-Fast Cycle (Four Metabolic States)**:
  - **Fed state** (~0-3 h post-meal): High insulin; glucose uptake, glycogen synthesis, lipogenesis, protein synthesis. Liver glycogen preferentially made from gluconeogenic precursors (lactate), not dietary glucose directly.
  - **Postabsorptive state** (~3-18 h): Glucagon rises; hepatic glycogenolysis → blood glucose; adipose lipolysis → FFA for muscle; gluconeogenesis from lactate, alanine, glycerol.
  - **Fasting state** (~18 h-2 days): Glycogen depleted; gluconeogenesis from amino acids (muscle proteolysis) dominates; ketone body production begins.
  - **Starvation state** (>2 days): Protein-sparing metabolic shift; accelerated lipolysis, glycerol becomes primary gluconeogenic precursor; brain adapts to ketone bodies (~2/3 of fuel by day 40); kidney contributes ~50% of glucose production.
  - When to use: Predicting metabolic fuel selection at any point in a 24-hour cycle or during prolonged energy deficit.

- **AMPK as Master Energy Sensor**: Activated by high AMP:ATP ratio (low energy status). Effects:
  - ↑ GLUT4 translocation (insulin-independent glucose uptake)
  - ↑ Fatty acid oxidation (phosphorylates ACC, reducing malonyl-CoA, disinhibiting CAT I)
  - ↓ Glycogen synthesis, ↓ Gluconeogenesis, ↓ Cholesterol synthesis, ↓ Protein synthesis (via mTOR inhibition)
  - When to use: Understanding the metabolic rationale for exercise, fasting, and metformin's mechanism of action.

- **Malonyl-CoA as Fuel Switch**: High malonyl-CoA → inhibits CAT I → blocks fatty acid entry into mitochondria → favors glucose oxidation and fatty acid synthesis. Low malonyl-CoA → permits β-oxidation.
  - When to use: Explaining why high-carbohydrate meals suppress fat oxidation and why fasting/exercise promote it.

- **Three Energy Systems for Muscle ATP**:
  - **ATP-Phosphocreatine system**: Immediate, 0-25 sec max effort (sprinting, weightlifting).
  - **Lactic acid system (anaerobic glycolysis)**: Rapid, 20-75 sec at max (200-800 m sprint). Glucose → 2 lactate + 2 ATP.
  - **Oxidative system (aerobic)**: Sustained, >2-3 min (endurance). Complete oxidation of glucose and fatty acids via TCA cycle + ETC.
  - When to use: Designing training programs and predicting fuel use by sport type.

## Key Concepts
- **Tissue-Specific Fuel Preferences**: Brain — glucose obligate (adapts to ketones in starvation); RBCs — glucose only (no mitochondria); Cardiac muscle — fatty acids preferred; Resting skeletal muscle — fatty acids; Exercising muscle — shifts from fat to glycogen as intensity increases.
- **VO2 max**: Maximum oxygen consumption rate; measure of cardiovascular fitness; used to normalize exercise intensity (% VO2 max).
- **Muscle Fiber Types**: Type I (slow-twitch, oxidative, fatigue-resistant, high mitochondria/myoglobin); Type IIa (fast-twitch, oxidative-glycolytic, intermediate); Type IIx (fast-twitch, glycolytic, fatigable, white).
- **Cori Cycle**: Muscle lactate → blood → liver → glucose → blood → muscle; critical during high-intensity exercise.
- **Cortisol**: Stimulates gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis, lipolysis, and muscle proteolysis; chronically elevated in prolonged fasting and overtraining.
- **Growth Hormone**: Stimulates lipolysis (especially visceral fat), protein synthesis, and hepatic glucose output; secreted during fasting and strenuous exercise.

## Fuel Selection by Exercise Intensity (% VO2 max)
| Intensity | Primary Fuel | Secondary Fuel | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|-------|
| 25% (light activity) | Plasma FFA (80-90%) | Plasma glucose (minor) | Adipose lipolysis provides FFA |
| 65% (moderate-high) | Muscle glycogen + Muscle TAG | Plasma FFA (~50%) | Both stored and circulating fuels |
| 85% (very high) | Muscle glycogen (majority) | Plasma glucose (increased) | FFA utilization declines due to limited blood flow |

## Prolonged Exercise (65-75% VO2 max, 4 hours)
- First 30 min: 2/3 energy from muscle glycogen + TAG stores.
- After 2 h: Muscle glycogen depleted; plasma FFA becomes dominant fuel.
- After 2 h: Carbohydrate ingestion required to maintain plasma glucose — hepatic gluconeogenesis alone insufficient.
- Endurance training adaptations: ↑ mitochondria, ↑ fatty acid oxidation, ↑ glycogen storage capacity, carbohydrate-sparing effect.

## Carbohydrate Loading Protocols
- **Classical regimen** (1960s): 2 sessions exhaustive exercise → 2 days <10% CHO → 3 days >90% CHO + rest → event day 7. Yields glycogen ~220 mmol/kg. Side effects: irritability, dizziness.
- **Modified regimen**: Tapered exercise over 5 days → 3 days 50% CHO → 3 days 70% CHO → rest day 6. Increases glycogen 20-40% above normal with fewer side effects.
- **"Train low, compete high"**: Deliberately train with low glycogen to enhance mitochondrial adaptations and fatty acid oxidation capacity, then consume high CHO before competition.

## Hormonal Regulation Summary
- **Insulin**: ↑ Glycogenesis, glycolysis, fatty acid synthesis, protein synthesis, GLUT4 translocation; ↓ Gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, proteolysis.
- **Glucagon**: ↑ Glycogenolysis (liver only), gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, ketogenesis, fatty acid oxidation.
- **Epinephrine**: ↑ Glycogenolysis (liver + muscle), glycolysis (muscle), lipolysis; ↓ insulin secretion, glycogen synthesis.
- **Cortisol**: ↑ Gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis, lipolysis, muscle proteolysis.
- **Growth Hormone**: ↑ Lipolysis (visceral), protein synthesis, hepatic glucose output.

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming all tissues respond to all hormones equally**: Skeletal muscle lacks glucagon receptors — only liver and adipose tissue respond directly to glucagon.
- **Fasting as weight loss without considering protein loss**: Early fasting (18-48 h) causes significant muscle proteolysis for gluconeogenesis; only in starvation does protein-sparing occur.
- **Carbohydrate loading without tapering exercise**: Glycogen supercompensation requires prior glycogen depletion plus adequate rest.
- **Assuming supplements replace food for athletes**: Only caffeine (3 mg/kg), creatine (≥3 g/day), bicarbonate (0.3 g/kg), beta-alanine (4-6 g/day), and nitrate have confirmed ergogenic effects.

## Key Takeaways
1. The liver is the metabolic hub — all absorbed nutrients first pass through it; it performs gluconeogenesis, glycogenesis, ketogenesis, ureagenesis, and lipoprotein assembly.
2. Malonyl-CoA is the molecular switch between fatty acid oxidation (low) and synthesis (high); regulated by AMPK phosphorylation of acetyl-CoA carboxylase.
3. During prolonged high-intensity exercise, carbohydrate ingestion (60-80 g/h, glucose + fructose) is required after ~2 hours to sustain performance.
4. The brain accounts for 20-25% of resting energy expenditure but only ~2% of body weight; mental activity does not increase brain energy use.
5. Muscle glycogen depletion is strongly correlated with fatigue during endurance events; trained athletes store more and use it more slowly.
6. Hormonal control follows an anabolic-catabolic axis: insulin dominates the fed state; glucagon + epinephrine + cortisol dominate fasting and exercise.
7. Endurance training increases fatty acid oxidation capacity ~100%, sparing glycogen and extending time to exhaustion.
