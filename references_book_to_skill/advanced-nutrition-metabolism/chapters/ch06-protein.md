# Chapter 6: Protein

## Core Idea
Proteins are the body's "primary" molecules — structural, catalytic, transport, immune, and signaling functions all depend on amino acid sequences. Protein nutrition is ultimately about amino acid availability: the balance of essential (indispensable), nonessential (dispensable), and conditionally indispensable amino acids, and the inter-organ trafficking that distributes nitrogen throughout the body.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Amino Acid Classification (Four Systems)**:
  - **Structure**: Aliphatic, hydroxylic, sulfur-containing, acidic/amides, basic, aromatic, imino.
  - **Net charge**: Negative (Asp, Glu), Positive (Lys, Arg, His), Neutral (all others).
  - **Polarity**: Polar (charged or uncharged side chains interact with water), Nonpolar (hydrophobic, buried in protein cores).
  - **Essentiality**: Essential/indispensable (9 amino acids — must be dietary); Conditionally indispensable (Tyr, Cys, Pro, Arg, Gln — become essential under stress, disease, or immaturity).
  - When to use: Essentiality for dietary planning; polarity for predicting protein structure and function.

- **Protein Quality Evaluation Framework**:
  - **PDCAAS** (Protein Digestibility-Corrected Amino Acid Score): Compares essential amino acid profile to reference pattern, corrected for digestibility. Values >1.0 truncated to 1.0.
  - **DIAAS** (Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score): Newer method using ileal rather than fecal digestibility; can exceed 1.0.
  - When to use: Comparing protein sources — animal proteins generally score higher than plant proteins due to more complete EAA profiles.

- **Inter-Organ Amino Acid Trafficking**:
  - Muscle → Alanine/Glutamine → Liver (gluconeogenesis, ureagenesis).
  - Muscle → Glutamine → Kidney (ammoniagenesis, acid-base balance).
  - Muscle → Branched-chain amino acids (BCAA) → direct oxidation in muscle.
  - Gut → Alanine → Liver (portal delivery).
  - When to use: Understanding nitrogen balance in fasting, trauma, and critical illness.

## Key Concepts
- **Transamination**: Transfer of amino group from amino acid to α-ketoglutarate → glutamate + α-keto acid; catalyzed by aminotransferases (ALT, AST); requires PLP (vitamin B6).
- **Urea Cycle**: Liver-exclusive pathway; ammonia (from glutamate dehydrogenase) + aspartate → urea; eliminates nitrogen with minimal toxicity.
- **Glucogenic vs. Ketogenic Amino Acids**: Glucogenic amino acids can be converted to glucose via pyruvate or TCA cycle intermediates; ketogenic amino acids (Leu, Lys) produce acetyl-CoA/acetoacetate only.
- **mTOR (mechanistic Target of Rapamycin)**: Master kinase integrating amino acid availability (especially leucine), energy status, and growth factor signals to regulate protein synthesis.
- **Ubiquitin-Proteasome Pathway**: Tags proteins with ubiquitin for degradation in the proteasome; major regulated proteolytic system.
- **Protein Turnover**: Continuous synthesis and degradation; ~300 g protein synthesized daily despite intake of ~70-100 g; allows rapid adaptation.
- **Reference Man/Woman**: Reference man: 15% body fat, 44.8% muscle; Reference woman: 27% body fat, 36% muscle.

## Mental Models
- **Amino acid pool as currency**: Free amino acids in plasma (~35-65 mg/dL) represent a small but rapidly turning-over pool — not a storage depot.
- **The nitrogen economy**: All body protein is functional; there is no "storage protein." Nitrogen balance reflects the difference between intake and losses.
- **Conditional essentiality**: Under metabolic stress (trauma, sepsis, burns), arginine and glutamine consumption exceeds endogenous synthesis capacity.

## Anti-patterns
- **Assuming high protein intake always builds muscle**: Without resistance exercise stimulus, excess amino acids are oxidized or converted to fat, not stored as protein.
- **Treating all plant proteins as "incomplete"**: Soy protein is complete; combining complementary plant proteins within the same day (not necessarily the same meal) is sufficient.
- **Ignoring protein distribution across meals**: Consuming 25-30 g high-quality protein per meal maximizes muscle protein synthesis; consuming all protein at dinner is suboptimal.

## Key Takeaways
1. Leucine is a key anabolic signal — it activates mTOR, stimulating muscle protein synthesis.
2. Skeletal muscle accounts for 25-35% of whole-body protein turnover and serves as the body's primary amino acid reservoir during fasting and stress.
3. The glucose-alanine cycle: muscle exports nitrogen as alanine (derived from pyruvate + amino group from BCAA catabolism); liver converts alanine to glucose and urea.
4. Lysosomal (autophagic) and proteasomal (ubiquitin-tagged) pathways are the two major systems for intracellular protein degradation.
5. RDA for protein: 0.8 g/kg body weight/day for adults; higher needs during growth, pregnancy, lactation, and recovery from illness.
6. Protein has the highest thermic effect of food (20-30% increase in energy expenditure) compared to carbohydrate (5-10%) and fat (0-5%).
