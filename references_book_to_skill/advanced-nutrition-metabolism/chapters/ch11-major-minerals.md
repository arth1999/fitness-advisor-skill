# Chapter 11: Major Minerals

## Core Idea
Major minerals — calcium, phosphorus, and magnesium — are required in amounts >100 mg/day and constitute the structural matrix of bone (~99% of body calcium, ~85% of phosphorus, ~55% of magnesium) while also serving as electrolytes, enzyme cofactors, and signaling molecules. Their homeostasis is hormonally regulated through PTH, calcitriol, and calcitonin.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Calcium Homeostasis Triad**:
  - **PTH (Parathyroid Hormone)**: Responds to low serum Ca2+ (sensed by CaSR on parathyroid gland). Actions: ↑ renal 1-hydroxylase (→ ↑ calcitriol), ↑ bone resorption (via osteoblast → osteoclast stimulation), ↑ renal Ca2+ reabsorption.
  - **Calcitriol (1,25-(OH)2 D)**: ↑ intestinal Ca2+ absorption (TRPV6 channels, calbindin D9k, Ca2+-ATPase), ↑ bone mineralization, ↓ PTH secretion (negative feedback).
  - **Calcitonin**: Secreted by thyroid C-cells when Ca2+ is high; ↓ bone resorption, ↓ renal Ca2+ reabsorption. Minor role in adult humans.
  - When to use: Interpreting abnormal calcium labs — low Ca2+ triggers PTH release; high PTH with normal/high Ca2+ suggests primary hyperparathyroidism.

- **Calcium Absorption Dual Mechanism**:
  - **Active transcellular** (duodenum, jejunum): Saturable, calcitriol-regulated. Dominates at low-moderate intakes (<400-500 mg/meal). Involves TRPV6 → calbindin D9k → PMCA1b Ca2+-ATPase.
  - **Passive paracellular** (jejunum, ileum): Concentration-dependent, nonsaturable. Dominates at high intakes.
  - Overall absorption ~25-30% in adults; infants ~60%.
  - When to use: Optimizing calcium dosing — split doses ≤500 mg maximize active transport; higher single doses rely on less efficient passive absorption.

## Key Concepts
- **Calcium**: RDA 1000 mg (19-50 yr), 1200 mg (>70 yr — declining absorption). Deficiency = rickets (children), osteomalacia/osteoporosis (adults), tetany. Best sources: dairy, sardines with bones, fortified foods. Absorption inhibitors: oxalic acid (spinach, rhubarb), phytic acid (whole grains), unabsorbed fatty acids. Enhancers: vitamin D, protein, lactose, acidic environment.
- **Phosphorus**: RDA 700 mg. Component of bone (hydroxyapatite), phospholipids, nucleic acids, ATP, pH buffer. Deficiency rare — widespread in food supply. Excess (especially from phosphate additives in processed foods) may impair calcium metabolism.
- **Magnesium**: RDA 400 mg (M), 310 mg (F; 19-30 yr). Cofactor for >300 enzymes; ATP must bind Mg2+ to be biologically active; stabilizes nucleic acid structure; regulates ion channels. Deficiency = neuromuscular hyperexcitability, cardiac arrhythmias. Sources: nuts, legumes, whole grains, leafy greens (chlorophyll contains Mg).

## Mental Models
- **The bone calcium reservoir**: Bone is not inert — it undergoes constant remodeling (osteoclast resorption, osteoblast formation). Serum Ca2+ is maintained at the expense of bone when intake is inadequate.
- **Mineral-mineral competition**: Divalent cations (Ca2+, Mg2+, Zn2+, Fe2+) compete for absorption via DMT1 and paracellular routes. Excessive supplementation of one can induce deficiency of another.
- **Phosphate as the unappreciated regulator**: High serum phosphate directly stimulates PTH secretion independent of calcium; processed food phosphate additives are a modern dietary concern.

## Anti-patterns
- **Supplementing calcium without vitamin D**: Without adequate vitamin D status, calcium absorption is severely limited regardless of intake.
- **Assuming all calcium supplements are equivalent**: Calcium carbonate requires gastric acid for solubilization and should be taken with meals; calcium citrate is better for those with low stomach acid (elderly, PPI users).
- **Treating magnesium as a minor nutrient**: Magnesium deficiency is common (refined diets) and contributes to insulin resistance, hypertension, and arrhythmias.

## Key Takeaways
1. Peak bone mass is achieved by ~age 30; calcium and vitamin D intake during adolescence and young adulthood is critical for osteoporosis prevention.
2. The Ca:P ratio matters: high phosphorus intake (processed foods, sodas) relative to calcium may promote bone loss via PTH-mediated resorption.
3. Magnesium status is difficult to assess — serum Mg represents <1% of total body Mg; RBC Mg or Mg loading test is more informative.
4. Oxalic acid in spinach binds calcium so effectively that only ~5% of spinach calcium is absorbed.
5. Calcitriol synthesis declines with age due to reduced renal 1-hydroxylase activity — explaining higher vitamin D and calcium recommendations for older adults.
