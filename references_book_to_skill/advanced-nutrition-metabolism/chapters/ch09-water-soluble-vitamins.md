# Chapter 9: Water-Soluble Vitamins

## Core Idea
Water-soluble vitamins (C and the B-complex) function primarily as coenzymes in energy metabolism and hematopoiesis, or as enzyme cosubstrates (vitamin C). They are generally not stored in large quantities, absorbed into portal blood, and excreted in urine when plasma levels exceed renal thresholds — requiring consistent dietary intake.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **B-Vitamin Functional Grouping**:
  - **Energy production & nutrient metabolism**: Thiamin (B1), Riboflavin (B2), Niacin (B3), Pantothenic acid, Biotin, Vitamin B6, Vitamin B12 — all serve as coenzymes in macronutrient oxidation.
  - **Hematopoiesis**: Folate and Vitamin B12 — required for DNA synthesis in red blood cell precursors.
  - **Gene expression**: Folate, B12, B6, Biotin, Pantothenic acid — involved in nucleotide synthesis and epigenetic regulation.
  - **Antioxidant/Enzyme cosubstrate**: Vitamin C — hydroxylation reactions (collagen, carnitine, neurotransmitters) and free radical reduction.
  - When to use: Identifying which vitamins are most likely deficient when specific metabolic pathways are impaired.

- **DRI Hierarchy**: EAR (Estimated Average Requirement — meets 50% of population needs) → RDA (EAR + 2 SD, meets ~97%) → AI (used when insufficient data for EAR) → UL (Tolerable Upper Intake Level).
  - When to use: Interpreting nutrient intake data — intakes below EAR likely inadequate; between EAR and RDA may be inadequate; above RDA and below UL are likely adequate.

## Key Concepts
- **Thiamin (B1)**: Coenzyme TDP (thiamin diphosphate); oxidative decarboxylation of α-keto acids (pyruvate dehydrogenase, α-KGDH), transketolase (pentose phosphate pathway). Deficiency = beriberi (dry: neuropathy; wet: cardiac failure). RDA: 1.1 mg (F), 1.2 mg (M).
- **Riboflavin (B2)**: Coenzymes FAD and FMN; electron transfer in TCA cycle, fatty acid oxidation, ETC. Deficiency = ariboflavinosis (cheilosis, glossitis, angular stomatitis). RDA: 1.1 mg (F), 1.3 mg (M).
- **Niacin (B3)**: Coenzymes NAD+ and NADP+; electron transfer in glycolysis, TCA cycle, fatty acid synthesis. Can be synthesized from tryptophan (60 mg Trp = 1 mg niacin). Deficiency = pellagra (4 D's: dermatitis, diarrhea, dementia, death). RDA: 14 mg (F), 16 mg (M) as NE.
- **Pantothenic Acid**: Component of Coenzyme A (CoA); acyl group transfer; fatty acid synthesis/oxidation, TCA cycle. AI: 5 mg.
- **Biotin**: Coenzyme for carboxylation reactions (pyruvate carboxylase, acetyl-CoA carboxylase); gene expression. AI: 30 μg.
- **Vitamin B6**: Coenzyme PLP (pyridoxal phosphate); transamination, decarboxylation, glycogen phosphorylase, heme synthesis. RDA: 1.3 mg.
- **Folate**: Coenzyme THF derivatives; one-carbon transfer for purine/pyrimidine synthesis; hematopoiesis. Deficiency = megaloblastic anemia. RDA: 400 μg DFE. UL: 1000 μg from supplements.
- **Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin)**: Coenzymes methylcobalamin (homocysteine → methionine) and adenosylcobalamin (methylmalonyl-CoA → succinyl-CoA); requires intrinsic factor for ileal absorption. Deficiency = megaloblastic anemia + neurological degeneration. RDA: 2.4 μg.
- **Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)**: Cosubstrate for prolyl/lysyl hydroxylases (collagen synthesis); reductant for dopamine β-monooxygenase (norepinephrine synthesis); antioxidant; regenerates vitamin E. Deficiency = scurvy. RDA: 75 mg (F), 90 mg (M). UL: 2000 mg.

## Mental Models
- **Coenzyme as reusable catalyst**: The vitamin-derived coenzyme (e.g., NAD+) participates in the reaction but is regenerated — explaining why vitamin requirements are small relative to substrate flux.
- **Deficiency progression**: Depletion → cellular dysfunction → biochemical lesion → clinical symptoms. Biochemical markers detect deficiency before clinical signs appear.
- **Folate trap**: B12 deficiency causes functional folate deficiency because 5-methyl-THF cannot be converted to THF without B12 — explaining why B12 deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia identical to folate deficiency.

## Anti-patterns
- **Using supplements instead of food sources**: Epidemiological evidence consistently shows food-based nutrient intake reduces chronic disease risk, while isolated supplements do not replicate this benefit.
- **Assuming high water-soluble vitamin doses are harmless**: Niacin (≥35 mg/day from supplements) causes flushing; B6 (>100 mg/day) causes irreversible neuropathy; vitamin C (>2 g/day) causes GI distress.
- **Treating all forms of a vitamin as equivalent**: Folic acid (synthetic, fully oxidized) has nearly twice the bioavailability of food folate; niacin as nicotinic acid causes flushing while nicotinamide does not.

## Key Takeaways
1. Food sources, not supplements, are the preferred vehicle for vitamin intake — whole foods provide synergistic compounds (phytochemicals, fiber) absent from pills.
2. Most water-soluble vitamins are absorbed in the duodenum and jejunum; B12 is the exception requiring the ileum and intrinsic factor.
3. The folate RDA for women of childbearing age (400 μg DFE) is designed to prevent neural tube defects; supplemental folic acid is recommended periconceptionally.
4. Alcoholism is a common cause of multiple B-vitamin deficiencies (especially thiamin, leading to Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome).
5. Vitamin C's absorption decreases with increasing dose: ~98% at 20 mg vs. <50% at >1 g — megadosing produces diminishing returns.
