# Metabolic Patterns: Reusable Frameworks from Gropper & Smith

## Pattern 1: The Metabolic Junction Pattern
**Core insight**: Pyruvate and acetyl-CoA are the two critical intersections where carbohydrate, lipid, and protein metabolism converge. Every macronutrient can be converted to acetyl-CoA; acetyl-CoA cannot be converted to glucose.

**Application**: When analyzing metabolic fate of any nutrient, trace it to either pyruvate (glucogenic) or acetyl-CoA (ketogenic/fat synthetic). This determines whether the carbon skeleton can contribute to blood glucose or must be oxidized/stored as fat.

**Tell**: "Is this substrate glucogenic or ketogenic?" If it can produce pyruvate or any TCA cycle intermediate beyond the decarboxylation steps, it is glucogenic. If it produces only acetyl-CoA or acetoacetyl-CoA, it cannot generate net glucose.

## Pattern 2: The Energy Charge Sensor Pattern
**Core insight**: AMPK is the master energy sensor activated by high AMP:ATP ratio (low energy). It simultaneously stimulates catabolic pathways and inhibits anabolic pathways through phosphorylation cascades.

**Application**: Use AMPK activation status to predict metabolic direction. AMPK active → fatty acid oxidation ON, synthesis OFF; glycolysis ON; gluconeogenesis OFF; protein synthesis OFF (mTOR inhibited). AMPK inactive → opposite pattern.

**Tell**: "Is the cell energy-stressed?" If yes (fasting, exercise, hypoxia), AMPK is active. If no (fed state, high glucose), AMPK is inactive. Metformin activates AMPK; this explains its glucose-lowering effect.

## Pattern 3: The Insulin-Glucagon Reciprocal Pattern
**Core insight**: Insulin and glucagon always push metabolism in opposite directions. Insulin dominates the fed state (anabolism, storage); glucagon dominates the fasted state (catabolism, mobilization). Their ratio, not absolute levels, often determines metabolic outcome.

**Application**: When assessing metabolic state, determine the insulin:glucagon ratio. High ratio = fed/anabolic. Low ratio = fasted/catabolic. This ratio is a more reliable predictor than either hormone alone.

**Tell**: "Fed or fasted?" Insulin:Glucagon > 1 → storage mode; < 1 → mobilization mode. The liver is the primary glucagon-responsive tissue; muscle lacks glucagon receptors.

## Pattern 4: The Tissue-Specific Fuel Preference Pattern
**Core insight**: Different tissues have non-negotiable fuel requirements. Brain/RBCs cannot oxidize fatty acids. Skeletal muscle is metabolically flexible. Liver processes all fuels but favors fatty acids for its own energy.

**Application**: In any metabolic scenario, first identify which tissues have fixed fuel requirements (brain, RBCs — glucose), then allocate remaining fuels to flexible tissues (muscle, liver, heart).

**Tell**: "What fuel does this tissue require?" Brain → glucose/ketones. RBCs → glucose only. Cardiac muscle → fatty acids preferred. Liver → fatty acids for self, exports glucose/ketones/VLDL for others.

## Pattern 5: The Absorption Competition Pattern
**Core insight**: Divalent cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Fe²⁺, Zn²⁺, Cu²⁺) share absorption transporters (DMT1) and paracellular pathways. Excessive intake of one inhibits absorption of others.

**Application**: When designing supplementation regimens or evaluating mineral status, consider competitive interactions. High-dose zinc supplements predictably induce copper deficiency. Calcium supplements reduce iron and zinc absorption.

**Tell**: "Is one mineral being supplemented far above RDA?" If yes, screen for subclinical deficiency of competing minerals.

## Pattern 6: The Fat-Soluble Vitamin Co-Requirement Pattern
**Core insight**: All fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) require the same conditions for absorption: dietary fat, bile, functional pancreas, intact intestinal mucosa, and chylomicron assembly. A defect in any one impairs absorption of all four.

**Application**: When a patient presents with one fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, screen for deficiencies of the other three. Conditions causing steatorrhea (cystic fibrosis, biliary atresia, pancreatic insufficiency) predictably cause multiple fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies.

**Tell**: "Is fat absorption compromised?" If yes, all four fat-soluble vitamins are at risk.

## Pattern 7: The Fed-Fast Fuel Selection Pattern
**Core insight**: Fuel selection follows a predictable temporal sequence after a meal:
- 0-3 h: Glucose is primary fuel; insulin suppresses fat oxidation.
- 3-18 h: Hepatic glycogen provides glucose; adipose lipolysis provides FFA for muscle.
- 18-48 h: Gluconeogenesis from amino acids dominates; ketogenesis begins.
- >48 h: Protein-sparing shift; glycerol and ketone bodies are primary fuels; brain adapts to ketones.

**Application**: Use time since last meal to predict metabolic fuel mix. The RQ shifts from ~0.85-1.0 (fed) to ~0.70-0.80 (fasted) to ~0.70 (starvation/ketosis).

**Tell**: "How long since the last meal?" This determines the dominant metabolic state and hormonal profile.

## Pattern 8: The Exercise Intensity Fuel Shift Pattern
**Core insight**: As exercise intensity increases, fuel selection shifts from predominantly fat (at rest and low intensity) to predominantly carbohydrate (at high intensity).

- <25% VO₂ max → mostly plasma FFA
- 25-50% VO₂ max → FFA favored; maximal fat oxidation at ~50%
- 65% VO₂ max → muscle glycogen + muscle TAG + plasma FFA (mixed)
- 85% VO₂ max → muscle glycogen dominates; fat oxidation declines
- Maximal sprint → ATP-PC system + anaerobic glycolysis

**Application**: Predict fuel utilization for any exercise type. Design nutrition strategies around fuel demands: fat-adaptation training for ultra-endurance; carbohydrate periodization for team sports and high-intensity events.

**Tell**: "What is the intensity and duration?" Intensity determines fuel type; duration determines when glycogen depletion and exogenous carbohydrate become necessary.

## Pattern 9: The Coenzyme-Substrate Coupling Pattern
**Core insight**: B-vitamins function as coenzymes in specific, identifiable pathways. A deficiency in a particular B-vitamin produces a predictable biochemical lesion — the accumulation of the substrate of the impaired enzyme, and deficiency of its product.

**Application**: Use biochemical markers to identify functional vitamin deficiencies before clinical signs appear. Elevated homocysteine → folate/B12/B6 inadequacy. Elevated methylmalonic acid → B12 deficiency specifically. Elevated xanthurenate → B6 deficiency.

**Tell**: "What metabolite is accumulating?" Trace it back to the vitamin-dependent enzyme that converts it.

## Pattern 10: The Leptin-Ghrelin Appetite Axis Pattern
**Core insight**: Appetite is a hormonally regulated homeostatic system, not a moral failing. Leptin (from adipocytes) suppresses hunger long-term. Ghrelin (from stomach) stimulates hunger short-term. Obesity involves disruption of this axis — either leptin resistance or blunted postprandial ghrelin suppression.

**Application**: When counseling on weight management, frame appetite as neuroendocrine, not willpower. Interventions that improve leptin sensitivity (weight loss, adequate sleep, reduced inflammation) and normalize ghrelin patterns (adequate protein at breakfast, regular meal timing) address the underlying biology.

**Tell**: "Is the appetite control system functioning?" Leptin resistance → persistent hunger despite adequate fat stores. Blunted ghrelin suppression → continued hunger after meals.
