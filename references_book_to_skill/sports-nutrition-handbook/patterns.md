# Patterns in Sports Nutrition Practice

This document captures recurring patterns used throughout *Sports Nutrition: A Handbook for Professionals, 6th Edition*. These patterns reflect systematic approaches that sports RDNs can apply across diverse athlete populations and contexts.

## 1. The Food-First Pattern

**Principle**: Prioritize whole foods over supplements to meet nutrition goals.

**Application**:
- Food-based solutions before supplement recommendations
- Whole-food equivalents for sports foods (fruit smoothie vs. protein powder; real food vs. energy gels)
- Supplements reserved for: diagnosed deficiencies, impractical food scenarios, evidence-based ergogenic aids

**Rationale**: Better nutrient synergy, lower cost, greater safety, reduced contamination risk, additional health benefits from food matrix.

## 2. The g/kg Body Weight Pattern

**Principle**: Express macronutrient recommendations in grams per kilogram body weight, not percentage of total energy.

**Application**:
- Carbohydrate: 3-12 g/kg/d based on training load
- Protein: 1.2-2.0 g/kg/d based on training type and goals
- Fat: ~1 g/kg/d or 20-35% energy, fine-tuned after CHO and protein

**Rationale**: Absolute amounts matter for physiology; percentages mislead when energy intakes vary widely (2,000 vs. 5,000 kcal/d).

## 3. The Nutrient Timing Pattern

**Principle**: Synchronize nutrient intake with exercise to optimize performance and recovery.

**Framework**:
- **Pre-exercise** (1-4 h): CHO 1-4 g/kg, low fat/fiber/protein
- **During exercise**: CHO based on duration (0-90+ g/h), fluid matching sweat rate
- **Post-exercise** (<4 h): CHO 1.0-1.2 g/kg/h + protein 0.25-0.3 g/kg
- **Pre-sleep**: Slow-digesting protein (~40 g casein) for overnight recovery

**Rationale**: Aligns nutrient availability with physiological windows for glycogen synthesis, protein synthesis, and hydration.

## 4. The Periodization Pattern

**Principle**: Vary nutrition strategies to match training cycles and goals.

**Application**:
- **Preparatory phase**: Support training adaptation; body composition goals addressed
- **Competition phase**: Optimize fuel stores; event-specific nutrition strategies
- **Transition/off-season**: Recovery; address deficiencies; weight management
- **CHO periodization**: Train high for key sessions; train low selectively for adaptation

**Rationale**: Nutrition must be dynamic, not static; matches varying energy demands, recovery needs, and performance goals.

## 5. The Risk-Benefit Assessment Pattern

**Principle**: Evaluate all supplements and nutrition interventions through cost, safety, efficacy, and legality lenses.

**Framework** (AIS ABCD):
- **Group A**: Evidence-supported; use in specific protocols
- **Group B**: Deserving of research; case-managed only
- **Group C**: Little proof; not provided
- **Group D**: Banned/high risk; should not be used

**Four questions**: Is it safe? Is it legal? Is it effective? Can I afford it?

**Rationale**: Systematic approach reduces risk, focuses resources on effective interventions, and maintains integrity.

## 6. The Individualization Pattern

**Principle**: Every recommendation must be tailored to the individual athlete.

**Variables to consider**:
- Sport demands (energy systems, duration, intensity, rules)
- Training phase and goals
- Body composition and weight management needs
- Personal preferences, culture, religion
- Food access, cooking skills, budget
- Gut tolerance to foods/fluids during exercise
- Medical conditions and medications
- Past experiences and feedback

**Rationale**: Genetic differences, training history, gut physiology, and psychological factors create large inter-individual variability in response to nutrition interventions.

## 7. The Assessment-to-Action Pattern

**Principle**: Follow the Nutrition Care Process (NCP) systematically.

**Four steps**:
1. **Assessment**: Collect data (dietary, anthropometric, biochemical, clinical, client history)
2. **Diagnosis**: Identify nutrition problems using PES format (Problem - Etiology - Signs/Symptoms)
3. **Intervention**: Plan and implement nutrition strategy (education, referral, food provision)
4. **Monitoring/Evaluation**: Track progress; adjust plan based on outcomes

**Rationale**: Standardized process ensures comprehensive, evidence-based, and accountable care.

## 8. The Hydration Protocol Pattern

**Principle**: Develop individualized hydration plans based on sweat rate and sweat sodium concentration.

**Application**:
- **Pre-exercise**: 5-7 mL/kg, 4 hours before; check urine color
- **During exercise**: Replace sweat loss (0.4-0.8 L/h general; customized by sweat rate testing)
- **Post-exercise**: 125-150% of weight loss in fluid with sodium

**Sweat rate calculation**: (Pre-BW - Post-BW in g + fluid intake mL - urine output mL) / exercise time in hours

**Rationale**: Individual sweat rates vary from <0.5 to >3 L/h; generic recommendations are inadequate.

## 9. The Body Composition Management Pattern

**Principle**: Weight management should be systematic, health-focused, and periodized.

**Four steps**:
1. **Assessment**: Comprehensive baseline (body comp, RMR, diet, training, psychosocial)
2. **Goal setting**: SMART goals; ~0.7% BW loss/week for fat loss; 0.5-0.75 lb/week lean gain
3. **Action plan**: Calorie adjustment (±200-500 kcal/d), macronutrient targets (g/kg), nutrient timing
4. **Evaluation**: Weekly weight monitoring, body composition every 4-8 weeks, performance markers

**Rationale**: Unmanaged weight practices are prevalent and dangerous in sport; systematic approach prioritizes health and performance.

## 10. The Athlete Population-Specific Adaptation Pattern

**Principle**: Adapt core nutrition principles to population-specific needs.

**Key populations and their primary adaptations**:
- **Youth**: Growth/development + training; Tanner staging; caution with supplements
- **College**: Food access, cooking skills, budget, time management
- **Masters/h4.**: Higher protein per meal (0.4 g/kg), vitamin D, calcium; anabolic resistance
- **Elite**: Precision testing; periodized nutrition; WADA compliance
- **Vegetarian**: Bioavailability considerations; B-12, iron, zinc, n-3 supplementation
- **Pregnant**: Fetal development nutrients; exercise modifications; gradual return
- **Diabetic**: Insulin-CHO coordination; frequent monitoring; hypoglycemia prevention

**Rationale**: Core principles (g/kg dosing, timing, periodization) remain constant but application details differ.

## 11. The Multidisciplinary Team Pattern

**Principle**: Sports nutrition is most effective when integrated with the full performance team.

**Collaborators**: Coaches, sports medicine physicians, athletic trainers, strength/conditioning coaches, sports psychologists, physical therapists, athlete's family.

**Rationale**: Isolated nutrition advice without coordination with training, medical, and psychological support is less effective and potentially harmful.

## 12. The Gut Training Pattern

**Principle**: Athletes must train their gut to tolerate competition nutrition, just as they train their muscles.

**Application**:
- Gradually increase CHO intake during training sessions
- Practice with specific products and timing that will be used in competition
- Identify and manage individual GI triggers
- Consider low FODMAP approaches for GI-sensitive athletes

**Rationale**: GI distress affects 30-50% of endurance athletes during events; gut tolerance is trainable.
