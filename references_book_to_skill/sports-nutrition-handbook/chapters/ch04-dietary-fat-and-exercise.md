# Chapter 4: Dietary Fat and Exercise

## Key Concepts

Dietary fat is an essential macronutrient for athletes. Triglycerides comprise >90% of dietary lipids. Fatty acids are classified by chain length, degree of saturation (saturated, MUFA, PUFA), and double-bond configuration (cis vs trans). Essential fatty acids (linoleic acid n-6, α-linolenic acid n-3) must come from diet.

## Fat Metabolism During Exercise

- At rest and low intensity: fat is the preferred fuel
- Maximum fat oxidation occurs at ~60-65% VO₂max
- As intensity increases, crossover to greater carbohydrate oxidation occurs
- Intramyocellular lipids (IMCL) provide local energy; increase with endurance training
- Plasma free fatty acids (FFA) from adipose tissue contribute more during prolonged exercise
- Females typically have higher IMCL and fat oxidation rates than males
- Endurance training increases IMCL and fat oxidation capacity

## Dietary Fat Recommendations

- **Total fat**: 20-35% of total energy (AMDR)
- **Minimum**: No less than 1 g/kg/d; chronic restriction below 20% energy may reduce diet quality, limit fat-soluble vitamin intake, and contribute to low energy availability
- **Saturated fat**: <10% of total calories
- **Trans fat**: As low as possible (<1% total calories)
- **n-6 PUFA**: 5-10% of energy
- **n-3 PUFA**: 0.6-1.2% of energy
- Emphasize MUFAs and PUFAs from plant sources and fatty fish
- Fat intake targets determined after carbohydrate and protein goals are established

## Fat Restriction

- Acute restriction (2-3 days) acceptable for making weight
- Not recommended pre-exercise (<1-2 hours) to prevent GI distress
- Chronic restriction risks: low energy availability, menstrual dysfunction, bone loss, impaired recovery

## High-Fat Diets and Fat Adaptation

**Metabolic adaptations do occur** with high-fat diets (5-14 days, 60-70% fat):
- Increased fat oxidation rates
- Decreased respiratory exchange ratio (RER)
- Increased IMCL stores
- Increased mitochondrial biogenesis markers

**However, performance benefits are NOT established**:
- May maintain submaximal endurance capacity but impairs high-intensity performance
- Downregulation of carbohydrate metabolism persists even after glycogen restoration
- Higher rating of perceived exertion (RPE)
- Diet periodization (5-14 days high-fat + 1-3 days CHO restoration) shows inconsistent results

**Ketogenic diets** (<10g CHO/day): Can maintain moderate-intensity capacity but sacrifice high-intensity ability and training quality.

## Training with Low Carbohydrate Availability

Related but distinct from high-fat diets. Strategies: fasted training, twice-daily sessions, post-exercise CHO restriction. Enhances mitochondrial biogenesis and fat oxidation at the cellular level. Potential for nutritional periodization, but optimal protocols remain unclear.

## n-3 Fatty Acid Supplementation

- May benefit athletes who chronically restrict fat intake
- Anti-inflammatory properties have NOT been shown to enhance performance
- Up to 3g EPA/DHA per day from diet or supplements considered safe
- Large doses may increase oxidative stress markers and bleeding risk

## Lipid-Based Supplements: Limited Efficacy

| Supplement | Efficacy |
|---|---|
| Conjugated Linoleic Acid (CLA) | Mixed body composition effects; no performance benefit |
| L-Carnitine | No effect on fat utilization or performance at recommended doses |
| Medium-Chain Triglycerides (MCT) | No performance improvement; may cause GI distress |
