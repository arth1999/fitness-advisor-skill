# Chapter 9: Anthropometric Measurements and Body Composition

## Key Concepts

Body composition assessment establishes baseline, sets appropriate weight ranges, and monitors changes in lean body mass and storage fat. No single method is universally superior; each has strengths and weaknesses. The most accurate methods are often not accessible or affordable.

## Body Composition Models

### 2-Component Model
- Divides body into fat mass (FM) and fat-free mass (FFM)
- Assumes FFM density is constant (1.1 g/cc) — not true for all populations
- Methods: hydrostatic weighing, air displacement plethysmography, skinfolds, BIA

### Multi-Component Models (3C, 4C)
- 3C: FM, total body water, bone-mineral-free lean tissue
- 4C: FM, total body water, bone mineral, residual
- More accurate; considered gold standard for research

## Measurement Methods

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **DEXA** | Low radiation, 3-compartment, regional analysis | Expensive, not portable, hydration status affects results |
| **Hydrostatic Weighing** | Historically gold standard, reliable | Uncomfortable, requires full submersion, residual volume measurement |
| **Air Displacement (BOD POD)** | Quick, comfortable, good for serial measures | Expensive, clothing/hair/temperature affect results |
| **Skinfolds** | Portable, inexpensive, good for tracking changes | Technician skill-dependent, assumes subcutaneous fat represents total fat |
| **Bioelectrical Impedance (BIA)** | Quick, portable, noninvasive | Highly sensitive to hydration, food intake, exercise, skin temperature |
| **Ultrasound** | Noninvasive, can measure muscle thickness | Limited standardization in athletes |
| **MRI/CT** | Gold standard for regional/tissue-specific measures | Very expensive, CT involves radiation |

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Standardize measurement conditions**: Same time of day, hydration status, recent exercise, menstrual cycle phase
2. **Use same method over time**: Different methods produce different absolute values; consistency enables trend monitoring
3. **Skinfold equations**: Use population-specific equations; generalized equations may not apply to athletes
4. **Track multiple measures**: Combine weight, skinfolds, girths, and performance markers
5. **Consider individual variability**: Body composition is influenced by genetics, training phase, nutrition, and recovery

## Body Composition Goals by Sport

- **Endurance athletes**: Lower body fat for efficiency (but minimum essential fat required: ~5% men, ~12% women)
- **Strength/power athletes**: Higher lean mass, moderate fat
- **Weight-class sports**: Precise weight management; body composition more important than weight alone
- **Aesthetic sports**: Often unrealistic ideals; risk of disordered eating

## Interpreting Results

- Communicate results sensitively; avoid triggering body image concerns
- Focus on performance outcomes rather than appearance
- Use ranges rather than single target values
- Monitor trends over time rather than single measurements
- Compare to sport-specific norms when available

## Limitations

- All methods have measurement error (typically 2-5% body fat)
- Hydration status affects most methods significantly
- No method directly measures body fat — all are estimates based on assumptions
- Athlete body composition may not fit general population prediction equations
