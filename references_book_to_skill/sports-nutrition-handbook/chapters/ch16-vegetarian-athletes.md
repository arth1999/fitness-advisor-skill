# Chapter 16: Vegetarian Athletes

## Key Concepts

Well-planned vegetarian and vegan diets can meet athletic needs and may provide health benefits (higher antioxidant intake, lower saturated fat). However, careful planning is required to address nutrients of concern that may be less bioavailable or absent from plant-based diets.

## Types of Vegetarian Diets

- **Lacto-ovo-vegetarian**: Includes dairy and eggs
- **Lacto-vegetarian**: Includes dairy, excludes eggs
- **Ovo-vegetarian**: Includes eggs, excludes dairy
- **Vegan**: Excludes all animal products
- **Pesco-vegetarian**: Includes fish (not strictly vegetarian)

## Energy Considerations

- Plant-based diets tend to have lower energy density and higher fiber → increased satiety
- Athletes with high energy needs may struggle to consume adequate calories
- Strategies: frequent meals, energy-dense plant foods (nuts, seeds, avocados, oils), liquid calories

## Protein — Primary Concern

- Plant proteins are generally less anabolic than animal proteins due to lower leucine content and incomplete EAA profiles
- **Soy**: Fast-digesting, higher leucine than most plant proteins, but inferior to milk/whey for hypertrophy
- **Quinoa**: Complete protein with superior leucine, lysine, and methionine content vs. soy
- **Protein blending**: Combining sources (grains + legumes) improves EAA profile
- **Quantity**: Vegetarian athletes may need 10-20% more total protein to compensate for lower digestibility (PDCAAS)
- **Leucine**: Target 2-3 g leucine per meal; may require larger portions or leucine-rich plant foods (soy, pumpkin seeds, lentils)
- **Fortified plant proteins**: Emerging area; genetic manipulation to improve amino acid profiles

## Key Micronutrient Concerns

### Iron
- **Non-heme only** in vegan diets; lower bioavailability (1-10% absorption vs. 10-25% for heme)
- Inhibitors in plant foods: phytates, oxalates, tannins, polyphenols
- **Enhancers**: Vitamin C with meals, soaking/sprouting/fermenting to reduce phytates
- Female vegetarian athletes at highest risk for iron deficiency
- Hepcidin response to training further reduces absorption

### Vitamin B-12
- **Absent from plant foods** (unless fortified)
- Vegan athletes MUST supplement or consume fortified foods regularly
- Deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia and neurological damage
- B-12 status must be assessed; serum B-12 and methylmalonic acid

### Zinc
- Lower bioavailability from plant sources due to phytates
- Rich plant sources: pumpkin seeds, hemp seeds (5 mg per ½ cup), legumes, nuts
- Food preparation techniques improve absorption (soaking, sprouting, leavening)

### Calcium
- Lacto-ovo vegetarians: dairy sources adequate
- Vegans: fortified plant milks, tofu set with calcium, leafy greens (low-oxalate: kale, bok choy), fortified juices
- Oxalates in spinach, chard, beet greens significantly reduce calcium absorption

### Vitamin D
- Limited food sources for everyone; vegans especially at risk
- Fortified plant milks, UV-exposed mushrooms
- Supplement with D-3 (lichen-derived vegan D-3 available)

### Omega-3 (n-3) Fatty Acids
- ALA from flaxseed, chia, walnuts; conversion to EPA/DHA is limited (5-10%)
- Algal DHA supplements available for vegans
- n-6:n-3 ratio often elevated in vegetarian diets

### Iodine
- Variable in plant foods (soil-dependent)
- Sea vegetables (kelp, nori) — but content highly variable; some excessive
- Iodized salt; supplementation may be needed

## Practical Strategies

1. **Protein at every meal**: Combine complementary plant proteins
2. **Vitamin C with iron-rich meals**: Enhance non-heme iron absorption
3. **Soak/sprout/ferment**: Reduce phytates in grains, legumes, seeds
4. **Fortified foods**: B-12, calcium, vitamin D in plant milks
5. **Regular monitoring**: Iron studies, B-12, vitamin D, zinc status
6. **Algal DHA**: For vegans not consuming fish
7. **Energy-dense plant foods**: Nuts, seeds, oils for high-energy-need athletes
