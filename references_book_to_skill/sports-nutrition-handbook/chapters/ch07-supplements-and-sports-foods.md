# Chapter 7: Supplements and Sports Foods

## Key Concepts

Sports dietitians take a "food-first" approach but must understand supplements and sports foods that are marketed to athletes. A systematic risk-benefit framework is essential for evaluating products. Hundreds of thousands of products exist; proper classification helps athletes make informed decisions.

## Defining Supplements

US (FDA/DSHEA 1994): A product intended to supplement the diet containing vitamins, minerals, herbs, amino acids, or concentrates/metabolites/extracts.

Different regulatory frameworks exist globally (EU, Australia TGA, etc.). Free-trade agreements complicate regulation; products may bypass local oversight.

## The AIS Sports Supplement Framework (ABCD Classification)

Developed by the Australian Institute of Sport. Four-tier hierarchy:

### Group A — Supported for Use
- **Sports Foods**: Sports drinks, gels, bars, liquid meals, whey protein, electrolyte replacements — practical nutrient sources when food is impractical
- **Medical Supplements**: Iron, calcium, multivitamin/mineral, vitamin D, probiotics — treat diagnosed deficiencies; require individual supervision
- **Performance Supplements**: Caffeine, β-alanine, bicarbonate, beetroot juice/nitrate, creatine — evidence-based ergogenic aids; use in individualized protocols

### Group B — Deserving of Further Research
Food polyphenols (quercetin, tart cherry juice, curcumin), antioxidants C and E, carnitine, HMB, glutamine, fish oils, glucosamine. May be provided under research or clinical monitoring.

### Group C — Little Meaningful Proof
Products with insufficient scientific evidence. Not provided within organized supplement programs.

### Group D — Banned/High Risk
Stimulants (ephedrine, DMAA), prohormones (DHEA, androstenedione), hormone boosters, glycerol. Should NOT be used by athletes.

## Cost-Benefit Analysis for Supplement Use

**Four questions athletes must answer**:
1. **Is it safe?** — Recognize ingredients, manufacturer reputation, correct dose, health impacts
2. **Is it legal?** — Permitted for sale in country, not banned by WADA, manufacturer reputation
3. **Is it effective?** — Evidence-based for their specific sport/event, correct protocol
4. **Can I afford it?** — Cost vs. budget, lower-cost food alternatives available

## Ergogenic Benefit Magnitude

When evidence-based supplements are used correctly, typical performance improvement is **1-3%**. While small, this is often meaningful in elite sport where winning margins are similar to the coefficient of variation in performance (2.7%).

## Placebo Effect in Supplement Use

The placebo effect in sport is real and measurable. Belief about receiving a beneficial product can alter performance by 1-3%. Athletes should be confident they are using effective products; uncertainty increases performance variability.

## Supplement Contamination and Anti-Doping

- **15% of tested supplements** in 2004 contained undeclared banned substances
- Contamination affects all product types including amino acids, protein powders, creatine, vitamins
- **Strict liability** under WADA Code: athletes are responsible for substances found in their body
- Common banned contaminants: prohormones, stimulants (DMAA), peptide hormones
- Weight-loss and pre-workout supplements highest risk for stimulant contamination

## Third-Party Certification Programs

Look for: **Informed Sport**, **NSF Certified for Sport**, **BSCG Certified Drug Free**. These test each batch for ~200 WADA-prohibited substances. No supplement is entirely risk-free, but certified products significantly reduce risk.

## Key Practice Points

1. Use a systematic framework (AIS ABCD) to classify and evaluate supplements
2. Document all supplement use (product name, batch number, source)
3. Sports dietitians should be key gatekeepers and information sources
4. Supplements do NOT replace a well-planned periodized meal plan
5. Focus on Group A products with proven evidence bases
6. Intramuscular/intravenous supplement administration requires medical supervision
