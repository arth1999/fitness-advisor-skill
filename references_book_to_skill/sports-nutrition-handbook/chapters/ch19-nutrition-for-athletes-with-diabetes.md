# Chapter 19: Nutrition for Athletes with Diabetes

## Key Concepts

Athletes with diabetes compete at the highest levels in every sport. The sports dietitian is an essential part of the treatment team, providing education and lifestyle strategies to optimize glycemic control while supporting training and performance. Type 1 diabetes is more common in the collegiate athlete population.

## Exercise and Glucose Metabolism

- Exercise increases muscle glucose uptake through insulin-independent mechanisms (GLUT4 translocation)
- This can cause hypoglycemia during and after exercise, especially in type 1 diabetes
- Counterregulatory hormone responses (epinephrine, glucagon, cortisol, growth hormone) may be blunted in diabetes
- Hypoglycemia risk persists for hours post-exercise (late-onset hypoglycemia)

## Blood Glucose Monitoring

- **Before exercise**: Establish baseline; target 90-250 mg/dL (5.0-13.9 mmol/L)
- **During exercise**: Every 30 minutes for prolonged activity; adjust CHO intake based on trends
- **After exercise**: Monitor for several hours; overnight monitoring for late-onset hypoglycemia
- **Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGM)**: Valuable tool; trends more informative than single readings

## Insulin Management

### For Type 1 Diabetes
- **Pre-exercise insulin reduction**: Typically 25-75% reduction in bolus insulin for meals before exercise; depends on exercise intensity/duration and timing
- **Pump therapy**: Temporary basal rate reduction (50-80%) starting 60-90 min before exercise; may suspend at exercise onset
- **Injection site**: Avoid injecting into exercising muscle (increases absorption rate)
- **Correction doses**: Use conservatively post-exercise

### For Type 2 Diabetes
- Exercise is a cornerstone of management
- May need medication adjustment with increased training (especially sulfonylureas and insulin)
- Metformin and GLP-1 agonists have low hypoglycemia risk

## Carbohydrate Needs for Glycemic Management

- **Pre-exercise**: 15-30 g CHO if blood glucose <90 mg/dL
- **During exercise**: 30-60 g CHO per hour for prolonged activities; adjust based on CGM/glucose trends
- **Post-exercise**: CHO to replenish glycogen + protein for muscle repair
- **Hypoglycemia treatment**: 15 g fast-acting CHO; recheck in 15 minutes; repeat if needed
- CHO needs for exercise are similar to non-diabetic athletes but must be coordinated with insulin adjustments

## Fuel Utilization in Diabetes

- Type and amount of fuel used depends on exercise type, intensity, duration, available fuel mix, and fitness level
- With good glycemic control, substrate utilization is similar to non-diabetic athletes
- Poor control (hyperglycemia) may impair fat oxidation and increase reliance on glycogen
- Hypoglycemia limits performance; prevention is key

## Special Considerations

### Hyperglycemia and Ketones
- If blood glucose >250-300 mg/dL with ketones: delay exercise; correct with insulin
- If blood glucose >250-300 mg/dL without ketones: may exercise with caution; monitor closely
- Dehydration and illness increase risk of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)

### Exercise at Altitude
- Carbohydrate utilization increases
- Insulin requirements may change
- Glucometer accuracy affected at altitude
- CGM reliability variable at altitude

### Extreme Endurance Events
- Requires detailed planning: insulin adjustments, CHO intake strategy, monitoring
- Late-onset hypoglycemia risk significantly elevated for 12-24 hours
- Support crew should be educated on diabetes management

## Nutrition Periodization for Diabetic Athletes

- **Training diet**: Consistent CHO intake matched to training; predictable insulin adjustments
- **Pre-competition**: Carbohydrate loading possible but requires insulin management expertise
- **Competition day**: Well-practiced, individualized plan; backup supplies essential
- **Recovery**: Immediate CHO + protein; vigilant post-exercise monitoring

## Team Approach

Optimal management requires:
- Endocrinologist/diabetologist
- Sports dietitian (CDCES — Certified Diabetes Care and Education Specialist preferred)
- Exercise physiologist or coach
- Athlete as active participant in decision-making

## Key Takeaways

1. Athletes with diabetes can compete at all levels with proper management
2. Frequent blood glucose monitoring before, during, and after exercise is essential
3. Insulin adjustments + CHO intake must be individualized and practiced
4. Hypoglycemia prevention takes priority; always carry fast-acting CHO
5. Late-onset hypoglycemia risk persists 12-24 hours post-exercise
6. CGM is a valuable tool but not a replacement for blood glucose checks
7. Multidisciplinary team approach optimizes outcomes
