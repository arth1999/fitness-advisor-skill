# Chapter 20: Nutrition for Short-Duration Very-High- and High-Intensity Sports

## Key Concepts

These sports require all-out or near-maximal effort (>90% VO₂max or 1RM) lasting seconds to minutes. Limited research exists compared with endurance sports. Energy systems are predominantly anaerobic (ATP-PC and glycolysis). Nutritional strategies focus on supporting training quality, optimizing power-to-weight ratio, recovery between efforts, and competition-day readiness.

## Sport Categories

**Very-High-Intensity (<30 seconds)**: 100m sprint, powerlifting, throwing events, jumping events, gymnastics vault
**High-Intensity (1-8 minutes)**: 400-800m running, track cycling sprints, swimming sprints

## Energy System Demands

- ATP-PC system: Primary for 0-10 seconds (explosive efforts)
- Anaerobic glycolysis: Dominant from 10 seconds to ~2 minutes
- High-intensity efforts >2 minutes: Increasing aerobic contribution (up to 50% at 4-5 minutes)
- Recovery between efforts relies on aerobic metabolism to restore PC and clear metabolites

## Macronutrient Recommendations

### Carbohydrate
- **Daily**: 3-10 g/kg/d depending on training volume
- Lower end for very-high-intensity, low-volume training
- Higher end for repeated sprint training with multiple bouts
- **Pre-competition**: 1-4 g/kg, 1-4 hours before
- **During competition**: Not typically needed for single-effort events; may be relevant for multi-heat competitions
- **Recovery**: 1.0-1.2 g/kg/h if multiple training sessions/competition heats

### Protein
- 1.4-2.0 g/kg/d
- Emphasis on post-training recovery (0.25 g/kg per meal, 4-5 meals)
- Leucine-rich sources to support power adaptations

### Fat
- 20-35% total energy
- Not acutely restricted unless for weight management (weight-class sports)
- Emphasis on quality (MUFA, PUFA)

## Body Composition and Weight Management

- Power-to-weight ratio is critical in many of these sports
- Many athletes compete in weight classes or have aesthetic requirements
- **Making weight**: Should be done with minimal fluid manipulation; fat restriction 2-3 days before weigh-in
- Low energy availability risk (especially in weight-class and aesthetic sports)
- Female athlete triad/RED-S screening essential

## Key Micronutrients

- **Iron**: Foot-strike hemolysis in sprinters; monitor ferritin
- **Calcium and Vitamin D**: Bone health critical for high-impact sports
- **Creatine**: Most evidence-based ergogenic aid for repeated high-intensity efforts
- **β-Alanine**: May improve performance in efforts lasting 1-4 minutes (increased muscle carnosine buffering)
- **Sodium Bicarbonate**: Buffering agent for events 1-7 minutes; GI distress is limiting factor
- **Caffeine**: Well-established ergogenic for power and sprint performance (3-6 mg/kg)

## Competition-Day Nutrition

- **Pre-event meal**: Low fat, low fiber, moderate protein, CHO-focused; 1-4 hours before
- **Between heats/events**: Easily digestible CHO (sports drinks, gels, fruit) if multiple efforts
- **Recovery between competition days**: CHO + protein immediately post-competition
- **Hydration**: Pre-hydrate; sweat rates generally lower than endurance but still significant

## Supplement Considerations

Sprint/power athletes are heavy users of supplements. Key evidence-based options:
- **Creatine monohydrate**: Loading 20 g/d × 5-7 days, maintenance 3-5 g/d
- **β-Alanine**: 3.2-6.4 g/d (split doses) over 4+ weeks
- **Caffeine**: 3-6 mg/kg, 60 min pre-event
- **Bicarbonate**: 0.3 g/kg, 60-90 min pre-event (GI issues common)

## Special Considerations

- Multi-heat/multi-event competitions (track meets, swimming): Fueling between events
- Weight-restricted sports: Recovery nutrition after weigh-in (aggressive refueling/rehydration)
- Injury prevention: Adequate energy availability and micronutrients for tendon/ligament health

## Key Takeaways

1. Daily CHO matches training volume (3-10 g/kg/d)
2. Protein supports muscle power and recovery (1.4-2.0 g/kg/d)
3. Body composition management must not compromise training quality
4. Evidence-based ergogenic aids: creatine, β-alanine, caffeine, bicarbonate
5. Recovery nutrition between multi-event competitions is critical
6. Screen for RED-S in weight-class and aesthetic sport athletes
