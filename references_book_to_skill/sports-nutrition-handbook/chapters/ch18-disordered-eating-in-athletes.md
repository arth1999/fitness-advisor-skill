# Chapter 18: Disordered Eating in Athletes

## Key Concepts

Disordered eating (DE) exists on a spectrum from mild restrictive behaviors to clinical eating disorders (EDs). Athletes are at higher risk than the general population due to sport-specific pressures. Early identification and multidisciplinary intervention are critical. The sports dietitian plays a key role in screening, referral, and treatment team participation.

## Spectrum of Disordered Eating

- **Subclinical DE**: Occasional restrictive dieting, food rules, body dissatisfaction
- **Clinical EDs**: Anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder, Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder (OSFED)
- **Orthorexia**: Obsession with "healthy" or "clean" eating — increasingly recognized in athletes
- **Compulsive exercise**: Exercise as compensation for eating, exercise despite injury/illness

## Risk Factors in Athletes

### Sport-Specific Factors
- **Weight-class sports**: Wrestling, rowing, jockeys, martial arts
- **Aesthetic sports**: Gymnastics, figure skating, diving, dance, bodybuilding
- **Endurance sports**: Distance running, cycling (perceived performance benefit from leanness)
- **Revealing uniforms**: Volleyball, swimming, cheerleading
- **Judged sports**: Subjectivity of appearance

### Individual Factors
- Perfectionism, high achievement orientation (common in elite athletes)
- Body dissatisfaction
- History of weight-related comments from coaches/parents
- Traumatic events
- Prior dieting behavior

## The Female Athlete Triad

Three interrelated conditions:
1. **Low Energy Availability** (with or without disordered eating)
2. **Menstrual Dysfunction** (amenorrhea, oligomenorrhea)
3. **Low Bone Mineral Density**

The Triad exists on a spectrum from optimal health to clinical pathology. Even one component warrants investigation.

## Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)

An expanded model (IOC 2014) recognizing that low energy availability affects:
- Menstrual function
- Bone health
- Endocrine function
- Metabolic rate
- Hematological function
- Growth and development (adolescents)
- Psychological function
- Cardiovascular health
- Gastrointestinal function
- Immune function
- Protein synthesis

RED-S affects BOTH male and female athletes.

## Screening and Identification

### Warning Signs
- Preoccupation with food, weight, body shape
- Significant weight changes
- Ritualistic eating behaviors
- Avoidance of eating with others
- Excessive exercise beyond training
- Stress fractures/recurrent injuries
- Cold intolerance, lanugo hair
- Parotid gland enlargement
- Dental erosion

### Screening Tools
- Preparticipation physical examination
- Eating disorder screening questionnaires
- Menstrual history (age of menarche, cycle regularity)
- Bone health history (stress fractures)
- Weight history

## The Role of the Sports Dietitian

### Assessment (within scope)
- Dietary intake patterns
- Weight history and body composition
- Nutrition knowledge and beliefs
- Eating behaviors and attitudes
- Gastrointestinal symptoms

### Referral
- **Psychologist/Psychiatrist**: Core treatment of ED psychopathology
- **Physician**: Medical monitoring (electrolytes, ECG, bone density)
- **Certified Eating Disorder Registered Dietitian (CEDRD)**: Specialized nutrition therapy

### Ongoing Role
- Provide evidence-based nutrition information
- Counter food myths and misinformation
- Support normalized eating patterns
- Collaborate with treatment team
- Monitor for RED-S in at-risk athletes

## Treatment Principles

- Multidisciplinary team approach is essential
- Nutrition therapy focuses on restoring adequate energy availability
- Meal plan structure without rigidity
- Address food fears and rules
- Weight restoration if indicated
- Bone health optimization
- Return-to-play criteria must include medical, psychological, and nutritional clearance

## Prevention

- **Coach education**: Avoid weight-related comments; focus on performance not appearance
- **Body composition testing**: Use caution; de-emphasize numbers; consider blind weighing
- **Nutrition education**: Food as fuel; performance benefits of adequate intake
- **Culture change**: Promote healthy body image in sport environments
- **Policy development**: Protocols for identification, referral, and return to play

## Key Takeaways

1. DE/EDs are more prevalent in athletes, especially in weight-sensitive sports
2. RED-S expands the Triad concept to males and multiple body systems
3. Sports dietitians should screen but refer to specialized ED treatment teams
4. Multidisciplinary treatment is essential
5. Prevention requires culture change in sport environments
6. Return-to-play requires medical, psychological, and nutritional clearance
