# Chapter 6: Fluid, Electrolytes, and Exercise

## Key Concepts

Water is the most biologically active molecule in the body. Maintaining euhydration supports cardiovascular and thermoregulatory function. Even 1% body weight loss from dehydration can impair performance, especially in warm environments. The thirst mechanism is an imprecise gauge of immediate fluid needs during exercise.

## Daily Fluid Needs

- **Sedentary adults**: AI is 3.7 L/d (men), 2.7 L/d (women) from all sources
- **Athletes**: May require >7-8 L/d; can exceed 10 L/d in hot conditions with heavy training
- ~80% from fluids, ~20% from food water content
- Daily fluid turnover: 8.3% TBW (sedentary) to 17%+ (heavy-sweating athlete)

## Hydration Before Exercise

- Drink 5-7 mL/kg body weight at least 4 hours before exercise
- If no urine output, drink additional 3-5 mL/kg 2 hours before
- Goal: start euhydrated with light-colored urine
- Hyperhydration offers no benefit; do not overdrink

## Hydration During Exercise

- Prevent >2% body weight loss from dehydration
- General guideline: 0.4-0.8 L/h, individualized by sweat rate
- Drink every 10-20 minutes
- Benefits of adequate fluid intake: lower heart rate, lower core temperature, higher stroke volume, lower perceived exertion, better performance

## Calculating Sweat Rate

**Sweat Loss (mL) = (Pre-exercise BW - Post-exercise BW in g) + Fluid Intake (mL) - Urine Output (mL)**

**Sweat Rate = Sweat Loss (mL) / Exercise Time (min)**

## Hydration After Exercise

- Drink 125-150% of body weight loss (1.25-1.5 L per kg lost)
- Plain water is a thirst quencher but NOT an effective rehydrator
- Sodium in fluids/foods maintains osmotic drive to drink and reduces urine output
- Snacks for rehydration: tomato juice, pretzels, pickles, crackers, sports drinks
- Avoid alcohol during rapid rehydration

## Electrolyte Balance

**Sweat composition**: Sodium and chloride are most abundant and variable (20-80 mmol/L each). Potassium, magnesium, and calcium losses are lower and more constant.

**Sodium replacement recommended when**:
- Sweat loss >4 L/d
- Heavy, salty sweaters (visible salt residue on skin/clothing)
- Prolonged exercise >2 hours
- History of muscle cramps

**Daily sodium needs for heavy sweaters may exceed the general population recommendation of 1,500 mg/d — the DRI notes this does NOT apply to highly active individuals.**

## Hyponatremia (Exercise-Associated)

Life-threatening condition from excessive water intake diluting plasma sodium (<135 mEq/L). Prevention: do not overdrink, consume sodium during prolonged exercise, avoid gaining weight during exercise.

## Special Environmental Considerations

- **Heat/humidity**: Profuse sweating, high electrolyte loss
- **Cold**: Increased respiratory water loss, sweat rates may still exceed 1 L/h under clothing
- **Altitude**: Increased respiratory and urinary water loss, reduced thirst and food intake
- **Water immersion**: Swimmers can sweat during intense workouts

## Dehydration and Performance

Dehydration increases heart rate, core temperature, perceived exertion, muscle glycogen use, and carbohydrate oxidation while decreasing stroke volume and cardiac output. Cross-country runners dehydrated by <1% body weight showed ~3% reduction in 10K performance.

## Practical Hydration Tips

1. Start exercise well-hydrated (light-colored urine)
2. Drink during exercise — early and often, but don't overdrink
3. Weigh yourself before and after to assess fluid balance
4. Don't rely on thirst alone during intense exercise
5. Include sodium during prolonged/heavy sweating
6. Don't restrict salt in the diet if you sweat heavily
7. Don't gain weight during exercise (indicates overdrinking)
