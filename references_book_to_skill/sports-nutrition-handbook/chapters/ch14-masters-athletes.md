# Chapter 14: Masters Athletes

## Key Concepts

Masters athletes (typically ≥35 years) represent a growing demographic in competitive sports. Aging brings physiological changes that affect nutrition requirements: decreased RMR, reduced muscle protein synthetic response, altered hormone profiles, and increased need for specific nutrients.

## Physiological Changes with Aging

- **Sarcopenia**: Age-related loss of muscle mass (~1-2% per year after age 50)
- **Anabolic resistance**: Reduced MPS response to protein feeding and exercise; older muscle requires higher leucine doses
- **Decreased RMR**: Due to loss of metabolically active lean tissue
- **Reduced maximal heart rate and VO₂max**: Though trainable
- **Decreased thirst sensitivity**: Higher dehydration risk
- **Bone loss**: Accelerated in postmenopausal women

## Energy Needs

- Generally lower than younger athletes due to decreased RMR and often reduced training volume
- Still use same estimation equations as younger athletes, but accurately quantify activity
- Energy deficit risk: appetite may not keep pace with reduced needs
- Weight management: LBM preservation is the priority, not just weight loss

## Protein Requirements

- **Higher per-meal doses needed**: 30-40 g (0.4 g/kg) high-quality protein per meal vs. 20-25 g for younger adults
- **Leucine requirement**: ~3 g per meal to overcome anabolic resistance
- **Daily intake**: 1.2-2.0 g/kg/d depending on training type
- **Timing**: Post-exercise and pre-sleep protein may be especially beneficial
- Combining resistance training with adequate protein is the most effective countermeasure to sarcopenia

## Micronutrient Considerations

- **Vitamin D**: Deficiency risk increases with age (decreased skin synthesis, less sun exposure). Supplementation with D-3 often warranted.
- **Calcium**: 1,200 mg/d for women >50 and men >70; important with declining bone density
- **Vitamin B-12**: Absorption decreases with age (reduced gastric acid); supplementation may be needed
- **Iron**: Deficiency less common in postmenopausal women; monitor in masters male endurance athletes
- **Antioxidants**: May help combat age-related oxidative stress but avoid high-dose supplementation that blunts training adaptations

## Hydration

- **Decreased thirst sensitivity** with aging increases dehydration risk
- Masters athletes lose proportionally more fluid per unit of sweat
- Kidney function decline may affect fluid/electrolyte handling
- Proactive hydration strategies essential

## Recovery Considerations

- Recovery takes longer with age due to reduced muscle repair capacity
- Adequate protein timing becomes more critical
- Sleep quality often declines with age; nutrition strategies to support sleep (tart cherry juice, tryptophan-containing foods)
- Joint health: omega-3 fatty acids, collagen-supporting nutrients (vitamin C, copper)

## Chronic Disease Management

Masters athletes may have:
- Hypertension, dyslipidemia, type 2 diabetes
- Medications affecting nutrient status or fluid balance
- Exercise recommendations must account for medical conditions

## Key Takeaways

1. Protein needs are HIGHER per meal for older athletes (0.4 vs. 0.25 g/kg)
2. Leucine threshold is higher (~3 g/meal)
3. Vitamin D and calcium are critical for bone health
4. Proactive hydration is needed due to reduced thirst
5. Recovery takes longer; nutrition timing matters more
6. Resistance training + adequate protein is the primary sarcopenia countermeasure
7. Monitor for drug-nutrient interactions with chronic disease medications
