# Chapter 21: Nutrition for High-Intensity, Intermittent Sports

## Key Concepts

Intermittent sports feature repeated bouts of high-intensity activity interspersed with lower-intensity recovery or rest. Examples: soccer, basketball, hockey, tennis, football, lacrosse. Energy demands combine anaerobic power with aerobic endurance. Nutrition must support both explosive efforts and sustained performance across matches/games lasting 60-90+ minutes.

## Physiological Demands

- Repeated sprints with incomplete recovery (~10-30 high-intensity efforts per game)
- High muscle glycogen utilization due to repeated anaerobic bursts
- Significant sweat and electrolyte losses (indoor and outdoor)
- Frequent physical contact and associated muscle damage
- Tournament formats often require multiple games in short time frames

## Macronutrient Recommendations

### Carbohydrate
- **Daily**: 5-10 g/kg/d depending on training/game schedule
- Higher end during heavy training periods and tournament play
- **Pre-game**: 1-4 g/kg, 1-4 hours before
- **During game**: 30-60 g/h where practical (halftime, sideline, breaks in play)
- **Post-game**: 1.0-1.2 g/kg immediately, repeat within 4 hours if next game <24 hours
- CHO mouth rinse may benefit performance even without ingestion during play

### Protein
- 1.4-2.0 g/kg/d
- Distributed across 4-5 meals for muscle repair and adaptation
- Post-game: 20-25 g high-quality protein with CHO
- Anti-inflammatory role: adequate protein supports immune function during heavy competition

### Fat
- 20-35% total energy
- Emphasis on anti-inflammatory fatty acids (n-3 PUFAs from fish)
- Pre-game: limit fat to promote gastric emptying

## Hydration Strategies

- High sweat rates common (indoor arenas, outdoor heat)
- Fluid loss of 1-3 L per game is typical
- Opportunities to drink: pre-game, halftime, timeouts, substitutions
- Sports drinks preferred: provide fluid, CHO, and electrolytes simultaneously
- Post-game rehydration: 150% of weight loss in fluid with sodium

## Nutrient Timing for Team Sports

### Training Days
- Pre-training: CHO 1-2 hours before
- During training: Water or sports drink based on intensity/duration
- Post-training: CHO + protein within 30-60 min

### Game Day
- Pre-game meal: 3-4 hours before (familiar, low-fat, moderate protein, high CHO)
- Pre-game snack: 1 hour before if needed (sports bar, banana)
- Halftime: Sports drink, fruit, easily digestible CHO sources
- Post-game: Immediate recovery nutrition; full meal within 2-3 hours

### Tournament/Multiple Games
- Recovery between games is priority
- CHO + protein immediately after each game
- Rehydration with sodium
- Easily digestible meals/snacks between games
- Plan ahead for food availability

## Key Micronutrients

- **Iron**: Monitor in female athletes; foot-strike hemolysis in court sports
- **Vitamin D**: Indoor sport athletes at risk; limited sun exposure
- **Calcium**: Bone health for contact sports
- **Antioxidants**: May support recovery; food-first approach

## Evidence-Based Supplements

- **Caffeine**: 3-6 mg/kg; improves sprint performance, reaction time, and reduces RPE
- **Creatine**: Benefits repeated sprint performance in some studies
- **β-Alanine**: May benefit high-intensity intermittent running
- **Beetroot juice/Nitrate**: Mixed evidence for intermittent sports
- **Sports drinks/gels**: Practical CHO delivery during competition

## Weight Management in Team Sports

- Body composition may affect position-specific performance
- Weight management should occur in off-season/pre-season, not in-season
- In-season energy restriction impairs performance and recovery
- Focus on performance nutrition rather than weight during competition season

## Special Considerations

- **Travel**: Plan nutrition for away games; pack familiar foods
- **Late games**: Recovery nutrition timing around sleep
- **Concussion**: Nutrition support for brain recovery (energy, protein, n-3, antioxidants)
- **Injury**: Maintain energy/protein intake to minimize lean mass loss

## Key Takeaways

1. CHO periodization: 5-10 g/kg/d based on training/game demands
2. Hydration with CHO-electrolyte beverages during competition
3. Nutrient timing around games is critical (pre, during, post, between)
4. Tournament play demands aggressive recovery nutrition
5. Iron and vitamin D are key micronutrients for indoor sport athletes
6. In-season weight management should be avoided
7. Caffeine and creatine are most evidence-based supplements
