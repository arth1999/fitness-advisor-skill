# Chapter 12: Child and Adolescent Athletes

## Key Concepts

Youth sports participation exceeds 50 million children in the US; 7.7 million at the high school level. Children are not miniature adults — their nutritional needs must account for growth, development, and maturation (Tanner staging). Parents and volunteer coaches often lack nutrition expertise.

## Growth and Development Considerations

- **Tanner Staging** (Sexual Maturity Ratings 1-5): Guides nutrition planning. Energy and nutrient needs vary by stage.
- Peak height velocity and peak bone mineral accrual occur during puberty.
- Bone mineral density reaches its nadir during peak height velocity — clinical significance for fracture risk.
- Early sport specialization increases overuse injury risk.

## Energy Needs

- Must support growth, development, AND training
- Children have higher RMR per kg than adults
- Energy deficiency impairs growth, delays puberty, compromises bone accrual
- Overweight/obesity in youth athletes is increasing; weight-teasing correlates with disordered eating

## Macronutrient Recommendations

- **Carbohydrate**: 3-12 g/kg/d based on activity; teens often fall short
- **Protein**: 1.35-1.6 g/kg/d; nitrogen balance studies support this range for adolescent athletes
- **Fat**: 20-35% energy; emphasize quality (PUFAs for brain development). DHA critical for myelination continuing into early adulthood. DHA intake falls short in most adolescents (40-160 mg/d vs need).

## Key Micronutrient Concerns

- **Vitamin D**: ~10.3% of US children deficient (<16 ng/mL); deficiency associated with muscle weakness, fatigue, and impaired bone accrual. Pediatric cutoffs differ from adult standards.
- **Calcium**: 1,000 mg/d (4-8y), 1,300 mg/d (9-18y). ~50% of lifetime bone calcium deposited during puberty. Inadequate intake increases lifetime osteoporosis risk.
- **Iron**: 9-16% of adolescent females iron deficient. Causes: rapid blood volume expansion, menarche, poor diet, undiagnosed celiac/H. pylori. Hepcidin (increased by intense training) further reduces absorption.

## n-3 Fatty Acids for Brain Development

Brain is ~60% fat; DHA accumulates in learning/memory areas (cortex, hippocampus). Adequate intake recommendations:
- 4-8 years: 0.9 g/d
- 9-13 years: 1.0-1.2 g/d
- 14-18 years: 1.1-1.6 g/d

## Ergogenic Aids in Youth

- **58% of high school athletes** report using ergogenic aids
- **1.2 million US athletes under 18** used supplements for sports enhancement (past 30 days)
- **Creatine**: ACSM and AAP advise AGAINST use in <18 years due to inadequate safety data
- Sports dietitians should exercise extreme caution with supplements in pediatric populations

## Nutrition Assessment in Youth

- Children have poor dietary recall; parents must be included
- May need 6-9 recalls for reliability in children, 3-6 in teens
- Alcohol screening: 24% of adolescent athletes report binge drinking
- Team sport athletes more likely to abuse alcohol than individual sport athletes

## Female Athlete Triad in Adolescents

Screen for low energy availability, menstrual dysfunction, and low BMD. Delayed menarche is a red flag. Energy deficiency during adolescence may permanently compromise peak bone mass.

## Practical Strategies

1. Use Tanner staging to tailor nutrition education
2. Assess developmental learning styles
3. Include parents in education
4. Focus on food-first approach for fueling
5. Prioritize calcium, vitamin D, iron, and DHA
6. Avoid recommending performance-enhancing supplements
