# Chapter 5: Vitamins, Minerals, and Exercise

## Key Concepts

Micronutrients enable macronutrient utilization and are critical for energy metabolism, oxygen delivery, antioxidant defense, bone health, and immune function. If energy intake is adequate and diet is varied, vitamin and mineral needs of athletes are generally similar to the general population (DRIs).

## Categories of Micronutrient Concern

### By Risk Due to Training
- **Energy metabolism**: B-vitamins (thiamin, riboflavin, B-6, niacin) as coenzymes in glycolysis, Krebs cycle, electron transport chain
- **Antioxidants**: Vitamins C, E, A, selenium — counter exercise-induced oxidative stress. Caution: excessive supplementation may blunt training adaptations
- **Red blood cell function**: Iron, vitamin B-12, folate — critical for oxygen delivery
- **Bone health**: Calcium, vitamin D, phosphorus, vitamin K
- **Immune function**: Vitamin C, zinc
- **Electrolytes**: Sodium, potassium, chloride, magnesium (lost in sweat)

### By Risk Due to Dietary Intake
- **Vegetarians/Vegans**: B-12, iron, zinc
- **Energy-restricted diets**: Broad micronutrient risk
- **Low-CHO diets**: May reduce thiamin, B-vitamins, vitamin C intake

### By Population Risk
- **Female athletes**: Iron (up to 60% may have depletion), calcium, vitamin D
- **Altitude training**: Iron for erythropoiesis; antioxidants for increased oxidative stress
- **Heat/humidity training**: Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium)

## Key Micronutrient Recommendations

| Nutrient | Key Concern | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Iron | Most common deficiency; impairs aerobic capacity | RDA; supplement only if depleted/deficient (ferritin <20 ng/mL) |
| Calcium | Bone health; intakes often below RDA | At least DRI; dairy sources highest bioavailability |
| Vitamin D | Widespread deficiency; muscle weakness | RDA; D-3 form preferred; 15-30 min sun exposure |
| Vitamin C | Upper respiratory tract infections | At least 100 mg/d; up to 500 mg/d for ultraendurance; UL = 2,000 mg/d |
| B-12 | Vegan athletes at risk | RDA; supplementation if dietary intake inadequate |
| Zinc | Immune function; sweat losses | RDA |

## Iron Assessment and Supplementation

- **Stages**: Iron depletion (low ferritin) → iron-deficiency anemia (low Hb)
- **Supplementation**: 100-150 mg elemental iron for anemia; 25-65 mg for depletion stage 1
- **Absorption enhancers**: Heme iron, vitamin C, meat protein factor, low iron stores
- **Absorption inhibitors**: Phytates, oxalates, tannins, excessive calcium/zinc
- Hepcidin (increased by intense exercise, inflammation) reduces iron absorption

## Antioxidants and Training Adaptations

Caution: High-dose antioxidant supplementation (vitamin C 1,000 mg/d) may blunt training-induced mitochondrial biogenesis and VO₂max improvements. Food-first approach to antioxidants is recommended over high-dose supplements.

## Vitamin D and Performance

- Low vitamin D associated with decreased muscle strength and power
- Deficiency linked to type II muscle fiber atrophy
- Male athletes with higher vitamin D levels show better aerobic capacity
- Supplement with D-3 (cholecalciferol), not D-2

## Calcium and Body Composition

Research on calcium's effect on weight loss is equivocal. Calcium from food (not supplements) may benefit body composition maintenance. Athletes should meet DRI primarily through food.
