# Chapter 13: College Athletes

## Key Concepts

College athletes face unique challenges: demanding training schedules, academic pressures, limited cooking skills/facilities, restricted food access, and often inadequate nutrition budgets. The collegiate sports dietitian must address both performance nutrition and life skills.

## Energy Availability

- Many college athletes report low energy intakes despite high training volumes
- Female athletes at particular risk: over 60% of collegiate female athletes desire weight loss
- Low energy availability contributes to the Female Athlete Triad and Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)
- Dining hall limitations: hours may not align with training, options limited, quality variable

## Common Nutritional Challenges

### Food Access and Skills
- Limited cooking facilities (dorm restrictions)
- Limited budget for food beyond meal plan
- Lack of meal planning and preparation skills
- Reliance on convenience/fast foods

### Scheduling Conflicts
- Early morning training before dining halls open
- Classes through lunch periods
- Evening practices interfering with dinner
- Travel for competition disrupting routines

### Weight Management Pressures
- Sport-specific body composition expectations
- "Making weight" in wrestling, lightweight rowing
- Leanness emphasis in endurance and aesthetic sports
- Body image concerns, especially among female athletes

## Alcohol and College Athletes

- Binge drinking prevalent; team sport athletes at higher risk
- Alcohol impairs recovery, hydration, glycogen repletion, muscle protein synthesis
- Empty calories displace nutrient-dense foods
- Higher rates among males, white athletes, and team sport participants

## Nutrition Strategies

### Dining Hall Navigation
- Teach athletes to build performance plates (CHO + protein + color)
- Identify nutrient-dense options within dining hall constraints
- Create grab-and-go options for early/late training

### Supplement Awareness
- College athletes are prime targets for supplement marketing
- NCAA has specific banned substance lists
- Education on supplement risks and third-party certification

### Travel Nutrition
- Plan ahead for competition travel
- Pack portable, familiar foods
- Hydration strategies for air travel and new environments

## Medical Nutrition Therapy

- **Diabetes**: Type 1 diabetic athletes need coordinated care; sports dietitian as part of treatment team (see Chapter 19)
- **GI issues**: Common in endurance athletes; FODMAP awareness may help
- **Injuries**: Nutrition support for healing (adequate energy, protein, micronutrients)
- **Disordered eating**: Referral to eating disorder specialists; multidisciplinary approach (see Chapter 18)

## NCAA Regulations

- Understand NCAA rules regarding meals, snacks, and supplements
- Athletics departments may provide limited meals/snacks per regulations
- Sports RDNs should be aware of compliance rules

## Key Takeaways

1. Assess food access, cooking skills, and budget in addition to dietary intake
2. Address body composition goals with evidence-based, health-first approach
3. Screen for disordered eating and the Female Athlete Triad
4. Educate on supplement safety and NCAA banned substances
5. Provide practical strategies for dining hall, travel, and time management
6. Coordinate care with athletic trainers, team physicians, and coaches
