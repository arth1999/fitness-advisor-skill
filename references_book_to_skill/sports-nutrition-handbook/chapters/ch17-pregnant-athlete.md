# Chapter 17: Nutrition and Exercise Guidance for the Pregnant Athlete

## Key Concepts

Pregnancy presents unique nutritional considerations for the athletic woman. Many athletes can continue training during pregnancy with appropriate modifications. Nutritional goals must support both maternal health, fetal development, and athletic performance where appropriate. Individualized guidance from a sports dietitian working with the obstetric care team is essential.

## Energy Needs During Pregnancy

- **First trimester**: No additional energy needed beyond baseline
- **Second trimester**: Additional ~340 kcal/d
- **Third trimester**: Additional ~452 kcal/d
- Energy needs increase further with continued training
- Adequate energy intake is critical — energy restriction during pregnancy is contraindicated
- Weight gain guidelines depend on pre-pregnancy BMI (IOM guidelines)

## Weight Gain Recommendations (IOM)

| Pre-pregnancy BMI | Total Weight Gain | Rate (2nd/3rd trimester) |
|---|---|---|
| Underweight (<18.5) | 28-40 lb (12.5-18 kg) | 1 lb/wk (0.5 kg/wk) |
| Normal (18.5-24.9) | 25-35 lb (11.5-16 kg) | 1 lb/wk (0.4 kg/wk) |
| Overweight (25-29.9) | 15-25 lb (7-11.5 kg) | 0.6 lb/wk (0.3 kg/wk) |
| Obese (≥30) | 11-20 lb (5-9 kg) | 0.5 lb/wk (0.2 kg/wk) |

## Macronutrient Considerations

- **Carbohydrate**: At least 175 g/d minimum; higher for training athletes
- **Protein**: 1.1 g/kg/d minimum; likely higher (1.2-1.5 g/kg/d) for training athletes
- **Fat**: Essential fatty acids critical for fetal brain development; DHA intake of 200-300 mg/d recommended
- Emphasize nutrient density over energy density

## Key Micronutrients

- **Folate/Folic Acid**: 600 mcg/d; neural tube defect prevention; critical in first trimester
- **Iron**: 27 mg/d; needs nearly double; supplementation often required
- **Calcium**: 1,000-1,300 mg/d; fetal skeletal development
- **Vitamin D**: 600 IU/d minimum; may need more if deficient
- **Choline**: 450 mg/d; important for fetal brain development
- **Iodine**: 220 mcg/d; thyroid function for fetal neurodevelopment
- **DHA/EPA**: 200-300 mg DHA/d; critical for fetal brain and retinal development

## Exercise During Pregnancy

- **ACOG guidelines**: 150 min/week moderate-intensity activity for uncomplicated pregnancies
- **Contraindications**: Placenta previa, incompetent cervix, multiple gestation with preterm labor risk, severe anemia, preeclampsia
- **Warning signs to stop**: Vaginal bleeding, dizziness, chest pain, calf swelling, decreased fetal movement, amniotic fluid leakage
- **Modifications**: Avoid supine exercise after first trimester, avoid contact sports, avoid activities with fall risk
- **Core temperature**: Avoid overheating; adequate hydration essential

## Hydration

- Increased blood volume requires more fluid
- Increased sweat rate sensitivity in some women
- Adequate hydration helps prevent preterm contractions
- Electrolyte needs may increase

## Gastrointestinal Considerations

- Nausea/vomiting (morning sickness): small frequent meals, bland carbohydrates, ginger
- Heartburn: avoid large meals, fatty/spicy foods, eat upright
- Constipation: increased fiber, adequate fluid, continued activity

## Postpartum Nutrition

- **Lactation**: Additional ~500 kcal/d; increased protein (1.1 g/kg/d) and fluid needs
- **Return to training**: Gradual; allow pelvic floor recovery (typically 6 weeks, longer for C-section)
- **Weight loss**: Gradual (0.5-1 lb/week); rapid loss may compromise milk supply
- Continued micronutrient support (iron if significant blood loss at delivery)

## Special Concerns for Athletes

- Maintaining appropriate exercise intensity (RPE-based rather than heart rate)
- Body image changes and expectations for return to pre-pregnancy form
- Pelvic floor rehabilitation before high-impact activity
- Diastasis recti assessment and management
- Energy availability for both training and lactation
