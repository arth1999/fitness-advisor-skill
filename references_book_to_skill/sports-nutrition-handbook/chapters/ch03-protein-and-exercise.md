# Chapter 3: Protein and Exercise

## Key Concepts

Protein contributes only ~2-4% of energy during exercise, but dietary protein is fundamental for muscle repair, remodeling, and accretion. Both resistance and endurance exercise profoundly influence muscle protein synthesis (MPS) and muscle protein breakdown (MPB). Net protein balance (NPB = MPS - MPB) is zero over the day in stable state; positive NPB is required for hypertrophy and adaptation.

## Daily Protein Recommendations

| Training Type | Protein (g/kg/d) |
|---|---|
| Endurance | 1.2-1.4 |
| Resistance | 1.6-1.7 |

Emphasis on timing and distribution: ~0.25 g/kg per meal, 4-5 meals/day.

## Protein Timing

- The "anabolic window" is wider than commonly believed; synergistic effect of exercise and feeding persists up to 24 hours post-exercise.
- Consuming protein within 3 hours post-exercise seems beneficial for lean mass and strength gains.
- Meta-analysis found no evidence that protein within ≤1 hour pre-/post-exercise augments hypertrophy beyond total daily intake distributed throughout the day.
- Pre-sleep protein (casein, ~40g) may augment overnight MPS and training adaptations.
- During ultraendurance exercise, protein + CHO coingestion improves whole-body NPB even without immediate performance benefit.

## Protein Type and Quality

**The Leucine Trigger**: ~2-3g leucine per meal is needed to maximally stimulate MPS. Animal proteins (whey, milk, beef, egg) are superior to plant proteins due to higher leucine content and EAA profiles.

| Protein Type | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| Whey | Fast-digesting, high leucine (14%), superior MPS stimulation |
| Casein | Slow-digesting, prolonged amino acid release, suitable pre-sleep |
| Soy | Fast-digesting, lower leucine, inferior to milk for hypertrophy |
| Milk | ~20% whey / 80% casein, superior to soy for lean mass and strength gains |

**Key finding**: Milk (500mL nonfat) post-exercise produces greater lean mass gains and fat mass losses compared with soy or CHO-only.

## Protein Quantity Per Meal

- Maximal MPS stimulation at ~20g high-quality protein (young adults)
- 40g provides no additional benefit — excess amino acids are oxidized
- Dose relative to body weight: ~0.24 g/kg/meal (range 0.18-0.30)
- Larger individuals (~100 kg) may need ~26g; smaller (~50 kg) ~16g
- Protein consumed beyond needs is oxidized or excreted, not stored as muscle

## Endurance Exercise and Protein

- Higher-intensity/longer-duration exercise increases amino acid oxidation
- Adequate energy intake is paramount — inadequate energy causes protein to be oxidized rather than used for synthesis
- Tournament-level endurance athletes may require 1.5-1.8 g/kg/d
- Female endurance athletes may have slightly higher requirements than males
- Mitochondrial protein synthesis is responsive to protein feeding post-exercise

## Amino Acid Supplementation — Not Recommended

- **Glutamine**: No effect on strength or lean mass gains
- **L-arginine**: No effect on muscle blood flow, nitric oxide markers, or MPS at rest or post-exercise
- Neither supplement is recommended for healthy adults

## Practical Takeaways

1. Distribute protein across 4-5 meals/day at ~0.25 g/kg each
2. Consume protein within 1-2 hours post-exercise
3. Prioritize nutrient-dense whole foods (milk, eggs, meat, fish) over isolated powders
4. Ensure adequate energy intake to spare protein for synthesis
5. Vegetarian athletes: consider quinoa, protein blends, or leucine-fortified plant proteins
