# Chapter 1: Physiology of Exercise

## Key Concepts

Exercise physiology studies the body's acute responses and chronic adaptations to exercise. Homeostasis is challenged by physical activity, requiring coordinated responses from cardiorespiratory, musculoskeletal, and metabolic systems.

## Energy Systems

Three metabolic pathways produce ATP for muscle contraction:

1. **ATP-PC System** (anaerobic, 0-10 seconds): Uses stored phosphocreatine for immediate, explosive movements. Limited by small PC stores.
2. **Anaerobic Glycolysis** (10 seconds - 2 minutes): Breaks down glucose/glycogen without oxygen. Produces lactate as a byproduct, which contributes to short-term fatigue via cellular acidosis.
3. **Oxidative Phosphorylation** (aerobic, 2+ minutes): Uses carbohydrates, fats, and proteins with oxygen via Krebs cycle and electron transport chain. Virtually unlimited ATP production as long as substrates and oxygen are available.

## The Crossover Concept

All systems operate concurrently. Fat is the preferred fuel at rest and low intensity (more ATP per gram, but slower). As intensity increases, there is greater reliance on carbohydrate because glucose is oxidized more rapidly and is more oxygen-efficient. Carbohydrate can produce ATP both aerobically and anaerobically.

## Muscle Fiber Types

- **Type I** (slow-twitch): High aerobic endurance, fat oxidation, prolonged contraction. Used in endurance events.
- **Type IIa** (fast-twitch oxidative): Moderate endurance, recruited for higher-intensity events of short duration.
- **Type IIx** (fast-twitch glycolytic): Poor endurance, used in explosive activities like sprints or weightlifting.

## Fatigue Mechanisms

- **Short-term (metabolic) fatigue**: Accumulation of lactate and hydrogen ions, decreased pH, impaired glycolysis when oxygen delivery cannot meet ATP demand.
- **Long-term (substrate) fatigue** ("bonking"): Glycogen depletion when dietary carbohydrate and gluconeogenesis cannot keep pace with muscle ATP demand.

## Training Principles

1. **Individuality**: Genetic factors determine body type (ectomorph, mesomorph, endomorph) and training responsiveness.
2. **Specificity**: Adaptations are specific to exercise mode, intensity, and duration.
3. **Progressive Overload**: Continuous increases in training stimulus are required for improvement.
4. **Periodization/Variation**: Structured cycles (macrocycles, mesocycles, microcycles) and training blocks optimize performance and prevent overtraining.
5. **Detraining**: Cessation leads to loss of cardiorespiratory and muscular gains within 10-20 days.

## Overtraining Syndrome (OTS)

OTS involves long-term performance decrements with physiological and psychological symptoms: persistent fatigue, decreased appetite, weight loss, sleep disruption, and mood disturbances. Recovery requires weeks or months.

## Scope of Practice for RDNs

RDNs should:
- Be familiar with Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans
- Discuss health benefits of exercise
- Assess physical activity history and readiness to change
- Refer to qualified exercise professionals (ACSM-certified, NSCA-certified) for fitness assessments and exercise prescriptions

RDNs should NOT prescribe exercise or conduct fitness assessments without appropriate additional certifications.
