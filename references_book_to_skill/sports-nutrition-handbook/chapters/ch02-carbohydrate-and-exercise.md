# Chapter 2: Carbohydrate and Exercise

## Key Concepts

Carbohydrate (CHO) is the predominant fuel for moderate-to-high-intensity exercise. Adequate stores of muscle glycogen (300-400g), liver glycogen (75-100g), and blood glucose (5g) are critical for performance. Depletion of endogenous CHO stores impairs both endurance and high-intensity intermittent exercise.

## Daily Carbohydrate Recommendations

Expressed in g/kg body weight/day, not percentage of energy:

| Training Load | CHO Intake (g/kg/d) |
|---|---|
| Very light (skill-based) | 3-5 |
| Moderate (60 min/d) | 5-7 |
| Moderate-high endurance (1-3 h/d) | 6-10 |
| Extreme (4-5+ h/d) | 8-12 |

Adjust for total energy needs, training feedback, and specific goals.

## Carbohydrate Loading (Glycogen Supercompensation)

- Increases muscle glycogen to ~210-230 mmol/kg (from typical 130-135 mmol/kg)
- Modern protocol: 36-48 hours rest + 10-12 g/kg/d CHO intake (no depletion phase needed)
- Benefits events >90 minutes; extends steady-state exercise ~20%, improves performance ~2-3%
- Repeated loading within 48 hours may be less effective
- Not effective for events <90 minutes or for increasing muscle girth in bodybuilders

## Pre-Exercise Carbohydrate

- 1-4 g/kg, consumed 1-4 hours before exercise
- Closer to exercise = smaller amount (1 g/kg at 1h, 4 g/kg at 4h)
- Low-GI foods may benefit when CHO cannot be consumed during exercise
- Low-fat, low-fiber, low-moderate protein for gastrointestinal comfort
- If unable to eat breakfast: ~30g easily digested CHO 5 min before exercise

## Carbohydrate During Exercise

Based on absolute intake (g/h), not body weight:

| Exercise Duration | Recommended CHO Intake |
|---|---|
| <30 min | Not necessary |
| 30-75 min (high-intensity) | Small amounts or mouth rinse |
| 1-2 h (endurance/intermittent) | 30 g/h |
| 2-3 h (endurance) | 60 g/h |
| >2.5 h (ultraendurance) | Up to 90 g/h (multiple transportable CHO) |

**Multiple Transportable Carbohydrates**: Glucose+fructose combinations exceed the 1 g/min absorption limit of glucose alone (SGLT1 saturation), achieving up to 75% higher oxidation rates. Performance benefit evident in events >2.5 hours.

**Mouth Rinse Effect**: CHO mouth rinse improves performance in 30-75 min high-intensity exercise via CNS activation, without requiring ingestion.

## Post-Exercise Carbohydrate

- When sessions <8 hours apart: 1.0-1.2 g/kg/h for first 4 hours, consumed every 15-30 min
- Adding ~0.3 g/kg/h protein (~20g) when CHO intake is suboptimal enhances glycogen synthesis
- Recovery snack: 0.25-0.3 g/kg high-quality protein + CHO
- Moderate-to-high GI foods may optimize glycogen resynthesis
- When recovery >24 hours: timing and pattern less critical if total intake is adequate

## Glycemic Index (GI) in Sport

- Low-GI pre-exercise: may provide sustained glucose release
- Moderate-to-high GI during and after exercise: promotes CHO oxidation and glycogen repletion
- When CHO is consumed during exercise, pre-exercise GI effects are negated
- GI should not be the sole criterion; individual tolerance and practicality matter

## Training with Low Carbohydrate Availability ("Training Low")

- Promotes mitochondrial biogenesis and fat oxidation adaptations
- Methods: fasted training, twice-daily training, restricting CHO post-exercise, "sleeping low"
- May enhance training adaptations but performance benefits are unproven
- Risks: compromised training intensity, immune suppression, increased muscle protein breakdown
- Best used as nutritional periodization: combine "train low" sessions with "train high" sessions
- Carbohydrate with high availability recommended for heavy training and pre-competition phases
