# Chapter 22: Nutrition for Endurance and Ultraendurance Sports

## Key Concepts

Endurance (>90 minutes) and ultraendurance (>4-5 hours) events present unique nutritional challenges: high energy expenditure, glycogen depletion risk, substantial fluid/electrolyte losses, GI distress, and suppressed appetite. Events include marathon, triathlon, ultrarunning, distance cycling, distance swimming, and cross-country skiing. Nutrition strategy is often the determining factor between success and DNF.

## Energy Demands

- **Training**: 4,000-8,000+ kcal/d during heavy training blocks
- **Competition**: 500-1,000 kcal/h expenditure; intake targets 30-50% of expenditure
- Energy deficit during events is expected and managed, not eliminated
- High energy expenditure can suppress appetite ("exercise-induced anorexia")
- Liquid calories become essential for meeting needs

## Carbohydrate Periodization for Endurance

### Daily Training Diet
- 6-12 g/kg/d depending on training volume
- Higher during heavy training blocks, lower during recovery
- CHO periodization: train high for key sessions, train low strategically for adaptation

### Carbohydrate Loading (Pre-Event)
- 36-48 hours rest + 10-12 g/kg/d CHO
- Low-fiber, low-residue foods to minimize GI weight and discomfort
- 1-4 g/kg pre-event meal 1-4 hours before start

### During Event CHO Intake
| Duration | CHO Target |
|---|---|
| 1-2 hours | 30 g/h |
| 2-3 hours | 60 g/h (single or multiple transportable CHO) |
| >2.5 hours | Up to 90 g/h (multiple transportable CHO required) |

**Multiple transportable carbohydrates** (glucose + fructose) overcome the 1 g/min SGLT1 absorption limit, achieving up to 75% higher oxidation rates and 8% performance improvement vs. glucose alone.

### Post-Event/Recovery
- 1.0-1.2 g/kg/h for first 4 hours
- Add 0.25-0.3 g/kg protein
- Frequent small feedings (every 15-30 min) if recovery time limited

## Hydration and Electrolytes

- Sweat rates: 0.5-3.0 L/h depending on conditions and individual
- Dehydration >2% body weight impairs performance
- Hyponatremia risk with overdrinking plain water (especially slower runners, >4-hour events)
- **Sodium**: 0.5-1.0 g/h during prolonged exercise when sweat losses are high
- Individualized hydration plans based on sweat rate testing
- Post-event: 150% of weight loss in fluid with sodium

## GI Issues in Endurance Sport

- 30-50% of endurance athletes experience GI symptoms during events
- Causes: reduced splanchnic blood flow, mechanical jarring, dehydration, concentrated CHO solutions
- **Gut training**: Practicing nutrition strategy during training to improve tolerance
- Low FODMAP approach may help some athletes
- Avoid high-fat, high-fiber, high-protein foods immediately before/during
- Isotonic or hypotonic beverages preferred over hypertonic

## Protein for Endurance

- 1.2-1.8 g/kg/d depending on training load
- During ultraendurance events: 5-10 g protein per hour may reduce muscle damage markers
- Post-training: 20-25 g high-quality protein stimulates MPS
- BCAA oxidation increases during prolonged exercise; adequate total protein intake is more important than BCAA supplementation

## Fat Utilization and Adaptation

- Fat contributes ~50% of energy at 65-75% VO₂max in trained athletes
- Fat adaptation diets (high-fat, low-CHO) increase fat oxidation but do NOT consistently improve performance
- May impair high-intensity capability and carbohydrate utilization
- Ketogenic diets capable of maintaining moderate-intensity capacity but sacrifice high-intensity performance
- Nutritional periodization with selective "train low" sessions may enhance mitochondrial adaptation

## Micronutrient Considerations

- **Iron**: Foot-strike hemolysis, GI bleeding, sweat losses; endurance athletes at high risk for deficiency
- **Calcium/Vitamin D**: Bone health; stress fracture prevention
- **Sodium**: Individualized replacement based on sweat sodium concentration
- **Antioxidants**: Increased oxidative stress; food-first approach preferred

## Ultraendurance-Specific Strategies

- **Pacing nutrition**: Front-load energy intake before fatigue suppresses appetite
- **Variety**: Flavor fatigue with sweet products; include savory options
- **Real food**: Some athletes tolerate real food (boiled potatoes, rice cakes, sandwiches) better than engineered products
- **Caffeine**: 3-6 mg/kg; sustained benefits over ultra-distances; consider timing for late-race
- **Night navigation**: Caffeine and CHO for overnight segments
- **Sleep deprivation**: Adequate energy and hydration support cognitive function

## Environmental Considerations

- **Heat**: Increased sweat rate, higher CHO oxidation, need for acclimatization strategies
- **Cold**: Increased energy expenditure (shivering), risk of hypothermia if underfueled
- **Altitude**: Increased respiratory water loss, higher CHO utilization, iron needs for erythropoiesis

## Key Takeaways

1. CHO needs scale with duration (30-90 g/h); multiple transportable CHO for events >2.5h
2. Individualize hydration based on sweat rate testing
3. Gut training is essential for competition nutrition tolerance
4. GI issues affect 30-50% of athletes; manage with food choices and training
5. Fat adaptation does not consistently improve performance
6. Recovery nutrition timing is critical with multiple training sessions
7. Iron status must be monitored in all endurance athletes
8. Ultraendurance requires innovative fueling beyond engineered products alone
