# Sports Nutrition: A Handbook for Professionals, 6th Edition — Agent Skill

## Overview

This skill provides evidence-based sports nutrition knowledge synthesized from the 6th edition of *Sports Nutrition: A Handbook for Professionals*, the authoritative reference for sports registered dietitian nutritionists. It covers four major sections: Sports Nutrition Basics, Sports Nutrition Assessment and Energy Balance, Principles in Practice, and Sports-Specific Nutrition Guidelines.

## When to Use This Skill

Apply this skill when:
- Assessing or advising on sports nutrition for athletes of any level
- Answering questions about macronutrient requirements for training and competition
- Evaluating supplements and sports foods
- Developing hydration strategies
- Managing body composition for athletic performance
- Addressing medical nutrition therapy needs in athletes (diabetes, eating disorders, pregnancy)
- Designing nutrition plans for specific sports or athlete populations
- Understanding exercise physiology as it relates to nutrition

## Core Principles

### 1. Food-First Approach
Prioritize whole foods to meet nutrition goals. Supplements fill specific gaps when food is impractical or a clinical deficiency exists.

### 2. g/kg Body Weight Dosing
Macronutrient recommendations are expressed in g/kg body weight, not percentages. This accounts for individual body mass and avoids percentage-based errors when energy intakes vary widely.

### 3. Nutrient Timing
Synchronize carbohydrate, protein, and fluid intake with exercise timing (before, during, after) to optimize performance and recovery.

### 4. Nutritional Periodization
Nutrition strategies must match training cycles: preparatory, competition, and transition phases require different approaches. Carbohydrate availability can be strategically manipulated (train high vs. train low).

### 5. Individualization
All recommendations must be tailored to the athlete's sport, training phase, body composition goals, food preferences, culture, budget, gut tolerance, and medical history.

## Quick-Reference Values

### Carbohydrate
- Daily: 3-12 g/kg (varies by training load)
- Loading: 10-12 g/kg/d × 36-48 h rest
- During: 0-90+ g/h (duration-dependent)
- Recovery: 1.0-1.2 g/kg/h (first 4 h)

### Protein
- Endurance: 1.2-1.4 g/kg/d
- Resistance: 1.6-2.0 g/kg/d
- Per meal: ~0.25 g/kg (young), ~0.40 g/kg (masters)
- Post-exercise: 20-25 g high-quality protein

### Hydration
- Pre-exercise: 5-7 mL/kg (4 h before)
- During: 0.4-0.8 L/h (individualize to sweat rate)
- Post-exercise: 1.25-1.5 L per kg BW lost
- Sodium: 0.5-1.0 g/h when sweat loss >4 L or >2 h exercise

### Weight Management
- Fat loss: ~0.7% BW/week; -200 to -500 kcal/d
- Lean gain: 0.25-0.75 lb/week; +300 to +500 kcal/d
- Protein during energy restriction: may need >2.0 g/kg/d

## Critical Safety Notes

1. **Scope of practice**: RDNs should not prescribe exercise or conduct fitness assessments without additional certification. Refer to ACSM/NSCA-certified professionals.
2. **Supplement safety**: Always recommend third-party certified products. Document all supplement use.
3. **Eating disorders**: Sports RDNs screen and refer to specialized ED treatment teams; do not treat alone.
4. **Hyponatremia**: Educate athletes not to overdrink; sodium intake during prolonged exercise; weight gain during exercise is a warning sign.
5. **Energy availability**: Low energy availability underlies RED-S and the Female Athlete Triad; always screen when working with weight-conscious athletes.
6. **Adolescent supplements**: Exercise extreme caution; creatine not recommended for <18 years.
