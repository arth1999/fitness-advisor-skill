# Part C: Environment (Chapters 23-27)

## Chapter 23: Heat

### Heat Physiology
- Body maintains core temperature ~37°C through balance of heat production and heat loss
- Exercise dramatically increases heat production (75-80% of metabolic energy converted to heat)
- Heat loss mechanisms: radiation, convection, conduction, evaporation (sweating)
- Evaporation becomes the dominant mechanism as ambient temperature rises
- Humidity impairs evaporation and significantly increases heat stress

### Heat-Related Illness Spectrum

**Heat Oedema:**
- Mild swelling of hands/feet in hot conditions
- Self-limiting; usually resolves with acclimatisation

**Heat Cramps:**
- Painful muscle spasms during/after prolonged exercise in heat
- Associated with sodium depletion
- Management: rest, cooling, sodium replacement (oral or IV)

**Heat Syncope:**
- Fainting after prolonged standing or sudden standing in heat
- Due to peripheral vasodilation and venous pooling
- Usually benign; manage with supine position + leg elevation + cooling

**Heat Exhaustion:**
- Core temp <40°C
- Symptoms: fatigue, weakness, headache, nausea, dizziness, profuse sweating, tachycardia, hypotension
- Mentation: intact (key differentiator from heat stroke)
- Management: stop exercise, move to cool environment, remove excess clothing, cool with fans/ice packs, oral or IV fluids

**Exertional Heat Stroke (MEDICAL EMERGENCY):**
- Core temp >40°C + NEUROCOGNITIVE CHANGES (agitation, confusion, delirium, coma)
- Multi-organ failure can develop rapidly
- Leading cause of death in sporting events
- IMMEDIATE aggressive cooling (ice water immersion preferred; if unavailable, rotating ice packs to neck/axillae/groin + fan + water spray)
- "Cool first, transport second"
- Target: reduce core temp to <39°C within 30 minutes
- Complications: rhabdomyolysis, DIC, acute renal failure, liver failure, cerebral oedema

### Risk Factors for Heat Illness
- High ambient temperature and humidity
- Dehydration
- Lack of heat acclimatisation
- High exercise intensity
- Obesity (increased insulation, higher metabolic heat production)
- Medications: diuretics, anticholinergics, beta blockers, stimulants
- Recent illness (fever)
- Previous history of heat illness
- Sunburn

### Heat Acclimatisation
- Requires 7-14 days of gradual heat exposure + exercise
- Adaptations: increased sweat rate, earlier onset of sweating, reduced sweat sodium concentration, expanded plasma volume, reduced heart rate at given workload, reduced core temperature
- Training in heat for limited periods (30-100 min/day) sufficient to induce adaptations
- Acclimatisation decays within ~2-3 weeks of no heat exposure

### Prevention Strategies
- Acclimatise before competition in hot conditions
- Hydrate appropriately (but avoid over-hydration — hyponatraemia risk)
- Monitor urine colour (pale straw = hydrated)
- Wear appropriate clothing (lightweight, light-coloured, breathable)
- Schedule events outside peak heat hours
- Wet bulb globe temperature (WBGT) monitoring for event modification/cancellation
- Know the warning signs
- Emergency action plan including cooling capacity at all hot-weather events

## Chapter 24: Cold

### Cold Physiology
- Body defends core temperature through: vasoconstriction (reduce heat loss), shivering (increase heat production), behavioural responses
- Wind chill dramatically increases heat loss
- Water immersion: heat loss ~25x faster than air at same temperature

### Cold-Related Injuries

**Hypothermia:**
- Core temp <35°C
- Mild (32-35°C): shivering, tachycardia, tachypnoea, confusion
- Moderate (28-32°C): loss of shivering, bradycardia, decreased consciousness
- Severe (<28°C): coma, ventricular fibrillation risk, apparent death
- Management: remove wet clothing, insulate, warm sweet drinks (if conscious), active external rewarming, avoid rough handling (VF risk)

**Frostnip:**
- Superficial freezing; numb, pale skin; no permanent damage
- Rewarm with skin-to-skin contact or warm water

**Frostbite:**
- Tissue freezing with ice crystal formation
- Affects extremities: fingers, toes, ears, nose, cheeks
- Appearance: waxy, pale, hard, insensitive
- Management: rapid rewarming in 37-39°C water (NOT hot); do not rub; protect from refreezing; surgical debridement may be needed

**Cold-Induced Asthma/Bronchospasm:**
- Triggered by cold, dry air
- Prevention: face mask/scarf to warm and humidify inspired air; pre-exercise bronchodilator

### Prevention
- Layered clothing (wicking base layer + insulating mid layer + wind/waterproof outer)
- Protect extremities (gloves, hat, appropriate footwear)
- Stay dry
- Adequate nutrition (fuel for heat production)
- Buddy system in remote/cold environments

## Chapter 25: Altitude

### Altitude Physiology
- Partial pressure of oxygen decreases with increasing altitude
- Hypobaric hypoxia triggers physiological responses
- Acute responses: increased ventilation (hypoxic ventilatory response), increased heart rate and cardiac output, pulmonary vasoconstriction
- Acclimatisation: increased red blood cell mass (erythropoietin), increased capillary density, increased mitochondrial density, rightward shift of oxyhaemoglobin dissociation curve, increased renal bicarbonate excretion

### Altitude Illness Spectrum

**Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS):**
- Headache + at least one of: nausea/vomiting, fatigue, dizziness, sleep disturbance
- Onset: 6-12 hours after ascent
- Typically self-limiting if altitude maintained; resolves with descent
- Prevention: gradual ascent, acetazolamide (125-250 mg BID starting day before ascent)

**High-Altitude Cerebral Oedema (HACE):**
- Severe AMS progressing to ataxia, confusion, altered consciousness, coma
- LIFE-THREATENING
- Immediate descent mandatory + oxygen + dexamethasone

**High-Altitude Pulmonary Oedema (HAPE):**
- Non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema
- Symptoms: dyspnoea at rest, cough (dry then productive/pink-frothy), chest tightness, reduced exercise tolerance >> normal AMS
- Onset: typically second night at altitude
- LIFE-THREATENING
- Immediate descent + oxygen + nifedipine

### Exercise at Altitude
- VO2max decreases ~1% per 100m above 1500m
- Endurance performance significantly impaired
- Sprint/power performance less affected (lower air resistance)
- Altitude training: "live high, train low" for erythropoietic benefit without compromising training intensity
- Acclimatisation: allowance of 3-5 days for moderate altitude; 1-2+ weeks for high altitude competition

## Chapter 26: Underwater

### Diving Physiology
- Increased ambient pressure underwater (1 atm per 10m depth)
- Boyle's law: gas volume inversely proportional to pressure
- Henry's law: gas dissolved in liquid proportional to partial pressure
- Nitrogen narcosis: at depth (>30m), nitrogen dissolved in tissues causes anaesthetic effect
- Decompression illness: rapid ascent causes dissolved nitrogen to form bubbles in tissues/blood

### Medical Conditions Affecting Diving Fitness
- Respiratory: asthma (controversial; well-controlled may dive; exercise-induced or cold-induced = high risk), pneumothorax (absolute contraindication), bullous lung disease
- Cardiac: PFO (increased DCI risk from paradoxical gas embolism), coronary artery disease, arrhythmias
- Neurological: epilepsy (absolute contraindication if uncontrolled; risk of drowning during seizure)
- ENT: inability to equalise middle ear/sinus pressure
- Diabetes: risk of hypoglycaemia underwater (hypoglycaemic unawareness amplified by nitrogen narcosis)
- Pregnancy: generally advised against diving

### Fitness to Dive Assessment
- Comprehensive medical evaluation including spirometry
- Exercise ECG for older divers or those with risk factors
- Assessment of ability to manage equipment and emergencies
- Annual review for active divers

## Chapter 27: Physical Activity and the Built Environment

### Key Concepts
- The built environment directly influences population physical activity levels
- Key elements: land use mix, street connectivity, residential density, access to public transport, availability of parks/green spaces, cycling and walking infrastructure, perceived safety

### Environmental Correlates of Physical Activity
- Walkability: mixed land use + connected streets + residential density -> more walking for transport
- Parks and open spaces: proximity and quality associated with higher recreational physical activity
- Cycling infrastructure: separated bike lanes and bike parking increase cycling rates
- Aesthetics and safety: perceived neighbourhood attractiveness and safety associated with more walking
- Stair use: building design influences stair vs elevator use (visible, attractive stairs increase use)

### Clinical Implications
- Acknowledge that individual behaviour change occurs within an environmental context
- Advocate for built environment changes that support active living
- Prescribe activities that fit the patient's environment
- Connect patients with local facilities and programs
- Consider environmental barriers when patients report difficulty maintaining activity

### The "7 Best Investments" (ISPAH) — Environmental Components
1. Built environment and transport policies that prioritise active travel
2. Community-wide programs involving multiple settings
3. School-based programs
4. Sports systems and programs promoting "sport for all"
5. Public education including mass media
6. Healthcare: routine physical activity assessment and counselling
7. Advocacy and leadership from all sectors
