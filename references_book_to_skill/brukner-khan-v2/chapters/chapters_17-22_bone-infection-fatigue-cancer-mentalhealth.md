# Part B: Managing Medical Problems — Bone Health, Infections, Fatigue, Cancer, Mental Health (Chapters 17-22)

## Chapter 17: Osteoporosis — Physical Activity for Bone Health

### Exercise and Bone Health Principles
- Bone responds to mechanical loading (Wolff's law)
- Weight-bearing exercise and resistance training are most effective for bone stimulation
- Site-specific effect: loading must target the skeletal sites at risk
- Peak bone mass achieved by ~30 years; childhood/adolescence is critical window
- Exercise throughout lifespan: build peak bone mass in youth; maintain in adulthood; slow loss in older age

### Exercise Prescription for Bone Health
| Parameter | Prescription |
|-----------|-------------|
| Type | Weight-bearing impact activities (jumping, hopping, running, dancing) + progressive resistance training |
| Intensity | Moderate to high (as tolerated; higher intensity = greater osteogenic stimulus) |
| Frequency | Weight-bearing: most days; Resistance: 2-3x/week |
| Duration | 30-60 min/session |
| Progression | Progressive overload of resistance; variety of impact loading directions |

### Special Considerations
- Vertebral osteoporosis: avoid forward flexion exercises + rotation (vertebral fracture risk)
- Balance training: essential for falls prevention in older adults with osteoporosis
- Postural exercises: reduce kyphosis progression
- Swimming/cycling: good for general health but NOT osteogenic (non-weight-bearing)
- Combine exercise with adequate calcium and vitamin D

## Chapter 18: Infections

### Exercise and Immune Function
- Moderate exercise: enhances immune function (J-curve relationship)
- Prolonged intense exercise: transient immune suppression ("open window" 3-72 hours post-exercise)
- Upper respiratory tract infection risk increases with high training loads

### Key Infectious Considerations in Athletes
- Fever: absolute contraindication to exercise (risk of myocarditis, heat illness)
- Myocarditis: can follow viral illness; presents with fatigue, dyspnoea, chest pain; requires complete rest and cardiac evaluation
- Infectious mononucleosis (glandular fever): splenomegaly risk; avoid contact sports for 3-4 weeks minimum; ultrasound-guided return to play
- Skin infections: common in contact sports (herpes gladiatorum, impetigo, tinea); exclusion from competition until treated
- Blood-borne viruses (HIV, Hepatitis B/C): universal precautions; no exclusion from sport based on status alone
- Immunisation: ensure athletes are up to date (tetanus, hepatitis B, influenza, etc.)

### Return to Activity After Infection
- "Neck check": symptoms above neck (runny nose, sore throat) — may exercise at reduced intensity
- Symptoms below neck (chest congestion, fever, myalgia) — rest until resolved
- Gradual return to full training after significant illness
- Red flags: persistent fatigue, exertional dyspnoea, chest pain — rule out myocarditis

## Chapter 19: The Tired Athlete

### Differential Diagnosis of Fatigue in Athletes

**Training-Related:**
- Overtraining syndrome / unexplained underperformance syndrome (UUPS)
- Inadequate recovery
- Periodisation issues

**Medical Causes:**
- Anaemia (iron deficiency most common in athletes)
- Viral/post-viral fatigue (EBV, CMV)
- Thyroid dysfunction
- Diabetes mellitus
- Coeliac disease
- Chronic fatigue syndrome
- Postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS)

**Nutritional:**
- Inadequate energy intake (RED-S — Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)
- Iron deficiency
- Vitamin D deficiency
- Inadequate carbohydrate intake for training load

**Psychological:**
- Depression
- Anxiety
- Eating disorders
- Stress/life factors

### Clinical Approach
- Thorough history: training load, nutrition, sleep, mood, life stressors, medical history
- Physical examination: pallor, thyroid, lymphadenopathy, cardiac
- Investigations: FBC, ferritin, iron studies, TSH, glucose, coeliac serology, vitamin D, CK, inflammatory markers
- Training diary analysis
- Consider RED-S screening in at-risk athletes (endurance, aesthetic, weight-class sports)

### Overtraining Syndrome
- Prolonged maladaptation to training stress
- Key features: persistent fatigue, performance decrement, mood disturbance
- Diagnosis of exclusion
- Management: rest, nutritional support, psychological support, gradual return

## Chapter 20: Cancer

### Physical Activity and Cancer

**Primary Prevention:**
- Strong evidence for reduced risk of colon, breast, and endometrial cancers with regular physical activity
- Mechanisms: reduced adiposity, improved insulin sensitivity, reduced inflammation, enhanced immune function, reduced GI transit time

**During Treatment:**
- Exercise is safe and beneficial during most cancer treatments
- Benefits: reduced cancer-related fatigue, improved aerobic fitness, strength, body composition, quality of life
- Adjust to treatment cycles: modify intensity during low WCC, low platelets, anaemia, photosensitivity

**After Treatment (Survivorship):**
- Reduced risk of cancer recurrence
- Reduced risk of second primary cancers
- Improved cardiovascular and metabolic health (cancer treatments increase CVD risk)
- Improved bone health (chemotherapy/radiation/hormonal therapy reduce bone density)

### Exercise Prescription for Cancer
| Parameter | During Treatment | Survivorship |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------|
| Aerobic | Moderate (Borg 11-14), 5-7 days/week, 20-30 min | 150 min/week moderate or 75 min/week vigorous |
| Resistance | 1-3 sessions/week, 1-4 sets, 6-12 reps to fatigue | 2-3x/week, progressive |
| Modifications | Split into shorter sessions; monitor blood counts | Individualise to treatment late effects |
| Contraindications | Extremely low WCC (infection risk), low platelets (bleeding), severe anaemia | Bone metastases (avoid high impact at affected sites) |

### Childhood Cancer
- Acute lymphocytic leukaemia (ALL): 30-40% of childhood cancer; >80% survival
- >95% of survivors develop at least one long-term complication
- Short-term exercise benefits: reduced fatigue, improved tolerance to chemotherapy
- Long-term exercise: reduces metabolic disease, obesity, osteoporosis, recurrence, second cancers
- Start exercise during inpatient period where possible

## Chapter 21: Physical Activity in the Prevention and Treatment of Depression

### Evidence for Exercise in Depression
- Exercise is effective for both prevention and treatment of depression
- Effect size comparable to pharmacotherapy and psychotherapy for mild-moderate depression
- Mechanisms: endorphin release, monoamine modulation, neuroplasticity (BDNF), reduced inflammation, improved self-efficacy, social interaction

### Exercise Prescription for Depression
| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Type | Aerobic (walking, running, cycling, swimming); resistance training as adjunct |
| Intensity | Moderate to vigorous |
| Frequency | 3-5x/week minimum |
| Duration | 30-60 min/session |
| Accumulated minimum | 150 min/week moderate |

### Clinical Pearls
- Group exercise provides additional benefit through social interaction
- Outdoor/"green" exercise may provide additional mental health benefits
- Start low, go slow — the depressed patient has low motivation and energy
- Focus on enjoyment and accomplishment, not performance
- Combine with evidence-based psychological treatment and medication as indicated

## Chapter 22: Anxiety Disorders

### Exercise and Anxiety
- Both acute (single session) and chronic (regular training) exercise reduce anxiety
- Aerobic exercise most studied; resistance training also effective
- Mechanisms: endorphin/endocannabinoid system, reduced sympathetic nervous system reactivity, distraction, mastery/self-efficacy

### Exercise Prescription for Anxiety
| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Type | Aerobic preferred; mind-body (yoga, tai chi) also beneficial |
| Intensity | Moderate; can start lower if vigorous causes panic-like sensations |
| Frequency | 3-5x/week |
| Duration | 20-60 min/session |

### Special Considerations
- Panic disorder: some patients may misinterpret exercise-induced physiological arousal (increased HR, breathing) as panic; educate about normal exercise physiology
- Gradual exposure to exercise sensations can be therapeutic
- Mind-body activities (yoga, tai chi) particularly effective for anxiety
- Breathing exercises as adjunct
