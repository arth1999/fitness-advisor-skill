# Part B: Managing Medical Problems — Cardiovascular (Chapters 9-10)

## Chapter 9: Sudden Cardiac Death in Sport

### Incidence
- Best estimate: 1:40,000 to 1:80,000 young competitive athletes/year
- Males 5:1 to 9:1 higher than females
- African-American athletes: 3.2x greater risk than white counterparts (NCAA)
- Risk increases with age; competitive athletes 2.5-3.6x relative risk vs non-athletes
- Highest risk sports: basketball (1:15,000 overall; 1:4,000 male African-American NCAA), men's soccer (1:24,000), men's American football (1:36,000)

### Aetiology by Age
**Young athletes (<35 years):** Primarily inherited/congenital structural and electrical abnormalities
- Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM): most common in USA
- Arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy (AC): most common in Italy
- Congenital coronary artery anomalies (CCAA)
- Ion channelopathies: LQTS, Brugada syndrome, CPVT
- Sudden arrhythmic death syndrome (SADS): up to 40% of cases — structurally normal heart

**Masters athletes (>50 years):** Overwhelmingly coronary artery disease (>80%)
- Stress-related plaque rupture in exercise-mediated prothrombotic environment
- ~50% have prodromal symptoms
- Pathological findings: severe multi-vessel atherosclerosis, myocardial scarring

**30-50 years:** Overlap between inherited and acquired causes — consider both

### Key Structural Causes

#### Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)
- Prevalence: 0.2% general population; 0.06-0.07% in athletes ("healthy cohort" effect)
- Autosomal dominant; sarcomeric protein gene mutations
- Characterised by LVH without systemic cause + myocardial disarray
- Diagnosis: Echo/CMR showing wall thickness >=15mm (M), >=13mm (F); 90%+ have abnormal ECG
- Risk markers: unheralded syncope, prior SCA, family history SCD <40yr, severe LVH >30mm, NSVT, attenuated BP response to exercise, LGE on CMR
- Management: disqualification from competitive sports except class 1A (low intensity)
- ICD not a licence to continue competing

#### Arrhythmogenic Cardiomyopathy (AC)
- Commonest cause of SCD in Italian athletes (13% of cases)
- Familial, autosomal dominant; desmosomal protein gene mutations
- Progressive fibro-fatty replacement of myocardium
- Left ventricular involvement in >70% at autopsy
- ECG: T-wave inversion V1-V3, prolonged QRS, epsilon waves, ventricular extrasystoles (LBBB morphology)
- Management: cease competitive sports except 1A; endurance exercise accelerates phenotype; ICD for high-risk

#### Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM)
- Rare cause of SCD (~4% of cases)
- LV dilatation + impaired systolic function
- Genetic or acquired (infection, inflammation, toxins)
- Management: disqualification except 1A; gentle exercise (30 min/day moderate) may offer prognostic benefit
- Pharmacotherapy: ACEi, beta blockers, +/- aldosterone antagonists

#### Congenital Coronary Artery Anomalies (CCAA)
- 0.5-1% of individuals; most common: RCA from left sinus
- Most deaths: anomalous left coronary artery origin
- Mechanism: mechanical compression between aorta and pulmonary artery during exercise
- ECG usually normal; stress test often does not induce ischaemia
- Diagnosis: CT coronary angiography or MR angiography
- Post-surgical correction: return to sport after 3 months if no infarction/LV dysfunction/VT

#### Marfan Syndrome
- Fibrillin gene mutation; autosomal dominant; prevalence 1:5000
- Triad: skeletal + cardiac (aortic dilatation/dissection) + ocular abnormalities
- Contraindicated: high-static sports (powerlifting, wrestling, judo, sprinting, rowing)
- Beta blockers and ARBs (losartan) for aortic protection
- Annual aortic surveillance; prophylactic root replacement at >50mm

### Key Electrical Causes (SADS)

#### Congenital Long QT Syndrome (LQTS)
- Ion channel mutations causing prolonged repolarisation
- LQT1: triggered by exercise/swimming
- LQT2: triggered by emotional stress/loud noises
- LQT3: triggered by rest/sleep
- Syncope as presenting symptom; if undiagnosed: 20% SCD in first year, 50% by 5 years

#### Catecholaminergic Polymorphic Ventricular Tachycardia (CPVT)
- Triggered by emotional stress and exercise
- Syncope commonly misdiagnosed as epilepsy or vasovagal events
- Often delayed diagnosis

#### Brugada Syndrome
- Incomplete RBBB with coved ST elevation V1-V3
- May be triggered by rest/sleep

#### Wolff-Parkinson-White Syndrome
- Accessory electrical pathway; delta wave on ECG
- Risk of rapid conduction during atrial fibrillation -> ventricular fibrillation

### Pre-Participation Cardiovascular Screening
- History: exertional chest pain, syncope, palpitations, excessive dyspnoea/fatigue, family history
- Physical exam: murmurs (dynamic LVOT obstruction), Marfan stigmata, BP (both arms), femoral pulses
- ECG: recommended by European Society of Cardiology; debated in USA
- Abnormal findings -> echocardiography -> CMR/CT/EP studies as indicated

### Secondary Prevention
- Widespread AED availability + emergency action plans
- Survival rates from SCA significantly improved with AED programs
- Primary prevention: identify at-risk individuals before catastrophic event

## Chapter 10: Cardiovascular Symptoms

### Five Key Symptoms to Evaluate

#### 1. Syncope and Exercise-Related Collapse

**Neurally Mediated (Vasovagal) Syncope:**
- Most common cause in young athletes
- More common in females; peaks in adolescence; resolves by age 30-40
- Triggers: fear, pain, excitement, prolonged standing, venepuncture
- Premonitory symptoms: lightheadedness, dizziness, flushing, nausea, tunnel vision, sweating
- Pale and clammy appearance
- Occurs while STANDING; unusual while sitting; rare while lying
- Brief loss of consciousness; prolonged post-event fatigue/nausea
- Diagnosis: clinical history; normal ECG = no referral needed

**Post-Exertional Syncope:**
- Occurs after exercise cessation (standing in huddle, at finish line)
- Usually neurally mediated (benign)
- Mechanism: muscle pump ceases, reduced venous return, forceful ventricular contraction against diminished volume -> reflex vasodilation + bradycardia

**Exertional Syncope (RED FLAG):**
- Occurs DURING exercise
- Can be from outflow obstruction (HCM, aortic stenosis) or arrhythmias
- Outflow obstruction: brief lightheadedness -> syncope if exercise continues
- Arrhythmic: abrupt loss of consciousness OR very brief prodrome; secondary injury common
- Prolonged exertional syncope = treat as cardiac arrest
- Requires comprehensive cardiac evaluation: ECG + Echo + stress ECG + CMR/CT + prolonged monitoring
- Refer to cardiologist even if preliminary work-up normal

#### 2. Syncope Mimics — Seizures and Collapse

**Seizure vs Syncope:**
- Syncope: faint first -> then myoclonic movements (seconds later)
- Seizure: myoclonic movements concurrent with loss of consciousness
- Seizure: aura, postictal confusion
- Syncope: tunnel vision, profound fatigue after
- Any unexplained seizure -> cardiovascular work-up (ECG minimum + Echo)

**Exertional Collapse (not syncope):**
- Heat stroke: core temp >40°C + neurocognitive changes (agitation, delirium -> multi-organ failure)
- Hyponatraemia: excessive water intake during endurance events -> fatigue, drowsiness, obtundation

#### 3. Exertional Chest Pain
- RED FLAG: chest pain severe enough to make athlete STOP exercising
- Young (<35): rule out CCAA, HCM — ECG + Echo + consider CMR
- Older (>35): stress test for ischaemic heart disease
- Pleuritic pain (worse with inspiration): pericarditis, musculoskeletal, pulmonary (PE, pneumonia)
- CAD in younger athletes: consider familial hypercholesterolaemia (look for xanthelasma, corneal arcus, xanthomata)

#### 4. Palpitations
- Common in young individuals; often benign (sinus tachycardia, non-cardiac)
- BENIGN: barely noticeable, no associated symptoms -> reassure
- CONCERNING: sudden onset OR associated lightheadedness/chest pain/dyspnoea -> cardiac evaluation
- Diagnosis: capture ECG during symptoms (ambulatory monitoring, stress test)
- SVT: not associated with SCD but usually needs treatment (Valsalva, beta blockers, CCBs, ablation)
- VT: concerning; associated with syncope, severe lightheadedness, SCD; refer cardiology

#### 5. Excessive Fatigue/Dyspnoea on Exertion
- Compare to individual's previous level OR performance-matched peers
- Recent onset or worsening = concerning
- Cardiac causes: HCM, aortic stenosis, DCM, congenital/acquired heart block, myocarditis (post-viral)
- Evaluation: ECG + Echo + labs (anaemia, thyroid) +/- stress ECG, CPET, PFT

### Physical Examination in Sports Cardiology
- Most cardiac diseases predisposing to SCD have NORMAL physical examination
- Exceptions: systolic ejection murmur of LVOT obstruction (HCM — only ~25% at rest, ~50% with exertion)
- Hypertension: found in ~6% of athletes at screening
- Marfan stigmata: high-arched palate, arachnodactyly, hyperlaxity, tall stature, long arm span
- BP: measure both arms + femoral pulses (coarctation screening)

### Hypertension Classification (2017 ACC/AHA)
| Category | SBP (mmHg) | DBP (mmHg) |
|----------|------------|------------|
| Normal | <120 | <80 |
| Elevated | 120-129 | <80 |
| Stage 1 | 130-139 | 80-89 |
| Stage 2 | >=140 | >=90 |
| Crisis | >180 | >120 |

### Hypertension Management in Athletes
- Evaluation: blood chemistries, haematocrit, urinalysis, ECG, renal US (paediatric)
- Target organ assessment: echocardiogram for all stage 1-2
- End-organ damage -> no competitive sport until BP controlled
- First-line medications: ACE inhibitors or ARBs (no decrease in aerobic capacity)
- Second-line: dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers
- AVOID: beta blockers and diuretics (reduce max exercise capacity; may be banned in sport)

### Heart Murmur Evaluation
- Auscultation: supine AND standing (with Valsalva)
- HCM murmur: early systolic, harsh, right upper sternal border, INCREASES with standing/Valsalva (decreased venous return)
- Aortic stenosis murmur: DIMINISHES with standing/Valsalva; increases with squatting
- Standing preferred to sitting for detecting dynamic LVOT obstruction

### When to Refer to Cardiology
- Exertional syncope (even if preliminary work-up normal)
- Exertional chest pain that makes athlete stop
- Palpitations with SVT/VT on ambulatory monitoring
- Abnormal ECG or echocardiogram
- Family history of SCD <40 years + symptoms
- Post-exertional syncope with classic prodrome + normal ECG = NO referral needed
- Benign palpitations with normal ambulatory monitoring = NO referral needed
