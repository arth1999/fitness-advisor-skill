# Part F: Practical Sports Medicine (Chapters 38-40)

## Chapter 38: Emergencies

### Emergency Preparedness
- Every sporting event and venue should have an emergency action plan (EAP)
- Components: personnel roles, communication, equipment, transport, receiving facility
- EAP should be rehearsed regularly
- Document and review all incidents

### Key Sporting Emergencies

#### Sudden Cardiac Arrest (SCA)
- IMMEDIATE recognition: unresponsive + abnormal breathing or no breathing
- Call for help + AED immediately
- High-quality CPR: 100-120 compressions/min, 5-6 cm depth, full recoil, minimise interruptions
- AED: apply as soon as available; follow prompts
- Continue CPR until advanced life support arrives or athlete shows signs of life
- Survival decreases ~10% per minute without CPR+AED
- AED programs at sporting venues save lives

#### Exertional Heat Stroke
- Core temp >40°C + neurocognitive changes
- IMMEDIATE aggressive cooling: cold water immersion (preferred); ice packs to neck/axillae/groin + fan + water spray if immersion not available
- "Cool first, transport second"
- Target: reduce core temp to <39°C within 30 minutes
- Rectal temperature monitoring (most accurate in heat stroke)

#### Exercise-Associated Collapse
- Common at endurance event finish lines
- Usually benign post-exertional syncope/neurally mediated
- Manage: supine with leg elevation; oral fluids if conscious and not hyperthermic
- BUT: must distinguish from heat stroke (check rectal temp) and cardiac arrest

#### Exertional Hyponatraemia
- Serum sodium <135 mmol/L
- Usually from excessive fluid intake relative to losses
- Risk factors: slow pace, >4 hours exercise, female, low body weight, excessive fluid intake, NSAID use
- Mild: fatigue, nausea, headache -> fluid restrict +/- oral hypertonic saline
- Severe: confusion, seizures, coma -> IV hypertonic saline (3% NaCl); LIFE-THREATENING
- Prevention: drink to thirst, not beyond

#### Anaphylaxis
- Recognise: rapid onset, urticaria, angioedema, respiratory distress, hypotension
- IM adrenaline (epinephrine) 0.5 mg (0.5 mL of 1:1000) anterolateral thigh — FIRST LINE
- Call emergency services
- Supine with leg elevation if hypotensive
- Oxygen, IV fluids, antihistamines, corticosteroids as adjuncts

#### Spinal Cord Injury
- Suspect in any athlete with neck/back pain, neurological symptoms, or mechanism of injury
- Manual in-line stabilisation of head and neck
- Do NOT remove helmet or shoulder pads unless trained and necessary for airway
- Log-roll technique for supine positioning
- Spine board for transport
- Maintain spinal precautions until cleared

#### Head Injury / Concussion
- Any suspected concussion: REMOVE from play, do not return same day
- Red flags requiring emergency transport: deteriorating consciousness, severe/increasing headache, repeated vomiting, seizure, focal neurology, restlessness/agitation
- SCAT5/Child SCAT5 for assessment (licensed healthcare professionals only)
- CRT5 (Concussion Recognition Tool 5) for non-medical personnel

#### Acute Asthma Exacerbation
- Severe: can't complete sentences in one breath, respiratory rate >30, pulse >120, SpO2 <90%
- Life-threatening: silent chest, cyanosis, exhaustion, confusion
- Management: inhaled beta-2 agonist (salbutamol) via spacer; oxygen if available; call emergency services if severe/life-threatening

#### Hypoglycaemia (Diabetic Athlete)
- Recognition: confusion, sweating, tremor, pallor, altered behaviour
- Conscious: 15-20g fast-acting CHO (glucose tablets, fruit juice, sugar); recheck BG in 15 min
- Unconscious: glucagon injection IM/SC (team staff should be trained); call emergency services; do NOT give oral fluids/food
- Always have glucose and glucagon available for diabetic athletes

### Emergency Equipment for Sporting Events
- AED
- Basic airway equipment
- Bag-valve-mask
- Oxygen
- Immobilisation equipment (cervical collar, spine board)
- Splints
- First aid supplies
- Cold water immersion tub or ice supply (heat illness)
- Rectal thermometer (heat illness)
- Blood glucose monitor + glucose + glucagon (if diabetic participants)
- Epinephrine auto-injector

## Chapter 39: Medical Coverage of Endurance Events

### Pre-Event Planning
**Medical Team Structure:**
- Medical director: overall responsibility
- Medical staff: physicians, physiotherapists, nurses, paramedics, sports trainers, podiatrists, massage therapists
- Volunteers: first aiders, communications
- Clear chain of command

**Risk Assessment:**
- Event type, distance, duration
- Environmental conditions (heat, cold, altitude)
- Participant numbers and demographics (elite vs recreational)
- Course profile and hazards
- Historical injury/illness data from previous events

**Medical Facilities:**
- Main medical tent at finish area
- Satellite medical stations along course
- Mobile medical teams (bikes, vehicles)
- Communication systems between all points
- Ambulance access and evacuation routes
- Receiving hospital coordination

**Pre-Event Communication:**
- Medical advice to participants (training, hydration, nutrition, heat acclimatisation)
- Warning signs for participants
- Medical conditions declaration on entry
- Medication and allergy information

### On-the-Day Medical Management
| Condition | Typical Location | Key Management |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Exercise-associated collapse | Finish line | Supine + leg elevation; check rectal temp to rule out heat stroke |
| Heat exhaustion | Course/finish | Cooling, fluids, rest |
| Heat stroke | Anywhere | Immediate aggressive cooling; transport |
| Hyponatraemia | Course/finish (slower participants) | Check sodium; fluid restrict (mild); hypertonic saline (severe) |
| Musculoskeletal injury | Course | First aid; transport; refer if needed |
| Hypoglycaemia | Course | Glucose; glucagon if unconscious |
| Cardiac arrest | Anywhere | CPR + AED per EAP |

### Post-Event
- Debrief: review incidents, medical encounters, system performance
- Medical record documentation
- Participant follow-up as needed
- Report for event organisers
- Update EAP based on learnings

## Chapter 40: Multisport Endurance Events

### Triathlon, Ironman, Ultramarathon, Adventure Racing

### Unique Medical Challenges
- Multiple disciplines in one event (swim, cycle, run)
- Prolonged duration (hours to days)
- Variable environmental conditions
- Cumulative physiological stress
- Nutrition and hydration over extended periods
- Sleep deprivation (ultra-endurance events)
- Equipment-related issues (wetsuits, bikes, footwear)

### Discipline-Specific Considerations

**Swimming:**
- Open water: cold water (hypothermia), poor visibility, currents
- Mass start: traumatic injuries, near-drowning
- Cardiac events: more common in swim leg (cold water immersion + exercise)
- Wetsuit issues: fit, overheating if warm water

**Cycling:**
- High-speed crashes: trauma (head, spine, fractures, abrasions)
- Prolonged stationary position: neck/back pain, ulnar neuropathy
- Hydration and nutrition on the bike
- Drafting legality and safety

**Running:**
- Most medical encounters occur in run leg
- Heat illness risk highest
- Exercise-associated collapse at finish
- Musculoskeletal injuries (blisters, cramps, strains)
- Hyponatraemia risk in slower marathon/ultra runners

### Medical Coverage Specifics for Multisport Events
- Extended medical coverage (up to 17 hours for Ironman)
- Transition area medical support
- Nutrition/hydration station medical surveillance
- Drop-out management and transport
- Support crew medical needs
- Multi-day events: cumulative issues (blister care, chafing, sleep management, psychological support)

### Common Medical Presentations by Phase
| Phase | Common Presentations |
|-------|---------------------|
| Pre-race | Anxiety, pre-existing injury concerns, medication queries |
| Swim | Hypothermia, near-drowning, cardiac events, trauma (mass start) |
| T1 | Trauma (wetsuit removal), hypothermia/hyperthermia |
| Bike | Trauma (crashes), overuse (neck, back, hands), dehydration |
| T2 | Cramps, postural hypotension |
| Run | Heat illness, hyponatraemia, GI symptoms, musculoskeletal, collapse |
| Post-finish | Exercise-associated collapse, heat exhaustion, hyponatraemia, blisters |
| Post-race (days) | DOMS, upper respiratory infections, follow-up of race-day issues |

### Medical Team Logistics
- Extended shifts (12-24+ hours) — staff rotation and rest
- Medical supply planning for full event duration
- Communication systems across long courses
- Multiple receiving hospital arrangements
- Family/spectator medical needs
