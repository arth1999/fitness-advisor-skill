# Part B: Managing Medical Problems — Obesity & Diabetes (Chapters 7-8)

## Chapter 7: Obesity

### Definition and Measurement
**BMI Categories:**
| Category | BMI (kg/m^2) |
|----------|-------------|
| Underweight | <18.5 |
| Normal | 18.5-24.9 |
| Overweight | 25.0-29.9 |
| Obesity I | 30.0-34.9 |
| Obesity II | 35.0-39.9 |
| Extreme Obesity III | >40.0 |

**Limitations of BMI:** Does not account for muscle mass. Less accurate in non-Caucasian populations.

**More Accurate Measures:**
- DXA scan (gold standard, most expensive)
- Bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA)
- Skinfold callipers (good for tracking change, same operator)
- Waist circumference (proxy for visceral fat)
- Waist-to-hip ratio (WHR): excess abdominal fat if >0.8 (F), >0.9 (M)
- Waist-to-height ratio: >0.53 (M), >0.54 (F) predicts obesity; >0.59 predicts abdominal obesity; more accurate than BMI

### Waist Circumference Thresholds
| Gender | Increased Disease Risk | High Disease Risk |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Female | >=80 cm | >=88 cm |
| Male | >=94 cm | >=102 cm |

### Visceral vs Subcutaneous Fat
- Subcutaneous: relatively harmless, found under skin on limbs/buttocks
- Visceral: dangerous, wrapped around organs (liver, heart, bowel)
- Visceral fat produces pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6) and less adiponectin
- Strongly associated with type 2 diabetes and coronary artery disease
- Rate of accumulation varies by sex and ethnicity

### Extent of the Problem
- >1.9 billion adults overweight (2016); >650 million obese
- 39% of adults overweight, 13% obese
- 41 million children <5 years overweight/obese
- 340 million children/adolescents 5-19 overweight/obese
- Childhood obesity risen from 4% (1975) to >18% (2016)
- Obesity nearly tripled since 1975
- Global economic impact: ~US$2 trillion/year (2.8% of global GDP)
- ~5% of all deaths worldwide

### Causes of Obesity (Multifactorial)
1. **Genetic factors:** Heritability estimates 40-70% but overestimated; narrow environmental variance in twin studies
2. **Socioeconomic disadvantage:** Strong relationship. Low SES = more processed food, less physical activity, fewer safe outdoor spaces
3. **Ethnicity:** Varies by country. US: highest in non-Hispanic black women (48.9%)
4. **Energy balance theory flaws:** Caloric restriction leads to increased appetite + reduced BMR. Weight regain common.
5. **Dietary Guidelines (1977+):** Low-fat recommendations led to increased sugar, ultra-processed foods, vegetable oils
6. **Sugar-sweetened beverages:** Strongest food-obesity association. Each additional serving associated with increased BMI and obesity in children and adults
7. **Insulin resistance:** Both cause and effect of obesity. Hyperinsulinaemia promotes fat storage.
8. **Ultra-processed foods:** >50% of US caloric intake. Contain multiple manufactured ingredients.
9. **Medical causes:** Hypothyroidism, PCOS, Cushing's syndrome
10. **Medications:** Antidepressants, anticonvulsants, some diabetes medications, corticosteroids, some antihypertensives
11. **Other:** Stress, poor sleep (20-30% higher caloric intake with sleep restriction), menopause, smoking cessation

### The Obesogenic Environment
- Food system drivers: increased supply of cheap, palatable, energy-dense foods; improved distribution; pervasive marketing
- Neighbourhood food environment: supermarket availability negatively associated with obesity; fast-food availability positively associated
- Built environment: mixed land use and urban sprawl most consistently associated
- Distal drivers: taxation regimes, marketplace regulation, social and economic policies

### Clinical Approach
- Obesity Disease (with health problems) vs Obesity (without health problems — at risk)
- ~70% of obese individuals have at least one established comorbidity
- Medical costs ~30% higher for obese individuals
- For every 5 known type 2 diabetes cases, 4 undiagnosed

### Exercise Role in Obesity
- Exercise plays a relatively minor role in weight LOSS (minimal long-term loss from exercise alone)
- Exercise is helpful for weight MAINTENANCE
- "You cannot outrun a bad diet"
- High levels of exercise may be required to maintain weight loss
- Sedentary behaviour has little effect on body weight/Obesity per meta-analysis
- Exercise has numerous metabolic benefits independent of weight loss

## Chapter 8: Diabetes Mellitus

### Types of Diabetes
| Feature | Type 1 | Type 2 |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Prevalence | 10-15% of diabetes | ~90% of diabetes |
| Pathophysiology | Autoimmune beta-cell destruction; absent endogenous insulin | Insulin resistance + relative insulin deficiency |
| Onset | Usually childhood/adolescence | Usually adulthood (increasingly in adolescents) |
| C-peptide | Low/undetectable | Detectable (endogenous insulin present) |
| Treatment | Insulin essential | Lifestyle + oral agents +/- insulin |
| Obesity association | No | ~80% of patients are obese |

### Diagnostic Criteria
- Fasting plasma glucose >=7 mmol/L (126 mg/dL)
- 2-hour post-OGTT >=11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dL)
- HbA1c >=6.5%
- Classic symptoms

### Pre-Exercise Evaluation (Diabetic Athlete)
**Focus on:**
- Cardiovascular: BP, postural drop, heart sounds, bruits, peripheral pulses
- Eyes: retinopathy, glaucoma, cataracts
- Peripheral neuropathy: especially feet
- HbA1c and fructosamine for long-term control
- ECG and stress testing at clinician discretion

**Pre-exercise clearance:** Not required for asymptomatic individuals starting low-moderate exercise (ADA). Required for high-intensity activities due to silent ischaemia risk (up to 50% T2DM have IHD at diagnosis).

### Blood Glucose Management for Exercise
| BG Level (mmol/L) | BG Level (mg/dL) | Action |
|-------------------|------------------|--------|
| <5.5 | <100 | Consume 10-30g CHO before exercise |
| 5.5-10 | 100-180 | Safe to exercise |
| 10-16.5 | 180-300 | Safe; no extra CHO needed (most cases) |
| >13.9 + ketones | >250 + ketones | Postpone; take supplemental insulin |
| >16.5 | >300 | Postpone regardless of ketones |

### Insulin Adjustment for Type 1 Diabetes
| Activity Type | Duration | Pre-exercise Insulin Reduction | CHO Intake |
|--------------|----------|-------------------------------|------------|
| Low intensity (walking, golf) | 20-30 min | Minimal | 15g CHO before if BG <5.5 |
| Moderate (jog, swim, cycle, tennis) | <1 hour | -25% short-acting | 15-30g before + every 30 min |
| Strenuous (marathon, triathlon) | >1 hour | -30-80% total dose | 30g (100-120 cal) every 30 min |
| Early morning (before food) | — | -20-50% evening basal; -30-50% morning regular (may omit) | — |
| Pump therapy | — | -50% basal rate 1hr before high-intensity | — |

### Exercise Prescription for Diabetes
**Aerobic:**
- 150 min/week moderate (spread >=3 days) OR 75 min/week vigorous/HIIT
- Split into small increments if needed
- Moderate: biking, walking, swimming, dancing, water aerobics
- Vigorous: jogging, aerobics, hockey, basketball, hiking

**Resistance (2-3x/week, non-consecutive days):**
- Start: 1 set, 10-12 reps, moderate weight
- Progress: 2 sets, 10-12 reps
- Advance: 3 sets, 8-10 reps, heavier weight
- Exercises: shoulder press, bicep curls, push-ups, lunge, squat, calf raise
- Target all major muscle groups
- May be more beneficial for those with comorbidities limiting aerobic activity

**Flexibility:** 2-3x/week

**Balance:** 2-3x/week (especially older adults, neuropathy)

### Complication-Specific Modifications
| Complication | Exercise Restrictions | Recommended Activities |
|-------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Autonomic neuropathy | Avoid high intensity, rapid postural changes, extreme temperatures | Water activities, stationary cycling |
| Peripheral neuropathy | Avoid foot trauma | Swimming, cycling, arm exercises, balance activities |
| Proliferative retinopathy | Avoid BP >170 systolic, prolonged Valsalva, head jarring | Walking, stationary cycling, swimming |
| Nephropathy (microalbuminuria/overt) | Avoid heavy lifting, high-intensity aerobic | Light-moderate, weight-bearing but low impact |

### Post-Exercise Hypoglycaemia Prevention
- Check BG before, during, and immediately after exercise
- Additional monitoring 4-6 hours post-exercise (LOPEH risk)
- Consume 1.5 g CHO/kg within 30 min of exhaustive exercise
- Additional 1.5 g CHO/kg 1-2 hours later
- Caffeine 30 min pre-exercise may reduce post-exercise nocturnal hypoglycaemia risk (T1DM)
- Avoid injecting insulin into exercising muscle
- Avoid alcohol post-exercise

### Practical Guidance for Diabetic Athletes
**Before exercise:**
- Know effects of different exercise types and environments on your BG
- Have fast-acting CHO available (fruit, juice, barley sugar, sport drinks)
- Eat 1-2 hours pre-exercise
- Hydrate (dehydration can mimic hypoglycaemia)
- Avoid injecting insulin into exercising muscle

**During exercise:**
- Intermittent: ingest CHO during breaks
- Long duration: regular CHO intake
- Hydrate

**After exercise:**
- BG may continue dropping for hours
- Ingest CHO post-exercise to replenish glycogen
- Avoid alcohol (dehydrates, lowers BG)
- Hydrate

**Equipment to carry:**
- Insulin medication
- Fast-acting CHO
- Medical ID bracelet
- Water
- Glucose monitor
- Comfortable shoes and socks

### Diabetes and Travel
- Carry physician's letter (insulin, needles, testing equipment)
- Copies of prescriptions
- Medications in original packaging
- Insulin in carry-on (not checked luggage — risk of freezing)
- Insulin stable at room temperature ~1 month
- East-west travel >5 time zones: adjust insulin doses
- Check BG at least every 6 hours during travel
- Pump therapy: pumps auto-reset to destination time

### High-Risk Sports (Diabetic Athletes)
Increased risk in: rock/mountain climbing, skydiving, other high-consequence activities. Hypoglycaemia impairs concentration and can be fatal. Develop personalized diabetic self-management program. Emphasize performance benefits of good control alongside health benefits.

### Type 2 Diabetes Specific
- All patients should participate in regular physical activity
- Lifestyle modification is first-line therapy (weight loss + activity + nutrition)
- If 2-3 months lifestyle trial unsuccessful: add metformin (preferred initial agent)
- Patients on sulfonylureas: risk of hypoglycaemia due to long half-life; may need dose reduction on exercise days
- HbA1c target: <7% after 3 months monotherapy; if not, add combination therapy

### Glucose-Lowering Agents (GLAs) Quick Reference
| Class | Examples | Hypoglycaemia Risk | Weight Effect |
|-------|----------|-------------------|---------------|
| Biguanides (Metformin) | Metformin | Rare | Neutral/loss |
| Sulfonylureas | Glyburide, Glipizide | Yes | Gain |
| GLP-1 agonists | Exenatide, Liraglutide | Rare | Loss |
| DPP-4 inhibitors | Sitagliptin, Saxagliptin | Rare | Neutral |
| SGLT2 inhibitors | Empagliflozin, Dapagliflozin | Rare | Loss |
| TZDs | Pioglitazone, Rosiglitazone | Rare | Gain |
| Insulins | Multiple | Yes | Gain |
