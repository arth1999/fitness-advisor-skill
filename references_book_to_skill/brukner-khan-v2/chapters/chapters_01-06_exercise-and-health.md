# Part A: Exercise and Health (Chapters 1-6)

## Chapter 1: Physical Inactivity — A Global Public Health Problem

### Key Facts
- 31% of adults globally are physically inactive (34% women, 28% men)
- 80% of 13-15 year olds are physically inactive
- Physical inactivity estimated to cause 3.2 million deaths annually
- Direct healthcare cost attributable to 5 major NCDs: US$53.8 billion (2013)
- Total economic cost including productivity losses: US$67.5 billion
- Physical inactivity accounts for: ~30% of CVD, 27% of diabetes, 21-25% of breast and colon cancer

### WHO GAPPA Targets (2025)
- 25% reduction of premature mortality from NCDs
- 10% relative reduction in prevalence of insufficient physical activity

### Seven Best Investments for Physical Activity (ISPAH)
1. Built environment and transport
2. Schools and educational institutions
3. Community and grassroots-based initiatives
4. Sports systems and programs
5. Public education
6. Healthcare
7. Advocacy and leadership

### Cost Comparison: Physical Inactivity vs Smoking
- Per-person cost of physical inactivity in Canada: ~$384/year
- Per-smoker cost in Canada: ~$3,280/year
- Physical inactivity per-person cost approximates smoking 11 cigarettes/week
- However: far more people are inactive than smoke, so total burden is comparable

### Key Takeaway
If physical inactivity decreased by 10%, >533,000 deaths avoided annually. If decreased by 25%, >1.3 million deaths avoided. The solution requires a whole-of-system approach.

## Chapter 2: Benefits and Risks of Physical Activity

### Physiological Response to Exercise
Physical activity is one of the most extreme stresses the body can experience. The response preserves:
- Cellular oxygenation
- Acid-base homeostasis
- Both critical for survival

### Evidence Hierarchy for Exercise Benefits
The benefits of regular physical activity include:
- All-cause mortality reduction: 15% (75-90 min/week), 40% (30 min x 6 days/week)
- Primary and secondary prevention of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, multiple cancers, osteoporosis, depression, anxiety, dementia
- Improved: mood, sleep, weight management, chronic inflammation, pain, quality of life, life expectancy

### Key Mechanisms
- Skeletal muscle acts as an endocrine organ during contraction
- Releases myokines that influence metabolism, inflammation, and organ function
- Exercise induces mitochondrial biogenesis, improves insulin sensitivity, enhances vascular function

### Risks of Exercise
- Sudden cardiac death in young athletes: incidence 1:40,000 to 1:80,000
- Musculoskeletal injury: primarily overuse; mitigated by gradual progression
- Exercise paradox: intense exertion temporarily increases cardiac event risk in susceptible individuals

### Dose-Response Relationship
- Clear graded dose response between physical activity volume and all-cause mortality, stroke, CVD risk
- Greatest risk reduction per unit time is for vigorous activity
- Most health gains occur at 3000-4000 MET-minutes/week

## Chapter 3: Prescribing Physical Activity — The Clinical Assessment

### The 5As Model of Behaviour Change

#### A1: Assess
**Assess current activity and readiness for change.**
- Type, frequency, intensity, duration of physical activity
- Sedentary behaviour in all domains
- Risks and contraindications

**Assessment Tools:**
- CSEP Get Active Questionnaire (formerly PAR-Q): pre-activity readiness
- Global Physical Activity Questionnaire (GPAQ)
- Scottish Physical Activity Screening Question (Scot-PASQ):
  1. In the past week, on how many days were you active for 30+ minutes?
  2. If <=4 days, have you been active for 150 minutes over the past week?
  3. If not, are you interested in being more active?
- Physical Activity Vital Sign: 2 questions on days/week and minutes/session
- Wearable trackers: pedometers, accelerometers, GPS, HR monitors

#### A2: Advise
**Provide brief, tailored advice about recommended activity levels.**
- WHO guidelines: 150 min moderate or 75 min vigorous per week
- Muscle strengthening 2x/week
- Balance training for 65+
- Limit sedentary behaviour
- "Any physical activity is better than none at all"

#### A3: Agree
**Agree on realistic, patient-driven SMART goals.**
- Specific: "Walk for 30 minutes per day before work"
- Measurable: "Time the walk, track distance"
- Achievable: "Start with 10 min, build by 5 min each week"
- Relevant: Matches patient's needs
- Time-based: "Reach goal by week 4"

#### A4: Assist
**Provide written prescription and overcome barriers.**
- Common barriers and responses (see cheatsheet.md)
- Written exercise prescription (see FITT-VP framework)
- Support materials: activity diaries, self-monitoring tools
- Identify a support person (friend/family)

#### A5: Arrange
**Plan follow-up at 3-6 months.**
- Same clinician ideally
- Referral networks: specialists, allied health, community programs
- Referral to SEM specialist for high-risk patients
- Reassess and adjust plan

### Time-Limited Counselling Adaptations
- 5 minutes: Assess (Vital Sign) + Advise (brief benefits) + Agree (open the door)
- 30 seconds: "On average, how many days/week do you exercise? Consider 22 min of walking daily."

### Enabling Factors
- Physical activity training in medical curricula
- System-level support for counselling
- Integration of physical activity as a vital sign
- Community referral networks

## Chapter 4: Prescribing Physical Activity — The Written Prescription

### FITT-VP Framework

#### Type
Four categories to consider:
- **Aerobic**: walking, running, hiking, cycling, swimming, dancing
- **Resistance**: bodyweight, free weights, machines, resistance bands, Pilates
- **Flexibility**: stretching, Pilates, yoga, tai chi
- **Balance**: yoga, tai chi, specific balance exercises

The best type is the one the patient enjoys and will do.

#### Time (Duration)
- Minimum: 150 min moderate or 75 min vigorous/week
- Equivalent: 22 min moderate activity/day
- ACSM: 20-60 min continuous aerobic activity daily
- Bouts can be as short as 10 min (some evidence says every step counts)
- Resistance: 1 set, 8-12 reps for 8-10 exercises covering major muscle groups
- Repetition maximum principle: lift until difficult to do another rep with good form

#### Intensity
**Methods of Measurement:**
- **Borg RPE Scale (6-20)**: Moderate = 12-14
- **Heart Rate**: Training range = 50-85% HRmax; HRmax = 208 - (0.7 x age)
- **METs**: Sedentary <1.6; Light 1.6-3.0; Moderate 3-6; Vigorous >6
- **Talk Test**: Moderate = can talk comfortably; Vigorous = breathing heavily, can't talk

#### Frequency
- Aerobic: 3-5 days/week minimum; 5-7 days optimal
- Resistance: >=2 days/week, at least 48 hours between same muscle groups
- Minimum thresholds: 300 kcal/session x 3 days/week OR 200 kcal/session x 4 days/week

#### Volume
- MET-minutes = METs x total minutes
- Minimum: 500-1000 MET-min/week
- Most health gains: 3000-4000 MET-min/week
- Example to accumulate 3000 MET-min/week: stairs 10 min + walk/cycle 25 min + vacuum 15 min + garden 20 min + run 20 min

#### Progression
- Order: Increase duration -> frequency -> intensity
- Rate: +5-10 min every 1-2 weeks over first 4-6 weeks
- After 1+ month: adjust frequency and intensity
- Continuously monitor for adverse effects
- Regress if not tolerated

### High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)
- Alternating short intense bursts (70-95% HRmax) with recovery (30-50% HRmax)
- Example: 30 sec sprint + 30 sec jog, repeated
- Greater VO2max improvement than endurance training
- More effective for vascular function, insulin sensitivity
- Safe for cardiac rehab (with stress testing for higher risk)
- Time-efficient: similar benefits in less time

### Sedentary Behaviour Minimisation
- Reduce screen time
- 5-min walking breaks every 45 min of sitting
- Calisthenics during TV commercials
- Stand/walk when on the phone
- Take stairs not elevator
- Sit-stand desk
- Park further away
- Active transport (cycle to work)

### Special Prescriptions
**Green Prescription (NZ, since 1998):** Written Rx + referral to support network + monthly phone/face-to-face support for <=6 months. Increases activity by ~1 hour/week, maintained 2-3 years.

**Physical Activity on Prescription (Sweden):** Written Rx based on FYSS book. 50,000 prescriptions in 2010. Adherence comparable to long-term drug treatments.

### Step Count Guidance
- Healthy adults: 4,000-18,000 steps/day
- US average: 5,100; Japan: 7,200; Australia: 7,400
- Target: >=7,000 steps/day minimum; 10,000 steps/day benchmark
- >=15,000 steps/day: zero cardiovascular disease risk factors observed
- For children: 11,000-15,000 steps/day (boys), 11,000-12,000 (girls)
- Moderate intensity = >=100 steps/min

### Example Rx: Low Back Pain (FYSS)
| Type | Time | Intensity | Frequency |
|------|------|-----------|-----------|
| Walking/Swimming/Cycling | >=30 min | 11-16/20 RPE, 60-80% HRmax | 5-7x/week |
| Abdominal/Back strengthening | 8-15 reps/day, 8-10 muscle groups | As tolerated | Daily |
| Yoga | 2 min/muscle group, 3 reps | To tension before discomfort | Daily |

## Chapter 5: Prescribing Physical Activity — Motivational Interviewing

### Why Behaviour Change Is Hard
Motivation is a dynamic state influenced by:
- Knowledge and understanding of health/disease
- Personal meaning and relevance of that knowledge
- Confidence in ability to change (self-efficacy)
- Facilitators and barriers to change

### Key Behaviour Change Theories

**Classic Learning Theories:** Complex behaviour broken into smaller parts (chunking). Gradual increments. Reinforcement and anticipated rewards drive maintenance (operant conditioning).

**Social Cognitive Theory:** Behaviour change affected by environmental influences, personal factors, and behaviour attributes. Central concept: self-efficacy.

**Reasoned Action Approach:** Intentions influenced by attitude + perceived norms + perceived behavioural control.

**Fogg Behaviour Model:** Behaviour = Motivation + Ability + Trigger. Pleasure-seeking and pain-avoiding drives.

**Transtheoretical Stages of Change:**
1. Precontemplation: Not thinking about change
2. Contemplation: Thinking about change but ambivalent
3. Preparation: Planning to change
4. Action: Performing the change
5. Maintenance: Sustaining change
6. Relapse: Return to old behaviour

### Motivational Interviewing — Four Guiding Principles

1. **Express Empathy:** Reflective listening, communicate respect and acceptance. Spend equal time listening and talking.

2. **Support Self-Efficacy:** Identify and highlight patient strengths. Build confidence through past successes.

3. **Develop Discrepancy:** Help patient see gap between current behaviour and desired future. Use decisional balance exercises.

4. **Roll with Resistance:** Avoid arguing. Use reflection, reframing, shifting focus. Resist the "righting reflex" — don't push the "correct" path.

### OARS — Fundamental Skills

**Open-ended questions:** "Tell me about your concerns with fitness."

**Affirmations:** "I think it's great you want to be more active and came in to discuss it."

**Reflections (Active Listening):** "You're beginning to worry about the impact your lack of exercise is having on your health."

**Summaries:** "Let me check I've understood everything we've been discussing so far..."

### Recognizing Change Talk
Statements indicating the patient is working on change:
- Desire: "I want/would like to..."
- Reason: "I would probably feel better if..."
- Ability: "I can/could/might be able to..."
- Need: "I have/ought to..."
- Commitment: "I will/intend to..."

### Maintaining Behaviour Change
- Initial change motivated by uncertain long-term outcomes (disease risk reduction)
- Maintenance requires at least one sustained motivator: enjoyment, satisfaction with outcomes, self-determination, congruence with values
- Anticipate barriers (see cheatsheet.md) and plan strategies

### Nudging
- Point-of-decision prompts for stair use: effective, evidence-based
- Make healthy choices the default/easier option
- Health behaviours driven more by automatic responses than conscious deliberation

## Chapter 6: Nutrition for Health

### Key Principles
- Focus on whole, real foods rather than ultra-processed products
- Reduce sugar-sweetened beverages (strongest food-obesity link)
- Re-examine dietary fat guidelines: original 1977 recommendations to reduce saturated fat lacked scientific basis at the time
- Balance omega-6 to omega-3 ratio (traditional ~1:1; current Western ~16:1)
- >50% of US calories now come from ultra-processed foods

### Dietary Guidelines History (1977 US / 1982 Australia)
The shift to low-fat, high-carbohydrate recommendations coincided with:
- Increased sugar and vegetable oil consumption
- Rise of ultra-processed "low-fat" foods with added sugar
- Dramatic obesity increase from ~1980 onward

### Obesity and Diet
- Caloric restriction produces short-term weight loss but triggers hormonal compensation:
  - Increased appetite (ghrelin, leptin, peptide YY, amylin, cholecystokinin changes)
  - Reduced BMR (energy conservation)
  - Weight regain common when diet ends
- Exercise: minimal role in generating weight loss; important for weight maintenance
- "You cannot outrun a bad diet" (Noakes, Phinney, Malhotra)
- High levels of exercise may be needed for weight maintenance (~300 min/week)

### Clinical Nutrition Guidance
- Individualized nutrition therapy with registered dietitian
- Focus on food quality over calorie counting
- Reduce ultra-processed foods
- Limit/eliminate sugar-sweetened beverages
- Consider whole-food, lower-glycaemic-load eating patterns
