# Part D: Specific Groups (Chapters 28-32)

## Chapter 28: Childhood and Adolescence

### Physiological Characteristics of Young Athletes

**Aerobic Capacity:**
- Absolute VO2max (L/min) increases with age and maturation
- Relative VO2max (mL/kg/min) remains stable in boys, DECLINES in girls throughout adolescence
- Peak increase in VO2 velocity coincides with peak height velocity

**Strength:**
- Gradual increases pre-pubertal (little difference between boys and girls)
- Accelerates in pubertal years; peak strength velocity coincides with peak weight velocity (6-12 months after peak height velocity)
- At sexual maturity: males have ~50% greater lower limb strength, ~2x upper body strength vs females

**Anaerobic Capacity:**
- Increases with age and maturation even when expressed relative to body weight
- Peak anaerobic performance: early 20s (women), late 20s (men)

### Maturation and Sport
- Early maturation confers advantages in most sports (strength, power, aerobic fitness)
- Early maturers over-represented in elite adolescent sport
- Birth month effect: those born early in competition year over-represented in many youth sports
- Weight-for-age competitions used in some contact sports to equalise opportunities

### Early Specialisation — Risks
- Social isolation, overdependence, burnout, manipulation, overuse injury, compromised growth and maturation
- American Medical Society for Sports Medicine: warns against early specialisation EXCEPT in early-entry sports (gymnastics, swimming, diving, figure skating)
- Elite athletes more often specialise in late teens vs early
- Recommendations:
  - Encourage free, unstructured play for motor skill development
  - Participate in a variety of sports before considering specialisation
  - Monitor children doing >hours/week than their age in single sport OR >16 hours/week intense training
  - Periodised strength and conditioning for all youth including inactive

### Exercise Prescription for Children with Chronic Disease

**Type 1 Diabetes:**
- Moderate aerobic + resistance 2x/week
- Pre-exercise BG: if <5.6 mmol/L, consume additional CHO
- Carbohydrate replacement for >30 min exercise (0.5-1 g/kg/h)
- Insulin pumps offer greater flexibility, reduce hypoglycaemia risk
- High-intensity intervals can reduce hypoglycaemia risk
- Avoid: extreme heat (symptoms overlap with hypoglycaemia), sports where hypoglycaemia catastrophic
- Elite athletes: TUE required for insulin

**Childhood Cancer:**
- Aerobic: moderate (Borg 11-14), 5-7 days/week, 20-30 min (split if needed)
- Resistance: 1-3 sessions/week, 1-4 sets, 6-12 reps to fatigue
- Start during inpatient period where possible
- Modifications: low WCC (infection risk), low platelets (bleeding risk), anaemia (exercise tolerance), photosensitivity (outdoor exercise), reduced bone density (contact risk)

**Cystic Fibrosis:**
- Combined aerobic + resistance program
- Exercise intensity guided by pre-training evaluation (lung function, cardiac function, baseline aerobic capacity)
- VO2peak is independent predictor of survival
- Both supervised and unsupervised programs improve outcomes
- Caution in heat: increased salt loss -> electrolyte replacement essential
- CF-related diabetes: manage hypoglycaemia risk

**Haemophilia:**
- Regular physical activity encouraged
- Prophylactic clotting factor: administer before contact/moderate-risk sports
- For every 1% increase in clotting factor -> 2% reduction in bleeding risk
- Contraindicated: sports with risk of head trauma (boxing)
- Benefits: improved muscle strength, bone density, cardiovascular health, quality of life

**Mitochondrial Myopathies:**
- Aerobic + resistance training combined, 3x/week, 60 min
- Rationale: address deconditioning, induce mitochondrial proliferation, gene shifting
- Exercise testing characteristics: low VO2peak, high RER, elevated lactate, low O2 pulse, high ventilatory equivalent
- Evidence from adult populations; limited paediatric data

**McArdle Disease (GSD V):**
- Low-moderate aerobic: 5x/week, 30-60 min
- Avoid: repetitive eccentric/plyometrics, high-intensity bursts
- High-intensity: glucose load 5 min prior
- 10-15 min low-intensity warm-up: utilises "second-wind" phenomenon
- Monitor for rhabdomyolysis (elevated CK, myoglobinuria)

**Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA):**
- Only ~25% meet daily PA guidelines
- Reduced aerobic fitness vs healthy peers
- High-intensity aerobic training: well tolerated; does NOT exacerbate joint symptoms
- Combined anaerobic + aerobic + resistance likely most beneficial
- Exercise does not exacerbate arthritis and may improve strength, fitness, bone density

**Joint Hypermobility Syndrome (JHS):**
- Combined aerobic + resistance: improve fitness, muscle strength, joint pain, fatigue
- Initial support from exercise professional: identify and correct altered movement patterns
- Knee injuries 5x more common in contact sports
- Contact/collision sports NOT contraindicated; may support conditioning goals

### Dyspnoea on Exertion — Paediatric Differential
| Feature | Poor Fitness | EIB | VCD |
|---------|-------------|-----|-----|
| Timing | Throughout exercise | End/after exercise | Intense exercise |
| Breath sounds | NAD | Expiratory wheeze | Inspiratory stridor |
| VO2peak | Low | Normal/High | Normal/High |
| Spirometry | NAD | >10% fall FEV1 | NAD |
| Bronchodilator | No response | Yes | No |

### Concussion in Children
- Child SCAT5: specific tool for ages 5-12 years
- Recovery may be prolonged vs adults
- Separate return-to-play guidelines for children
- Most youth sport has NO formal medical coverage -> many concussions undiagnosed
- Community education critical

## Chapter 29: Female-Specific Considerations

### Key Areas
- Female Athlete Triad / RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)
- Menstrual cycle effects on performance and injury risk
- Bone health: higher risk of stress fractures with amenorrhoea/oligomenorrhoea
- Iron deficiency: common in female athletes (menstrual losses + inadequate intake)
- Pregnancy and exercise
- Pelvic floor dysfunction in female athletes
- Breast support and exercise

### Exercise Prescription for Female Athletes
- Weight-bearing exercise essential for bone health
- Adequate energy availability critical (45 kcal/kg FFM/day minimum)
- Monitor menstrual function as indicator of energy status
- Iron status monitoring (ferritin, haemoglobin)
- Individualise around menstrual cycle if symptoms affect performance

### Pregnancy and Exercise
- Previously active: continue moderate-intensity exercise; 150 min/week is safe
- Previously sedentary: start gradually; walking/ swimming recommended
- Avoid: supine exercise after first trimester, contact sports, fall-risk activities, overheating, scuba diving
- Warning signs to stop: vaginal bleeding, abdominal pain, amniotic fluid leakage, dyspnoea before exertion, dizziness, headache, chest pain, calf pain/swelling, preterm labour
- Benefits: reduced risk of gestational diabetes, pre-eclampsia, excessive weight gain; improved mood, sleep, recovery

## Chapter 30: Transgender and Intersex Athletes

### Key Considerations
- Evolving area of sports medicine with changing policies
- IOC and sporting federation guidelines continue to develop
- Key issues: eligibility criteria, hormone therapy effects, fairness vs inclusion
- Medical management of transgender athletes: hormone therapy monitoring, bone health, cardiovascular risk
- Intersex variations (DSD): complex; managed case-by-case per sport-specific policies

## Chapter 31: Older People

### Exercise Benefits in Older Adults
- Cardiovascular health and mortality reduction
- Maintenance of muscle mass and strength (sarcopenia prevention)
- Bone health and fracture prevention
- Falls prevention (balance training)
- Cognitive function and dementia risk reduction
- Maintenance of independence and quality of life
- Management of chronic conditions (OA, diabetes, CVD, depression)

### Exercise Prescription for Older Adults
| Component | Prescription |
|-----------|-------------|
| Aerobic | 150 min/week moderate OR 75 min/week vigorous (or combination) |
| Resistance | 2-3x/week, 8-10 exercises, 10-15 reps, major muscle groups |
| Balance | >=3 days/week (especially if poor mobility or falls history) |
| Flexibility | 2-3x/week, all major muscle-tendon groups |
| Progression | Gradual; start with lower intensity/volume; monitor tolerance |

### Special Considerations
- Comorbidities: tailor exercise to individual conditions (see Part B)
- Polypharmacy: consider medication effects on exercise response
- Falls risk assessment before prescribing
- Osteoarthritis: non-weight-bearing or aquatic options if land-based painful
- Cognitive impairment: simple instructions, supervision may be needed
- "Start low, go slow" applies doubly
- Walking groups and community programs: social benefits enhance adherence

### Falls Prevention
- Balance training: single leg stands, tandem stance, heel-to-toe walking, tai chi
- Strength training: lower limb emphasis (sit-to-stand, step-ups, calf raises)
- Environmental modification: home safety assessment

## Chapter 32: The Person with Disability

### Principles of Exercise Prescription for Disability
- Individualised assessment of abilities, limitations, and goals
- Consider medical issues specific to the disability (autonomic dysfunction, thermoregulation, pressure areas, contractures)
- Equipment modification and adaptive techniques
- Same FITT-VP framework; modify as needed for the individual
- Focus on what the person CAN do

### Key Considerations by Disability Type
**Spinal Cord Injury:**
- Autonomic dysreflexia risk (T6 and above): monitor BP during exercise
- Impaired thermoregulation: caution in heat/cold
- Pressure area prevention
- Upper limb overuse injury risk from wheelchair propulsion/transfers
- Electrical stimulation-assisted exercise for lower limbs

**Cerebral Palsy:**
- Spasticity management around exercise
- Contracture prevention through stretching
- Gait and mobility focus
- Fatigue may be significant limiting factor

**Amputation:**
- Prosthetic fit and skin integrity
- Energy cost of ambulation higher with more proximal amputation
- Contralateral limb overuse risk

**Intellectual Disability:**
- Simple, clear instructions
- Social support and supervision as needed
- Consider Special Olympics and similar programs

**Visual/Hearing Impairment:**
- Environmental safety; guide or tandem options
- Clear communication methods for instruction

### General Prescription Principles
- Start with achievable goals to build confidence and self-efficacy
- Include carers/support workers in the plan
- Refer to specialised exercise professionals where available
- Link with disability sports organisations and programs
- Monitor for secondary complications of disability
