# Part B: Managing Medical Problems — Respiratory, GI, Urinary, Neurological (Chapters 11-14)

## Chapter 11: Respiratory Symptoms During Exercise

### Key Respiratory Conditions in Athletes

#### Exercise-Induced Bronchospasm (EIB)
- Airway narrowing triggered by exercise
- Diagnostic criterion: >=10% fall in FEV1 post-exercise
- Timing: typically at end or after exercise (not during)
- Auscultation: expiratory wheeze
- Responds to bronchodilators
- Often coexists with vocal cord dysfunction (VCD)

#### Vocal Cord Dysfunction (VCD)
- Paradoxical vocal cord adduction during inspiration
- Timing: during intense exercise (especially competition vs training)
- Auscultation: inspiratory stridor (not wheeze)
- Typical presentation: high-achieving female adolescent athlete, often previously diagnosed with "asthma" but no response to bronchodilators
- Often associated with anxiety and GORD
- Diagnosis: flexible laryngoscopy with exercise (gold standard)
- Management: breathing exercises (nasal breathing, pursed lip, diaphragmatic); speech pathology; treat underlying anxiety/GORD

#### Differential Diagnosis of Dyspnoea on Exertion
| Feature | Poor Fitness | EIB | VCD |
|---------|-------------|-----|-----|
| Timing | Throughout exercise | End/after exercise | Intense exercise |
| Breath sounds | NAD | Expiratory wheeze | Inspiratory stridor |
| VO2max | Low | Normal/High | Normal/High |
| Spirometry | NAD | >10% fall in FEV1 | NAD |
| Bronchodilator response | No | Yes | No |
| Flow-volume loops | NAD | Obstructive expiratory | Flattened inspiratory loop |

### Other Respiratory Considerations
- Spontaneous pneumothorax
- Respiratory tract infections and exercise
- Pulmonary function testing in athletes
- Exercise testing with flow-volume loops for comprehensive assessment

## Chapter 12: Gastrointestinal Symptoms

### Exercise-Related GI Symptoms
- Common in endurance athletes
- Range from mild discomfort to significant pathology
- Mechanisms: reduced splanchnic blood flow during exercise, mechanical factors, nutritional factors

### Key Conditions
- Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD): common; exacerbated by exercise; may trigger VCD
- Exercise-induced nausea and vomiting
- Runner's diarrhoea
- GI bleeding (occult or overt)
- Ischaemic colitis (rare but serious)

### Management Principles
- Dietary modification: timing of meals, composition, fibre management
- Hydration strategies
- Medication as appropriate (antacids, H2 blockers, PPIs)
- Red flags: significant bleeding, severe pain, weight loss, family history of GI malignancy

## Chapter 13: Haematuria and Other Urinary Symptoms

### Exercise-Related Haematuria
- Common in contact sports and long-distance running
- Usually benign and self-limiting
- Must exclude pathological causes

### Differential Diagnosis
- Traumatic haematuria (contact sports)
- Non-traumatic exercise-induced haematuria
- Rhabdomyolysis-related
- Urinary tract infection
- Renal calculi
- Malignancy (must be excluded, especially in older athletes)

### Clinical Approach
- History: timing, associated symptoms, training load, fluid intake
- Urinalysis: microscopy, culture
- Imaging if indicated: ultrasound, CT
- Red flags: visible haematuria >72 hours, recurrent episodes, associated pain, constitutional symptoms

## Chapter 14: Neurological Conditions

### Key Neurological Presentations in Athletes

#### Headache
- Primary exercise headache (benign)
- Exertional headache (may be vascular)
- Post-traumatic headache (concussion-related)
- Red flags: thunderclap onset, meningism, focal neurology, altered consciousness

#### Seizure Disorders
- Seizure vs syncope differentiation (see Chapter 10)
- Exercise-induced seizures: ensure cardiovascular work-up
- Management of athletes with known epilepsy: safety considerations by sport
- Avoid sports where seizure would cause catastrophic injury (climbing, diving, aviation sports)
- Water sports: close supervision always required

#### Peripheral Nerve Entrapments
- Common in specific sports (e.g., cyclist's palsy — ulnar nerve)
- Carpal tunnel syndrome
- Tarsal tunnel syndrome
- Morton's neuroma
- Management: activity modification, equipment adjustment, physiotherapy, surgical referral if refractory

#### Concussion (Neurological Aspects)
- See Clinical Sports Medicine Volume 1 (Injuries) for detailed protocols
- Child-specific: Child SCAT5 tool for ages 5-12
- Prolonged recovery in children/adolescents
- Separate return-to-play guidelines for paediatric population

#### Stroke in Athletes
- Rare but devastating
- Consider arterial dissection (especially after neck trauma/torsion)
- Patent foramen ovale (PFO) and paradoxical embolism: present in ~25% of adults
- Sickle cell trait and exertional collapse

### General Neurological Assessment in Sport
- Full neurological examination for any suspected CNS pathology
- Consider imaging (CT/MRI) for acute presentations
- Neurophysiology studies for peripheral nerve lesions
- Red flags: focal neurology, altered consciousness, severe/sudden headache, neck pain with neurology
