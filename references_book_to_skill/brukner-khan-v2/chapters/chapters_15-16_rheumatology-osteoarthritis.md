# Part B: Managing Medical Problems — Rheumatology & Osteoarthritis (Chapters 15-16)

## Chapter 15: Rheumatological Conditions

### Key Principle
Not every musculoskeletal complaint in an active person is a mechanical sports injury. Consider rheumatological disease when the clinical pattern does not fit a typical sports injury pattern.

### Features Suggesting Inflammatory Disorder
- Insidious onset (vs acute traumatic)
- Prolonged stiffness after rest (morning stiffness)
- Pain worse at night
- Improvement with moderate activity
- Constitutional features: fever, malaise, weight loss
- Elevated inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP)
- Good response to NSAIDs
- No history of trauma

### Sex Ratios in Common Rheumatological Diseases
| Disease | Female:Male Ratio |
|---------|-------------------|
| Sjogren's syndrome | 9:1 |
| Systemic lupus erythematosus | 7:1 |
| Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) | 3:1 |
| Systemic sclerosis | 3:1 |
| Osteoarthritis (knee) | 1.5-4:1 |
| Psoriatic arthritis | 1:1 |
| Ankylosing spondylitis | 1:3 |

### Anatomical Structures Affected
- Joint: arthritis (inflammatory, infective, degenerative)
- Bone: osteitis, apophysitis, periostitis, stress reactions, fracture
- Muscle: strain, tear, compartment syndrome
- Ligament: sprain, tear
- Tendon: tendinosis, tenosynovitis, paratenonitis, tear
- Bursa: bursitis
- Skin: nodules, rash, nail changes
- Nerve: entrapment, neuropathy

### Clinical Presentations

#### Monoarthritis (Single Swollen Joint) — No Trauma
**Common causes:** Septic arthritis (DO NOT MISS), gout, pseudogout, Lyme disease, reactive arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, peripheral ankylosing spondylitis, osteoarthritis

**Less common:** Pigmented villonodular synovitis, malignancies (synovial sarcoma), monoarticular RA, sarcoidosis, haemophilia

**Septic Arthritis — Must Not Miss:**
- Direct bacterial joint invasion
- Knee and hip most vulnerable
- High index of suspicion in prosthetic joints or immunocompromised
- Most common organism: Neisseria gonorrhoea (young adults)
- Other organisms: Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus
- Gonococcal arthritis: may present with skin rash; treat with ceftriaxone + azithromycin

**Gout:**
- Monosodium urate crystal deposition
- Affects mainly males and postmenopausal females
- Acute attacks: commonly great toe, wrist
- Chronic: polyarthritis, tophi, kidney stones, urate nephropathy
- Red flag: premenopausal female with "gout"

**Lyme Disease:**
- Vector: deer tick (Ixodes scapulares)
- Bacteria: Borrelia burgdorferi (USA), B. afzelii, B. garinii (Europe)
- Highest risk: children 5-14 years and adults 40-50 years
- Three phases: arthritis in late stages
- Commonly affects knee
- Treatment: doxycycline, amoxicillin, or cefuroxime

#### Polyarthritis / Polyarthralgia
Consider: RA, psoriatic arthritis, SLE, systemic sclerosis

#### Low Back Pain and Stiffness
Consider: Ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, enteropathic arthritis. Look for sacroiliac tenderness, restricted lumbar range of motion, enthesitis.

#### "Pain All Over"
Consider: Fibromyalgia, SLE, overlap syndromes

### Important History Questions
1. Any history of trauma or overuse?
2. Which joint(s) affected?
3. Timeline: acute vs chronic?
4. First incident or recurring?
5. Is there an inflammatory component?
6. Any exposure to infective agents?
7. Any treatment used (pharmacological and non-pharmacological)?
8. Any back involvement?
9. Any systemic features (fever, weight changes, skin, eye)?
10. Any extra-articular manifestations?

### Extra-Articular Manifestations
| System | Key Signs |
|--------|-----------|
| Skin | Psoriatic plaques, nail dystrophy, erythema nodosum, tophi, rheumatoid nodules, butterfly rash (SLE) |
| Eyes | Conjunctivitis (ReA), uveitis (AS, psoriatic, EnA, JIA, SLE), dry eyes (Sjogren's) |
| CV | Atherosclerosis (all inflammatory diseases), pericarditis/myocarditis/endocarditis, conduction disorders |
| Pulmonary | Pleural effusion, nodules, interstitial fibrosis |
| GI | Gastritis/PU (often iatrogenic), IBD with spondyloarthropathy |
| Renal | Glomerulonephritis, interstitial disease |
| Neurological | Carpal tunnel, peripheral neuropathy, autonomic impairment, myelopathy |
| Haematological | Anaemia (various types), neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, thrombocytosis |

### Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)
- Umbrella term for inflammatory/autoimmune arthritis in children <16 years
- Five types: systemic arthritis, oligoarthritis (most common, ~50%), polyarthritis, psoriatic arthritis, enthesitis-related arthritis
- Important complication: uveitis (~1 in 3)
- Exercise: does not exacerbate arthritis; may improve strength, fitness, bone density

## Chapter 16: Osteoarthritis

### Modern Definition (OARSI)
"...a disorder involving movable joints characterised by cell stress and extracellular matrix degradation initiated by micro- and macro-injury that activates maladaptive repair responses including pro-inflammatory pathways of innate immunity."

### Key Facts
- OA is NOT simply "wear and tear" — it is a complex, multifactorial whole-joint disease
- At least 50% of susceptibility to hand and hip OA is genetic
- Knee OA more driven by biomechanical factors
- Metabolic syndrome components (obesity, diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia) implicated in pathogenesis
- Pain has a biopsychosocial model: structural pathology + joint factors + extra-articular factors + central sensitisation + sleep + depression

### Epidemiology
- Joints with highest prevalence: hands (43.3%), knees (23.9%), hips (10.9%)
- DALYs increased 35% between 1990-2015
- Risk factors for knee OA: obesity (2.6x), previous knee trauma (3.9x), hand OA, female gender, older age
- Recreational running: PROTECTIVE (prevalence 3.5% vs 10.2% in sedentary controls; 13.3% in competitive runners)
- Elite sport with prior ACL injury or meniscectomy: increased prevalence

### PRACTICE PEARL
"All individuals without significant comorbidities should engage in regular physical activity. There is no evidence to suggest regular physical activity undertaken recreationally increases the risk of osteoarthritis."

### Joint-Specific Diagnosis

**Hip OA:**
- Clinical diagnosis: age >45, activity-related joint pain, morning stiffness <30 min (NICE)
- Exam: loss of hip range of motion into rotation, groin pain on movement
- Radiography: may not correlate with symptoms (pain without radiographic change; radiographic change without pain)

**Knee OA:**
- Risk factors: age >50, female, high BMI, previous knee injury, malalignment, joint laxity, occupational/recreational usage, family history, Heberden's nodes
- History: usage-related pain, worse end of day, relieved by rest, "giving way", morning stiffness <30 min, night pain
- Exam: crepitus, restricted/painful ROM, bony enlargement, absent/modest effusion
- Patellofemoral OA risk factors: stair descent, squatting, quadriceps weakness, patella malalignment

### Exercise in OA Management

**Hip OA:**
| Modality | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| Land-based exercise (combined) | Small-moderate effect on pain and function |
| Aquatic exercise | Small short-term benefit on pain, disability, QoL |
| Nordic walking | Benefits > supervised strength training alone |
| Pre-op exercise | Reduces pain before hip replacement |

**Knee OA:**
| Modality | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| Land-based exercise (any type) | Reduces pain, improves function 2-6 months post-program |
| High-intensity resistance | Large sustained improvement in knee strength |
| Cycling | Improves pain in mild-moderate knee OA |
| Tai chi | Similar to PT; greater improvement in depression and physical QoL |
| Aquatic | Small short-term benefit |
| Yoga (hatha) | Improves pain and perceived function |
| Pre-op neuromuscular exercise | Improves ADL and pain (small effect) |

### Weight Management in OA
- 5.1 kg weight loss over 10 years: >50% decrease in odds of developing OA
- 10% reduction in body weight: up to 50% symptomatic pain improvement
- Each 1 kg weight loss: 2.2 kg reduction in peak knee joint load
- BMI >30: 2.81-fold increased risk of knee OA over 10 years; 7-fold over 22 years

### Comorbidity-Adapted Exercise Protocols (de Rooij et al.)
1. **Physiological adaptations:** Coronary disease, heart failure, hypertension, T2DM, COPD, obesity
2. **Behavioural adaptations:** Chronic non-specific pain, non-specific low back pain, depression
3. **Environmental adaptations:** Visual or hearing impairments

### Prevention — Joint Injury Prevention
- Neuromuscular/proprioceptive training: 50% reduction in ACL injuries
- Overall knee injury risk reduced in soccer players with injury prevention programs
- Cross-training to prevent overuse
- Load-monitoring systems (smartphone apps) for overtraining detection
- Prehabilitation programs for at-risk athletes

### OA Management Traffic-Light System

**GREEN (Advocated):**
- Education and self-management
- Exercise (land-based, aquatic, tai chi, yoga, cycling, Nordic walking)
- Weight management
- Biomechanical interventions (braces, orthotics, walking stick) as adjuncts
- Topical NSAIDs (similar efficacy to oral, lower systemic exposure)
- Positive communication to enhance self-efficacy

**YELLOW (Caution / Limited Role):**
- Oral NSAIDs: risk-stratify for GI and CV; use minimum dose/duration
- Duloxetine: for chronic pain with central sensitisation; independent analgesic effect
- Paracetamol: previously first-line; now evidence shows only minimal short-term benefits
- IA corticosteroids: short-term relief (2-4 weeks) but long-term use may cause cartilage volume loss

**RED (Discouraged):**
- Glucosamine and chondroitin supplements
- Opioids: small-modest effect but high adverse event rate; NOT recommended
- Repeated IA corticosteroids: no long-term benefit; possible harm
- Hyaluronic acid (viscosupplementation): small but clinically irrelevant benefit; increased serious adverse events
- PRP: promising but low-quality evidence; heterogeneous preparations
- Mesenchymal stem cells: not proven safe or effective; limited to clinical trials
