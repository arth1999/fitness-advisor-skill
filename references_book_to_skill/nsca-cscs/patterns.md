# Patterns — When to Use What (NSCA CSCS Decision Rules)

## Program Design Patterns

### Use the 7-Step Design Process when… creating any resistance training program.
Sequence: Needs Analysis → Exercise Selection → Frequency → Order → Load/Reps → Volume → Rest. Do not skip steps; each builds on the previous.

### Use a split routine when… the athlete is intermediate/advanced AND the goal is hypertrophy during off-season AND time allows higher training volumes per muscle group.
Do NOT use split routines when… in preseason or in-season where maximal/rapid force production and technical skill are prioritized.

### Use total-body workouts when… the athlete is a beginner, OR in preseason/in-season, OR time is limited.

### Use power exercises first in the session when… the program includes Olympic lifts, their derivatives, or ballistic exercises.
Rationale: These require the highest neuromuscular demand, skill, and metabolic freshness.

### Use alternating lower/upper body order when… training untrained individuals OR time is very limited.
Rationale: Allows recovery between body areas without lengthening the session.

### Use the push/pull alternation when… you want to reduce fatigue in the same muscle group across exercises AND maintain movement quality.

### Use "heavy/light" day variation when… training frequency is ≥3×/week AND the primary goal is strength.
Assign heavy-day loads at the prescribed intensity; light-day loads at ~80% of heavy day with same rep goals.

### Use microdosing when… total training time is constrained during in-season AND frequency needs to be maintained.
Rationale: Subdivide weekly volume across multiple short-duration bouts to mitigate fatigue.

## Load Prescription Patterns

### Use 1RM testing when… the athlete is intermediate/advanced AND has technique experience in the exercises being tested.
Do NOT use 1RM when… the athlete is untrained, inexperienced, injured, or medically supervised. Use 3RM-5RM testing instead.

### Use %1RM assignment when… you need precise loading for core exercises AND reliable 1RM data exists.

### Use xRM testing based on goal reps when… you want direct load determination without computation for a specific rep scheme.

### Use RPE/RIR when… you want autoregulation to account for daily fluctuations in readiness.
But combine with an objective method (%1RM or SRB) — do NOT use as the sole method. Limit autonomy with inexperienced athletes.

### Use SRB when… you want multi-set autoregulation with 5% loading ranges AND weekly adjustment without frequent RM testing.

### Use heavy loads (≥85% 1RM, ≤6 reps) when… the primary goal is maximal strength for core exercises.
But limit assistance exercises to ≥8RM even in strength phases.

### Use moderate loads (67-80% 1RM, 8-12 reps) when… the primary goal is hypertrophy.
But heavier loads (>80% 1RM) with higher sets and lower reps (e.g., 10×3) can also be prescribed to target type II fibers more specifically.

### Use light loads (≤67% 1RM, ≥12 reps) when… the primary goal is muscular endurance.

### Use 75-90% 1RM for weightlifting derivatives when… training power in catching variations (hang clean, power clean, snatch).

### Use 30-45% 1RM for jump shrug, 100-140% 1RM for mid-thigh pull/shrug when… targeting velocity-dominant vs. force-dominant pulling derivatives respectively.

### Use the 2-for-2 rule for load progression when… working with novice athletes.
But be aware this may eventually promote training to failure in experienced athletes.

### Increase load by 2.5-5% per week when… using SRB or a relative progression model.

## Rest Period Patterns

### Use 2-5 min rest when… training for strength or power.
Rationale: Allows near-complete ATP-PCr resynthesis; permits higher quality subsequent sets.

### Use 2-3 min (multijoint) / 60-90 s (single-joint) when… training for hypertrophy.

### Use ≤30 s rest when… training for muscular endurance OR circuit training.

### Base rest on actual load per exercise, not just the global training goal.
Example: A 12RM assistance exercise in a strength program may need only 60-90 s, even though the program's overall rest prescription is 2-5 min.

## Plyometric Patterns

### Use ballistic plyometrics when… introducing plyometric concepts to beginners OR training upper body (throws, slams).

### Use rebound plyometrics when… the athlete has developed sufficient strength base AND you need greater SSC and neurophysiological contributions.

### Do NOT use depth jumps when… the athlete is prepubescent (open epiphyseal plates), OR weighs >220 lb (100 kg) with poor strength ratios, OR has inadequate base strength (<1.5× BW back squat).

### Use 48-72 hours between plyometric sessions when… designing frequency.
Rationale: Power measurements impaired up to 72 h post-session; adequate recovery prevents overtraining.

### Use work-to-rest ratios of 1:5 to 1:10 when… designing plyometric sets.
Rationale: This is power training — NOT aerobic conditioning.

### Use 80-100 contacts (beginner, low intensity) when… starting a plyometric program.
Progress to 120-200 contacts (advanced, low-moderate intensity) or 20-40 contacts (advanced, high intensity).

## Speed & Agility Patterns

### Use pre-planned COD drills when… developing the physical capacity to change direction (COD ability).

### Use reactive drills (visual/auditory stimuli, small-sided games) when… developing true agility (perception-action coupling).

### Prioritize RFD development when… the sport action time is <200 ms (most explosive sport movements).
Rationale: Maximal force production takes ≥300 ms — athletes cannot express maximal force in typical sport time frames.

### Train eccentric strength for deceleration when… developing COD and agility capabilities, especially for direction changes >45 degrees.

---

## Aerobic Endurance Patterns

### Use extensive endurance training for 80% of total training volume when… building/maintaining aerobic base (even for elite distance runners).
Rationale: Develops cardiovascular function, mitochondrial capacity, fat utilization while minimizing fatigue.

### Use pace/tempo training when… improving lactate threshold and race-pace specificity.

### Use interval training when… the athlete has established a firm aerobic base AND needs to increase VO₂max.
But use sparingly — it is very stressful.

### Use HIIT when… time is limited AND the athlete is well-trained.
Rationale: Similar aerobic benefits in less time; but requires adequate recovery (W:R ≥ 1:5 for long intervals).

### Use the Karvonen method when… you have measured resting HR and want more precise intensity prescription than %MHR alone.

### Use %VO₂max ↔ %HRR equivalency when… converting metabolic targets to heart rate targets (e.g., 70% VO₂max = 70% HRR = 81% MHR).

### Increase only ONE variable at a time (frequency, intensity, OR duration) when… progressing the program.
Limit weekly increase to ≤10% for general population under normal conditions.

## Periodization Patterns

### Use the parallel model when… training youth, novices, or developmental athletes.
Rationale: Multilateral development is needed; training loads are lower and tolerable.

### Use the sequential model when… training intermediate/advanced athletes who need greater variation and targeted development blocks.
Rationale: Addresses inability to simultaneously develop all motor abilities at high levels.

### Use the emphasis model when… athletes have congested competitive schedules (team sports, tactical athletes).
Rotate emphasis every ~2 weeks with varying degrees of maintenance and detraining on de-emphasized qualities.

### Use 4-week mesocycles (3 loading + 1 deload) when… structuring periodized blocks.
Rationale: Most common duration; allows fatigue accumulation followed by supercompensation.

### Do NOT extend the second transition (active rest) beyond 4 weeks when… transitioning between seasons.
Rationale: Longer periods require a much longer preparatory period to regain performance.

### Use maintenance loads (85-93% 1RM, low-moderate volume) when… the in-season extends many months (team sports).
Modulate microcycle by microcycle based on competition schedule; monitoring is critical.

## Rehabilitation Patterns

### Use POLICE, not PRICE, when… managing acute injury in the inflammatory phase.
Rationale: Optimal Loading is now emphasized over strict Rest; early pain-free movement improves outcomes.

### Use submaximal isometric exercise at multiple joint angles when… the athlete is in the fibroblastic repair phase.
Rationale: Maintains neuromuscular function without disrupting new collagen fibers; but strength gains are joint-angle specific.

### Use closed kinetic chain exercises when… transitioning toward functional, sport-specific movement in the remodeling phase.
Rationale: Most sport activities are performed with feet fixed to the playing surface; better joint stability.

### Progress from general → sport-specific exercises when… moving through rehabilitation phases.
Example (ankle sprain → basketball): balance exercises → strength exercises → plyometric exercises → sprinting exercises.

### Do NOT use pain as the sole guide for return-to-play when… the athlete reports reduced pain.
Rationale: Pain decreases well before tissue healing is complete; use criteria-based progression with objective measures.

### Apply the SAID principle to rehabilitation when… designing the program.
Example: Marathon runner → high reps, endurance emphasis; Weightlifter → low reps, high intensity, power emphasis.

## Special Population Patterns

### Use biological age, not chronological age, when… grouping or prescribing for youth athletes.
Rationale: Two children of same chronological age can differ biologically by up to 5 years.

### Reduce volume/intensity and reinforce movement quality when… an adolescent is around PHV (peak height velocity).
Rationale: Injury risk elevated; changes in center of mass and muscle imbalances occur.

### Focus on neural adaptations, not hypertrophy expectations when… training prepubescent children.
Rationale: Low circulating androgens (testosterone 20-60 ng/100 mL); gains primarily through motor unit activation, synchronization, and coordination.

### Use 2-3 sessions/week for youth, 2-3 sessions/week for older adults when… designing frequency for special populations.

### Avoid the Valsalva maneuver when… training older adults, especially those with cardiovascular conditions.
Rationale: Sudden rise in systolic/diastolic BP; alternative breathing patterns should be taught.

### Include balance and flexibility training alongside resistance training when… training older adults.
Rationale: RT alone does NOT prevent falls; multidimensional programs are required.

### Begin with 40-50% 1RM, 1 set × 8-12 reps for older adults when… initiating a resistance training program.
Progress to 2-3 sets × 6-12 reps at 70-85% 1RM, 2-3×/week as tolerated.

### Pursue early prepubertal neuromuscular training when… aiming to reduce ACL injury risk in female athletes.
Rationale: Motor development experience and training opportunities before puberty optimize adaptations; continue across lifespan.

### Modify exercise positions during pregnancy when… in second and third trimesters.
Avoid supine positions; use incline, side-lying, or vertical positions. Continue pelvic floor exercises.
