# Ch19: Program Design and Technique for Plyometric Training

## Definition and Purpose

Plyometric exercise = quick, powerful movement using a prestretch/countermovement that involves the **stretch-shortening cycle (SSC)**. Purpose: increase power of subsequent movements by using elastic components of muscle/tendon AND the stretch reflex.

**Classification**:
- **Ballistic**: Minimally include neuromuscular SSC contributions (e.g., squat jump, medicine ball overhead throw)
- **Rebound**: Require physiomechanical SSC contributions (e.g., drop jumps, repeated countermovement jumps)

## The Stretch-Shortening Cycle (SSC)

Three phases (Table 19.1):

| Phase | Action | Physiological Event |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| I - Eccentric | Stretch of agonist muscle | Elastic energy stored in SEC; muscle spindles stimulated |
| II - Amortization | Pause between I and III | Type Ia afferents synapse with alpha motor neurons; signal transmission |
| III - Concentric | Shortening of agonist fibers | Elastic energy released from SEC; alpha motor neurons stimulate agonist |

- **Amortization phase is critical**: Must be kept short. Elastic energy losses: 0.35s delay = 25% loss, 0.9s = 52%, 1s = 55%, 1.5s = 70%.
- **Slow SSC**: >0.25 s ground contact
- **Fast SSC**: <0.25 s ground contact
- **Stretch rate matters**: Higher stretch rate = greater muscle recruitment and concentric force.

## Program Design Variables

### Needs Analysis
Evaluate sport, position, anthropometry (heavier = exponentially higher loads), and training status.

### Mode
- **Lower body**: Hops (same foot takeoff/landing), bounds (alternating feet), jumps (two feet), depth jumps (step off box)
- **Upper body**: Throws, slams, pulls, pushes
- **Trunk**: Lower body anchored, upper body anchored, throws

### Intensity
Determined by type of drill, technique, equipment arrangement, and movement complexity. **Continuum from low → high** (Figure 19.2):
- Low: skipping, pogo jumps, squat jumps
- Moderate: hurdle jumps, bounding, box jumps
- High: depth jumps, depth hops

**Factors affecting intensity (Table 19.5):**
- Points of contact (single > double leg)
- Speed (higher horizontal velocity = higher intensity)
- Technique (stiffness and posture affect GCT and GRF)
- Strength (higher strength-to-bodyweight reduces relative intensity)
- Directional change (requires deceleration/reacceleration)
- Vertical height (higher CG = greater landing force)
- Mass (heavier = more stress; external load increases intensity)

### Frequency
Typically 1-4 sessions/week; 48-72 hours between sessions. Sport and season dependent.

### Recovery
- Between repetitions: 5-10 s for depth jumps
- Between sets: 2-5 min
- Work-to-rest ratio: 1:5 to 1:10
- Power measurements impaired up to 72h post-session; strength unchanged

### Volume (Table 19.6: Contacts per session)

| Experience | Low Intensity | Moderate Intensity | High Intensity |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Beginner | 80-100 | Not recommended to 40 | Not recommended |
| Intermediate | 100-120 | 20-100 | Not recommended to 20 |
| Advanced | 120-200 | 20-120 | 20-40 |

Upper body: 3-5 sets of 3-5 reps per exercise.

### Program Length
6-10 weeks typical; vertical jump improvements as early as 4 weeks.

### Progression
Systematic increase in frequency, volume, and intensity. As intensity increases, volume decreases — but relationship is variable depending on training status.

## Age Considerations

### Youth/Adolescents
- Depth jumps and high-intensity lower body plyometrics **contraindicated** for prepubescent children (open epiphyseal plates)
- 7-8 year olds successfully trained in progressive plyometric programs
- Twice-weekly, 8-10 weeks, starting at 50-60 jumps/session
- Focus on technique and gradual progression from simple to complex

### Masters
- Consider strength levels (sarcopenia) and preexisting orthopedic/joint conditions
- Depth jumps and single-leg exercises used cautiously or contraindicated with joint degeneration
- Same design principles as adults, with emphasis on lower intensity/volume and monitoring of pain

## Plyometrics and Resistance Training Integration

- Combine lower body RT with upper body plyometrics, and vice versa
- Heavy RT and plyometrics on same day not usually recommended
- **Complex training**: RT + plyometrics in same session for postactivation potentiation
  - **Contrast training**: Alternating high-load and high-velocity exercises set-by-set
  - **Ascending complex**: High-velocity exercise followed by high-force exercise
  - **Descending sets**: Multiple high-force exercises before high-velocity

## Safety: Strength Prerequisites

Theoretical strength minima for lower body plyometrics:
- In-place jumps (pogos, CMJ): Minimal strength required
- Repeated hops/jumps: ~1.0 × bodyweight back squat
- Bounding, depth jumps from ≤12 in (30 cm): ~1.5 × bodyweight
- Depth/box jumps >12 in: ~2.0 × bodyweight

**Athletes >220 lb (100 kg)**: Use caution with high-volume/high-intensity plyometrics. Depth jump box height ≤18 in (46 cm).

## Safety: Landing Technique

- Shoulders over knees, knees over toes
- Ankle dorsiflexion, knee and hip flexion on landing
- Avoid dynamic valgus (inward knee movement)
- Land with "active flat foot" — ankles dorsiflexed, then aggressively plantarflex before contact
- "Credit card rule": Heels stay off ground; slide credit card under heels

## Depth Jump Specifications
- Recommended height: 16-42 in (41-107 cm)
- Norm: 30-32 in (76-81 cm)
- Maximum effective: ~48 in (122 cm) — beyond this amortization phase extends excessively

## Summary: Steps for Implementing Plyometric Training

1. Evaluate athlete (sport and training history)
2. Establish sport-, position-, and athlete-specific goals
3. Teach proper jumping, landing, and throwing technique
4. Assign program design variables (intensity, frequency, recovery, volume)
5. Properly progress the program
