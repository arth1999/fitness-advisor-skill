# Ch21: Program Design for Aerobic Endurance and Metabolic Training

## Factors Related to Aerobic Endurance Performance

1. **Maximal aerobic capacity (VO₂max)** — High correlation with endurance performance; trainable but diminishing returns in elite athletes
2. **Lactate threshold (LT)** — Speed/%VO₂max at which blood lactate begins accumulating; better predictor than VO₂max
3. **Maximal lactate steady state (MLSS)** — Exercise intensity where lactate production = lactate clearance; synonymous with critical power (CP) and critical speed (CS); considered best predictor of all
4. **Exercise economy** — Energy cost at a given velocity; athletes with high economy expend less energy; affected by technique, body mass, positioning
5. **Fuel utilization** — High percentage of type I fibers; efficient fat utilization spares glycogen

## Five-Step Aerobic Program Design Variables

### Step 1: Exercise Mode
Select activity that mimics competition movement pattern. Cross-training for injury periods or multisport athletes only — must be equal in intensity and duration to maintain VO₂max.

### Step 2: Training Frequency
| Athlete Level | Off-Season | Preseason | In-Season | Postseason |
|--------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Beginner | 3-4 | 3-5 | 3-5 | 2-3 |
| Advanced | 5-6 | 6-7 | 5-6 | 3-5 |

Higher intensity/longer duration = less frequent training needed for recovery.

### Step 3: Training Intensity — The Five-Zone Model (Table 21.1)

| Zone | %VO₂max | %HRmax | Lactate (mmol/L) | Duration |
|------|---------|--------|-----------------|----------|
| 1 | 50-60% | 60-72% | 0.8-1.5 | 1-6 h |
| 2 | 66-80% | 72-82% | 1.5-2.5 | 1-3 h |
| 3 | 81-87% | 82-87% | 2.5-4.0 | 50-90 min |
| 4 | 88-93% | 88-92% | 4.0-6.0 | 30-60 min |
| 5 | 94-100% | 93-100% | 6.0-10.0 | 15-30 min |

**Intensity monitoring methods:**
- **Heart rate**: Karvonen method (HRR-based) or %MHR method
  - Karvonen: THR = (HRR × intensity%) + RHR
  - %MHR: THR = APMHR × intensity%
  - APMHR = 220 - age (Fox equation, SD ±10-12 bpm)
- **%VO₂max ↔ %HRR ↔ %MHR relationship (Table 21.2)**:
  - 50% VO₂max = 50% HRR = 66% MHR
  - 70% VO₂max = 70% HRR = 81% MHR
  - 85% VO₂max = 85% HRR = 92% MHR
- **RPE** (0-10 scale)
- **Power meters** (cycling) — metabolic rate closely related to mechanical power
- **METs** (clinical populations): 1 MET = 3.5 ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹

### Step 4: Exercise Duration
Inverse relationship with intensity. Longer duration → lower intensity. Use predicted **time** rather than distance to set duration (accounts for terrain, environment).

### Step 5: Exercise Progression
- General population: ≤10% increase per week in frequency, intensity, OR duration
- Increase only one variable at a time
- Can exceed 10% for short periods with monitoring
- Adjust HR/intensity zones as fitness improves

## Types of Aerobic Endurance Training (Table 21.4)

| Type | Frequency/Week | Duration | Intensity |
|------|---------------|----------|-----------|
| **Extensive endurance** | 1-2 | Race distance or longer (~30-120 min) | ≤65% VO₂max |
| **Pace/Tempo** | 1-2 | ~20-30 min | At LT; at or slightly above race pace |
| **Interval** | 1-2 | 3-5 min (W:R = 1:1) | Close to VO₂max |
| **HIIT** | 1 | 30-90 s (W:R = 1:5) | Greater than VO₂max |
| **Fartlek** | 1 | ~20-60 min | Varies (LSD ↔ pace/tempo) |

### Key principles:
- ~80% of training volume at extensive endurance intensity (even for elite distance runners)
- Interval training: not until firm aerobic base established; very stressful, use sparingly
- HIIT: total several minutes above 90% VO₂max needed per session; short (<45s) or long (2-4 min) intervals elicit different adaptations
- Pace/tempo: develops race pace sense; steady (20-30 min continuous) or intermittent (tempo intervals)

### Minimum Effective Dose (MED)
Apply the minimum training stimuli to maximize adaptations while minimizing damage/injury. Also consider **maximum tolerated dose (MTD)** — the ceiling where benefits are still measurable but side effects (fatigue, microtrauma) high.

## Season-Based Programming

### Off-Season (Base Training)
Develop large aerobic base. Long duration, low intensity. Progress duration first, then intensity.

### Preseason
Increase intensity by incorporating all training types. Maintain or reduce duration. Address individual weaknesses.

### In-Season (Competition)
Low-intensity, short-duration days before competitions. Maintain strengths, improve weaknesses. Racing counts as training stress.

### Postseason (Active Rest)
Low duration, low intensity. Rehab injuries. Strength work on weak areas.

## Special Issues

- **Cross-training**: Maintains conditioning during injury; must match primary mode intensity/duration
- **Detraining**: Rapid loss of adaptations when stimulus removed; maintain reduced frequency + intensity if possible
- **Tapering**: Reduces fatigue while maintaining fitness; peaking lasts 7-14 days
- **Concurrent training**: Combine RT and aerobic training; consider interference effects
- **Recovery sessions**: Intentional low-intensity, short-duration for recovery — NOT a training stimulus; zone 1 only
