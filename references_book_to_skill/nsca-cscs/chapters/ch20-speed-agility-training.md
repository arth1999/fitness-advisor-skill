# Ch20: Program Design and Technique for Speed and Agility Training

## Key Definitions

- **Speed**: Skills and abilities needed to achieve high movement velocities
- **Change-of-direction (COD) ability**: Skills and abilities to explosively change movement direction, velocity, or mode (predetermined, no stimulus)
- **Agility**: Skills and abilities to change direction, velocity, or mode **in response to a stimulus** (perception–action coupling)

**Critical distinction**: COD = physical capacity only; Agility = physical + perceptual-cognitive components.

## Physics of Sprinting, COD, and Agility

### Rate of Force Development (RFD)
- Most sport actions occur in 0-200 ms; maximal force takes ≥300 ms
- RFD (change in force / change in time) is more important than maximal force for sprinting
- Heavy resistance training + explosive-ballistic training both improve RFD

### Impulse
- Product of force × time; area under force–time curve
- Change in impulse = change in momentum = cause of movement
- **Net impulse** dictates magnitude of velocity change (impulse–momentum relationship)

### Practical implications:
- Sprinting: Produce forces rapidly with time constraints shorter than maximal force production
- COD/Agility: Must produce braking forces (braking impulse) to decelerate; greater COD angle and entry velocity = greater impulse required

## Running Speed

**Sprint speed = step length × step frequency**

- Elite sprinters: 2.70 m step length, 4.63 steps/s; velocity 11.0-12.5 m/s
- Team sport athletes: ~8.0-9.5 m/s
- Elite sprinters produce most vertical force in first half of stance phase — deviates from classic spring-mass model
- Elite vs. novice: longer step length from properly directed vertical forces, similar swing-leg repositioning time

### Spring-Mass Model (SMM)
- Leg acts as spring during running gait
- Muscle stiffness and tendon compliance regulated by SSC
- Compression begins at foot strike → midstance compression → push-off extension
- SMM describes non-elite sprinters well; elite sprinters deviate from classic SMM

## Sprinting Phases and Technique

### Acceleration Phase
- Forward body lean, gradually reducing to upright
- Low heel recovery; piston-like foot action
- Center of mass ahead of foot in early steps
- Forward-oriented shin angles become more vertical

### Maximum Velocity Phase
- Slight forward lean, neutral pelvis
- Arms: hand driven below waist on downstroke, return to chin height (~90° elbow)
- Legs: high knee position, negative foot speed before touchdown
- Foot touches down close to under COM
- Minimal knee and ankle flexion during support

### Technical errors (common):
- Overstriding
- Arms crossing midline
- Insufficient hip extension
- Excessive back-side mechanics

## COD and Agility Mechanics

### Plant Phase
- COD <45°: typically fast SSC activity
- COD >45°: typically slower SSC activity (longer ground contact for braking impulse)
- COD ground contact: ~0.15-0.6 s (vs. sprint acceleration 0.17-0.2 s, max velocity 0.09-0.11 s)
- Agility ground contact: ~0.23-0.25 s

### Neurophysiological considerations
- Eccentric strength training is specific to contraction velocity
- Agility requires perceptual-cognitive abilities: visual scanning, anticipation, decision making, reaction time
- Offensive vs. defensive situations change brain processing strategy

## Methods of Developing Speed

- **Sprint technique training**: Resisted sprinting (sled towing), assisted sprinting (downhill/overspeed), form drills
- **Strength training**: Heavy resistance for force production; ballistic exercises for RFD
- **Plyometric training**: Develops SSC function and reactive strength
- **Mobility**: Ensures optimal force application angles

## Methods of Developing Agility

- **COD drills**: Pre-planned direction changes at varying angles
- **Perceptual-cognitive training**: Reactive drills with visual/auditory stimuli; small-sided games; video-based decision making
- **Eccentric strength**: For braking and deceleration capabilities

## Program Design

- Speed sessions require full recovery (work:rest ≥ 1:5)
- Prioritize quality over volume
- Integrate with resistance training phases
- Undulating speed focus within weekly microcycles
- Monitoring via timing gates, GPS, and subjective readiness
