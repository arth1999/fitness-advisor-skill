# Ch23: Rehabilitation, Reconditioning, and Medical Issues

## Five Principles of Rehabilitation and Reconditioning

1. Healing tissues must not be overstressed
2. Athlete must fulfill specific criteria to progress from one phase to another
3. Program must be based on current clinical and scientific research
4. Program must be adaptable to each individual's specific requirements and goals
5. Rehabilitation is team-oriented — all allied health team members work together

## Allied Health Team

| Member | Role |
|--------|------|
| **Team physician** (MD/DO) | Medical care, diagnosis, medication, final return-to-play decision |
| **Athletic trainer** (ATC) | Day-to-day physical health; injury evaluation, rehabilitation, prevention |
| **Physical therapist** (SCS/OCS) | Pain reduction, function restoration, long-term rehab strategies |
| **Strength & conditioning professional** (CSCS) | Reconditioning program design; exercise technique and biomechanics expertise |
| **Exercise physiologist** | Conditioning design based on metabolic responses to exercise |
| **Nutritionist/RD** | Nutrition for tissue recovery |
| **Sport psychologist** | Mental stress coping strategies |

**Communication is essential**: Weekly meetings. Use referral forms with indications (required treatment) and contraindications (prohibited activities).

## Types of Injury

### Macrotrauma (specific, sudden overload)
- **Bone**: Contusion or fracture
- **Joint**: Dislocation (complete displacement) or subluxation (partial)
- **Ligament**: Sprain (Grade I: partial tear, no instability; Grade II: partial tear, minor instability; Grade III: complete tear, full instability)
- **Muscle**: Contusion (direct trauma) or strain (indirect; Grade I: painful but strong; Grade II: partial tear, weak/painful; Grade III: complete tear, painless/weak)
- **Tendon**: Rupture

### Microtrauma (overuse injury)
- **Bone**: Stress fracture
- **Tendon**: Tendinitis (inflammatory) → Tendinopathy (degenerative, minimal inflammation)
- Causes: Training errors, suboptimal surfaces, faulty biomechanics, insufficient motor control, decreased flexibility, skeletal malalignment

## Tissue Healing Phases (Table 23.1)

### Phase 1: Inflammatory Response Phase
- Timeline: ~72 hours post-injury (longer with compromised blood supply)
- Characteristics: Pain, swelling, redness; decreased collagen synthesis; increased inflammatory cells
- **Treatment goal**: Prevent disruption of new tissue; use POLICE (Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation) rather than PRICE
- **Exercise**: No active exercise for injured area; maintain function of cardiorespiratory system and uninjured areas
- **Ice/cryotherapy**: Recommended in acute inflammatory stage as part of POLICE; 15 min applications; cold-water immersion (<59°F/<15°C) for larger areas
- **Note**: Routine cryotherapy beyond acute phase not recommended — may impair protein synthesis

### Phase 2: Fibroblastic Repair Phase
- Timeline: Begins ~72 hours; lasts up to ~2 months
- Characteristics: Collagen fiber production (type III); decreased collagen organization; decreased inflammatory cells; scar tissue formation
- **Treatment goal**: Prevent excessive atrophy and joint deterioration; avoid disruption of new collagen fibers while introducing low-load stresses
- **Exercise**: Submaximal isometric (pain-free); isokinetic (continuous passive motion); isotonic (concentric/eccentric, slow speed initially); neuromuscular control training (unstable surfaces, eyes closed, varied speeds)
- **Mechanotransduction**: Eccentric exercise promotes healing via mechanical message → cellular DNA upregulation → collagen synthesis

### Phase 3: Maturation–Remodeling Phase
- Timeline: Begins ~few weeks; lasts months to years
- Characteristics: Proper collagen fiber alignment (type I); increased tissue strength; collagen aligns along lines of stress
- **Treatment goal**: Optimize tissue function; transition to return-to-play
- **Exercise**: Joint angle-specific strengthening; velocity-specific muscle activity; closed kinetic chain → open kinetic chain; sport-specific exercises; progressive plyometrics
- **Return-to-play criteria**: Range of motion, strength (≤10% side-to-side difference acceptable), functional testing, patient-reported outcomes

**Critical warning**: Pain levels often decrease well before tissue healing is complete (Figure 23.3). Do not use pain as sole guide for return to play.

## Program Design for Rehabilitation

### Resistance Training Protocols

| Protocol | Design | Progression |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **DeLorme** | 3 × 10: 50%, 75%, 100% 10RM | Light → Heavy (built-in warm-up) |
| **Oxford** | 3 × 10: 100%, 75%, 50% 10RM | Heavy → Light (needs non-fatiguing warm-up) |
| **DAPRE (Knight)** | 4 sets: 50% 6RM × 10, 75% 6RM × 6, 100% 6RM × max, adjusted 4th set | Adjustment based on set 3 reps (Table 23.2) |

**SAID principle applies**: Program design matches sport demands. Marathon runner: 2-3 × 15-20 reps at ≤50% → 50-75% 1RM. Weightlifter: 4-5 × 3-8 reps at ≥75% 1RM in remodeling phase.

### Key program design concepts:
- **Closed kinetic chain** (distal segment fixed, e.g., squat): Increased joint stability, functional movement patterns
- **Open kinetic chain** (distal segment free, e.g., leg extension): Isolated muscle/joint focus
- Both types occur simultaneously in sport; both have a place in rehab
- Train uninvolved limb → cross-education effect improves involved limb strength

## Reducing Risk of Injury and Reinjury

- **Previous injury** is the single most substantial risk factor for future injury
- Upper extremity: Strengthen external rotators; sleeper stretch for internal rotation; Throwers Ten program
- Lower extremity: Neuromuscular control during landing/cutting; proper plyometric technique; eccentric hamstring exercises dramatically reduce hamstring injury risk
- Structured programs: Sportsmetrics, PEP (Prevent Injury and Enhance Performance)

## Medical Conditions to Recognize

### Rhabdomyolysis
- Skeletal muscle breakdown → myoglobin release → kidney damage risk
- Symptoms: Severe muscle pain/weakness, dark urine (cola-colored), swelling, fatigue, nausea
- **Action**: Immediately discontinue activity, lie client down, hydrate, monitor, refer to medical professional

### Sickle Cell Trait
- Recognize sickling collapse vs. heat collapse

### Temperature-Induced Illnesses
- Heat exhaustion, heat stroke, hypothermia
