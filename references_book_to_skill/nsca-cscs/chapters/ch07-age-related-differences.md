# Ch7: Age-Related Differences — Youth and Older Adults

## Key Definitions

- **Childhood**: Before development of secondary sex characteristics
- **Adolescence**: Between childhood and adulthood
- **Youth/Young athlete**: Both children and adolescents
- **Older/Senior**: Men and women >65 years
- **Athleticism**: Ability to repeatedly execute a variety of movements with precision and confidence requiring motor skill competency, balance, coordination, strength, power, speed, agility, and endurance
- **Long-term athletic development**: Development of athleticism over time to enhance fitness, reduce injury risk, and improve health and well-being

---

## Youth Populations

### Growth, Maturation, and Development

- **Growth**: Increase in body size
- **Development**: Natural progression from prenatal to adulthood
- **Maturation**: Progress toward mature state; timing, tempo, and magnitude vary between systems
- **Chronological age vs. biological age**: Two children of same age can differ biologically by up to 5 years
- **Peak height velocity (PHV)**: Maximum rate of growth; mean ~13.8 yr boys, ~11.9 yr girls
- **PHV occurs at ~91-92% of predicted adult height**
- Risk of injury elevated around PHV, especially with high growth rates
- **Gold standard for maturation**: Skeletal age (X-ray); practical alternative: somatic assessment (% of predicted adult height)

### Youth Physical Development (YPD) Model (Figure 7.1)

All fitness components are trainable at all stages. Key principles:
- Consistent focus on **muscle strength + motor skill competency** as foundations
- Begin with low structure/movement exploration → structured/targeted as children develop
- Training adaptations: predominantly neural pre-puberty → neural + hormonal post-puberty
- **Hypertrophy training emphasis begins around PHV** (adolescence), not pre-adolescence
- Speed and power trainable throughout, with increased emphasis in adolescence

### Responsiveness to Resistance Training

**Strength gains:**
- Untrained preadolescents: ~30-40% strength increase in 8-20 weeks
- Adolescents can attain gains ~50% greater than children (absolute)
- Programs >23 weeks produce larger gains; >80% 1RM produces larger gains
- Pre-puberty: **Neural adaptations primary** (motor unit activation, synchronization, firing frequency) — not hypertrophy (low circulating androgens: 20-60 ng/100 mL testosterone)
- Post-puberty: **Hypertrophy + neural adaptations** (testosterone surges to ~600 ng/mL in males)
- Emerging evidence: some muscle architecture changes possible in prepubertal children with long-term (>10 month) training

**Combined training (strength + plyometrics) > strength training alone**

**Detraining**: Training-induced gains in children are impermanent; tend to return to untrained control values during detraining. 1×/week retains ~67% of 2×/week strength gains.

### Program Design for Youth

**Key guidelines:**
- Begin at level commensurate with physical/technical abilities, maturity, and goals
- **Children are not miniature adults** — do not superimpose adult programs
- Underestimate abilities; gradually increase volume and intensity
- 2-3 nonconsecutive sessions/week
- Assess maturity status for individualized programming
- Focus on technique over load; use grading of technical performance
- **Athletic Motor Skill Competencies (AMSC)** framework (Figure 7.4): foundational movements underpinning all athletic movements
- Periodized program throughout the year
- During rapid growth (around PHV): reinforce quality movement, target flexibility restrictions, correct imbalances, possibly reduce volume/intensity
- Complaints of pain during growth spurt → consider overuse injury (not "growing pains"); refer to medical practitioner

**Dispelled Myths:**
- Resistance training does NOT stunt bone growth (it stimulates bone remodeling)
- Resistance training IS safe with proper technique, loading, and instruction
- Resistance training benefits ALL youth, not just young athletes
- Children do NOT need to wait until puberty to benefit
- Girls do NOT become "bulky" (low androgens)
- 1RM testing IS safe in children and adolescents with proper guidelines

**Early sport specialization risks**: Higher injury rates, overuse injuries, burnout. Sampling diverse sports = better long-term development.

## Older Adults

### Age-Related Changes

| Variable | Effect of Aging | Effect of Resistance Training |
|----------|----------------|------------------------------|
| Muscular strength | ↓ | ↑ |
| Muscular power | ↓ (faster than strength) | ↑ |
| Muscular endurance | ↓ | ↑ |
| Muscle mass (sarcopenia) | ↓ | ↑ |
| Muscle fiber size | ↓ (type II selective denervation) | ↑ |
| Metabolic capacity | ↓ | ↑ |
| Resting metabolic rate | ↓ | ↑ |
| Body fat | ↑ | ↓ |
| Bone mineral density | ↓ (osteopenia/osteoporosis) | ↑ |
| Physical function | ↓ | ↑ |

- **Sarcopenia**: Progressive, generalized skeletal muscle disorder → falls, fractures, disability, mortality
- After 30: ↓ cross-sectional area, ↓ muscle density, ↓ tendon compliance, ↑ intramuscular fat
- 40% of women 55-64, 45% 65-74, 65% 75-84 unable to lift 10 lb (4.5 kg)
- Power recedes faster than strength with aging

### Neuromuscular Changes
- Seniors rely on increased muscle cocontraction to compensate for balance difficulties
- Reduced mechanical power during push-off phase of walking
- **Resistance training alone does NOT prevent falls** — must include balance and flexibility training
- Multidimensional, periodized programs needed: resistance + balance + aerobic + flexibility

### Responsiveness to Resistance Training
- Previously sedentary older men: doubled knee extensor, tripled knee flexor strength in 12 weeks
- Very old (87-96 yr): significant strength improvements in 8 weeks
- High-velocity power training may have greater influence on explosive force than traditional RT
- Dose-response relationship: higher intensity = greater strength and power improvements
- **Bone**: ≥2 sessions/week high-velocity RT increases BMD; adaptations lost if stopped >6 months
- **Detraining**: Partial preservation at 12 weeks; recoverable with retraining
- **Nutrition**: Adequate protein essential for hypertrophy; dietary modifications + RT > RT alone

### Program Design for Older Adults

- Pre-screen with medical history and risk factor questionnaire
- **Avoid Valsalva maneuver** (sudden BP spike; contraindicated in coronary disease, hypertension)
- Start: 1 set × 8-12 reps at 40-50% 1RM. Progress to: 2-3 sets × 6-12 reps at 70-85% 1RM, 2-3×/week
- Power training: 1-3 sets × 6-10 reps at 40-60% 1RM with high concentric velocity
- 48-72 hours recovery between sessions
- Early phase emphasis: education, building confidence, proper technique, minimizing soreness
- Progress from machines → free weights → advanced balance/plyometric exercises
- Home-based training can provide meaningful adaptation; supervised is preferable
- **Motivators**: Social support, qualified professionals, improving daily activities, decreasing fear of falls

**Dispelled Fears**:
- RT is safe with individualized, technique-focused programming
- RT can be modified for cardiovascular/musculoskeletal contraindications
- Gains achievable with ≤60 min/session, 2 days/week; "exercise snacking" (<10 min bouts) works
- Home-based RT effective
- RT increases strength without necessarily adding significant bulk
