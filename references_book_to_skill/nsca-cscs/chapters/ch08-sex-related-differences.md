# Ch8: Sex-Related Differences and Implications for Resistance Training

## Key Physical Differences (Adult)

| Characteristic | Females vs. Males |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Body fat | Higher |
| Muscle mass | Lower |
| Bone mineral density | Lower |
| Total body weight | Lighter |
| Shoulder-to-hip ratio | Narrower shoulders, broader hips |
| Maturation timing | Earlier (complete ~age 18) |
| Growth plates | Close by ~age 20 |
| Peak bone density | ~age 30 |

Decline of muscle mass and bone density begins in mid-30s without sustained RT; accelerates after ~age 50 (menopause).

## Training Adaptations in Females

### Musculoskeletal
- Similar **relative** hypertrophy gains (up to 16 weeks) to males when sophisticated measurement techniques used
- Absolute hypertrophy less than males due to lower testosterone
- Higher intensity RT → greater osteogenesis
- Preadolescence = opportune time for bone-enhancing activities
- **Primary RT goal for females**: Maintain muscle mass and bone mineral density

### Metabolic
- Males: slightly higher proportion of type II fibers → greater glycolytic capacity
- Females: greater proportion of type I fibers → greater fat oxidation, less carbohydrate/protein oxidation
- Female endurance athletes more fatigue-resistant in prolonged events; ~33% greater mitochondrial respiration
- Better running economy across velocities despite lower VO₂max

### Endocrine
- **Testosterone**: Much lower in females but still important for absolute muscle development; high variability in females explains variable RT responsiveness; chronic training may produce acute testosterone elevations
- **Growth hormone**: Higher resting GH in females; similar exercise-induced rises; decreases with chronic training (possibly greater sensitivity)
- **Cortisol**: Attenuated exercise response vs. males; higher cortisol sensitivity; female sex hormones modulate sympathetic/adrenal/HPA axis activity

### Menstrual Cycle Considerations
- **Estradiol**: Protective effect on skeletal muscle (antioxidant, membrane stabilizer); promotes protein synthesis over degradation; high levels may decrease power/performance and increase ligament injury risk; low levels → increased muscle cell death, reduced mass/strength
- **Progesterone**: Rises in second half of cycle
- Symptoms (cramping, nausea, headaches) may negatively affect training; individualize programming

## Injury and Health Considerations

### ACL Injury
- Female athletes: 2× higher relative rate in comparable sports
- Most significant factor: **neuromuscular deficiency** leading to dynamic knee valgus
- Most injuries from noncontact mechanisms (deceleration, lateral pivoting, landing)
- Reduction strategy: progressive overload RT, plyometrics, agility, balance training BEFORE puberty and across lifespan

### Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (PFPS)
- Females >2× more likely; hip mechanics key contributor pre-puberty
- Focus on lower extremity biomechanics, exercise technique, imbalances

### Bone Stress Injuries
- Higher rate in female athletes; related to RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)
- Monitor workload, bone health, vitamin D; prescribe osteogenically stimulating exercises

### Concussions
- Higher incidence in some sports (soccer, basketball, softball); longer recovery with prolonged symptoms
- Individualized return-to-play based on clinical presentation

### Mental Health
- Female athletes higher risk for burnout, eating disorders, anxiety, depression
- Elite female athletes 2× more likely to self-report depression
- 40% of collegiate female athletes report overwhelming anxiety
- Early detection critical

### Sleep
- Female collegiate athletes especially at risk for sleep deprivation (<7 h/night)
- Sleep education improves sleep quality and recovery

### Pelvic Floor Dysfunction (PFD)
- Urinary incontinence (UI): 5× more than male athletes, 2.7× more than sedentary females
- 27% prevalence in female athletes for UI (36% for SUI)
- High-impact/high-loading sports increase SUI incidence
- Prevention: pelvic floor strengthening, proper engagement technique

## Pregnancy and Resistance Training

### General Guidelines (Table 8.2):
- Continue activity at prepregnancy levels in first trimester; monitor for adverse signs
- Use combined heart rate + RPE to monitor intensity
- Initiate pelvic floor exercises immediately
- Second trimester: **Avoid supine positions and exercises** (decreased venous return); switch to incline, side-lying, or vertical
- Third trimester: Modify based on comfort; continue to avoid supine positions
- Postpartum: Pelvic floor/core stabilization exercises for 12-24 weeks minimum; gradual return to activity; longer recovery with Cesarean

### Key physiological changes across trimesters (Table 8.1):
- Cardiac output ↑20% (1st), ↑40% (2nd)
- Blood volume ↑50% by third trimester (34 weeks)
- Changes in center of gravity, increased joint laxity
- Most changes return to prepregnancy 1-2 weeks postpartum; ligament laxity may persist up to 5 months

## Recommendations for Female Athletes

1. Year-round, periodized conditioning including RT, plyometrics, speed/agility, flexibility
2. Teach and ensure correct movement mechanics (jumping, landing, twisting, cutting)
3. General + specific dynamic warm-up before every session
4. Preparticipation screening by sports medicine physician
5. Augmented feedback within training to optimize biomechanics and skill transfer
6. Mental health resources and sleep education
7. PFD education and referral to specialized physical therapist when needed
