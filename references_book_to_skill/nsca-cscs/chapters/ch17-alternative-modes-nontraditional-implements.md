# Ch17: Exercise Technique for Alternative Modes and Nontraditional Implement Training

## General Guidelines

Same as traditional RT: stable body position, correct grip, proper breathing. With structural exercises (>80% MVC or lighter loads to failure), the Valsalva maneuver may be unavoidable and beneficial for spinal stability.

## Bodyweight Training Methods

- Uses body weight as resistance; includes push-ups, pull-ups, sit-ups, calisthenics, gymnastics, yoga
- Limited to body weight → does not significantly affect absolute strength
- Increasing intensity: increase reps (shifts to endurance), or change movement pattern (e.g., elevated-leg push-up)
- **Benefits**: Specific to anthropometrics, closed-chain, develops relative strength, improves body control, versatile, low cost

### Suspension Training (e.g., TRX)
- Resistance dictated by strap angle; closer to anchor = higher resistance
- Example: inverted row at 75° from ground = 79.4% BW; at 30° = 37.4% BW
- Limited to body weight unless weighted vest used
- **Benefits**: Wide exercise range, portable, minimal storage, adjustable difficulty, challenges proprioception

## Core Stability and Balance Training

### Anatomical Core Definition
Axial skeleton + all soft tissues with proximal attachments originating on axial skeleton. Includes pelvic and shoulder girdles.

### Key Finding: Ground-Based Free Weights > Isolation Core Exercises
- Ground-based free weight exercises (squat, deadlift, push press, snatch, trunk rotation exercises) offer **similar or greater core muscle activation** compared to traditional isolation core exercises
- Core isolation exercises most beneficial for **injured athletes in rehabilitation** who cannot adequately load free weight exercises
- Isolated core training shows limited transfer to sport performance in healthy athletes

### Machines vs. Free Weights
- Machine training: better for isolated muscle targeting (hypertrophy); but stabilizer activation ~30% lower (e.g., Smith machine squat vs. free weight squat)
- Machine-based strength gains may have negligible or detrimental effect on athletic movement patterns
- **Free weight ground-based exercises = ideal combination of specificity and instability for strength/power development**

### Instability Devices (Swiss balls, wobble boards, foam pads)
- Increase core muscle activation BUT **reduce agonist force production** (<70% of stable conditions) and reduce RFD and power output
- Muted performance improvements vs. stable training in athletes; lack of dynamic correspondence to sport
- **Best use**: Rehabilitation setting (reduces low back pain, improves knee/ankle stabilization) and introductory training before progressing to ground-based free weights
- Tilt/roller board perturbation training → 5× more likely successful return to sport post-ACL
- **Not recommended** as primary training method for healthy athletes

## Variable-Resistance Training Methods

### Rationale
Constant external loads don't match changing mechanical advantages throughout ROM. Variable resistance attempts to maximize force application across full ROM.

### Chain-Supplemented Exercises
- Provides linear increase in resistance as chains unfurl from floor
- **Reserve for experienced intermediate/elite athletes** with stable technique
- Two application methods:
  1. Chains touch floor from fully extended position
  2. Chains suspended, only touch floor at bottom of movement (preferred)
- Method 2 benefits: reduced load at bottom allows faster acceleration; possible within-rep postactivation potentiation; more rapid SSC due to eccentric unloading

**Chain resistance calculation**: Sum absolute chain resistance at top + bottom positions, divide by 2; subtract average from barbell load.

### Resistance Band Exercises
- Tension = Stiffness × Deformation (Hooke's law); both curvilinear and linear deformation regions
- 3.2-5.2% variation between supposedly equal bands → 8-19% difference in tension
- May substitute ~35% of total load to increase peak power (~13%); 20% band load may increase concentric RFD
- **Band load calculation**: Same as chains — average top + bottom resistance, subtract from target load

### Application of Accentuated Eccentric Loading (AEL)
- Muscles produce up to 50% more force eccentrically vs. concentrically
- Constant loads = submaximal eccentric loading → reduced motor unit recruitment, reduced stimulus for adaptations
- AEL prescribes greater load during eccentric phase, reduced load during concentric phase
- Targets hypertrophic and maximal strength adaptations

## Nontraditional Implement Training

Includes: kettlebells, sandbags, logs, tires, heavy ropes, water-filled implements, strongman implements

Key principles:
- Often involve offset/uneven loading
- Greater stabilization demands than barbells
- Grip often the limiting factor
- Develops functional strength through unconventional movement patterns
- Useful for variety and addressing training monotony
