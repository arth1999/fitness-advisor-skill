# Ch22: Periodization

## Definition

Periodization = the logical and systematic process of sequencing and integrating training interventions to manage the training process and increase the potential of achieving peak performance at appropriate time points.

**Periodization ≠ Programming**: Periodization = macro-management (scaffold, direction); Programming = micro-management (modes, methods, specific loads).

## Central Concepts

### General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)
Three-stage stress response (Selye):
1. **Alarm phase**: Accumulation of fatigue, soreness, stiffness, reduced performance
2. **Resistance phase**: Adaptation to stimulus; supercompensation beyond baseline
3. **Exhaustion phase**: Inability to adapt → overreaching/overtraining. **Avoid this.**

Psychological/emotional stress increases fatigue magnitude and lengthens recovery.

### Stimulus–Fatigue–Recovery–Adaptation Theory
Greater workload → more fatigue → longer recovery before adaptation. Recovery does not always need to be complete before next session (light/heavy day modulation). No new stimulus = involution/detraining.

### Fitness–Fatigue Paradigm
Training creates both fitness and fatigue aftereffects that summate to preparedness. **Fatigue dissipates at a rate two-thirds faster than fitness.** Varying training loads allows fatigue to drop while retaining fitness → elevated preparedness.

## Three Models of Periodization (Figure 22.5)

### 1. Parallel Training Model
- All training factors targeted simultaneously in each session/day/microcycle
- Also known as "complex-parallel" or "concurrent" approach
- Best for: **Youth athletes, novices, developmental athletes**
- Concern: Total training load must continually increase; may exceed tolerance in advanced athletes

### 2. Sequential Training Model
- One or limited number of training factors organized in logical pattern toward targeted outcome
- Addresses limitations of parallel model
- Best for: **Intermediate/advanced athletes** needing greater training stimulus
- Used successfully in weightlifting, XC skiing, cycling, track & field, team sports

### 3. Emphasis Training Model
- Contains aspects of both parallel and sequential models
- Multiple training factors targeted simultaneously with varying degrees of emphasis
- Emphasis rotated every ~2 weeks
- Some factors: adaptation loads; others: maintenance loads; others: minimal dose
- Best for: **Intermediate/advanced athletes with congested competitive schedules** (team sports, tactical athletes)

## Periodization Periods (Traditional Model — Stone, O'Bryant, Garhammer)

### 1. Preparatory Period (Off-Season)

**General Preparatory Phase → Hypertrophy/Strength-Endurance Phase:**
- Intensity: Low to moderate (50-75% 1RM)
- Volume: High (3-6 sets × 8-20 reps)
- Goal: Increase work capacity, muscle mass, strength-endurance

**Specific Preparatory Phase → Basic Strength Phase:**
- Intensity: High (80-95% 1RM)
- Volume: Moderate to high (2-6 sets × 2-6 reps)
- Goal: Increase strength of sport-specific muscles

### 2. First Transition Period (Preseason)

**Strength/Power Phase:**
- Intensity: Low to very high (30-95% 1RM, exercise-dependent)
- Volume: Low (2-5 sets × 2-5 reps)
- Goal: Elevate strength and translate to power; mixed training approach (heavy + light loads)
- Last week: reduced volume/intensity for recovery before competitive period

### 3. Competitive Period (In-Season)

**Peaking (1-2 weeks):**
- Intensity: Very high to very low (50% to ≥93% 1RM)
- Volume: Very low (1-3 sets × 1-3 reps)
- Can maintain peak performance for only 7-14 days

**Maintenance (long season, team sports):**
- Intensity: Moderate to high (85-93% 1RM)
- Volume: Low to moderate (~2-5 sets × 3-6 reps)
- Modulate microcycle by microcycle based on competition schedule
- Monitoring critical

### 4. Second Transition Period (Postseason — Active Rest)
- 1-4 weeks (max) of recreational activities at low intensity/volume
- Rehabilitate injuries; physical and mental refresh
- Prolonged >4 weeks → much longer preparatory period needed

## "Linear" vs. "Undulating" Periodization

**Key insight**: The traditional model is NOT linear — it contains nonlinear variation at microcycle and mesocycle levels. The term "linear periodization" creates a false narrative because removing linearity from training is a central tenet of periodization.

**Traditional model**: Same set × rep scheme across training days, vary load (e.g., 4×6 @ 85%, 75%, 65% across the week). Volume-load undulates naturally.

**Daily undulating model**: Different set × rep schemes AND loads each training day (e.g., 4×6RM, 3×10RM, 5×3RM). Research shows mixed results vs. traditional.

**Concern with undulating**: Higher volume-load sessions may increase accumulated fatigue, decrease preparedness, and elevate injury risk in higher-level athletes.

## Applying Seasons to Periodization (Figure 22.7)

- **Off-season** = Preparatory period; may do several hypertrophy/basic strength mesocycle rotations
- **Preseason** = First transition (strength/power); capitalize on off-season; NOT the time to build foundations
- **In-season** = Competitive period; 3-4 week mesocycle blocks with unloading microcycle before key contests
- **Postseason** = Second transition; active rest ≤4 weeks
