# SKILL: Brukner & Khan's Clinical Sports Medicine Vol 1 — Injuries

## Source
Brukner & Khan's Clinical Sports Medicine, Volume 1: Injuries, 5th Edition (2017).
Authors: Peter Brukner, Ben Clarsen, Jill Cook, Ann Cools, Kay Crossley, Mark Hutchinson, Paul McCrory, Roald Bahr, Karim Khan.

## What This Skill Covers
The definitive sports medicine textbook on diagnosis, management, and prevention of musculoskeletal sports injuries. Organised by anatomical region of symptom (not by diagnostic category), making it directly usable for clinical reasoning.

## Core Clinical Frameworks (from this book)
1. **Acute injury management**: POLICE (Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation) — NOT RICE
2. **Tendinopathy continuum**: Cook-Purdam model — Reactive → Tendon Dysrepair → Degenerative
3. **Bone stress injury (BSI) classification**: Low-risk vs high-risk anatomical sites + MRI grading (Fredericson 1–4)
4. **Return-to-play**: StARRT framework (Strategic Assessment of Risk and Risk Tolerance)
5. **Pain classification**: Input-dominated (nociceptive, neuropathic) vs centrally dominated (central sensitisation)
6. **LBP triage**: Serious pathology → Specific pathoanatomical → Non-specific
7. **Concussion**: SCAT, ChildSCAT, graduated RTP protocol, red flags for urgent imaging
8. **Injury prevention**: Risk identification → Surveillance → Risk profiling → Targeted prevention program
9. **Clinical decision rules**: Ottawa ankle rules, Ottawa knee rules
10. **Masquerading conditions**: Systematic approach to identifying non-musculoskeletal causes of pain

## Key Clinical Pearls
- Diagnose by tissue, not just by region — the same joint can have ligament, tendon, bone, cartilage, or nerve pathology
- Always screen for red flags and masquerading conditions before attributing symptoms to a sports injury
- The kinetic chain matters — dysfunction in one region often causes symptoms in another
- Rehabilitation is not linear — use the "winding road" model to set patient expectations
- Load management is the central principle in overuse injury prevention and treatment
- Pain is an output of the brain, not an input from tissues — treat the patient, not the scan
- Shared decision-making with athletes is essential — the clinician advises, the athlete decides
