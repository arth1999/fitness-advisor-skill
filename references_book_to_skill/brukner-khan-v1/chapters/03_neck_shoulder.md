# Neck Pain and Shoulder Pain (Chapters 23–24)

## Chapter 23: Neck Pain (Gwendolen Jull, Deborah Falla)

### Anatomical Considerations
- Cervical spine is the most mobile spinal region — vulnerable to both acute trauma and overuse
- Upper cervical (C0–C2): 50% of cervical rotation (C1–2), 50% of flexion-extension (C0–1)
- Lower cervical (C3–C7): remainder of movement, common site of degenerative change
- Close relationship with vertebral artery, spinal cord, nerve roots, brachial plexus
- Neck pain can refer to: head (cervicogenic headache), shoulder, arm, interscapular region, face

### Clinical Approach
**History**: mechanism, pain location and behaviour, headache, dizziness/vertigo, upper limb symptoms (paraesthesia, weakness), dysphagia, gait disturbance. Red flags: gait disturbance, upper motor neuron signs, drop attacks, vertebrobasilar insufficiency symptoms (5 Ds: dizziness, diplopia, drop attacks, dysarthria, dysphagia).

**Imaging** — indications for cervical spine imaging:
- Major trauma
- Neurological deficit (upper or lower limb)
- Suspected vertebral artery dissection (neck pain + neurological symptoms)
- Suspected fracture in at-risk patient (ankylosing spondylitis, osteoporosis, metastatic disease)
- Persistent pain not responding to conservative treatment

**Physical Examination**:
- Posture: forward head posture, shoulder position
- Active ROM: flexion, extension, rotation, lateral flexion
- Passive ROM: quality, end-feel, reproduction of symptoms
- Palpation: tenderness over facet joints, muscles (suboccipital, upper trapezius, levator scapulae, scalenes)
- Neurological screen: myotomes (C4–T1), dermatomes, deep tendon reflexes (biceps C5, brachioradialis C6, triceps C7)
- Special tests: Spurling's (foraminal compression — radiculopathy), vertebral artery test (pre-manipulation screen), upper limb tension tests (ULTT — neural mechanosensitivity)
- Craniocervical flexion test (deep neck flexor endurance and control)
- Sensorimotor assessment: joint position error (laser to target with eyes closed), balance, oculomotor control

### Management of Mechanical Neck Pain
1. **Education**: nature of condition, prognosis (generally good), self-management strategies
2. **Pain management**: manual therapy for short-term relief, exercise for long-term improvement
3. **Manual therapy**: mobilisation (passive oscillatory movements within/at end-range) and manipulation (high-velocity low-amplitude thrust)
   - Pre-manipulation screening: vertebral artery testing, instability testing (Sharp-Purser for atlantoaxial instability)
4. **Neural tissue mobilisation**: for patients with neural mechanosensitivity
5. **Training motor function**: deep neck flexor training (craniocervical flexion), endurance training (sustained loading), co-contraction training
6. **Training sensorimotor control**: joint position sense, balance, oculomotor exercises
7. **Maintenance program**: ongoing exercise to prevent recurrence

### Specific Neck Pain Conditions
- **Cervicogenic headache**: see Chapter 21. Management targets upper cervical dysfunction.
- **Acute wry neck (torticollis)**: sudden onset of neck pain and deformity. Usually resolves within days. Conservative management (analgesia, gentle movement, heat).
- **Cervical nerve injury ("burner/stinger")**: acute compression or traction of brachial plexus or cervical nerve roots. Common in contact sports (rugby, American football). Transient upper limb burning/weakness — resolves within minutes. Persistent symptoms >24 hours or bilateral symptoms require imaging.

## Chapter 24: Shoulder Pain (Ann Cools)

### Functional Anatomy and Biomechanics
- **Static stabilisers**: glenoid labrum, glenohumeral ligaments (superior, middle, inferior — especially anterior band of IGHL), joint capsule, coracohumeral ligament
- **Dynamic stabilisers**: rotator cuff (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis) — provides concavity compression
- **Scapular stabilisers**: trapezius (upper, middle, lower), serratus anterior, rhomboids, levator scapulae, pectoralis minor
- **Scapular role**: provides stable base for glenohumeral movement, maintains optimal glenoid position for humeral head containment, facilitates force transfer from lower limb and trunk to upper limb
- Scapulohumeral rhythm: 2:1 ratio (2° GH : 1° scapulothoracic) for first 30–60°, then 1:1

### Causes of Shoulder Pain — Overview
- Acute: fracture (clavicle, proximal humerus), dislocation (glenohumeral, AC joint), rotator cuff tear (acute in young, degenerative in older)
- Overuse: rotator cuff tendinopathy, biceps tendinopathy, SLAP lesions, GIRD, scapular dyskinesis, internal impingement (overhead athletes)

### Clinical Approach
**History**: acute vs gradual onset, mechanism (traumatic dislocation, throwing injury), location of pain, aggravating movements, instability symptoms (apprehension, subluxation, dead arm), night pain, hand dominance, sport/position, training history.

**Physical Examination**:
1. Inspection: posture, scapular position (at rest = "scapular dyskinesis"), muscle wasting (deltoid, supraspinatus, infraspinatus), deformity (AC joint, clavicle fracture, dislocated shoulder)
2. Active ROM: flexion, abduction, external rotation (arm at side and at 90° abduction), internal rotation (hand behind back — reach to spine level)
3. Passive ROM: assess capsular pattern (external rotation most limited, then abduction, then internal rotation)
4. Resisted movements: abduction (supraspinatus), external rotation (infraspinatus/teres minor), internal rotation (subscapularis), elbow flexion (biceps), Speed's test (biceps)
5. Special tests:
   - **Impingement**: Neer sign, Hawkins-Kennedy test, painful arc (60–120° abduction)
   - **Rotator cuff**: empty can/Jobe (supraspinatus), external rotation lag sign (infraspinatus), lift-off and belly press (subscapularis)
   - **Instability**: apprehension test (anterior), relocation test, anterior drawer, sulcus sign (inferior), jerk test (posterior), load and shift
   - **Labral**: O'Brien's active compression (SLAP), crank test
   - **AC joint**: cross-body adduction (scarf test), O'Brien's
   - **Biceps**: Speed's test, Yergason's test
6. **Symptom modification tests**: reassess painful movement after modifying the contributing factor (e.g., scapular repositioning, humeral head relocation)
7. **Scapular assessment**: Kibler classification of scapular dyskinesis (Type I — inferior angle prominence, Type II — medial border prominence, Type III — superior border prominence)
8. **Kinetic chain screening**: thoracic mobility, hip ROM (especially internal rotation), core stability, single-leg balance

### Key Outcome Measures
- DASH (Disabilities of Arm, Shoulder, Hand)
- SPADI (Shoulder Pain and Disability Index)
- ASES (American Shoulder and Elbow Surgeons) score
- Western Ontario Shoulder Instability Index (WOSI) — for instability

### Imaging
- X-ray: AP, lateral (scapular Y or axillary), outlet views. Essential after first dislocation to rule out fracture.
- Ultrasound: rotator cuff integrity, biceps tendon, dynamic assessment
- MRI: labral pathology, rotator cuff, bone marrow (stress)
- MR arthrogram: labral tears (SLAP, Bankart), capsular assessment
- CT: complex fractures, bone loss (glenoid, Hill-Sachs lesion)

### Specific Shoulder Conditions

#### Rotator Cuff Injuries
**Rotator cuff tendinopathy**: overuse in overhead athletes or middle-aged with degenerative change. Pain with overhead activities, night pain. Management: load modification, rotator cuff strengthening (external rotation, abduction), scapular stabilisation, address kinetic chain. Corticosteroid injections give short-term relief but may impair tendon healing — use judiciously.

**Rotator cuff tears**: partial-thickness vs full-thickness. Acute tears in young (traumatic) — consider surgical repair. Degenerative tears in older patients — trial of conservative management first (exercise, load modification). Red flags for surgical referral: acute traumatic full-thickness tear in young athlete, persistent symptoms despite 3–6 months conservative treatment, significant functional deficit.

#### Shoulder Instability — Classification
1. **TUBS** (Traumatic, Unidirectional, Bankart lesion, Surgery commonly needed):
   - Mechanism: traumatic dislocation (usually anterior-inferior from abduction + external rotation)
   - Pathology: Bankart lesion (anteroinferior labral tear), Hill-Sachs lesion (posterolateral humeral head compression fracture)
   - Management: acute reduction → immobilisation (3–6 weeks in internal rotation for young, shorter in older to avoid stiffness) → rehabilitation → consider surgery (arthroscopic Bankart repair) in young contact athletes
   
2. **AIOS** (Acquired Instability due to OverStress):
   - Sport-specific acquired instability in overhead athletes from repetitive microtrauma
   - No single traumatic event
   - Management: primarily rehabilitation — rotator cuff strengthening, scapular control, kinetic chain integration

3. **AMBRI** (Atraumatic, Multidirectional, Bilateral, Rehabilitation-appropriate, Inferior capsular shift if surgery):
   - Atraumatic onset, often bilateral, multidirectional laxity
   - Treatment: REHABILITATION FIRST (prolonged, 6–12 months). Surgery (inferior capsular shift) only if rehabilitation fails.

#### Biceps-Related Pathology and SLAP Lesions
- SLAP (Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior) tear at biceps anchor
- Common in overhead athletes (throwing, swimming)
- Diagnosis: history + O'Brien's test + MRI arthrogram
- Management: initially conservative (scapular rehabilitation, rotator cuff strengthening, kinetic chain). Surgery (SLAP repair or biceps tenodesis) for failed conservative treatment.

#### GIRD — Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit
- Pathological loss of internal rotation (>20° deficit compared to non-throwing side)
- Results from posterior capsule tightness → humeral head anterior-superior migration during throwing → internal impingement
- Treatment: posterior capsule stretching ("sleeper stretch"), cross-body adduction stretch
- Part of the thrower's comprehensive rehabilitation program

#### Scapular Dyskinesis
- Altered scapular position and motion
- Kibler classification: Type I, II, III
- **Scapular Rehabilitation Algorithm**:
  1. Scapular awareness and control (scapular orientation, setting exercises)
  2. Scapular muscle strengthening (trapezius — especially lower, serratus anterior)
  3. Integration into functional movement patterns (closed chain → open chain → sport-specific)
  4. Kinetic chain integration (lower limb → trunk → scapula → arm)

#### AC Joint Injuries
- Acute AC joint injury: Rockwood classification (I–VI). Type I–II: conservative. Type IV–VI: surgical.
- Chronic AC joint pain: OA, osteolysis of distal clavicle (weightlifters). Conservative first. Distal clavicle excision if persistent.

#### Special Considerations for the Overhead Athlete
- Kinetic chain integration: every throw starts from the ground — legs → trunk → scapula → shoulder → elbow → hand
- The Thrower's Program: comprehensive rehabilitation addressing all kinetic chain links
- Return to play criteria: full pain-free ROM, strength ≥90% of contralateral, normal scapular mechanics, normal kinetic chain function, completion of interval throwing program without pain

### Return to Play Following Shoulder Injury
- Sport-specific functional testing
- Graduated return to throwing (interval throwing program)
- Criteria-based (not time-based) progression
- Full kinetic chain function restored before RTP
