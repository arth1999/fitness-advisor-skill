# Leg, Calf, and Achilles Region Pain (Chapters 38–40)

## Chapter 38: Leg Pain

### Clinical Approach
Leg pain ("shin splints") requires precise diagnosis — multiple entities present similarly:
1. Bone stress injury (medial tibial stress fracture, anterior tibial stress fracture, fibular stress fracture)
2. Medial tibial stress syndrome (MTSS)
3. Chronic exertional compartment syndrome (CECS)
4. Nerve entrapments
5. Vascular entrapments
6. Referred pain

### Key Differentiating Features — History
| Feature | Bone Stress | MTSS | CECS |
|---------|-------------|------|------|
| Pain onset | Gradual, worsening | Gradual, diffuse | Predictable with exercise |
| Pain location | Focal (<5 cm) | Diffuse (>5 cm), posteromedial border | Variable by compartment |
| Pain timing | With activity, may persist after | With activity, eases with rest | Tightness/cramping with exercise, resolves quickly with rest |
| Night pain | May be present | No | No |
| Neurological symptoms | No | No | May have paraesthesia (deep posterior compartment) |
| Palpation | Focal bone tenderness | Diffuse periosteal tenderness | Usually non-tender at rest, compartments feel tight post-exercise |

### Specific Conditions

#### Medial Tibial Stress Fracture
- **Low-risk BSI** (posteromedial border = compression side)
- Focal bone tenderness (point tenderness), pain on percussion, pain with hopping (single-leg)
- MRI: bone marrow oedema ± fracture line
- Management: rest from impact activity (4–8 weeks typically), graduated return to running, address biomechanics (overpronation), address bone health (vitamin D, calcium, energy availability), gradual return to sport

#### Stress Fracture of the Anterior Cortex of the Tibia
- **HIGH-RISK BSI** (anterior cortex = tension side)
- Dreaded black line on X-ray (chronic non-union). Often seen in jumping athletes (basketball, dancers).
- Poor healing capacity — high rate of non-union
- Management: prolonged non-weight-bearing, electromagnetic stimulation, surgery (intramedullary nailing) often required for non-union
- Expect 6–12+ months until RTP

#### Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome (MTSS — "Shin Splints")
- Diffuse pain along posteromedial border of tibia
- Pathology: tibial periostitis ± bone stress reaction. NOT a stress fracture.
- Risk factors: overpronation, sudden increase in training load (running volume/surface change), female sex, higher BMI, previous MTSS
- Management: relative rest from impact activity, address contributing factors (foot orthoses for overpronation, load management, calf flexibility/strengthening), graduated return to running (pain-guided). MTSS can progress to stress fracture if not managed.
- Prevention: graduated training programs, appropriate footwear, address biomechanics

#### Chronic Exertional Compartment Syndrome (CECS)
- Exercise-induced increase in intracompartmental pressure → ischaemia and pain
- Most common in anterior and lateral compartments, also deep posterior
- Clinical features:
  - Predictable onset with a specific duration/intensity of exercise (e.g., always at 10 minutes of running)
  - Pain, tightness, "fullness" in affected compartment(s)
  - Anterior compartment: anterior shin pain. Deep posterior: deep calf pain, may have plantar paraesthesia (tibial nerve branches).
  - Symptoms resolve completely within minutes of stopping exercise
  - Examination at rest: often normal. Post-exercise: compartment feels tense/tight.
- Diagnosis: compartment pressure testing (pre-exercise, immediately post-exercise, and at intervals after). Diagnostic criteria: resting pressure >15 mmHg, post-exercise >30 mmHg, or delayed return to baseline.
- Management: conservative (load modification, gait retraining, shoe modification) often unsuccessful; surgical fasciotomy for confirmed CECS (high success rate, 80–90%). Anterior and lateral compartments accessible via single incision.

#### Other Causes of Leg Pain
- **Stress fracture of the fibula**: low-risk, conservative management. Focal tenderness over fibula.
- **Nerve entrapments**: superficial peroneal nerve (anterolateral leg), saphenous nerve (medial leg), common peroneal nerve (at fibular neck). Positive Tinel's.
- **Vascular entrapments**: popliteal artery entrapment (exertional leg/calf pain, diminished pulses with provocation)
- **Referred pain**: lumbar spine (L4–S1), SIJ
- **Periosteal contusion**: direct blow to shin → localised pain, normal imaging
- **Fractured tibia/fibula**: acute traumatic fracture (not stress). Deformity, inability to weight-bear. X-ray diagnosis.

## Chapter 39: Calf Pain

### Anatomy
- Superficial posterior compartment: gastrocnemius (medial and lateral heads), soleus, plantaris
- Deep posterior compartment: tibialis posterior, flexor digitorum longus, flexor hallucis longus
- Gastrocnemius: two-joint muscle (crosses knee and ankle) — more vulnerable to strain
- Soleus: one-joint muscle (crosses ankle only)
- Medial head of gastrocnemius is most commonly strained

### Specific Conditions

#### Gastrocnemius Muscle Strains
- **"Tennis leg"**: acute strain of medial head of gastrocnemius (myotendinous junction)
- Mechanism: sudden push-off (acceleration), jumping, or forced dorsiflexion with knee extended
- Sudden calf pain, may hear "pop" (but NOT always Achilles rupture — check Thompson test)
- Tenderness over medial gastrocnemius, pain with resisted plantarflexion and passive dorsiflexion (knee extended), swelling/bruising may appear days later
- Grading: I (few fibres, minimal function loss), II (partial tear, moderate function loss), III (complete tear/rupture — rare in gastrocnemius)
- Management: initial protection + optimal loading (heel raise, crutches if needed), progressive stretching and strengthening, grade I RTP 1–3 weeks, grade II RTP 3–8 weeks
- Must rule out DVT in calf pain with swelling

#### Soleus Muscle Strains
- Less common but more disabling than gastrocnemius strains
- More insidious onset, often in distance runners
- Pain deep to gastrocnemius, especially with knee flexed (isolates soleus from gastrocnemius)
- Longer RTP than gastrocnemius strains

#### Vascular Causes — Claudicant-Type Calf Pain
- **Intermittent claudication**: exertional calf pain that resolves with rest. Rare in young athletes — consider popliteal artery entrapment syndrome (PAES), cystic adventitial disease, or external iliac artery endofibrosis (in cyclists — high-volume cycling).
- **Popliteal artery entrapment syndrome (PAES)**: abnormal relationship between popliteal artery and medial head of gastrocnemius → compression during exercise → claudication. Diminished pedal pulses with active plantarflexion or passive dorsiflexion. Doppler/MR angiography for diagnosis. Surgical release.
- **Deep venous thrombosis (DVT)**: calf pain, swelling, warmth, tenderness. Risk factors: recent travel, immobilisation, OCP, previous DVT, thrombophilia. D-dimer + ultrasound. Anticoagulation.
- **Superficial thrombophlebitis**: tender, palpable, erythematous cord along superficial vein. Usually benign. Manage with NSAIDs, compression, continue mobilisation.

#### Nerve Entrapments
- **Sural nerve**: lateral calf/foot paraesthesia
- **Tibial nerve (tarsal tunnel syndrome)**: medial ankle/foot paraesthesia
- **Common peroneal nerve**: lateral leg/foot drop

#### Conditions NOT to Be Missed
- **Acute compartment syndrome**: severe pain (out of proportion), pain with passive stretch, tense compartment, neurological deficit. SURGICAL EMERGENCY — immediate fasciotomy. NOT to be confused with CECS.
- **Achilles tendon rupture**: sudden calf pain, "snap", positive Thompson test (see Chapter 40). Often misdiagnosed as calf strain.

## Chapter 40: Pain in the Achilles Region

### Clinical Perspective
Achilles pain is common, and multiple structures in the Achilles region can be the source:
1. Midportion Achilles tendinopathy (2–6 cm proximal to insertion)
2. Insertional Achilles tendinopathy (at calcaneal insertion)
3. Retrocalcaneal bursitis
4. Superficial calcaneal (Achilles) bursitis
5. Posterior ankle impingement
6. Sever's disease (calcaneal apophysitis in children)
7. Achilles tendon rupture

### Midportion Achilles Tendinopathy
- Most common form of Achilles tendinopathy
- Location: 2–6 cm proximal to calcaneal insertion (watershed zone — poor blood supply)
- Clinical features: morning stiffness, pain at start of activity (warm-up phenomenon), pain after activity, tenderness, thickened tendon, crepitus
- Contributing factors: sudden increase in training load (running, jumping), reduced ankle dorsiflexion, subtalar overpronation, poor footwear, obesity, age, male sex, fluoroquinolone antibiotics
- Pathology: Cook-Purdam model — reactive (acute overload, thickened tendon on imaging) → tendon dysrepair (collagen disorganisation, neovascularisation) → degenerative (advanced breakdown)
- **Imaging**:
  - Ultrasound: tendon thickness, hypoechoic areas, neovascularisation on power Doppler, partial tears
  - MRI: tendon thickening, intrasubstance signal, peritendinous oedema
- **Management**:
  1. **Load modification**: reduce running volume, avoid plyometrics. NOT complete rest — maintain loading within pain limits.
  2. **Exercise therapy** (cornerstone of treatment):
     - Eccentric heel drops (Alfredson protocol): 3 sets × 15 reps, twice daily, 12 weeks. Both knee straight (gastrocnemius) and knee bent (soleus). Go up on both feet, down on injured foot. Pain during exercise is acceptable (≤5/10 VAS).
     - Heavy slow resistance (HSR): alternative to eccentric-only. 3×/week, progressive loading.
     - Isometric exercise: analgesic effect for acute on chronic pain. 5 × 45-second holds.
  3. **Address kinetic chain**: ankle dorsiflexion mobility, hip strength, core stability
  4. **Footwear modification**: heel raise (reduces tendon load), appropriate shoes
  5. **Adjunct therapies** (limited/controversial evidence): ESWT, PRP injection, sclerosing injections, GTN patches, massage, needling
  6. **Surgery**: only for refractory cases after ≥6 months of quality conservative treatment. Options: open debridement, minimally invasive stripping, percutaneous tenotomy. Post-operative rehabilitation prolonged (6–12 months).

### Insertional Achilles Tendinopathy — The "Enthesis Organ"
- Pathology at the Achilles insertion on calcaneus, often with associated retrocalcaneal bursitis
- The "enthesis organ" concept: insertion involves tendon, bursa, fat pad, and bone as a functional unit
- Key difference from midportion: **compression** is the critical load (dorsiflexion compresses insertion against calcaneus)
- Management differs from midportion:
  - Eccentric heel drops must be done on a FLAT surface (NOT over a step — that increases compression and aggravates the condition)
  - Heel raise reduces dorsiflexion compression
  - Avoid deep dorsiflexion activities
  - Surgery (debridement + reattachment ± removal of Haglund's deformity) more commonly required than for midportion

### Retrocalcaneal Bursitis
- Inflammation of bursa between Achilles tendon and posterior-superior calcaneus (Haglund's deformity — prominent posterosuperior calcaneal tuberosity)
- Pain anterior to Achilles tendon, palpable tenderness on either side of tendon (not through the tendon itself)
- Aggravated by dorsiflexion
- Management: heel raise (reduces impingement), NSAIDs, corticosteroid injection into bursa (NOT into tendon), surgical excision of bursa ± calcaneal prominence if refractory

### Achilles (Superficial Calcaneal) Bursitis
- Inflammation of bursa between skin and Achilles tendon insertion
- Pain, swelling posterior to tendon (superficial)
- From friction (ill-fitting shoes)
- Management: footwear modification, padding

### Posterior Ankle Impingement
- Pain posterior to ankle, aggravated by forced plantarflexion (ballet — en pointe, soccer — kicking)
- Os trigonum (accessory ossicle) or prominent posterior talar process (Stieda process) can impinge
- Tenderness posterior to ankle (between Achilles and peroneal tendons), positive forced plantarflexion test
- Management: conservative (rest, physiotherapy). Surgical excision of os trigonum/Stieda process if refractory.

### Sever's Disease
- Calcaneal apophysitis in children (8–12 years, peak at 10–11)
- Heel pain during/after activity, especially running and jumping. Tenderness at calcaneal apophysis (squeeze test — pain on medial-lateral compression of posterior calcaneus).
- Self-limiting (resolves when apophysis fuses). Management: activity modification, heel raise, calf stretching, reassurance.

### Acute Achilles Tendon Rupture (Complete)
- Mechanism: sudden forced dorsiflexion or push-off. Often in middle-aged "weekend warrior" males.
- Clinical: sudden severe pain (often described as "being kicked in the calf" or feeling a "snap"), palpable gap, inability to stand on toes, positive Thompson test (squeezing calf does NOT produce plantarflexion — pathognomonic). Still able to weakly plantarflex using posterior tibial tendon and toe flexors.
- Imaging: clinical diagnosis in most cases. Ultrasound/MRI if diagnosis uncertain or to assess gap distance.
- **Management**:
  - **Surgical repair**: lower re-rupture rate (1–2% vs 8–10%), but surgical risks (wound infection, nerve injury). Stronger, earlier return. Recommended for young, active patients.
  - **Conservative (functional bracing)**: no surgical risks but higher re-rupture rate. Historically with casting, but modern functional bracing with early weight-bearing and controlled ROM produces similar outcomes to surgery. Suitable for older, less active patients.
  - Current evidence: functional rehabilitation (non-surgical) with early mobilisation can achieve outcomes comparable to surgery IF a functional protocol is used (not rigid casting). Shared decision making with patient.
- Rehabilitation: prolonged — 6–9 months until full RTP. Early weight-bearing, progressively increasing ROM and strengthening.

### Referred Pain to Achilles Region
- Lumbar spine (S1–2)
- Tibialis posterior tendinopathy (medial ankle pain)
- Peroneal tendinopathy (lateral ankle pain)
