# Elbow, Wrist, and Hand Injuries (Chapters 25–27)

## Chapter 25: Elbow and Arm Pain

### Key Conditions

#### Lateral Elbow Tendinopathy ("Tennis Elbow")
- Most common overuse injury of the elbow
- Pathology: tendinopathy of common extensor origin (primarily extensor carpi radialis brevis — ECRB), NOT "tendinitis"
- Mechanism: repetitive wrist extension, especially in backhand stroke in tennis (excessive wrist movement, poor technique)
- Clinical features: pain over lateral epicondyle, aggravated by gripping and resisted wrist extension (with elbow extended). Tenderness localised to ECRB origin (just anterior and distal to lateral epicondyle).
- Differential diagnosis: radial tunnel syndrome (posterior interosseous nerve entrapment — pain more distal in forearm, resisted supination + middle finger extension), referred pain from cervical spine (C5–6), radiocapitellar joint pathology.
- Imaging: not routinely required. Ultrasound may show tendon thickening, hypoechoic areas, neovascularisation.
- Management:
  1. Load modification (reduce aggravating activities)
  2. Counterforce bracing (reduces load on ECRB origin)
  3. Therapeutic exercise: progressive loading of wrist extensors (isometric → isotonic → eccentric, heavy slow resistance)
  4. Manual therapy: lateral glide mobilisation with pain-free grip (Mulligan MWM)
  5. Address contributing factors: grip size, racquet weight/balance, string tension, technique (reduce wrist snap)
  6. Corticosteroid injection: improves pain short-term but worse outcomes at 6–12 months compared to wait-and-see or exercise — NOT recommended as first-line
  7. PRP, autologous blood, prolotherapy: mixed evidence
  8. Surgery (ECRB release): only after failed 6–12 months of quality conservative treatment

#### Medial Elbow Pain
- Medial epicondyle apophysitis ("Little Leaguer's Elbow"): traction apophysitis in skeletally immature throwers. Pain over medial epicondyle. Management: rest from throwing until asymptomatic, address throwing mechanics, gradual return to throwing.
- Medial collateral ligament (MCL) sprain/tear: valgus overload in throwers. Pain over medial elbow, instability with valgus stress at 30° flexion. MRI if suspect tear. Conservative management for partial tears; surgical reconstruction (Tommy John surgery) for complete tears in elite throwers.
- Ulnar nerve entrapment (cubital tunnel syndrome): medial elbow pain + ulnar paraesthesia (ring and little fingers). Tinel's positive at cubital tunnel. Nerve conduction studies if persistent.
- Flexor-pronator tendinopathy ("Golfer's Elbow"): less common than lateral. Pain over medial epicondyle, aggravated by resisted wrist flexion and pronation.

#### Posterior Elbow Pain
- Olecranon bursitis: swelling over olecranon. Traumatic (haemorrhagic) vs inflammatory (gout, RA) vs infective (red, hot, tender — aspirate for microbiology). Conservative unless infected.
- Posterior impingement: olecranon tip and fossa impingement in throwing (valgus extension overload). Osteophytes at olecranon tip.
- Triceps tendinopathy: posterior elbow pain with resisted elbow extension.

#### Anterior Elbow Pain
- Distal biceps tendinopathy/rupture: anterior elbow pain with resisted elbow flexion and supination. Rupture: acute pop, ecchymosis, proximal retraction of biceps muscle, weakness. Surgical repair for complete rupture (acute).
- Anterior capsule strain: hyperextension injury.

#### Loose Bodies / Osteochondritis Dissecans (OCD)
- OCD of the capitellum: in young throwers/gymnasts. Lateral elbow pain, mechanical symptoms (locking, clicking). X-ray ± MRI. May require surgical fixation if unstable.

### Return to Play After Elbow Injury
- Pain-free clinical examination
- Full ROM
- Strength ≥90% contralateral
- Successful completion of interval throwing program (for throwers)
- Technical correction completed
- Adequate protection (bracing/taping if indicated)

## Chapter 26: Wrist Pain

### Clinical Approach by Anatomical Zone
- Radial wrist pain: de Quervain's tenosynovitis, scaphoid fracture/non-union, trapeziometacarpal (CMC) OA, intersection syndrome
- Central/dorsal wrist pain: dorsal wrist impingement (gymnasts), scapholunate ligament injury, Kienbock's disease (lunate avascular necrosis), ganglion
- Ulnar wrist pain: TFCC (triangular fibrocartilage complex) tear, extensor carpi ulnaris (ECU) instability/tendinopathy, ulnar impaction syndrome, hamate fracture (hook)
- Volar wrist pain: carpal tunnel syndrome, flexor carpi radialis tendinopathy

### Key Conditions

#### de Quervain's Tenosynovitis
- Stenosing tenosynovitis of first dorsal compartment (abductor pollicis longus and extensor pollicis brevis)
- Pain over radial styloid, aggravated by thumb movement and ulnar deviation
- Finkelstein's test: pain with thumb tucked in fist + ulnar deviation
- Common in racquet sports, golf, rowing
- Management: activity modification, thumb spica splint, NSAIDs, corticosteroid injection (high success rate), surgery (release of first dorsal compartment) if refractory

#### Scaphoid Fracture
- MOST COMMONLY MISSED wrist fracture — high risk of non-union and avascular necrosis (proximal pole)
- Mechanism: fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH)
- Clinical: anatomical snuffbox tenderness, pain with axial compression of thumb
- X-ray: scaphoid views (AP, lateral, scaphoid). Initial X-rays may be normal — treat as fracture if high clinical suspicion and repeat X-ray at 10–14 days, or immediate CT/MRI
- Management: undisplaced (cast immobilisation, 6–12 weeks), displaced or proximal pole (surgical fixation)
- Red flag: persistent snuffbox tenderness after "normal" X-ray = assume scaphoid fracture until proven otherwise

#### TFCC (Triangular Fibrocartilage Complex) Tear
- Ulnar-sided wrist pain, clicking
- Mechanism: forced ulnar deviation + rotation, or fall on pronated extended wrist
- Examination: ulnar fovea tenderness, piano key sign (DRUJ instability), ulnocarpal stress test
- MRI arthrogram for diagnosis
- Management: conservative (splinting, physiotherapy) for degenerative tears; surgical for traumatic tears in athletes, especially with DRUJ instability

#### Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
- Median nerve compression at carpal tunnel
- Paraesthesia in median nerve distribution (thumb, index, middle, radial half of ring finger)
- Night symptoms common, shaking hand provides relief
- Phalen's test, Tinel's at carpal tunnel, median nerve compression test
- Nerve conduction studies if persistent
- Management: night splint (neutral wrist position), activity modification, corticosteroid injection, surgical release if failed conservative

#### Dorsal Wrist Impingement (Gymnast's Wrist)
- Dorsal wrist pain from repetitive hyperextension loading (gymnastics, weightlifting)
- Tenderness over dorsal rim of distal radius, pain with passive wrist hyperextension
- X-ray may show dorsal rim osteophytes
- Management: rest from aggravating activity, strengthening, technique modification, surgical debridement if refractory

#### Ganglion
- Most common wrist mass
- Dorsal: arises from scapholunate ligament (60–70%); volar: from radioscaphoid or scaphotrapezial joint
- Usually asymptomatic but may cause pain, weakness, cosmetic concern
- Diagnosis: clinical (transillumination). Ultrasound/MRI if atypical.
- Management: reassure (50% resolve spontaneously), aspiration ± corticosteroid (high recurrence), surgical excision if symptomatic (still 10–30% recurrence)

## Chapter 27: Hand and Finger Injuries

### Functional Anatomy Principles
- Intrinsic muscles (interossei, lumbricals, thenar, hypothenar) + extrinsic tendons (flexor digitorum profundus/superficialis, extensor digitorum)
- Extensor mechanism: complex interplay of extrinsic extensor and intrinsic muscles
- Flexor tendon zones: zone II ("no man's land") has poorest prognosis for flexor tendon repair due to poor blood supply and adhesion risk

### Key Injuries

#### Mallet Finger
- Avulsion of terminal extensor tendon from distal phalanx — inability to actively extend DIP joint
- Mechanism: forced flexion of extended DIP joint (ball hitting fingertip)
- X-ray: check for bony avulsion fragment (>30% articular surface may require surgical fixation)
- Management: continuous splinting of DIP in full extension for 6–8 weeks (24/7, do NOT remove even for bathing). Then night splinting for additional 2–4 weeks.

#### Jersey Finger (Flexor Digitorum Profundus Avulsion)
- Avulsion of FDP tendon from distal phalanx — inability to actively flex DIP joint
- Mechanism: forced extension while gripping (grabbing a jersey)
- EMERGENCY REFERRAL — requires surgical repair within 7–10 days
- Most commonly ring finger

#### Boutonniere Deformity
- Disruption of central slip of extensor tendon at PIP joint
- PIP flexion + DIP hyperextension deformity
- Mechanism: forced flexion of PIP joint, or volar dislocation of PIP joint
- Management: PIP extension splinting (leave DIP free to flex) for 6 weeks

#### Volar Plate Injury
- Hyperextension injury of PIP joint → volar plate rupture ± avulsion fracture
- Pain, swelling, tenderness at volar aspect of PIP joint
- May have PIP hyperextension laxity
- Management: buddy taping to adjacent finger, early active ROM, avoid hyperextension for 4–6 weeks

#### Skier's Thumb (Gamekeeper's Thumb)
- Acute rupture of ulnar collateral ligament (UCL) of thumb MCP joint
- Mechanism: forced abduction of thumb (fall on outstretched hand with ski pole)
- Examination: valgus stress at MCP (compare to contralateral — >30° difference or lack of firm endpoint). Test in full extension AND 30° flexion (to isolate proper UCL from accessory).
- Stener lesion: adductor aponeurosis interposed between torn UCL and its insertion → prevents healing → SURGICAL REPAIR INDICATED
- X-ray: rule out avulsion fracture
- Management: incomplete tear without instability → thumb spica cast 4–6 weeks. Complete tear or Stener lesion → surgical repair.

#### Metacarpal Fractures
- "Boxer's fracture": fracture of 5th metacarpal neck (punching mechanism)
- Acceptable angulation: 5th MC neck up to 50–70° (depends on which metacarpal — 5th tolerates most angulation, 2nd/3rd tolerates least)
- Management: closed reduction + buddy taping + early mobilisation for most boxer's fractures (even with significant angulation). Surgical fixation if rotational deformity, significant shortening, or multiple metacarpal fractures.

#### PIP Joint Dislocation
- Dorsal dislocation most common (volar plate rupture, split in collateral ligaments)
- Reduce under digital block
- Post-reduction: active ROM within stable range, buddy taping. Avoid hyperextension.
- Volar dislocation: rare but central slip disruption → treat as boutonniere

#### Phalangeal Fractures
- Assess for rotational deformity (clinical — check nail alignment with finger partially flexed)
- Management: most stable fractures → buddy taping, early ROM. Unstable, displaced, intra-articular → surgical fixation.

#### Surgical Referrals Following Hand Injury (Red Flags)
- Any flexor tendon laceration
- Jersey finger (FDP avulsion)
- Boutonniere deformity with displaced bony fragment
- Skier's thumb with Stener lesion or complete UCL rupture
- Irreducible dislocation
- Open fractures
- Amputations
- Nerve injury (loss of sensation)
- Vascular compromise
- Intra-articular fractures with >1–2 mm step-off
