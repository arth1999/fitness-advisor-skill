# Knee Injuries (Chapters 35–37)

## Chapter 35: Acute Knee Injuries

### Clinical Approach — Key Question
**"Does this patient have a significant knee injury?"**

### History — Critical Elements
- **Mechanism**: contact vs non-contact, direction of force (valgus, varus, hyperextension, rotation), foot position, position of knee at time of injury
- **"Pop" sound**: strongly suggests ACL tear (but not 100% specific — also patellar dislocation, meniscal tear, fracture)
- **Immediate vs delayed swelling**: haemarthrosis (within 2 hours) = ACL tear, patellar dislocation, osteochondral fracture, peripheral meniscal tear. Reactive effusion (12–24 hours) = less severe injury, meniscal tear.
- **Ability to continue playing**: inability to continue suggests significant injury (but some athletes can continue with an ACL tear)
- **Giving way / instability**: suggests ligamentous injury
- **Locking**: bucket-handle meniscal tear (can't fully extend)
- **Previous knee injury**

### Physical Examination
1. **Inspection**: swelling (haemarthrosis vs effusion), deformity, bruising, muscle wasting
2. **Palpation**: temperature, tenderness (joint line — meniscal; collateral ligaments; patella; tibial plateau; head of fibula; Gerdy's tubercle)
3. **Range of motion**: active and passive. Loss of extension = mechanical block (bucket-handle meniscus, loose body, ACL stump)
4. **Ligament testing**:
   - Lachman test (ACL — most sensitive test, 30° flexion)
   - Anterior drawer (ACL — less sensitive than Lachman)
   - Pivot shift (ACL — specific but requires patient relaxation)
   - Posterior drawer (PCL)
   - Valgus stress at 0° and 30° (MCL)
   - Varus stress at 0° and 30° (LCL / posterolateral corner)
   - Dial test at 30° and 90° (posterolateral corner — >10° increased external rotation compared to contralateral)
5. **Meniscal tests**: joint line tenderness, McMurray test, Thessaly test (weight-bearing 5° and 20° flexion with rotation)
6. **Patellar assessment**: apprehension test (patellar instability)
7. **Extensor mechanism**: straight leg raise (can they actively extend the knee against gravity?)

### Immediate Referral Indications
- Inability to perform straight leg raise (extensor mechanism rupture — quadriceps or patellar tendon)
- Gross deformity (knee dislocation, fracture-dislocation)
- Vascular compromise (absent distal pulses — popliteal artery injury. EMERGENCY — knee dislocation can cause intimal tear even with normal pulses initially. Check ABI.)
- Neurological compromise (foot drop — peroneal nerve injury)

### Key Outcome Measures
- KOOS (Knee injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score)
- IKDC Subjective Knee Form
- Lysholm Knee Score
- Tegner Activity Scale

### Imaging — Ottawa Knee Rules
X-ray indicated if ANY of:
- Age ≥ 55 years
- Isolated tenderness of patella (no other bony tenderness)
- Tenderness at head of fibula
- Inability to flex knee to 90°
- Inability to weight-bear (4 steps) immediately AND in emergency department

### Specific Acute Knee Injuries

#### Meniscal Injuries
- Acute tears in young (traumatic — twisting with foot planted) vs degenerative tears in older (>40, minimal trauma)
- Medial meniscus more commonly injured than lateral
- Clinical: joint line tenderness, effusion, mechanical symptoms (clicking, locking — bucket-handle tear), positive McMurray/Thessaly
- MRI: meniscal tear (grade III signal communicating with articular surface on T2/STIR)
- Management:
  - Conservative: small, stable tears in vascular zone (red-red zone). Physiotherapy, progressive loading.
  - Surgical (arthroscopic): bucket-handle tears causing locking, large unstable tears in young athletes, tears causing persistent symptoms despite conservative care. Meniscal repair preferred over meniscectomy where possible (preserve meniscus → reduce OA risk). Repair only possible in peripheral (red-red or red-white) zone tears.
- Rehabilitation after meniscal surgery: varies by procedure (meniscectomy — accelerated; repair — protected weight-bearing, limited ROM initially)

#### MCL (Medial Collateral Ligament) Injury
- Most common knee ligament injury (valgus force)
- Grading: I (tenderness, no laxity), II (partial tear, some laxity, firm endpoint), III (complete tear, gross laxity, no endpoint)
- Isolated MCL injuries: conservative management (bracing, early ROM, progressive strengthening)
- Combined with ACL injury: MCL treated conservatively first, then ACL reconstruction once MCL healed (typically 4–6 weeks)
- RTP: Grade I (1–2 weeks), Grade II (2–4 weeks), Grade III (4–8 weeks)

#### ACL (Anterior Cruciate Ligament) Injury
- Mechanism: non-contact pivot (deceleration + change of direction with knee in valgus and internal rotation — "valgus collapse"), or contact (direct valgus force). Skiing — "phantom foot" mechanism.
- Female athletes have 2–8× higher risk than males (neuromuscular, biomechanical, hormonal factors)
- Clinical: acute haemarthrosis (80% probability of ACL tear, meniscal tear, or osteochondral fracture in adult with acute haemarthrosis), positive Lachman (>3 mm increased anterior translation, soft endpoint), positive pivot shift
- MRI: ACL discontinuity, bone bruise pattern (lateral femoral condyle + posterolateral tibial plateau — characteristic of ACL tear mechanism)
- **Surgical vs Conservative Treatment Decision**:
  - Factors favouring surgery: young age, high-level pivoting/contact sport, instability with ADLs, concomitant injuries (meniscal tear requiring repair, other ligament), high activity demands
  - Factors favouring conservative: older age, non-pivoting sports, low activity demands, isolated ACL, willing to modify activity
  - "Coper" vs "non-coper": some patients compensate well without ACL (copers); others have persistent instability (non-copers). A trial of conservative management with rehabilitation is appropriate for many patients.
- ACL reconstruction: autograft (hamstring, patellar tendon, quadriceps) or allograft. Patellar tendon graft = gold standard for young athletes (higher failure rate with hamstring in young, allograft in young).
- **Associated injuries**: "Unhappy triad" (O'Donoghue) = ACL + MCL + medial meniscus. Lateral meniscal tears common with acute ACL injury.
- **Rehabilitation after ACL injury**: prolonged (9–12 months minimum before RTP). Phases: acute (control swelling, regain extension), strengthening (progressive, closed-chain → open-chain), neuromuscular training (proprioception, plyometrics, agility), sport-specific, RTP testing (strength ≥90% contralateral, hop tests ≥90% LSI, quality of movement assessment, psychological readiness — ACL-RSI).

#### PCL (Posterior Cruciate Ligament) Injury
- Mechanism: direct blow to anterior tibia with knee flexed (dashboard injury), hyperextension
- Clinical: posterior sag sign (best test — observe tibial step-off compared to contralateral with both knees at 90° flexion), posterior drawer
- Isolated PCL tears: conservative management. PCL has good healing capacity.
- Combined with other ligaments: may require surgical reconstruction
- Rehabilitation: avoid hamstring contraction in early phase (hamstrings also pull tibia posteriorly → increases stress on PCL graft)

#### LCL / Posterolateral Corner (PLC) Injury
- Less common, often missed
- Mechanism: varus force, hyperextension + external rotation
- PLC is critical for knee stability — missed PLC injuries are a common cause of failed ACL/PCL reconstruction
- Testing: varus stress at 0° and 30°, dial test (>10° increased external rotation at 30° only = isolated PLC; increased at both 30° and 90° = combined PLC + PCL)
- Management: acute complete PLC injuries usually require surgical repair/reconstruction (within 2–3 weeks)

#### Patellar Dislocation
- Mechanism: twisting with knee in valgus + internal rotation, or direct blow
- Usually lateral dislocation
- Clinical: may have spontaneously reduced before assessment, effusion/haemarthrosis, tenderness over medial patellofemoral ligament (MPFL — torn), positive apprehension test
- Associated: osteochondral fracture (medial patellar facet or lateral femoral condyle)
- Management: reduce (if still dislocated), conservative for first-time dislocation (bracing, VMO training, hip strengthening). Surgery considered for recurrent instability, significant osteochondral fracture, or high-level athlete.

#### Patellar Tendon Rupture / Quadriceps Tendon Rupture
- Patellar tendon: typically <40 years, sudden forceful quadriceps contraction
- Quadriceps tendon: typically >40 years
- Clinical: inability to actively extend knee (or significant extensor lag), palpable gap, high-riding patella (patellar tendon rupture — patella alta) or low-riding patella (quadriceps tendon rupture)
- REQUIRES SURGICAL REPAIR

#### Other Acute Knee Conditions
- **Fractures**: patella, tibial plateau, femoral condyle. X-ray ± CT.
- **Fat pad impingement**: acute haemorrhage into infrapatellar (Hoffa's) fat pad from hyperextension injury
- **Bursal haematoma**: prepatellar or infrapatellar bursa from direct blow
- **Ruptured hamstring tendon**: distal hamstring tendons (rare). Can be confused with posterolateral corner injury.
- **Superior tibiofibular joint injury**: acute dislocation (rare). Lateral knee pain, palpable prominence of fibular head.

## Chapter 36: Anterior Knee Pain

### Clinical Approach — Key Principle
Anterior knee pain is a symptom, NOT a diagnosis. Determine the specific tissue source and contributing factors.

### Key Conditions

#### Patellofemoral Pain (PFP)
- MOST common cause of anterior knee pain in active individuals
- Definition: pain around or behind the patella, aggravated by at least one activity that loads the patellofemoral joint in a flexed position (squatting, stairs, running, jumping, prolonged sitting — "theatre sign")
- NOT a diagnosis of exclusion — make a positive diagnosis
- Contributing factors:
  - **Proximal**: hip muscle weakness (abductors, external rotators, extensors) → femoral internal rotation and adduction → increased lateral patellofemoral joint stress
  - **Local**: quadriceps weakness (especially vastus medialis obliquus — VMO), delayed VMO activation, patellar maltracking
  - **Distal**: foot overpronation → tibial internal rotation → femoral internal rotation → increased lateral PFJ stress
  - **Training load**: sudden increase in running volume, hill running, stair training
- Physical examination: assessment of contributing factors (not just special tests — the "source" of pain is often NOT at the knee). Assess hip strength, quadriceps strength and timing, foot posture, dynamic alignment (single-leg squat — observe femoral adduction/internal rotation, pelvic drop, dynamic knee valgus), patellar mobility and tilt.
- Management: MULTIMODAL — addresses the contributing factors identified on assessment:
  - Hip strengthening (especially abductors and external rotators)
  - Quadriceps strengthening (with emphasis on VMO timing — but evidence for isolated VMO training is limited; general quadriceps strengthening is effective)
  - Foot orthoses (if overpronation is a significant contributing factor)
  - Patellar taping (short-term pain relief — facilitates exercise participation)
  - Load management (modify aggravating activities)
  - Gait/movement retraining (reduce dynamic knee valgus)
  - Patient education (nature of condition, prognosis, self-management)

#### Patellofemoral Instability
- **Primary**: inherent anatomical risk factors (patella alta, trochlear dysplasia, increased TT-TG distance, ligamentous laxity)
- **Secondary**: acquired instability following a patellar dislocation with MPFL tear
- Management: conservative for first dislocation. Surgery (MPFL reconstruction ± tibial tubercle transfer) for recurrent instability.

#### Patellar Tendinopathy ("Jumper's Knee")
- Tendinopathy of the patellar tendon (typically proximal insertion at inferior pole of patella)
- Common in jumping sports (basketball, volleyball, high jump, long jump)
- Clinical: localised tenderness at inferior pole of patella, pain with jumping/landing, stiff after prolonged sitting, warm-up phenomenon (pain at start of activity, eases, then worsens after)
- Imaging: ultrasound or MRI (thickened tendon, hypoechoic areas, neovascularisation on Doppler)
- Management using Cook-Purdam model:
  - **In-season (still competing)**: load management (modify training, not complete rest), isometric exercise for analgesia, continue to compete if tolerable
  - **Off-season**: progressive loading program (eccentric squatting on decline board, heavy slow resistance), address biomechanics (landing technique, hip/knee control)
  - Corticosteroid injections NOT recommended (increased risk of rupture, poor long-term outcomes)
  - PRP, ESWT, sclerosing injections: variable evidence
  - Surgery: only for refractory cases after prolonged (6–12 months) quality conservative management

#### Less Common Causes of Anterior Knee Pain
- **Fat pad irritation/impingement (Hoffa's)**: localised pain either side of patellar tendon, especially with terminal knee extension. Management: avoid terminal extension, taping.
- **Osgood-Schlatter lesion**: apophysitis of tibial tuberosity in adolescents (10–15 years). Pain, swelling over tibial tuberosity. Self-limiting; managed with activity modification and stretching/strengthening.
- **Sinding-Larsen-Johansson lesion**: apophysitis at inferior pole of patella in adolescents (10–13 years). Self-limiting.
- **Quadriceps tendinopathy**: less common than patellar tendinopathy. Pain at superior pole of patella.
- **Prepatellar bursitis**: superficial swelling over patella from direct pressure (prolonged kneeling — "housemaid's knee"). Usually conservative.
- **Synovial plica**: thickened synovial fold, usually medial. Can cause snapping/clicking with knee flexion. Diagnosis of exclusion. Arthroscopic resection if persistently symptomatic.

## Chapter 37: Lateral, Medial, and Posterior Knee Pain

### Lateral Knee Pain

#### Iliotibial Band (ITB) Friction Syndrome
- Most common cause of lateral knee pain in runners, cyclists
- Pathology: compression of highly innervated fat and connective tissue between ITB and lateral femoral epicondyle, NOT friction (revised understanding)
- Clinical: lateral knee pain at approximately 30° of knee flexion (when ITB passes over lateral femoral epicondyle during foot strike in running), tender over lateral femoral epicondyle, positive Noble compression test and Ober test
- Contributing factors: hip abductor weakness (gluteus medius — key factor → Trendelenburg/pelvic drop → increased ITB tension), increased hip adduction and knee internal rotation during running, sudden increase in training load, running on cambered surface (always run with traffic — same side)
- Management:
  - Load modification (reduce running volume, avoid downhill running)
  - Hip abductor strengthening (critical)
  - ITB stretching and foam rolling (but may work through neural desensitisation rather than tissue lengthening)
  - Address running biomechanics (reduce pelvic drop, crossover gait)
  - Corticosteroid injection: short-term relief but does not address cause
  - Surgery (ITB release): rare, only for refractory cases

#### Other Causes of Lateral Knee Pain
- Lateral meniscus abnormality (tear, cyst)
- LCL injury
- Proximal tibiofibular joint injury
- Biceps femoris tendinopathy
- Popliteus tendinopathy
- Referred pain from lumbar spine (L5 dermatome over lateral knee)

### Medial Knee Pain
- **Patellofemoral syndrome**: PFJ pain can present medially
- **Medial meniscus abnormality**: medial joint line tenderness, mechanical symptoms
- **Osteoarthritis and chondral injuries** of medial compartment
- **MCL injury/bursitis**: pes anserine bursitis (medial tibial pain, common in runners, especially with tight hamstrings). Pes anserine tendons: sartorius, gracilis, semitendinosus.
- **Medial plica syndrome**
- **Referred pain from hip (especially in children/adolescents)**

### Posterior Knee Pain

#### Clinical Evaluation
- Differentiate intra-articular from extra-articular pathology
- Key structures: popliteal fossa contents — popliteal artery, popliteal vein, tibial nerve, common peroneal nerve, popliteus muscle/tendon, posterior capsule, posterior horns of menisci, PCL, hamstring tendons, gastrocnemius origins

#### Key Conditions
- **Baker's cyst (popliteal cyst)**: distended gastrocnemius-semimembranosus bursa communicating with knee joint. Usually secondary to intra-articular pathology (meniscal tear, OA) producing effusion. Management: treat the underlying intra-articular pathology. Cyst aspiration/injection rarely indicated.
- **Biceps femoris tendinopathy**: lateral posterior knee pain. Distinguish from LCL.
- **Popliteus tendinopathy**: deep posterior knee pain, aggravated by downhill running
- **PCL injury**: see Chapter 35
- **Posterior horn meniscal tear**: posterior joint line tenderness, positive McMurray
- **Vascular claudication**: popliteal artery entrapment syndrome (PAES) — exertional calf/posterior knee pain in young athletes. Diminished pulses with active plantarflexion or passive dorsiflexion. Doppler/angiography for diagnosis.
- **Deep venous thrombosis (DVT)**: calf pain, swelling, tenderness. Wells score + D-dimer + ultrasound. RED FLAG — risk of pulmonary embolism.
