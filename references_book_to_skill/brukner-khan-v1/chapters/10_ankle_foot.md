# Ankle and Foot Pain (Chapters 41–43)

## Chapter 41: Acute Ankle Injuries

### Functional Anatomy
- Ankle mortise: medial malleolus (tibia), tibial plafond, lateral malleolus (fibula)
- Ligaments: lateral ligament complex (anterior talofibular — ATFL = most commonly injured, calcaneofibular — CFL, posterior talofibular — PTFL), medial (deltoid) ligament, syndesmosis (anterior inferior tibiofibular ligament — AITFL, posterior, interosseous membrane)

### Clinical Perspective
- Lateral ankle sprain is the MOST COMMON sports injury overall
- Differential diagnosis is critical — not all ankle injuries are sprains (see Ottawa ankle rules for fracture exclusion)
- Most ankle sprains recover well, but 20–30% develop chronic ankle instability or persistent pain

### History
- Mechanism: inversion (lateral ligament), eversion (medial ligament/syndesmosis), degree of force
- Sound: "pop" or "snap" (ligament tear, tendon subluxation, or fracture)
- Ability to continue: can they weight-bear? (if not, consider fracture)
- Immediate vs delayed swelling: immediate (<2 hours) suggests more severe injury (complete ligament rupture, fracture)
- Previous ankle sprains

### Examination
- Inspection: swelling, bruising (may appear hours to days later — lateral ligaments: anterolateral; syndesmosis: proximal medial), deformity
- Palpation: follow Ottawa rules sites systematically (medial malleolus, lateral malleolus, base of 5th metatarsal, navicular, anterior talofibular ligament, calcaneofibular ligament, proximal fibula — Maisonneuve fracture, Achilles tendon, peroneal tendons)
- ROM: active and passive (compare to contralateral)
- Ligament testing:
  - Anterior drawer (ATFL integrity — increased anterior translation compared to contralateral)
  - Talar tilt (CFL integrity — increased inversion compared to contralateral)
  - External rotation stress test / squeeze test (syndesmosis)
- Functional tests: weight-bearing, single-leg stance, single-leg heel raise

### Ottawa Ankle Rules — X-ray Indicated if:
- Bone tenderness at posterior edge or tip of **medial malleolus** (6 cm), OR
- Bone tenderness at posterior edge or tip of **lateral malleolus** (6 cm), OR
- Inability to **bear weight** (4 steps) both immediately AND in emergency department

Reduced unnecessary X-rays by 30–40%. High sensitivity (nearly 100%) for clinically significant fractures.

### Lateral Ligament Injuries
- ATFL most commonly injured (inversion + plantarflexion), then CFL, then PTFL
- Grading: I (stretch, no laxity), II (partial tear, mild-moderate laxity, firm endpoint), III (complete tear, gross laxity, no endpoint)
- **Initial management** (POLICE):
  - Protection: crutches if non-weight-bearing, ankle brace/tape for support
  - Optimal loading: early weight-bearing as tolerated (better outcomes than prolonged immobilisation)
  - Ice, compression, elevation
- **Functional rehabilitation** (mainstay of treatment):
  - Early protected weight-bearing
  - ROM exercises (calf stretch, alphabet)
  - Progressive strengthening (resisted bands, heel raises)
  - Proprioceptive training (single-leg stance → wobble board → dynamic balance)
  - Sport-specific drills
- **Grade III injuries**: functional rehabilitation is still the preferred approach for most athletes (surgery reserved for elite athletes with significant mechanical instability or failed conservative management). Cast immobilisation for 10 days → functional brace → rehabilitation.
- RTP: Grade I (1–2 weeks), II (2–4 weeks), III (4–8 weeks)

### Medial (Deltoid) Ligament Injuries
- Less common — requires greater force (eversion)
- Often associated with syndesmosis injury or fractures
- Management similar to lateral — functional rehabilitation. More prolonged recovery than lateral.

### Significant Ankle Fractures
- **Weber classification**: A (below syndesmosis), B (at syndesmosis), C (above syndesmosis — unstable, syndesmosis disrupted)
- **Maisonneuve fracture**: proximal fibula fracture + syndesmosis disruption + deltoid ligament injury. MUST palpate proximal fibula in every ankle injury. If proximal fibula tender → full-length tibia-fibula X-ray.
- Stable fractures: below syndesmosis, minimal displacement → cast/boot
- Unstable fractures: above syndesmosis, displaced, medial injury → surgical fixation

### Persistent Pain After Ankle Sprain — "The Difficult Ankle"
Systematic re-evaluation if symptoms persist > 6 weeks:

1. **Was the diagnosis correct?** — Missed fracture (Ottawa rules initially negative but stress fracture or osteochondral lesion), Maisonneuve fracture (missed proximal fibula tenderness), syndesmosis injury (often undertreated)
2. **Osteochondral lesion of the talar dome (OLT)**:
   - Cartilage ± bone injury from talar dome impacting tibial plafond during sprain
   - Anterolateral (inversion + dorsiflexion) or posteromedial (inversion + plantarflexion)
   - Symptoms: persistent ankle pain, swelling, clicking/locking, giving way
   - MRI diagnosis
   - Management: conservative first (rest, physiotherapy). Arthroscopic surgery (microfracture, debridement, or cartilage repair) if persistent.
3. **Avulsion fracture of the base of 5th metatarsal**:
   - CRITICAL DISTINCTION: avulsion fracture (peroneus brevis insertion — benign, conservative) vs Jones fracture (metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction — HIGH-RISK for non-union, requires prolonged non-weight-bearing or surgical fixation)
   - Jones fracture: at zone 2 (1.5 cm from base), poor vascular supply. High risk of non-union.
4. **Ankle impingement syndromes**:
   - Anterolateral: hypertrophied ATFL or synovial tissue trapped in lateral gutter. Pain with dorsiflexion, tenderness over anterolateral ankle.
   - Anterior: osteophytes on anterior tibia and/or talar neck (from repeated forced dorsiflexion — soccer, ballet). Pain with dorsiflexion, palpable osteophytes.
   - Posterior: see Chapter 40. Os trigonum/Stieda process impingement with plantarflexion.
   - Management: conservative first (physiotherapy). Arthroscopic debridement if persistent.
5. **Peroneal tendon pathology**:
   - Dislocation/subluxation: peroneal tendons snap anteriorly over lateral malleolus. Palpable snap with active eversion/dorsiflexion. Superior peroneal retinaculum torn. Acute: surgical repair. Chronic: retinaculum reconstruction or groove deepening.
   - Longitudinal split tear: peroneus brevis. Lateral ankle pain, swelling posterior to lateral malleolus. MRI/ultrasound. Conservative first; surgical debridement/repair if persistent.
6. **Sinus tarsi syndrome**: pain in sinus tarsi (anterior to lateral malleolus). After lateral ligament injury — chronic inflammation/scarring. Management: corticosteroid injection into sinus tarsi, proprioceptive training.
7. **Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) type 1**:
   - Disproportionate pain, hyperalgesia, allodynia, colour/temperature changes, swelling
   - After even minor trauma (including ankle sprain)
   - Early recognition critical — early mobilisation, desensitisation, vitamin C (prophylaxis), referral to pain specialist
8. **Stress fracture of the talus, calcaneus, or navicular**: persistent weight-bearing pain. MRI diagnosis.

## Chapter 42: Ankle Pain (Non-Acute)

### Medial Ankle Pain

#### Tibialis Posterior Tendinopathy
- Most common cause of medial ankle pain in runners
- Tibialis posterior: primary dynamic stabiliser of medial longitudinal arch. Tendinopathy → progressive flatfoot deformity.
- Clinical: pain posterior to medial malleolus, aggravated by push-off, single-leg heel raise (pain and/or weakness — inability to perform), "too many toes" sign (from forefoot abduction in flatfoot deformity)
- Ultrasound/MRI: tendinopathy ± partial tear
- Management: load modification, foot orthoses (arch support), progressive strengthening (heel raise progression, resisted inversion), address biomechanics. Surgery (tendon transfer + osteotomy) for severe deformity.

#### Flexor Hallucis Longus (FHL) Tendinopathy
- Common in ballet dancers (repetitive push-off, en pointe)
- Pain posteromedial ankle, may have triggering of great toe
- Management: conservative (rest, physiotherapy, technique modification)

#### Tarsal Tunnel Syndrome
- Tibial nerve compression at tarsal tunnel (posteromedial ankle, behind medial malleolus)
- Paraesthesia/burning in medial ankle and plantar foot
- Tinel's positive at tarsal tunnel
- Nerve conduction studies
- Management: address compression (footwear, orthoses), corticosteroid injection, surgical release if refractory

#### Medial Malleolar Stress Fracture
- HIGH-RISK BSI. Prolonged non-weight-bearing ± surgical fixation.

### Lateral Ankle Pain (Non-Acute)
- **Peroneal tendinopathy/tear**: see "Difficult Ankle" above
- **Sinus tarsi syndrome**: pain and tenderness in sinus tarsi. Management: corticosteroid injection, proprioceptive training, address hindfoot biomechanics.
- **Anterolateral impingement**: see "Difficult Ankle" above

### Anterior Ankle Pain
- **Anterior ankle impingement**: osteophytes on anterior tibia and/or talar neck. Pain with forced dorsiflexion. Common in soccer players (repetitive kicking — plantarflexion-dorsiflexion transition). X-ray: anterior osteophytes. Management: conservative (dorsiflexion mobility, load modification). Arthroscopic excision of osteophytes if persistent.
- **Tibialis anterior tendinopathy**: anterior ankle pain, pain with resisted dorsiflexion

## Chapter 43: Foot Pain

### Rearfoot Pain

#### Plantar Fasciopathy ("Plantar Fasciitis")
- MOST common cause of plantar heel pain
- Pathology: degenerative fasciopathy (fasciosis) of plantar fascia origin at medial calcaneal tubercle — NOT primarily inflammatory
- Clinical: plantar medial heel pain, worst with FIRST STEPS in the morning (post-static dyskinesia — pathognomonic), eases with walking, worsens after prolonged standing/walking
- Risk factors: obesity, prolonged standing, reduced ankle dorsiflexion, pes cavus or planus, sudden increase in training load
- Examination: point tenderness at plantar fascia origin (medial calcaneal tubercle), pain with windlass test (passive dorsiflexion of great toe — stretches plantar fascia), limited ankle dorsiflexion
- Imaging: not routinely required. Ultrasound (thickened plantar fascia >4 mm, hypoechoic areas). X-ray (calcaneal spur — NOT the cause of pain; present in asymptomatic individuals). MRI (fascial thickening, perifascial oedema).
- **Management**:
  1. **Load management**: reduce aggravating activities (running, prolonged standing)
  2. **Stretching**: plantar fascia-specific + calf stretching (gastrocnemius and soleus). Most effective when done before getting out of bed.
  3. **Strengthening**: progressive loading of plantar fascia (towel curls, heel raises, and eventually loaded heel raises with a towel under toes)
  4. **Taping**: low-Dye taping provides short-term support and pain relief
  5. **Foot orthoses**: prefabricated or custom. Evidence for short-term pain relief.
  6. **Night splint**: maintains ankle in dorsiflexion overnight. Modest evidence.
  7. **ESWT**: evidence for chronic plantar fasciopathy. 3 sessions.
  8. **Corticosteroid injection**: short-term relief (weeks), but risk of plantar fascia rupture and fat pad atrophy. Use sparingly, maximum 2–3 injections.
  9. **PRP injection**: emerging evidence, may be superior to corticosteroid in medium term.
  10. **Surgery** (plantar fascia release): only for refractory cases after ≥6–12 months of comprehensive conservative treatment. Risk: lateral column overload, nerve injury.

#### Other Causes of Rearfoot Pain
- **Fat pad atrophy**: central heel pain (NOT medial), in older individuals. Loss of cushioning. Management: heel cup, cushioning footwear.
- **Calcaneal stress fracture**: bone tenderness on medial-lateral calcaneal squeeze. MRI for diagnosis. Low-risk — conservative management.
- **Tarsal tunnel syndrome**: see Chapter 42 (medial ankle pain). May present as heel pain.
- **Baxter's nerve entrapment**: first branch of lateral plantar nerve → medial heel pain. Normal plantar fascia on examination/imaging. Diagnostic nerve block.
- **Sever's disease**: calcaneal apophysitis in children. See Chapter 40.

### Midfoot Pain

#### Navicular Stress Fracture
- **HIGH-RISK BSI**. Vague dorsal midfoot pain, insidious onset. Common in sprinters, jumpers.
- Examination: "N spot" tenderness (proximal dorsal navicular). Pain on hopping.
- X-ray: often normal. CT or MRI for diagnosis.
- Management: NON-WEIGHT-BEARING CAST for 6 weeks minimum. Surgical fixation if delayed union/non-union. Slow return to sport.

#### Midfoot Sprain / Lisfranc Injury
- Injury to tarsometatarsal (Lisfranc) joint complex
- Mechanism: forced plantarflexion + rotation (foot caught in stirrup, or direct crush)
- Clinical: midfoot swelling, inability to weight-bear, tenderness over Lisfranc joint (base of 2nd metatarsal), pain with passive abduction and pronation of forefoot with hindfoot held fixed
- X-ray: weight-bearing AP and lateral (bilateral comparison). Look for widening between 1st and 2nd metatarsal bases, dorsal displacement of metatarsal bases on lateral. CT if suspicion persists.
- Management: undisplaced stable → cast/non-weight-bearing 6–8 weeks. Unstable/displaced → surgical fixation (screw or suture button). Can be missed — high suspicion in midfoot injuries.

#### Other Midfoot Causes
- **Extensor tendinopathy**: dorsal foot pain. Management: footwear modification, activity modification.
- **Metatarsal stress fractures**: see Forefoot Pain below
- **Tarsal coalition**: congenital bony or fibrous connection between tarsal bones → rigid flatfoot, recurrent ankle sprains, midfoot pain in adolescents. CT for diagnosis.

### Forefoot Pain

#### Metatarsal Stress Fracture
- 2nd or 3rd metatarsal neck most common ("march fracture")
- Focal bone tenderness, pain with weight-bearing and toe walking
- Mechanism: sudden increase in walking/running (military recruits, runners changing training)
- X-ray: may be normal for 2–3 weeks (periosteal reaction then visible). Callus formation on follow-up X-ray confirms.
- LOW-RISK (metatarsal shafts 2–4). Conservative: rest from impact activity, stiff-soled shoe or boot if pain severe, gradual return to activity (4–6 weeks).
- HIGH-RISK: 5th metatarsal base (Jones fracture — see Chapter 41), sesamoids. Prolonged non-weight-bearing ± surgery.

#### Metatarsalgia
- Generalised forefoot pain under metatarsal heads
- Multiple causes: overuse (increased training), footwear (minimal cushioning), foot deformity (cavus foot, claw toes), interdigital neuroma, stress fracture, Freiberg's disease
- Identify and treat specific cause

#### Morton's Neuroma (Interdigital Neuroma)
- Perineural fibrosis of interdigital nerve, usually 2nd/3rd webspace (between 3rd and 4th metatarsals)
- Clinical: burning/tingling in affected toes, feeling of "walking on a pebble", relief by removing shoes and massaging foot, Mulder's click (palpable click with metatarsal squeeze + plantar pressure)
- Worse with narrow shoes, high heels
- Ultrasound/MRI for diagnosis
- Management: footwear modification (wide toe box, low heel), metatarsal pad (just proximal to metatarsal heads), corticosteroid injection ± local anaesthetic (diagnostic and therapeutic), surgery (neurectomy) if refractory

#### Turf Toe
- Sprain of plantar capsuloligamentous complex of 1st metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint
- Mechanism: forced hyperextension of great toe (tackled from behind on artificial turf)
- Pain, swelling, limited ROM at 1st MTP
- Grading: I (stretch), II (partial tear), III (complete tear with instability)
- Management: rest, stiff-soled shoe or turf toe plate, taping to limit extension. Grade III may require surgery.
- High morbidity — can be career-limiting for elite athletes (loss of push-off)

#### Sesamoid Pathology
- Sesamoid stress fracture, sesamoiditis (inflammation), bipartite sesamoid (normal variant)
- Pain under 1st MTP head, worse with push-off and dorsiflexion of great toe
- Management: off-loading (sesamoid pad, stiff-soled shoe), rest from impact. Sesamoid stress fracture — HIGH-RISK (poor healing, may progress to non-union or avascular necrosis). Prolonged protected weight-bearing ± surgical excision.

#### Freiberg's Disease (Avascular Necrosis of Metatarsal Head)
- Usually 2nd metatarsal head, adolescent females (10–18 years)
- Pain, swelling, limited ROM at affected MTP joint
- X-ray: flattening, sclerosis, fragmentation of metatarsal head
- Management: conservative (off-loading, stiff-soled shoe). Surgery for advanced cases.

#### Hallux Valgus (Bunion)
- Lateral deviation of great toe with medial prominence of 1st MTP joint
- More common in females, tight narrow shoes
- Pain over medial prominence, difficulty finding footwear
- Management: footwear modification (wide toe box), orthoses, spacer. Surgery (osteotomy) only for persistent disabling pain.

#### Hallux Rigidus
- Degenerative OA of 1st MTP joint → pain and stiffness, especially with dorsiflexion (push-off)
- Osteophytes restrict dorsiflexion
- Management: stiff-soled shoe / rocker bottom sole (reduces MTP dorsiflexion demand), NSAIDs, intra-articular corticosteroid injection. Surgery (cheilectomy — removal of dorsal osteophytes) for refractory cases; arthrodesis for severe OA.
