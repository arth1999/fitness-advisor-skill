# Part C: Practical Sports Medicine — Special Populations (Chapters 44–48)

## Chapter 44: The Younger Athlete

### The Young Athlete Is Unique
- NOT simply a "small adult"
- Non-linearity of growth — growth spurts alter biomechanics, strength, flexibility, and injury risk
- Maturity-associated variation — children of the same chronological age can differ markedly in biological maturity
- Unique response to skeletal injury — growing bone (physis, apophysis) responds differently than mature bone

### Key Principle — The Weakest Link
In the skeletally immature athlete, the physis (growth plate) and apophysis are weaker than surrounding ligaments and tendons. Therefore:
- An injury that would cause a ligament sprain in an adult causes a PHYSEAL FRACTURE in a child
- An injury that would cause a tendinopathy in an adult causes an APOPHYSITIS in a child
- Always check growth remaining before surgical decisions

### Acute Fractures in Children
- Salter-Harris classification of physeal fractures (I–V)
- Type I: through physis only (may be radiographically occult)
- Type II: through physis + metaphysis (most common)
- Type III: through physis + epiphysis (intra-articular)
- Type IV: through metaphysis + physis + epiphysis (intra-articular)
- Type V: crush injury of physis (may cause growth arrest)
- Higher Salter-Harris types (III, IV, V) → higher risk of growth disturbance → require orthopaedic referral

### Overuse Injuries of the Physis
- Physeal stress injury from repetitive loading (gymnasts — distal radius, throwers — proximal humerus)
- Management: rest from aggravating activity until asymptomatic (usually 4–6 weeks). May require prolonged rest.

### Regional Conditions in Young Athletes

**Shoulder Pain**:
- Proximal humeral physeal stress fracture ("Little Leaguer's Shoulder"): throwing athletes 11–16 years. Pain over proximal humerus. Rest from throwing 2–4 months.
- Glenohumeral instability: may be atraumatic (AMBRI). Rehabilitation first.
- AC joint injuries: physeal separation rather than true AC dislocation in children <16 (distal clavicle physis is last to close — age 22–25).

**Elbow Pain**:
- Medial epicondyle apophysitis ("Little Leaguer's Elbow"): most common elbow injury in young throwers (9–12 years). Pain over medial epicondyle with throwing. Management: rest from throwing, pitch count limits, technique correction, gradual return.
- OCD of capitellum: see Chapter 25.
- Medial epicondyle avulsion fracture: from acute valgus force. May require surgical fixation if displaced.

**Wrist Pain**:
- Distal radial physeal stress fracture ("Gymnast's Wrist"): repetitive weight-bearing on extended wrist. Pain over distal radius, aggravated by handstands/vaulting. Rest from weight-bearing through wrists.

**Back Pain**:
- Spondylolysis: see Chapter 29. High prevalence in young athletes in extension-loading sports.
- Postural abnormalities: scoliosis screening (Adam's forward bend test). Scheuermann's disease (thoracic kyphosis from vertebral endplate changes). Postural vs structural.

**Hip Pain**:
- SCFE (Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis): EMERGENCY. Adolescent 12–15 years, often overweight. Presents as hip OR knee pain. Limp, externally rotated gait, limited internal rotation. Immediate non-weight-bearing and orthopaedic referral.
- Perthes disease (Legg-Calve-Perthes): child 3–12 years, hip/groin/knee pain, limp, limited abduction and internal rotation. Refer to paediatric orthopaedics.
- Apophysitis: AIIS (rectus femoris), ASIS (sartorius), iliac crest.

**Knee Pain**:
- Osgood-Schlatter lesion: see Chapter 36.
- Sinding-Larsen-Johansson lesion: see Chapter 36.
- Patellofemoral pain: common in adolescents, especially females. Address hip strength and biomechanics.
- Osteochondritis dissecans (OCD): femoral condyle (usually medial). Knee pain, swelling, mechanical symptoms. May require surgical fixation if unstable.
- Anterior knee pain in children: always check hip (rule out SCFE, Perthes).

**Foot Pain**:
- Sever's disease: calcaneal apophysitis. See Chapter 40.
- Kohler's disease: avascular necrosis of navicular (4–7 years). Self-limiting.
- Freiberg's disease: avascular necrosis of metatarsal head. See Chapter 43.

### Painless Abnormalities of Gait in Children
- In-toeing: femoral anteversion (w-sitting position), tibial torsion, metatarsus adductus. Most resolve spontaneously.
- Flat feet: flexible flat feet are normal in children (arch develops with age). Only treat if symptomatic.
- Bow legs (genu varum) / Knock knees (genu valgum): normal variants at different ages. Pathological if asymmetric, severe, or not following expected timeline.

## Chapter 45: Military Personnel

### Key Differences from Civilian Sports Medicine
- Special military culture: hierarchical command structure, "can-do" attitude, reluctance to report injuries
- Unique physical demands: heavy load carriage (20–40+ kg), prolonged marching, running in boots
- Injury = threat to operational readiness and unit effectiveness

### Epidemiology
- Highest injury rates during initial training (basic training — sudden increase in physical activity)
- Lower limb overuse injuries predominate: MTSS, stress fractures, patellofemoral pain, ITB syndrome, Achilles tendinopathy
- Blisters: most common injury in military training — directly impacts training completion
- Parachuting injuries: ankle fractures/sprains, vertebral compression fractures

### Common Military Injuries
- Overuse injuries of the lower limb: same as civilian runners but exacerbated by load carriage, boots, and enforced training schedules
- MTSS: see Chapter 38
- Stress fractures: metatarsal, tibial, femoral. Femoral neck — HIGH-RISK. See Chapter 4 and Chapter 31.
- Blisters: prevention (well-fitted boots, moisture-wicking socks, foot powder, lubrication). Treatment: drain if large/tender (sterile technique), leave roof intact, protective dressing.
- Parachuting injuries: PLF (parachute landing fall) training to reduce risk. Ankle braces for all paratroopers.

### Risk Factors for Injury in Military Settings
- Previous injury (strongest predictor)
- Low physical fitness on entry
- Female sex (higher injury rates — possibly due to load carriage relative to body size)
- High BMI or low BMI
- High weekly running distance (>30 km/week)
- Limited running experience prior to enlistment
- Competitive behaviours (pushing through pain)
- Smoking

### Injury Prevention in the Military
- Injury surveillance systems
- Graduated training programs
- Load management (not all runs should be in boots with packs)
- Appropriate footwear
- Nutritional optimisation (vitamin D, calcium, energy availability)
- Injury prevention exercises (neuromuscular training)
- Early intervention for overuse symptoms (not "soldiering on")

## Chapter 46: Periodic Medical Assessment (PMA) of Athletes

### Purposes of the PMA
1. Identification of medical conditions that contraindicate participation
2. Assessment of known injuries and illnesses
3. Review of current medications and supplements
4. Education (injury prevention, anti-doping, nutrition, recovery)
5. Baseline testing (concussion — SCAT, cardiac — ECG)
6. Development of athlete rapport
7. Screening (but NOT screening for "all possible problems" — targeted to conditions where early detection changes outcome)

### Who, When, and What

**Who is being assessed?** The assessment must be tailored to:
- Sport and position (specific injury risks)
- Geographical location (altitude, heat, travel)
- Age (growth-related conditions in young, age-related screening in older)
- Sex (female athlete triad / RED-S, specific injury risks)
- Level of competition

**When to perform**:
- Pre-season (most common)
- Post-injury (before clearance to return)
- Before major events/tournaments
- At specific career transitions

**What to include** (core components):
- Medical history (personal and family)
- Injury history (all previous injuries, current symptoms)
- Medications and supplements (check against WADA prohibited list)
- Cardiovascular screening (history + physical examination + ECG in many settings)
- Concussion baseline (SCAT)
- Musculoskeletal examination (targeted to sport-specific injury patterns)
- Functional movement assessment (optional — evidence for injury prediction is limited)
- Special investigations as indicated (blood tests — iron, vitamin D; imaging — only if clinically indicated)

### Clearance or Restriction from Play
- The PMA should result in a clear decision: cleared for full participation, restricted participation, or not cleared
- Reasons for restriction must be documented with a management plan for clearance
- Consent must be obtained — athletes must understand what is being tested and why

### Who Should Perform the PMA?
- Qualified sports medicine physician familiar with the specific sport demands
- May involve multidisciplinary team (physician, physiotherapist, exercise physiologist, nutritionist)

## Chapter 47: Working and Travelling with Teams

### The Medical Support Team
- Core team: sports medicine physician, physiotherapist, conditioning specialist
- Key attributes: communication, mutual respect, clearly defined roles and responsibilities, conflict resolution skills

### Medical Indemnity and Trauma Training
- Ensure appropriate medical indemnity insurance for all locations where care is provided
- Maintain current emergency trauma training (ATLS/EMST, sports-specific emergency care)
- Know the scope of practice and when to refer

### Medical Equipment
- Medical kit must be appropriate to: sport, location, distance from definitive care, number of athletes
- Emergency equipment: defibrillator (AED), airway management equipment, spinal immobilisation equipment, splints, emergency medications
- Diagnostic equipment: blood pressure, pulse oximeter, glucometer, urine dipstick, thermometer
- Therapeutic equipment: wound care, taping supplies, basic pharmacology (analgesics, anti-inflammatories, antibiotics if remote), cryotherapy

### Core Principles of Team Care
1. Pre-season preparation: PMA, emergency action plan, medical history review, baseline testing
2. Injury prevention: implement evidence-based prevention programs
3. Injury management: acute care, rehabilitation oversight, RTP decision-making
4. Communication: regular communication with coaches, athletes, other medical staff
5. Documentation: accurate, contemporaneous medical records (medico-legal requirement)
6. Confidentiality: athlete medical information is confidential — share only with consent
7. Holistic care: consider psychosocial factors, not just physical injuries

### Emergency Action Plans (EAP)
- MUST be in place before any sporting event
- Should cover: common emergencies specific to the sport, location, access routes, communication, designated roles, equipment location, nearest hospital with capabilities (neurosurgery, trauma, cardiac)
- Rehearsed regularly

### Preparing to Travel with a Team
**Before travel**:
- Medical screening of all travellers
- Vaccinations (routine + travel-specific)
- Malaria prophylaxis if indicated
- Medications plan (sufficient supply of regular medications, emergency medications, travel medications)
- Medical kit prepared for destination (consider climate, remoteness, altitude)
- Research: local medical facilities, evacuation options, medical insurance, reciprocal healthcare agreements
- Emergency contact information for all athletes (next of kin)

**During travel**:
- Hydration promotion
- DVT prophylaxis for long-haul flights (compression stockings, mobilisation, exercises)
- Adjust medication timing to destination time zone

**On arrival**:
- Acclimatisation (temperature, altitude, time zone)
- Identify and inspect local medical facilities
- Establish communication protocols
- Brief athletes on health and safety

**Journey home**:
- Continue injury management during travel
- Post-travel medical debrief
- Arrange follow-up for any unresolved issues

### Jet Lag
- Pathophysiology: circadian rhythm desynchronisation with destination light-dark cycle
- Symptoms: sleep disturbance, fatigue, reduced cognitive and physical performance, GI disturbance
- Prevention:
  - Gradually shift sleep schedule before departure (if feasible)
  - On flight: set watch to destination time, sleep/wake accordingly
  - Hydration (avoid alcohol and caffeine)
  - Melatonin (0.5–5 mg at desired bedtime at destination) — evidence for reducing jet lag severity
  - Light exposure strategy (morning light to advance, evening light to delay the circadian clock)
- Symptomatic treatment: short-term hypnotic for sleep (not for use during flight)

## Chapter 48: Career Development in Sport and Exercise Medicine

### The Development of Sport and Exercise Medicine (SEM)
- SEM has evolved from elite athlete care to a broad specialty serving everyone who wishes to be active
- Exercise medicine: emphasis on physical activity for prevention and management of chronic disease

### Key Behaviours for a Successful Career
1. Develop broad clinical skills (MSK medicine, exercise prescription, internal medicine basics, emergency care)
2. Understand the sport(s) — attend training, watch matches, learn the demands and culture
3. Build relationships: coaches, athletes, administrators, other health professionals
4. Communicate effectively: adapt communication to audience (athlete vs coach vs colleague)
5. Commit to lifelong learning: SEM is rapidly evolving
6. Seek mentorship and be a mentor
7. Maintain work-life balance and professional boundaries
8. Develop a subspecialty interest (e.g., specific sport, specific injury type, specific population)
9. Contribute to the SEM community: teaching, research, administration
10. Dual qualifications can be advantageous (physician + physiotherapist, physician + exercise physiologist)

### Global Perspectives
- SEM training pathways vary by country (primary specialty vs subspecialty)
- Languages: proficiency in additional languages opens international opportunities
- Women in SEM: increasing representation; addressing barriers to entry and advancement
- Global SEM network: international conferences, journals, and collaborations
