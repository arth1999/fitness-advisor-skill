# Part B: Head, Concussion, and Face (Chapters 20–22)

## Chapter 20: Sports Concussion (Paul McCrory et al.)

### Definition
Traumatic brain injury induced by biomechanical forces, resulting in rapid onset of short-lived neurological impairment that resolves spontaneously. May or may not involve loss of consciousness.

### On-Field Management
1. **Red Flags Check** — if ANY present, activate emergency procedures:
   - Neck pain or tenderness
   - Double vision
   - Weakness or tingling/burning in arms or legs
   - Severe or increasing headache
   - Seizure or convulsion
   - Loss of consciousness
   - Vomiting
   - Increasingly restless, agitated, or combative
   - Deteriorating conscious state
   - Visible skull deformity
2. **Pocket CRT / SCAT assessment**: Maddocks questions, GCS, cervical spine exam
3. **If concussion confirmed**: immediate removal from play, no same-day RTP (mandatory)
4. **Serial monitoring** for 4 hours post-injury (risk of deterioration)
5. **Relative rest** 24–48h, then gradual return to normal activities

### Confirming the Diagnosis (SCAT)
- Symptom evaluation (22 symptoms, severity score)
- Cognitive assessment: orientation, immediate memory, concentration (digits backwards, months in reverse order)
- Balance examination: modified BESS (Balance Error Scoring System)
- Delayed recall
- Neck examination

### Indications for Urgent Neuroimaging / Hospital Referral
- GCS < 15 at 2 hours post-injury
- Suspected open or depressed skull fracture
- Signs of basal skull fracture (raccoon eyes, Battle's sign, haemotympanum, CSF otorrhoea/rhinorrhoea)
- Vomiting ≥ 2 episodes
- Age > 65 years
- Focal neurological deficit
- Seizure
- Coagulopathy (including anticoagulant/antiplatelet medication)
- Dangerous mechanism (ejected from vehicle, pedestrian struck, fall >1m or 5 stairs)

### Factors Influencing Need for Specialist Referral (Table 20.2)
- Duration of symptoms > 10–14 days
- Multiple concussions in close succession
- Persistent symptoms despite conservative management
- Abnormal findings on clinical assessment
- Pre-existing conditions (migraine, mental health, learning disability)
- High-risk sport with high recurrence risk

### Graduated Return to Sport Protocol (minimum 24h/stage, minimum 6 days)
| Stage | Activity | Goal |
|-------|----------|------|
| 1 | Symptom-limited daily activities | Gradual reintroduction |
| 2 | Light aerobic exercise | Increase HR |
| 3 | Sport-specific exercise (running drills) | Add movement |
| 4 | Non-contact training drills | Exercise, coordination, cognitive load |
| 5 | Full-contact practice (after medical clearance) | Restore confidence, functional assessment |
| 6 | Return to sport | |

**If symptoms recur at any stage**: return to previous asymptomatic stage for 24h before re-progression.

### The "Difficult" Concussion (symptoms > 10–14 days)
- Refer to specialist multidisciplinary concussion service
- Contributing factors: cervicogenic, vestibular, psychological, sleep disturbance
- Management: targeted rehabilitation addressing each contributing factor

### Children and Concussion
- ChildSCAT3 for children 5–12 years
- More conservative approach required
- Children take longer to recover
- Cognitive rest (school absence, reduced screen time, reduced cognitive load)
- Return to learn BEFORE return to sport
- Do NOT return to contact sport until completely symptom-free AND medically cleared

### Risk of Premature RTP
- Second impact syndrome (rare but catastrophic — diffuse cerebral oedema from second impact before first resolved)
- Increased risk of further concussion
- Prolonged recovery
- Potential long-term cognitive effects (CTE — chronic traumatic encephalopathy, association but causation not established)

## Chapter 21: Headache (Toby Hall)

### Clinical Approach to Headache in Sport
1. Rule out red flags and serious causes
2. Differentiate primary vs secondary headache

### Red Flags for Headache (Require Investigation)
- New onset in patient > 50 years
- Thunderclap onset (maximal intensity < 1 minute)
- Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, jaw claudication)
- History of cancer
- Recent head trauma
- Associated neurological symptoms (other than visual aura)
- Worsening with Valsalva, exertion, sexual activity
- Progressive worsening over time
- Papilloedema on fundoscopy

### Primary Headache Types
1. **Migraine**: unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, nausea/vomiting, photophobia/phonophobia, with or without aura. Triggered by exertion in some athletes.
2. **Primary exercise headache**: bilateral, pulsating, brought on by sustained physical exertion, lasts 5 min–48 h, no underlying pathology. Diagnosis of exclusion.
3. **Tension-type headache**: bilateral, pressing/tightening, mild-moderate, no nausea, ± photophobia or phonophobia.

### Secondary Headache Types
1. **Cervicogenic headache**: referred from upper cervical spine (C1–C3). Unilateral, provoked by neck movement/sustained postures. Key examination: C1-2 flexion rotation test, upper cervical tenderness.
2. **Post-traumatic headache**: following head/neck trauma. Often mixed migraine + tension-type features. Treat underlying cervical dysfunction.
3. **External compression headache**: from tight headgear/helmet.
4. **High-altitude headache**: above 2500 m, part of acute mountain sickness spectrum.
5. **Hypercapnia headache**: from breath-hold diving/swimming.

### Clinical Measurement
- Headache diary (frequency, intensity, duration, triggers, associated symptoms)
- Neck Disability Index (NDI)

### Management Principles
- Treat specific cause if identified (e.g., cervical mobilisation for cervicogenic headache)
- Migraine: triptans, prophylaxis if frequent (beta-blockers, topiramate, amitriptyline)
- Address contributing factors: neck dysfunction, stress, sleep, nutrition, hydration
- Gradual warm-up before exertion

## Chapter 22: Face, Eyes, and Teeth

### Nose
- **Epistaxis (nosebleed)**: direct pressure to nasal alae for 10–20 min, lean forward. If persists → nasal packing. Refer if bleeding persists despite packing.
- **Nasal fractures**: deformity, swelling, epistaxis. Reduce after swelling subsides (3–7 days) or immediately if severe. Septal haematoma → emergency — drain immediately to prevent septal necrosis/saddle deformity.
- **Septal haematoma**: boggy swelling on nasal septum. EMERGENCY — requires immediate incision and drainage.

### Ear
- **Auricular haematoma ("cauliflower ear")**: drain immediately, compression dressing to prevent re-accumulation. Untreated → permanent deformity.
- **Perforated eardrum**: hearing loss, pain. Usually heals spontaneously (weeks). Keep dry. Refer if persistent.
- **Otitis externa ("swimmer's ear")**: pain, discharge, tenderness on tragus traction. Topical antibiotic/steroid drops. Keep ear dry.

### Eyes — ALL eye injuries require thorough examination
**Indications for immediate ophthalmologist referral**:
- Hyphaema (blood in anterior chamber)
- Penetrating injury / ruptured globe
- Lens dislocation
- Vitreous haemorrhage
- Retinal detachment (flashes, floaters, curtain/shadow in vision)
- Corneal laceration
- Decreased visual acuity
- Irregular pupil
- Severe eye pain despite topical anaesthetic

- **Corneal abrasion/foreign body**: fluorescein staining, topical antibiotic, patch if large. Do NOT patch if contact lens wearer.
- **Subconjunctival haemorrhage**: benign, resolves spontaneously 1–2 weeks.
- **Eyelid injuries**: consider canaliculus involvement if near medial canthus → ophthalmologist referral.
- **Hyphaema**: blood in anterior chamber, visible fluid level. BED REST, eye shield, ophthalmologist referral. Risk: re-bleeding, glaucoma, corneal staining.
- **Lens dislocation**: quivering iris (iridodonesis) on eye movement. Immediate ophthalmologist referral.
- **Vitreous haemorrhage**: loss of red reflex, hazy appearance. Bed rest; severe cases require vitrectomy.
- **Retinal detachment**: flashes, floaters, visual field defect. OPHTHALMOLOGICAL EMERGENCY — surgery within 24–48 hours.

### Teeth
- **Classification of dental injuries**: enamel fracture, enamel-dentine fracture, crown-root fracture, root fracture, luxation, avulsion
- **Avulsed permanent tooth**: EMERGENCY. Hold by crown (not root), rinse with saline/milk (don't scrub), reimplant immediately if possible (best prognosis if <30 min). If cannot reimplant, store in milk/saline/saliva. Refer to dentist immediately.
- **Other dental trauma**: examine by dentist as soon as possible. Close long-term follow-up needed (years).
- **Prevention**: properly fitted mouth guards reduce dental injuries and referrals.

### Facial Fractures
- Initial management: maintain airway (mandibular/maxillary fractures may require emergency reduction)
- Exclude head injury and cervical spine injury
- Inspect oral cavity for bleeding/dental damage
- CT is imaging modality of choice (has supplanted plain radiographs)
- **Orbital fracture**: ± globe trauma. Check extraocular movements (entrapment of inferior rectus), infraorbital nerve sensation.
- **Zygomaticomaxillary complex fracture**: flattening of cheek, trismus, infraorbital nerve paraesthesia.
- **Maxillary fractures (Le Fort)**: Le Fort I (floating palate), II (pyramidal — through maxilla, nasal bones, orbits), III (craniofacial disjunction).
- **Mandibular fractures**: malocclusion, sublingual haematoma, inferior alveolar nerve paraesthesia. Panoramic radiograph/CT.
- **Temporomandibular injuries**: pain, clicking, locking, limited mouth opening. Usually conservative management.
