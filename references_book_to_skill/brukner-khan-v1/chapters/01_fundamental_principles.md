# Part A: Fundamental Principles (Chapters 1–19)

This part covers the foundational knowledge for all sports medicine practice.

## Chapter 1: Sport and Exercise Medicine — The Team Approach
- The SEM team: physician, physiotherapist, conditioning specialist as minimum core
- Multiskilling, roles, responsibilities, and communication
- The SEM model: from injury to return to play — a continuum managed by a multidisciplinary team
- Challenges: diagnosis, treatment, meeting individual needs
- The coach-athlete-clinician triad — managing conflicting priorities
- "Love thy sport" — understand the sport to treat the athlete

## Chapter 2: Integrating Evidence into Shared Decision Making
- Evidence-Based Practice (EBP) = best research evidence + clinical expertise + patient values
- Accessing and appraising research: risk of bias, interpreting treatment effects
- Shared decision making: communicate risks and benefits to the patient; the clinician advises, the athlete decides
- Challenges to EBP: time constraints, access to literature, research quality

## Chapter 3: Sports Injuries — Acute
- Pathophysiology by tissue type:
  - **Bone**: fracture, periosteal contusion
  - **Hyaline cartilage**: chondral and osteochondral injuries
  - **Fibrocartilage**: acute tear (meniscus, labrum), disc herniation
  - **Joint**: dislocation, subluxation
  - **Ligament**: sprain/tear (Grade I–III)
  - **Muscle**: strain/tear, contusion, myositis ossificans, acute compartment syndrome, cramp
  - **Tendon**: acute tear/rupture
  - **Fascia**: tear/rupture
  - **Bursa**: traumatic bursitis
  - **Nerve**: neuropraxia
  - **Fat pad**: contusion/bruise
  - **Skin**: laceration, abrasion

## Chapter 4: Sports Injuries — Overuse
- **Bone stress injury (BSI)**: continuum from stress reaction to stress fracture
  - Pathophysiology: repetitive loading > remodelling capacity → microdamage accumulation
  - Risk factors: training load errors, biomechanics, bone health (RED-S/female athlete triad), nutrition, hormones
  - Diagnosis: clinical (focal bone tenderness, pain on loading/percussion) + MRI (gold standard)
  - Classification: combine anatomical site risk (high-risk vs low-risk) + MRI grade (Fredericson 1–4)
  - Management: vary by site. Low-risk = relative rest + graduated loading. High-risk = strict non-weight-bearing + prolonged rehabilitation ± surgery
  - Return-to-sport times vary by site-MRI grade combination (see cheatsheet.md)
- **Apophysitis**: traction injury at apophysis in skeletally immature (Osgood-Schlatter, Sever's)
- **Tendinopathy** (Cook-Purdam model): reactive → tendon dysrepair → degenerative
  - Key: tendon pathology is on a continuum, not distinct stages
  - Rehabilitation principles: load management, exercise-based (eccentric or HSR), address kinetic chain
- **Chronic exertional compartment syndrome**: exercise-induced pressure elevation, resolves at rest
- **Myofascial pain**: trigger points (controversial entity — may just represent sore spots)
- **Osteitis/periostitis**: inflammation of bone and periosteum from overuse
- **Enthesopathy**: pathology at tendon-bone junction
- **Bursitis**: chronic bursal irritation from overuse/friction
- **Nerve entrapment**: chronic compression neuropathy
- **Skin overuse**: blisters, skin infections

## Chapter 5: Pain — Why and How Does It Hurt? (Lorimer Moseley)
- Pain is NOT nociception — nociception is input; pain is output (the brain's evaluation of threat)
- Peripheral sensitisation: lowered threshold of peripheral nociceptors after tissue injury
- Central sensitisation: increased responsiveness of CNS nociceptive neurons
- The brain decides: pain is a protective response based on the brain's evaluation of danger, not tissue state
- In persistent pain, the brain changes (cortical reorganisation, altered body schema)
- Treatment must address the complex system — not just tissues

## Chapter 6: Pain — The Clinical Aspects
- **Input-dominated pain**: primarily driven by nociceptive input from tissues
  - Nociceptive: linked to tissue loading, proportionate, mechanical pattern
  - Neuropathic: nerve injury, burning/shooting, dermatomal distribution
  - Referred pain: perceived distant from source (sclerotomal, myotomal)
- **Centrally dominated pain**: central sensitisation — widespread, disproportionate, poor localisation
- Pain is always a biopsychosocial phenomenon
- Motor adaptation to pain: altered movement patterns can persist after pain resolves
- Language matters: how clinicians talk about pain influences outcomes (avoid nocebo, catastrophising language)
- Treatment: target the dominant mechanism. Input-dominated = address tissue. Centrally dominated = pain neuroscience education, graded activity, address psychosocial factors

## Chapter 7: Beware — Conditions That Masquerade as Sports Injuries
- **How to recognise a masquerade**:
  - Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, malaise, night sweats)
  - Constant pain, unrelieved by rest or position change
  - Night pain (particularly bone pain that wakes the patient)
  - Symptoms out of proportion to objective findings
  - Bilateral symptoms with no mechanical explanation
  - Unusual age (very young or old for the activity)
  - Symptoms that do not fit a recognisable MSK pattern
- **Key masquerading categories**:
  - Rheumatological: inflammatory arthritis, spondyloarthropathies, crystal arthropathies, connective tissue diseases
  - Infectious: osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, discitis
  - Neoplastic: primary bone tumours (osteosarcoma, Ewing's sarcoma), metastases, multiple myeloma
  - Vascular: DVT, arterial insufficiency, popliteal artery entrapment
  - Neurological: peripheral neuropathy, motor neuron disease, radiculopathy from non-MSK cause
  - Endocrine/metabolic: osteomalacia, Paget's disease, hyperparathyroidism
  - Visceral referred: gynaecological, GI, renal, pancreatic
  - Haematological: sickle cell disease, haemophilia
- Action: if a masquerade is suspected, do not attribute symptoms to a sports injury. Investigate appropriately. Refer if needed.

## Chapter 8: Introduction to Clinical Biomechanics
- Lower limb ideal biomechanics: neutral stance alignment, joint motion patterns
- Gait cycle phases: loading (heel strike → foot flat), midstance, propulsion, swing
- Running biomechanics: heel strike vs forefoot strike patterns, influence of fatigue
- **Lower limb biomechanical assessment**:
  - Structural (static): Leg length, femoral anteversion, knee alignment (varus/valgus), foot posture (Foot Posture Index), subtalar neutral
  - Functional tests: single-leg squat, hop tests, step-down test
  - Dynamic: running gait analysis (video)
  - Sport-specific: sport-specific movement patterns
- Footwear assessment (Footwear Assessment Tool)
- Conditions related to suboptimal lower limb biomechanics
- Management: biofeedback, movement pattern retraining, foot orthoses, taping
- **Upper limb biomechanics** (throwing):
  - Kinetic chain: force generation from ground up (legs → trunk → shoulder → elbow → hand)
  - Scapular biomechanics in throwing — normal and abnormal
  - Abnormal scapular biomechanics → shoulder pain, impingement, labral injury
  - GIRD — pathological glenohumeral internal rotation deficit
  - Common throwing biomechanical abnormalities

## Chapter 9: Biomechanical Aspects of Injury in Specific Sports
- **Cycling**: knee pain (saddle height, cleat position), low back pain (bike fit)
- **Cricket fast bowling**: mixed action → increased injury risk; lumbar spine stress fractures from extension + rotation
- **Golf**: wrist pain (lead wrist), shoulder pain, hip pain, low back pain
- **Rowing**: low back pain (flexion loading), chest wall pain (rib stress fractures), wrist/forearm pain, knee pain
- **Swimming**: shoulder pain (impingement from repetitive overhead), medial knee pain (breaststroke kick)
- **Tennis**: lateral elbow pain (backhand technique), shoulder injuries (serve)

## Chapter 10: Training Programming and Prescription
- Principles: periodisation, overload, specificity, individualisation
- Conditioning: endurance, speed, agility, resistance, flexibility training
- Training load management for performance enhancement

## Chapter 11: Core Stability (Paul Hodges)
- Core stability = control of trunk position and motion over pelvis for optimal force production in kinetic chain activities
- Three neural strategies for lumbopelvic control: feedforward, feedback, and voluntary
- Motor control involves whole system — not a single muscle (transversus abdominis is part of a system, not the "core")
- Motor control training = motor learning approach (cueing, feedback, progression)
- Interplay between motor control and pain biology
- Clinical application: assessment of motor control → training of motor control → progression to functional tasks

## Chapter 12: Preventing Injury
- Conceptual approach: injury = internal risk factors + external risk factors + inciting event
- Risk management model:
  1. Review literature (risk identification)
  2. Injury surveillance within team
  3. Season analysis — risk profiling
  4. Periodic medical assessment — intrinsic risk factors
  5. Develop targeted prevention program
  6. Implement, monitor compliance, adjust
- Specific prevention programs:
  - Hamstring strains: Nordic hamstring exercise (eccentric)
  - Ankle sprains: proprioceptive training (balance board), bracing/taping
  - ACL injuries: neuromuscular training (e.g., FIFA 11+, PEP program)
  - Overuse injuries: load management, technique modification, structured training programs

## Chapter 13: Recovery
- Recovery strategies: sleep (most important), nutrition, hydration, active recovery, compression garments, hydrotherapy, massage
- Monitoring recovery: subjective (wellness questionnaires, RPE), objective (HRV, jump tests)
- Overtraining syndrome: persistent performance decrement despite continued training; requires prolonged rest

## Chapter 14: Clinical Assessment — Moving from Rote to Rigorous (Chad Cook)
- Differential diagnosis = 3-step process: list → rank → test
- Diagnostic accuracy metrics: sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, likelihood ratios
- Reliability (intra-rater, inter-rater) — a test must be reliable before it can be valid
- Clinical utility: a test must change management to be useful
- Bias in diagnostic assessment: spectrum bias, verification bias, review bias
- The formal diagnostic assessment: clinical findings + diagnostic tests → post-test probability

## Chapter 15: How to Make the Diagnosis
- "Diagnosis" may mean tissue diagnosis OR functional diagnosis OR pathoanatomical diagnosis
- Keys to accurate diagnosis:
  - Allow enough time — a rushed history misses the diagnosis
  - Be a good listener
  - Know the sport — understand the demands and common injuries
  - Discover exact circumstances of injury
  - Obtain accurate symptom description (at time of injury AND current)
  - History of previous similar injury, other injuries, general health
  - Consider WHY the problem occurred: training, equipment, technique, overtraining, psychological, nutritional
  - Training/activity history — recent changes are critical
  - Determine importance of sport to the athlete
- Physical examination routine: inspection → palpation → range of motion → special tests → functional assessment
- Attempt to reproduce symptoms
- Assess local tissues, referred pain, neural mechanosensitivity
- Always examine the spine
- Biomechanical examination
- Functional testing
- Differential diagnosis formulation
- Diagnostic imaging: know what you're looking for BEFORE ordering
- Five imaging habits of effective clinicians: (1) know the question, (2) know sensitivity/specificity, (3) know limitations, (4) don't image when pre-test probability is very low, (5) correlate imaging with clinical findings

## Chapter 16: Patient-Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs)
- PROMs are essential for measuring what matters to patients
- Criteria for a good PROM: appropriate for the setting, measures relevant dimensions, unidimensional items, reliable, responsive
- Practical use: measure at baseline and at intervals to track progress

## Chapter 17: Treatment of Sports Injuries
- **Therapeutic exercise**: mechanotherapy (loading stimulates repair), motor control training, exercise-induced hypoalgesia
- **Acute injury management — POLICE**: Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation
  - "Optimal Loading" replaces "Rest" — early controlled loading promotes healing
  - Do no HARM: Heat, Alcohol, Running, Massage (in acute phase)
- **Manual treatments**: joint mobilisation/manipulation, soft tissue therapy, dry needling, neurodynamic techniques
- **Taping**: proposed mechanisms (mechanical, neuromuscular, placebo), evidence of efficacy
- **Electrophysical agents**: therapeutic ultrasound, TENS, NMES, interferential, laser, electromagnetic therapy, ESWT
- **Medications**:
  - Analgesics: paracetamol first-line for mild-moderate pain
  - NSAIDs: effective for acute pain but may impair tissue healing (bone, tendon, cartilage) — use short courses judiciously
  - Neuropathic pain: gabapentinoids, TCAs, SNRIs
  - Local anaesthetic injections
  - Corticosteroids: short-term benefit, but caution in tendinopathy (may increase recurrence)
  - Prolotherapy, sclerosant, hyaluronic acid, Traumeel, Actovegin
- **Nutraceuticals**: glucosamine/chondroitin (limited evidence), vitamin D (screen and supplement), omega-3, green tea polyphenols
- **Biologics**: PRP (mixed evidence, may benefit some tendinopathies), autologous blood injections, cell therapy
- **Surgery**: arthroscopic (most MSK surgery) vs open

## Chapter 18: Principles of Sports Injury Rehabilitation
- Effective planning is essential — develop a rehabilitation roadmap
- Goal setting: SMART goals co-created with the athlete
- **Four rehabilitation phases**:
  1. **Acute**: protect, control pain/swelling, maintain fitness of uninjured areas
  2. **Restore ADLs**: regain ROM, basic strength, normalise gait/movement patterns
  3. **Return to sport**: sport-specific strength, power, endurance, agility, proprioception, gradual reintroduction to training
  4. **Prevention of re-injury**: ongoing maintenance program, address all risk factors
- When rehabilitation stalls: revisit diagnosis, analyse load, address psychosocial factors, ask a colleague, consider multidisciplinary meeting

## Chapter 19: Return to Play
- **StARRT Framework** (Strategic Assessment of Risk and Risk Tolerance):
  - Step 1 — Tissue Health: symptoms, signs, diagnostic tests, healing stage
  - Step 2 — Tissue Stresses: planned activity (FITT), sport-specific biomechanics, functional capacity, psychological readiness
  - Step 3 — Risk Tolerance Modifiers: competitive importance, athlete desire, financial factors, long-term health, external pressures
  - Decision: RTP if Risk Assessment (Step 1+2) ≤ Risk Tolerance (Step 3)
- RTP decisions involve multiple outcomes — not just re-injury risk but overall athlete wellbeing
- Who decides? The clinician advises; the athlete ultimately decides in a shared decision-making model
- Conflicts arise from differing perspectives of clinician, athlete, coach, agent, family
- A transparent, structured framework minimises conflict
