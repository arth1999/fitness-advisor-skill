# Buttock, Hip, and Groin Pain (Chapters 30–32)

## Chapter 30: Buttock Pain

### Clinical Approach
Buttock pain requires differentiation between:
1. Local pathology in the buttock region
2. Referred pain from lumbar spine (most common cause of buttock pain)
3. Referred pain from hip joint
4. Referred pain from sacroiliac joint

### Key Conditions

#### Myofascial Buttock Pain
- Localised muscle pain in gluteal muscles
- Trigger points in gluteus maximus, medius, minimus, or piriformis
- May refer pain to buttock, posterior thigh, or lateral hip
- Management: soft tissue therapy, dry needling, stretching, addressing underlying biomechanics (gluteal weakness or overload)

#### Referred Pain from Lumbar Spine
- Most common cause of buttock pain
- Somatic referred pain: deep, aching, poorly localised. From disc, facet joint, SIJ.
- Radicular pain: sharp, shooting, dermatomal. From nerve root compression/irritation.
- Examination: lumbar spine assessment (ROM, palpation, neuro exam, dural tension tests)
- Management: treat the underlying lumbar pathology

#### Proximal Hamstring Tendinopathy
- Insertional tendinopathy at ischial tuberosity
- Pain over ischial tuberosity, aggravated by sitting (especially hard surfaces), running, accelerating
- Tenderness directly over ischial tuberosity
- Resisted knee flexion in prone (at various angles) reproduces pain
- More common in runners, sprinters, hurdlers
- Management: as for other tendinopathies — load modification, progressive loading (isometric → eccentric/HSR), address biomechanics (anterior pelvic tilt).

#### Sacroiliac Joint (SIJ) Dysfunction
- Controversial diagnosis — SIJ is a very stable joint with minimal movement (<4°)
- Pain over SIJ region (PSIS area), may refer to buttock, groin, posterior thigh
- Diagnosis: requires multiple positive pain provocation tests (≥3 of: distraction, compression, thigh thrust, Gaenslen's, sacral thrust). Single test unreliable.
- Management: manual therapy, stabilisation exercises, belt/bracing

#### Less Common Causes
- **Piriformis syndrome**: controversial — sciatic nerve compression by piriformis muscle. Buttock pain + sciatica. Diagnosis of exclusion.
- **Ischiofemoral impingement**: impingement between lesser trochanter and ischium. Pain with stride lengthening.
- **Posterior thigh compartment syndrome**: rare
- **Proximal hamstring tendon rupture**: acute avulsion from ischial tuberosity. Sudden pain, palpable defect, bruising. Surgical repair considered in active individuals with >2 cm retraction.
- **Avulsion fracture of ischial tuberosity**: in skeletally immature from forceful hamstring contraction

### Conditions Not to Be Missed
- **Spondyloarthropathies**: ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic arthritis, reactive arthritis. Inflammatory buttock pain (alternating buttock pain, morning stiffness > 60 min, improves with activity, young male). Sacroiliitis on MRI. HLA-B27.
- **Stress fracture of the sacrum**: deep buttock pain, bone tenderness. MRI diagnosis.

## Chapter 31: Hip Pain

### Clinical Approach — Key Principle
Hip pain is challenging because:
- Hip joint pathology often presents as groin pain, not lateral hip pain
- Hip pathology can refer to knee (especially in children/adolescents — SCFE, Perthes)
- Lateral hip pain is usually NOT from the hip joint

### Key Conditions by Anatomical Location

#### Anterior Hip / Groin Pain
**Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI)**:
- Cam type: bony prominence at femoral head-neck junction abuts acetabular rim
- Pincer type: acetabular overcoverage
- Clinical: groin pain, worse with prolonged sitting, flexion activities, sports. FADIR test (Flexion + ADduction + Internal Rotation) positive — reproduces pain.
- Imaging: X-ray (AP + lateral/frog-leg views — alpha angle, cross-over sign). MRI/MR arthrogram for labral tear.
- Sequelae: labral tears, chondral damage, early OA
- Management: conservative (activity modification, strengthening, movement retraining). Surgery (arthroscopic osteochondroplasty + labral repair) if failed conservative care and significant structural impingement.

**Labral tear**:
- Often associated with FAI, also from trauma (twisting, hyperextension)
- Groin pain, clicking, catching, giving way
- FADIR test positive
- MR arthrogram for diagnosis
- Management: conservative (physiotherapy). Arthroscopic surgery (repair vs debridement) if conservative fails.

**Hip osteoarthritis**:
- Groin pain, stiffness, limited ROM (internal rotation first and most affected)
- Older athletes (>50) or younger with predisposing factors (FAI, dysplasia, previous trauma)
- X-ray: joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis/cysts
- Management: conservative (exercise, weight management, analgesia), total hip replacement for end-stage disease

#### Lateral Hip Pain
**Greater trochanteric pain syndrome (GTPS) — NOT "trochanteric bursitis"**:
- Typically gluteus medius/minimus tendinopathy ± bursitis. Isolated bursitis is rare.
- Pain over greater trochanter, worse lying on that side, walking, stairs
- Tenderness over greater trochanter (often posterior to the most prominent point — gluteal tendon insertion)
- Resisted hip abduction in side-lying reproduces pain (especially with hip in adduction — stretch position)
- Common in runners, especially women aged 40–60
- Management: load modification (avoid side-lying on that side, avoid hip adduction postures), progressive loading of gluteal tendons (isometric → isotonic → eccentric/HSR), address biomechanics (Trendelenburg gait, pelvic drop)

**Iliotibial band (ITB) syndrome**: see Chapter 37 (Lateral Knee Pain)
**Referred from lumbar spine**: upper lumbar (L1–3) can refer to lateral hip

#### Posterior Hip / Buttock Pain
See Chapter 30 (Buttock Pain)

#### Conditions NOT to Miss
- **Avascular necrosis of femoral head**: groin pain, limited ROM. Risk factors: corticosteroid use, alcohol excess, sickle cell disease. MRI diagnosis. Requires orthopaedic referral.
- **Stress fracture of femoral neck**: groin or anterior thigh pain, worse with weight-bearing. HIGH-RISK if tension side (superior cortex) — may require surgical fixation. Compression side (inferior cortex) — conservative.
- **SCFE** (Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis): adolescent 12–15 years, often overweight, groin or knee pain, limp, limited internal rotation. ORTHOPAEDIC EMERGENCY — non-weight-bearing and immediate referral.
- **Perthes disease**: child 3–12 years, hip/groin/knee pain, limp, limited internal rotation and abduction.
- **Septic arthritis**: acute hip pain, fever, unable to weight-bear. MEDICAL EMERGENCY. Bloods (CRP, ESR, WCC, blood cultures), hip aspiration, IV antibiotics, surgical washout.

### Key Outcome Measures
- iHOT (International Hip Outcome Tool)
- HOOS (Hip disability and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score)
- HAGOS (Copenhagen Hip and Groin Outcome Score) — for young to middle-aged active patients

## Chapter 32: Groin Pain

### The Complexity of Groin Pain
Groin pain in athletes is frequently multifactorial — multiple entities often coexist. The Doha agreement meeting established clinical entities for groin pain in athletes:

### Clinical Entities (Doha Agreement)

1. **Adductor-related groin pain**:
   - Pain on resisted hip adduction
   - Tenderness at adductor longus origin (pubic bone)
   - Most common type. Acute: adductor strain (commonly adductor longus). Overuse: adductor tendinopathy, often associated with pubic-related groin pain.

2. **Iliopsoas-related groin pain**:
   - Pain on resisted hip flexion (especially in shortened position)
   - Tenderness over iliopsoas (at or distal to inguinal ligament)
   - May have associated snapping hip (internal snapping — iliopsoas tendon over iliopectineal eminence)

3. **Inguinal-related groin pain** ("sports hernia", "Gilmore's groin", "inguinal disruption"):
   - Pain at inguinal canal region
   - Tenderness at superficial inguinal ring (no palpable hernia)
   - Pain aggravated by Valsalva manoeuvre, coughing, sit-ups
   - Pathology: weakness or tear in posterior inguinal wall (transversalis fascia), NOT a true hernia
   - More common in males, soccer players, hockey players
   - Management: conservative first (core stability, adductor strengthening). Surgical repair of posterior inguinal wall if failed conservative.

4. **Pubic-related groin pain** (osteitis pubis):
   - Tenderness at pubic symphysis ± pubic bones
   - Pain with resisted adduction and sit-ups
   - Associated with kicking sports, sudden change of direction
   - X-ray: symphyseal irregularity, sclerosis, cystic change (can be normal variant). MRI: bone marrow oedema at pubic symphysis.
   - Management: rest, progressive loading, address biomechanics, core stability. Prolonged course (months).

5. **Hip-related groin pain**:
   - Pain reproduced by hip examination (FADIR test)
   - FAI, labral tear, OA, other hip joint pathology
   - Always examine the hip joint in groin pain

6. **Other causes**:
   - Referred from lumbar spine (T12–L2)
   - Nerve entrapment (ilioinguinal, genitofemoral, obturator)
   - Stress fracture (pubic ramus, femoral neck)
   - Inguinal or femoral hernia (true hernia — palpable cough impulse)
   - Urogenital pathology (testicular, prostatitis, UTI, ovarian, endometriosis)
   - Spondyloarthropathy (sacroiliitis referring to groin)
   - Apophysitis (AIIS — rectus femoris; ASIS — sartorius) in young athletes

### Clinical Approach
- History: location, onset, aggravating activities (kicking, sprinting, change of direction, sit-ups, coughing), associated symptoms (testicular, urinary)
- Physical examination:
  - Palpation: adductor origin, pubic symphysis, superficial inguinal ring, iliopsoas, hip joint
  - Resisted adduction (in different positions)
  - Resisted hip flexion
  - FADIR test (hip joint)
  - Cough impulse (true hernia)
  - Lumbar spine examination
- Investigations:
  - X-ray: AP pelvis (pubic symphysis, hip joints)
  - MRI: bone marrow oedema, hip joint pathology, soft tissue
  - Ultrasound: dynamic assessment for hernia, tendon pathology
  - Diagnostic injection: can help identify pain source

### Management Principles
- Recognition that multiple entities often coexist — address ALL identified contributors
- Progressive loading program addressing all relevant muscle groups (adductors, abdominals, hip flexors, gluteals)
- Copenhagen adduction exercise (eccentric) for adductor-related groin pain
- Core stability and pelvic control
- Sport-specific rehabilitation
- Address biomechanics and training load
- Surgery indicated only for specific entities (inguinal disruption, FAI) that fail comprehensive conservative management
