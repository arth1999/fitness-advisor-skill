# Thoracic Pain and Low Back Pain (Chapters 28–29)

## Chapter 28: Thoracic and Chest Pain

### Clinical Approach — Key Principle
Thoracic/chest pain in athletes requires careful evaluation because it can represent:
1. Musculoskeletal chest wall pain (most common, benign)
2. Life-threatening visceral pathology (cardiac, pulmonary, aortic, oesophageal)

### Red Flags — Conditions NOT to Miss
- **Cardiac**: exertional chest pain/pressure (angina), associated dyspnoea, diaphoresis, radiation to arm/jaw. Young athletes CAN have cardiac events (hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, anomalous coronary artery, myocarditis). ECG + troponin.
- **Pneumothorax**: sudden pleuritic chest pain, dyspnoea. Hyper-resonance, reduced breath sounds. CXR.
- **Pulmonary embolism**: pleuritic pain, dyspnoea, haemoptysis, risk factors (recent travel, immobilisation, OCP, previous DVT/PE). CTPA.
- **Aortic dissection**: severe tearing interscapular pain, pulse deficit, widened mediastinum on CXR. EMERGENCY.
- **Oesophageal rupture** (Boerhaave's): severe chest pain after vomiting. Subcutaneous emphysema. EMERGENCY.

### Musculoskeletal Causes of Thoracic Pain
- **Costovertebral joint dysfunction**: localised pain, tenderness over costotransverse joint, pain with deep inspiration, rotation
- **Rib stress fracture**: common in rowers (posterolateral ribs), golfers, throwers. Focal bone tenderness, pain with inspiration, positive rib spring test. Bone scan or MRI for diagnosis.
- **Costochondritis / costosternal joint pain**: tenderness at costochondral/sternocostal junctions. Common in rowers from repetitive loading.
- **Intercostal muscle strain**: acute onset with twisting, coughing, or direct trauma
- **Tietze syndrome**: painful, non-suppurative swelling of costochondral junctions (usually 2nd rib). Self-limiting.
- **Slipping rib syndrome**: hypermobility of 8th–10th rib causing clicking/pain with movement
- **Referred pain from thoracic spine**: thoracic facet joint or disc pathology referring to chest wall
- **Muscle contusion**: direct trauma in contact sports

### Assessment
- History: mechanism, location, aggravating factors (inspiration, movement), associated symptoms (dyspnoea, palpitations, fever, cough)
- Examiniation: inspection, palpation (bones, joints, muscles), active/passive ROM (cervical, thoracic, shoulder), rib spring test, neuro assessment, cardiorespiratory exam
- Imaging: CXR (if any red flag or traumatic mechanism), bone scan/MRI (stress fracture), CT (complex fractures, visceral pathology)

## Chapter 29: Low Back Pain (Peter O'Sullivan et al.)

### The Multidimensional Nature of LBP
LBP is NOT a single diagnosis — it is a symptom with multiple potential contributors:
- Physical factors (tissue pathology, loading)
- Lifestyle factors (sedentary behaviour, poor sleep, smoking)
- Psychosocial factors (fear-avoidance, catastrophising, depression, stress, job dissatisfaction)
- Neurophysiological factors (central sensitisation, altered pain processing)
- Individual factors (genetics, comorbidities, past experiences)

### The Triaging Process: Three Key Questions

#### Question 1: Is There Serious Pathology? (RED FLAGS)
- Age < 20 or > 55 (new onset LBP)
- History of cancer (particularly breast, lung, prostate, thyroid, kidney, multiple myeloma)
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fever, night sweats, recent infection
- Saddle anaesthesia (CAUDA EQUINA SYNDROME — SURGICAL EMERGENCY)
- Recent onset bladder or bowel dysfunction (retention or incontinence)
- Progressive neurological deficit (bilateral sciatica, leg weakness)
- Thoracic pain (more concerning than lumbar for serious pathology)
- Constant, progressive, non-mechanical pain (worse at night, not relieved by rest)
- IV drug use, immunosuppression (HIV, long-term corticosteroids, transplant)
- Significant trauma (fall from height, MVA — especially in osteoporotic patient)

**Cauda equina syndrome**: saddle anaesthesia + bladder/bowel disturbance + bilateral sciatica = EMERGENCY MRI and neurosurgical referral.

#### Question 2: Is There a Specific Pathoanatomical Diagnosis?

**Stress fracture of the pars interarticularis (lumbar spondylolysis)**:
- Most common specific structural cause of LBP in young athletes (adolescents)
- High-risk sports: cricket fast bowling (mixed action), gymnastics, diving, weightlifting, tennis (serve), throwing — all involve repeated lumbar extension ± rotation
- Clinical: unilateral LBP, worse with extension (especially single-leg hyperextension — stork test — pain on ipsilateral side)
- Imaging: CT (best for bony detail — pars defect), MRI (marrow oedema suggestive of active stress reaction). SPECT sensitive for active lesion.
- Classification: early stress reaction (good prognosis with rest) → stress fracture → established non-union (poorer prognosis)
- Management: rest from aggravating sport (usually 3–6 months for healing), bracing (controversial), rehabilitation (core stability, address technique, kinetic chain), gradual return to sport only when asymptomatic

**Spondylolisthesis**:
- Forward slippage of vertebra on vertebra. Most commonly L5 on S1 (isthmic type from bilateral pars defects).
- May be asymptomatic or cause LBP ± radicular symptoms (L5 nerve root)
- Grading: I (<25% slip), II (25–50%), III (50–75%), IV (>75%), V (spondyloptosis)
- Management: most respond to conservative management (core stability, hamstring flexibility, avoid hyperextension). Surgery (fusion) for high-grade slips, progressive slip, or persistent symptoms despite conservative care.

**Acute radiculopathy ± nerve root compression**:
- Leg pain > back pain
- Dermatomal distribution (L3–S1)
- Positive neurological findings: sensory deficit, motor weakness (myotomal), reflex loss
- Positive dural tension signs: SLR (L4–S1), femoral nerve stretch test (L2–4)
- MRI is gold standard imaging — only indicated if neurological deficit, persistent symptoms > 6 weeks despite conservative care, or red flags
- Management: most resolve conservatively (time, analgesia, physiotherapy). Epidural corticosteroid injection for severe radicular pain. Surgery (microdiscectomy) for: cauda equina syndrome, progressive neurological deficit, intractable pain despite 6+ weeks conservative care.

**Vertebral endplate changes (Modic type 1)**:
- MRI finding of vertebral endplate oedema. May represent an inflammatory pain generator.
- Associated with degenerative disc disease
- Management: conservative (exercise, load management). Role of intradiscal therapies unclear.

#### Question 3: If Neither — Non-Specific LBP
80–90% of LBP in athletes is non-specific (no identifiable pathoanatomical cause). Manage by clinical sub-grouping:

**Movement-based sub-groups**:
1. **Flexion pattern**: pain with bending forward, sitting, lifting. Contributing factors: limited hip flexion → excessive lumbar flexion. Management: hip mobility, motor control training for flexion, avoid sustained flexion postures.
2. **Extension pattern**: pain with standing, walking, extension activities. Management: extension control exercises, postural correction, core stability.
3. **Movement control impairment**: pain when the spine moves uncontrolled into a provocative direction during functional tasks. Management: motor control training — teach the patient to control the symptomatic movement.
4. **Multidimensional**: significant psychosocial contributors (high fear-avoidance, catastrophising, depression, stress). Management: pain neuroscience education, graded activity/exposure, address psychosocial factors (CBT principles), multidisciplinary approach.

### Clinical Approach — History
- Pain location, radiation, behaviour (mechanical vs non-mechanical)
- Aggravating and relieving factors (flexion vs extension)
- 24-hour pattern (morning stiffness — inflammatory; night pain — serious pathology)
- Red flag screening
- Mechanism of onset (specific incident vs insidious)
- Sport and training history — identify load errors, technique issues
- Psychosocial screening: fear-avoidance beliefs, catastrophising, depression, stress, compensation/legal issues

### Physical Examination
- Posture (standing, sitting)
- Active ROM (flexion, extension, lateral flexion — note pattern, range, pain provocation)
- Passive ROM (overpressure at symptom-limited range)
- Neurological examination (myotomes L2–S1, dermatomes, reflexes — patellar L4, Achilles S1, Babinski)
- Dural tension tests (SLR, femoral nerve stretch test, slump test)
- Palpation (spinous processes, paraspinal muscles, SIJ)
- Hip examination (ROM, FADIR test) — hip pathology commonly mimics LBP
- Sacroiliac joint provocation tests (pain provocation tests — multiple positive tests required, single test unreliable)
- Motor control assessment (ability to dissociate hip from lumbar movement, ability to maintain neutral spine under load)
- Functional assessment: squat, single-leg squat, sport-specific movements

### Imaging — When to Image
- Red flags present → urgent MRI
- Neurological deficit → MRI
- Suspected spondylolysis (young athlete, extension pain) → CT or MRI
- Persistent symptoms > 6 weeks despite appropriate conservative care → MRI
- Pre-operative planning
- Do NOT image for acute non-specific LBP without red flags — overdiagnosis of incidental findings causes harm

### Rehabilitation Following Low Back Pain
1. **Sporting technique**: correct faulty technique contributing to load (e.g., mixed action in fast bowling)
2. **Optimal motor control**: train trunk muscle coordination for spine protection
3. **Flexibility**: address relevant flexibility deficits (hip flexors, hamstrings, thoracic spine)
4. **Graduated return to sport**: progressive loading, sport-specific movement patterns
5. **Prevention**: ongoing maintenance program, load management, regular screening
