# Anterior and Posterior Thigh Pain (Chapters 33–34)

## Chapter 33: Anterior Thigh Pain

### Key Conditions

#### Quadriceps Contusion ("Corked Thigh" / "Charley Horse")
- Most common acute thigh injury — direct blunt trauma to quadriceps
- Mechanism: direct impact (knee, helmet, ball, equipment) into quadriceps muscle belly
- Severity grading based on knee flexion ROM:
  - Mild: >90° knee flexion → RTP 5–7 days
  - Moderate: 45–90° knee flexion → RTP 2–4 weeks
  - Severe: <45° knee flexion → RTP 6–12 weeks

**Acute Management**:
- Immediately: knee flexed to maximum tolerated position (stretch the muscle to limit haemorrhage volume) and held with compression bandage. Apply ice.
- Maintain in flexed position (elastic wrap) for first 24 hours
- Pain-free isometric quadriceps contractions as soon as tolerated
- Gradual increase in knee flexion ROM
- When >120° knee flexion achieved → progressive strengthening, cycling, jogging
- DO NOT aggressively massage or passively stretch — risk of myositis ossificans

**Complications**:
- **Myositis ossificans**: heterotopic bone formation within muscle (usually vastus intermedius). Risk factors: severe contusion, early aggressive massage/stretch, premature RTP. Management: rest until maturation (6–12 months — confirmed by X-ray showing well-defined cortical margins). Surgical excision only if functionally limiting after full maturation.
- **Acute compartment syndrome**: rare but surgical emergency. Increasing pain (out of proportion), pain with passive stretch, tense compartment, ± neurological/vascular compromise.
- **Large haematoma**: aspiration may be considered (risk of infection, re-accumulation)

#### Quadriceps Strain / Tear
- Acute muscle strain from forceful contraction or stretch
- Common in sprinting (rectus femoris — two-joint muscle, most commonly strained quadriceps muscle)
- **Distal quadriceps strain**: pain proximal to patella. Distinguish from quadriceps tendinopathy.
- **Proximal rectus femoris strain**: pain at AIIS. May present as groin pain.
- **Complete rectus femoris tear**: palpable defect, significant functional loss, may require surgical repair in elite athletes
- Muscle strain grading: Grade I (few fibres), Grade II (partial tear), Grade III (complete rupture)
- Management: as for other acute muscle strains — initial protection + optimal loading, progressive rehabilitation

#### Avulsion Injury (Young Athletes)
- AIIS avulsion (rectus femoris) or ASIS avulsion (sartorius)
- Mechanism: sudden forceful contraction (kicking, sprinting)
- Tenderness at apophysis, pain with resisted muscle contraction
- X-ray confirms avulsion fragment
- Management: most conservative (rest + progressive loading). Surgery for significantly displaced fragments (>2 cm).

#### Stress Fracture of the Femur
- Anterior thigh or groin pain, worse with weight-bearing
- Femoral shaft: low-risk site. Femoral neck: HIGH-RISK IF TENSION SIDE (superior cortex).
- MRI diagnosis
- Femoral neck tension side: non-weight-bearing ± surgical fixation

#### Nerve Entrapments
- **Meralgia paraesthetica**: lateral femoral cutaneous nerve entrapment → numbness/burning in anterolateral thigh. Aggravated by tight clothing/belts, obesity, pregnancy. Conservative management.
- **Femoral nerve injury**: anterior thigh weakness (quadriceps), sensory loss, reduced patellar reflex. Rare in sports.

#### Referred Pain
- Lumbar spine (L2–4) → anterior thigh
- Hip joint → anterior thigh/groin (especially in children — SCFE, Perthes)

## Chapter 34: Posterior Thigh Pain (Hamstring Injuries)

### Functional Anatomy
- Hamstring group: biceps femoris (long and short heads), semitendinosus, semimembranosus
- All except short head of biceps femoris cross both hip and knee → two-joint muscles, high injury risk
- Functions: hip extension, knee flexion. During sprinting, eccentrically control hip flexion and knee extension in late swing phase (critical injury phase).

### Clinical Approach
- History: mechanism (sprinting vs stretching), acute vs gradual onset, "pop" sensation, ability to continue, location (proximal vs mid-belly vs distal), previous injury
- Physical examination: inspection (bruising — may appear 24–48h later, distal to injury site), palpation (defect, tenderness), ROM (straight leg raise for hamstring length, pain with passive SLR), resisted knee flexion (at various angles to isolate medial vs lateral hamstrings — 0° = both, 90° = medial, 15° = lateral), slump test (neural mechanosensitivity)
- Investigations: MRI (best for assessing extent, anatomical location, retraction, tendon involvement) or ultrasound. MRI findings correlate with RTP time.

### Making the Diagnosis — Integrating Assessment
From history and examination, classify into:

### Type I: Sprinting-Related Acute Hamstring Strain
- Mechanism: high-speed running, typically in late swing phase (hamstrings eccentrically contract to decelerate knee extension and hip flexion)
- Typically involves biceps femoris long head (myotendinous junction)
- Sudden onset posterior thigh pain, may hear/feel "pop"
- Can't continue sprinting
- Prognosis: worse than Type II — longer RTP, higher recurrence rate
- Risk factors: previous hamstring injury (strongest), age, hamstring weakness (especially eccentric), fatigue, limited flexibility (especially with neural tension), quadriceps imbalance, lumbo-pelvic control deficits

### Type II: Stretch-Related Hamstring Injury
- Mechanism: excessive combined hip flexion + knee extension (dancers, high kick, hurdle stretch)
- Typically involves semimembranosus and proximal tendon
- Onset may be more gradual than Type I
- Often proximal (close to ischial tuberosity), may involve free tendon
- Prognosis: generally better — faster RTP, lower recurrence

### Management of Hamstring Injuries
**Phase 1 (Acute — first few days)**: protection, optimal loading (pain-free ROM, isometric contractions), ice, compression. Avoid excessive stretch.

**Phase 2 (Subacute — after acute pain settles)**: progressive loading. Eccentric hamstring exercises (Nordic hamstring exercise, Romanian deadlifts, single-leg deadlifts). Address neural mechanosensitivity (slump stretch progression). Lumbo-pelvic control exercises. Progressive running (straight line jogging → tempo running → sprinting).

**Phase 3 (Return to sport)**: sport-specific drills, change of direction, maximal sprinting. Graduated return to training and competition.

### Prognosis Factors for Worse Outcome
- Involvement of proximal free tendon (longer RTP, higher recurrence)
- >50% cross-sectional area on MRI
- Palpable defect
- Proximity to ischial tuberosity
- Previous hamstring injury
- Referred pain pattern (suggests neural involvement)
- Type I (sprinting) mechanism

### Risk Factors for Acute Hamstring Strain
**Intrinsic**:
- Previous hamstring injury (STRONGEST risk factor — 3–6× increased risk)
- Older age
- Hamstring weakness (especially eccentric)
- Hamstring-quadriceps strength imbalance
- Reduced hamstring flexibility (but evidence inconsistent)
- Neural mechanosensitivity (positive slump test)
- Lumbo-pelvic control deficits (anterior pelvic tilt → pre-stretched hamstrings)
- Fatigue (injuries more common late in halves/games)

**Extrinsic**:
- Inadequate warm-up
- Sudden increase in training load (volume, intensity)
- Poor playing surface

### Prevention
- Nordic hamstring exercise (eccentric hamstring training) — strongest evidence
- Graduated training load management
- Address lumbo-pelvic control
- Adequate warm-up
- Include sprinting in regular training (don't just play matches)
- Previous injury rehabilitation must be comprehensive before RTP

### Referred Pain to Posterior Thigh
- Lumbar spine (L5–S2): most common source of referred posterior thigh pain. Somatic referred or radicular. Differentiate by lumbar examination, dural tension tests (SLR, slump), neurological exam.
- Sacroiliac joint
- Hamstring tendinopathy (proximal — see Chapter 30 Buttock Pain)
- Vascular claudication (rare in young athletes but consider popliteal artery entrapment)
