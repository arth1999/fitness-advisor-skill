# Clinical Patterns & Frameworks — Brukner & Khan Vol 1

## 1. THE UNIVERSAL DIAGNOSTIC FRAMEWORK (Chapters 14–15)

Every injury assessment follows this sequence:

```
HISTORY → PHYSICAL EXAMINATION → DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS → INVESTIGATIONS → DIAGNOSIS → TREATMENT
```

### History Components (must cover ALL)
1. **Exact circumstances** of injury (mechanism, position, force, sound)
2. **Symptom description**: pain location, character, onset (sudden vs gradual), behaviour (mechanical pattern), severity
3. **Previous similar injury** (strongest predictor of future injury)
4. **Other injuries** (kinetic chain)
5. **General health**: medications, allergies, medical conditions, family history
6. **Training/activity history**: recent changes in load (volume, intensity, frequency, surface, equipment, technique)
7. **Consider WHY the problem occurred**: overtraining, biomechanics, equipment, technique, psychological factors, nutritional factors
8. **Importance of sport to the athlete**: influences compliance, RTP urgency

### Physical Examination Sequence
1. Look (inspection — swelling, deformity, atrophy, posture)
2. Feel (palpation — temperature, tenderness, defect, crepitus)
3. Move (active ROM, passive ROM, resisted movements — distinguishes contractile from non-contractile)
4. Special tests (specific diagnostic manoeuvres for suspected pathology)
5. Functional assessment (sport-specific movements)
6. Always examine the joint above and below
7. Always examine the contralateral side for comparison
8. Assess neural mechanosensitivity
9. Screen the kinetic chain
10. Reproduce the patient's symptoms if possible

### The Five Key Imaging Habits
1. Know the clinical question before ordering imaging
2. Know the sensitivity and specificity of the test you are ordering
3. Know the limitations of each imaging modality
4. Do not order imaging when the pre-test probability of pathology is very low
5. Always correlate imaging findings with clinical findings — treat the patient, not the scan

## 2. TISSUE-BASED DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS PATTERN

For ANY anatomical region, systematically consider each tissue type:

| Tissue | Acute Pathology | Overuse/Chronic Pathology |
|--------|----------------|--------------------------|
| **Bone** | Fracture, periosteal contusion | Bone stress injury (stress reaction → stress fracture), apophysitis (young athletes) |
| **Articular Cartilage** | Chondral fracture, osteochondral injury | Chondropathy, osteoarthritis |
| **Fibrocartilage** | Acute tear (meniscus, labrum, disc) | Degenerative tear |
| **Joint** | Dislocation, subluxation | Instability, synovitis |
| **Ligament** | Sprain/tear (Grade I–III) | Laxity, chronic instability |
| **Muscle** | Strain/tear (Grade I–III), contusion, cramp | Myofascial pain, chronic compartment syndrome |
| **Tendon** | Tear/rupture (acute) | Tendinopathy (reactive → dysrepair → degenerative) |
| **Bursa** | Traumatic bursitis (haemorrhagic) | Chronic bursitis, impingement |
| **Nerve** | Neuropraxia | Nerve entrapment, chronic radiculopathy |
| **Fascia** | Acute tear/rupture | Fasciopathy (e.g., plantar fasciopathy) |
| **Skin** | Laceration, abrasion, blister | Callus, corn, skin infection |

## 3. TRAINING LOAD ERROR PATTERN (Causes of Overuse Injury)

When an athlete presents with an overuse injury, identify the training load error:

### Excessive Total Training Load
- Too much volume (distance, repetitions, time)
- Too high intensity
- Too frequent sessions
- Insufficient recovery between sessions

### Sudden Change in Training (The "Too Much, Too Soon" Pattern)
- Rapid increase in volume (>10% per week is a risk)
- Return to full training after a break without graduated reintroduction
- Pre-season intensity spikes
- Competition schedule congestion

### Change in Training Surface
- Hard surfaces → increased ground reaction forces → bone stress
- Uneven surfaces → altered biomechanics → muscle/tendon overload
- Cambered surfaces → asymmetrical loading
- Track → cross-country transition

### Change in Equipment
- New or worn-out footwear
- Different footwear type (minimalist, motion control)
- Change in racquet (weight, grip size, string tension)
- Change in bicycle setup (saddle height, cleat position)

### Change in Technique
- New skill acquisition with poor biomechanics
- Technical flaw producing abnormal tissue loads
- Coaching change introducing different movement patterns

## 4. KINETIC CHAIN DYSFUNCTION PATTERN

Pain at one site often originates from dysfunction at a distant site in the kinetic chain:

| Symptom Site | Common Kinetic Chain Source |
|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Shoulder pain** (thrower) | Hip weakness → trunk rotation deficit → scapular dyskinesis → excessive glenohumeral load, contralateral hip internal rotation deficit |
| **Elbow pain** (thrower) | GIRD → increased valgus stress at elbow |
| **Low back pain** | Hip tightness (limited hip flexion → excessive lumbar flexion), thoracic stiffness, hamstring tightness |
| **Anterior knee pain** | Hip abductor/external rotator weakness → femoral internal rotation → patellofemoral malalignment, foot overpronation |
| **Lateral knee pain** (ITB) | Hip abductor weakness (gluteus medius) → Trendelenburg → increased ITB tension |
| **Achilles tendinopathy** | Limited ankle dorsiflexion, hip weakness → altered load distribution |
| **Medial tibial stress syndrome** | Foot overpronation → excessive eccentric load on tibialis posterior and soleus |
| **Hamstring strain** | Anterior pelvic tilt → hamstring pre-stretched, gluteal inhibition → hamstring overload, limited hip flexion |

### Kinetic Chain Assessment
- Always examine the entire kinetic chain, not just the site of pain
- For lower limb: foot → ankle → knee → hip → pelvis → lumbar spine
- For upper limb: hand → wrist → elbow → shoulder → scapula → thoracic spine → lumbar spine → hips
- Identify the weakest link — this is often NOT at the site of pain

## 5. PAIN PATTERN DIFFERENTIATION

### Mechanical vs Non-Mechanical Pain
| Feature | Mechanical | Non-Mechanical (suspect masquerade) |
|---------|------------|--------------------------------------|
| Onset | Related to activity | Unrelated, may be present at rest |
| Relief | Rest | Not relieved by rest |
| Pattern | Predictable, reproducible | Unpredictable |
| Night pain | Only after activity, relieved by position change | Constant, wakes from sleep |
| Morning stiffness | Brief (<30 min) if present | Prolonged (>60 min — inflammatory) |
| Systemic symptoms | Absent | May have fever, weight loss, malaise |

### Tissue Source Differentiation by Pain Behaviour
| Tissue | Pain Characteristic | Aggravating Factors | Relieving Factors |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Bone** | Deep, boring, constant when severe | Weight-bearing, impact | Unloading |
| **Joint** | Deep ache, intermittent, worse with stiffness | Prolonged position, end-range movement | Gentle movement, unloading |
| **Ligament** | Sharp, localised with stretch | Stressing injured ligament | Protection, unloading |
| **Muscle** | Dull ache, cramping, may be referred | Contraction, stretch of injured muscle | Rest, gentle stretch |
| **Tendon** | Localised, warms up with activity but hurts after | Repetitive loading, stretch | Not rest alone — responds to optimal loading |
| **Nerve** | Burning, shooting, electric, numbness/tingling | Nerve stretch/compression positions | Changing position |
| **Bursa** | Localised with direct pressure | Direct compression | Off-loading compression |

## 6. OVERUSE INJURY PATTERN BY ANATOMICAL REGION

### Shoulder Pain — Differential Diagnostic Pathway
```
1. Rule out: referred pain from cervical spine, thoracic outlet, visceral (phrenic nerve/diaphragm — C3-5)
2. Age < 25 → instability (TUBS vs AMBRI), atraumatic instability (AIOS)
3. Age 25–45 → impingement, rotator cuff tendinopathy, labral (SLAP), biceps pathology, AC joint
4. Age > 45 → rotator cuff tear (degenerative), adhesive capsulitis, AC joint OA
5. Overhead athlete → GIRD, internal impingement, SLAP, scapular dyskinesis
6. Always: check cervical spine, assess scapulohumeral rhythm, screen kinetic chain
```

### Anterior Knee Pain — Differential Diagnostic Pathway
```
1. Rule out: hip referred pain (particularly in adolescents — SCFE), serious pathology
2. Determine tissue source:
   - Retropatellar/parapatellar, worse with stairs/sitting → patellofemoral pain (PFP)
   - Infrapatellar tendon, focal tenderness → patellar tendinopathy
   - Fat pad tenderness either side of patellar tendon → fat pad irritation
   - Swelling + locking/giving way → patellar instability, chondral injury
   - Tibial tuberosity pain in adolescent → Osgood-Schlatter
   - Diffuse anterior pain after trauma → patella fracture, patellar tendon rupture
3. For PFP, identify contributing factors:
   - Proximal: hip weakness (abductors, external rotators), quadriceps weakness
   - Local: VMO timing deficit, patellar maltracking
   - Distal: foot overpronation, rearfoot eversion
   - Training: load errors, running biomechanics
```

### Low Back Pain — Diagnostic Triage Pathway
```
BASIC TRIAGE (3 questions):
1. Is there serious pathology? → RED FLAGS screen
2. Is there a specific pathoanatomical diagnosis? → spondylolysis, spondylolisthesis, radiculopathy, Modic changes
3. If neither → Non-specific LBP → clinical sub-group by movement pattern

RED FLAGS → immediate investigation/referral:
- Cauda equina syndrome (saddle anaesthesia, incontinence — EMERGENCY)
- Cancer history + new back pain
- Infection (fever, IV drug use, immunosuppression)
- Vertebral fracture (major trauma, osteoporosis, prolonged corticosteroid use)

SPECIFIC DIAGNOSES TO IDENTIFY:
- Spondylolysis: young athlete, extension-loading sport, pain on single-leg hyperextension
- Spondylolisthesis: palpable step, hamstring tightness, may be asymptomatic
- Radiculopathy: dermatomal pain ± sensory/motor deficit, positive SLR, MRI correlate
- Modic type 1: MRI finding (endplate oedema), may be pain generator

NON-SPECIFIC LBP SUB-GROUPS:
- Flexion pattern → pain sitting/flexion, limited hip mobility
- Extension pattern → pain standing/walking, poor extension control
- Movement control impairment → pain with uncontrolled movement
- Multidimensional → high psychosocial factors (fear-avoidance, catastrophising, depression)
```

### Groin Pain — Differential Diagnostic Pathway
```
GROIN PAIN IN ATHLETES — CONSIDER:
1. Adductor-related: pain on resisted adduction, tenderness at adductor origin
2. Iliopsoas-related: pain on resisted hip flexion, tenderness over iliopsoas
3. Inguinal-related: pain at inguinal canal, tenderness at superficial inguinal ring (sports hernia)
4. Pubic-related: tenderness at pubic symphysis ± osteitis pubis
5. Hip-related: FADIR test positive (femoroacetabular impingement, labral tear, OA)
6. Other: referred from lumbar spine, SIJ, stress fracture (pubic ramus, femoral neck), nerve entrapment (ilioinguinal, genitofemoral, obturator)
7. RED FLAGS: testicular pain, inguinal hernia (cough impulse), systemic symptoms → spondyloarthropathy
```

### Acute Knee Injury — Serious Injury Detection
```
KEY QUESTION: "Does this patient have a significant knee injury?"

IMMEDIATE REFERRAL INDICATIONS:
- Unable to straight-leg raise (ruptured extensor mechanism)
- Gross deformity (dislocation, fracture)
- Vascular compromise (absent pulses — popliteal artery injury)
- Neurological compromise (foot drop — peroneal nerve)

HAEMARTHROSIS (tense knee effusion within 2 hours of injury):
In adults, acute haemarthrosis = 80% chance of ACL tear, meniscal tear, or osteochondral fracture
In children, also consider patellar dislocation

KEY HISTORY ELEMENTS:
- Mechanism: contact vs non-contact, direction of force, foot position
- "Pop" sound → ACL tear
- Immediate vs delayed swelling → haemarthrosis (immediate, within 2h) vs reactive effusion (delayed, 12–24h)
- Giving way → ligamentous instability
- Locking → meniscal tear (bucket handle)
- Ability to continue playing → if could not, suggest significant injury
```

### Hamstring Strain — Type Classification (Askling)
```
TYPE I: Sprinting-related
- Mechanism: high-speed running, late swing phase
- Location: typically involves biceps femoris long head
- Prognosis: generally worse than Type II, longer RTP, higher recurrence

TYPE II: Stretch-related
- Mechanism: excessive lengthening (dancers, kicking, high kick)
- Location: typically involves semimembranosus and proximal tendon
- Prognosis: generally better, less recurrence

Prognosis factors for worse outcome:
- Proximity to ischial tuberosity
- >50% cross-sectional area involvement
- Palpable defect
- Previous hamstring injury
- Referred pain (suggesting neural involvement)
```

### Ankle Sprain — The Difficult Ankle
```
Acute ankle sprain with persistent pain > 6 weeks = "The Difficult Ankle"

SYSTEMATIC RE-EVALUATION:
1. Was the original diagnosis correct? — Consider fractures missed on initial X-ray (Ottawa rules)
2. Intra-articular pathology:
   - Osteochondral lesion of the talar dome (OLT) → characteristic anterolateral or posteromedial location
   - Ankle impingement (anterolateral, anterior, posterior)
3. Tendon pathology:
   - Peroneal tendon dislocation/subluxation → palpable snap with eversion
   - Peroneal tendon tear (longitudinal split)
   - Tibialis posterior tendinopathy
4. Instability:
   - Mechanical (ligament laxity — anterior drawer, talar tilt)
   - Functional (proprioceptive deficit — balance deficits)
5. Other:
   - Fracture of anterior process of calcaneus, lateral or posterior process of talus
   - Base of 5th metatarsal fracture (avulsion vs Jones fracture — different management!)
   - Sinus tarsi syndrome
   - Complex regional pain syndrome type 1 (CRPS)
   - Superior peroneal retinaculum injury
```

## 7. THE TENDON PAIN PATTERN

### Midportion vs Insertional Tendinopathy
| Feature | Midportion | Insertional |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Location | 2–6 cm proximal to insertion | At bone-tendon junction |
| Mechanism | Tensile overload | Compression + tension |
| Key aggravating factor | Stretch + load | Compression (e.g., dorsiflexion in Achilles) |
| Management difference | Stretch may help | Stretch often aggravates — avoid compression |

### Tendon Pain Behaviour — The Warm-Up Phenomenon
- Pain at the start of activity → decreases as activity continues (warm-up) → returns and worsens after activity → stiffness the next morning
- This pattern strongly suggests tendinopathy
- The presence of a warm-up phenomenon = reactive or dysrepair stage (reversible)
- Absence of warm-up phenomenon + constant pain = may be more degenerative

## 8. CONCUSSION PATTERN — On-Field Decision Algorithm

```
SUSPECTED CONCUSSION:

STEP 1: RED FLAGS CHECK
If ANY red flag present → emergency management, ambulance, hospital
(RED FLAGS: neck pain, double vision, weakness/tingling, severe/increasing headache,
seizure, LOC, vomiting, agitated/combative, deteriorating consciousness)

STEP 2: On-Field Assessment (Pocket CRT / SCAT components)
- Maddocks questions (venue, half, who scored, team last week, result last week)
- Glasgow Coma Scale
- Cervical spine assessment

STEP 3: IF CONCUSSION CONFIRMED
→ Remove from play immediately
→ NO same-day return to play (mandatory)
→ Serial monitoring for deterioration (first 4 hours critical)
→ No alcohol, no driving, no medications without medical advice
→ Relative rest (24–48h) then gradual return to activity
→ Graduated RTP protocol (minimum 6 days)

STEP 4: Urgent CT if:
- GCS < 15 at 2 hours, skull fracture signs, ≥2 vomiting episodes, 
  seizure, focal neurological deficit, coagulopathy, age >65

STEP 5: The "Difficult" Concussion (symptoms > 10–14 days)
→ Refer to specialist multidisciplinary concussion service
```

## 9. THE YOUNG ATHLETE PATTERN — Unique Considerations

### Growth-Related Conditions
- Apophysitis (traction at tendon-bone growth plate junction) → Osgood-Schlatter, Sever's, SLJ, medial epicondyle apophysitis ("Little Leaguer's elbow")
- Physeal stress fractures (growth plate) → distal radius (gymnasts), proximal humerus (throwers)
- SCFE (Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis) → hip/groin/knee pain in overweight adolescent 12–15 years. EMERGENCY — requires immediate non-weight-bearing and orthopaedic referral
- Perthes disease → hip pain, limp, limited internal rotation in child 3–12 years

### Key Differences from Adults
- Growing bone (physis) is the weakest link — injury pattern differs
- Ligaments are stronger than physes → what would be a ligament sprain in an adult is a physeal fracture in a child
- Non-linearity of growth — growth spurts alter biomechanics and injury risk
- Bone remodels faster → shorter healing times for fractures
- Always check growth remaining (Tanner staging, bone age) before surgical decisions

## 10. THE MUSCLE CONTUSION PATTERN — Quadriceps Contusion Severity

### Severity Grading and RTP
| Grade | ROM (knee flexion) | RTP Time |
|-------|-------------------|----------|
| Mild | >90° | 5–7 days |
| Moderate | 45–90° | 2–4 weeks |
| Severe | <45° | 6–12 weeks |

### Key Complications
- **Myositis ossificans**: heterotopic bone formation in muscle. Risk factors: severe contusion, early aggressive massage/passive stretch, premature RTP. Management: avoid aggravating factors, wait for maturation (6–12 months) before surgical excision if needed.
- **Compartment syndrome**: rare but surgical emergency. Assess distal pulses, sensation, pain with passive stretch.

## 11. THE RECOVERY PATTERN

### Factors That Impair Recovery
From Chapter 13, identify barriers when rehabilitation stalls:

1. **Inadequate load management** (training volume/intensity too high)
2. **Inadequate recovery periods** between sessions
3. **Sleep disturbance** (<7 hours, poor quality)
4. **Nutritional deficits** (energy availability, protein, vitamin D, calcium, iron)
5. **Psychosocial factors** (stress, anxiety, depression, fear-avoidance, catastrophising)
6. **Environmental challenges** (family, work, facilities access)
7. **Incorrect initial diagnosis** — revisit the diagnosis and re-examine
8. **Failure to address kinetic chain dysfunction**
9. **Premature progression** through rehabilitation phases
10. **Non-compliance** with rehabilitation program

### When Recovery Stalls — Action Plan
1. Revisit the diagnosis and functional assessment
2. Analyse total daily/weekly load — is there enough recovery?
3. Address psychosocial barriers (open questions, probe psychological status)
4. Consider a period of reduced rehabilitation — a few days off may help
5. Ask a colleague — multidisciplinary meeting, second opinion, referral back to physician/surgeon

## 12. INJURY PREVENTION PATTERN — The Inciting Event Model

Injury occurs when the sum of internal and external risk factors predisposes the athlete, and an inciting event (mechanism of injury) triggers the actual injury.

### Three Categories of Risk Factor
1. **Internal (intrinsic)**: age, sex, body composition, previous injury, strength deficits, flexibility, biomechanics, anatomical alignment, fitness level, psychological state
2. **External (extrinsic)**: training load, equipment, environment, playing surface, rules/refereeing, coaching
3. **Inciting event**: the specific biomechanical event that causes injury (e.g., valgus collapse for ACL, sudden acceleration for hamstring strain)

### Prevention Program Design
1. Identify specific risk factors for the injury in question
2. Select evidence-based interventions targeting those risk factors
3. Implement with adequate dose and compliance
4. Monitor injury rates to evaluate effectiveness
5. Adjust based on feedback
