# Cheatsheet — Brukner & Khan Clinical Sports Medicine Vol 1

## 1. ACUTE INJURY MANAGEMENT: POLICE (replaces RICE)

| Component | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| **P**rotection | Protect the injured tissue from further damage (brief period of unloading — days, not weeks) |
| **O**ptimal Loading | Replace rest with graded, controlled mechanical loading to stimulate repair |
| **I**ce | Analgesic effect, reduce swelling, limit secondary injury. Apply 20 min every 2 hours |
| **C**ompression | Elastic bandage or compression garment to limit oedema |
| **E**levation | Elevate injured limb above heart level to reduce hydrostatic pressure |

**Do NO HARM**: No Heat, No Alcohol, No Running/activity, No Massage (in first 48–72 hours)

## 2. BONE STRESS INJURY (BSI) — Risk Stratification

### High-Risk Sites (require aggressive management, longer RTP)
- Anterior cortex of tibia (tension side)
- Femoral neck (superior cortex)
- Base of 5th metatarsal
- Sesamoids of great toe
- Tarsal navicular
- Pars interarticularis
- Medial malleolus
- Patella

### Low-Risk Sites (conservative management)
- Posteromedial tibial border
- Fibula
- Femoral shaft
- Pelvis (pubic ramus, sacrum)
- Calcaneus
- Metatarsal shafts (2–4)

### MRI Grading (Fredericson classification)
| Grade | MRI Finding | Approximate RTP |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Periosteal oedema only (STIR) | 3–4 weeks |
| 2 | Bone marrow oedema on T2/STIR | 4–6 weeks |
| 3 | Bone marrow oedema on T1 and T2 | 8–12 weeks |
| 4 | Frank fracture line visible | 12–16+ weeks |

## 3. TENDINOPATHY: Cook-Purdam Continuum Model

| Stage | Pathology | Clinical Features | Management |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|------------|
| **Reactive** | Acute overload, proteoglycan increase, no collagen change | Acute pain, swollen tendon | Load reduction, isometric exercise, short-term NSAIDs |
| **Tendon Dysrepair** | Attempted healing, collagen disorganisation, neovascularisation | Persistent pain, variable function | Progressive loading, eccentric exercise, address biomechanics |
| **Degenerative** | Advanced collagen breakdown, cell death, little healing capacity | Chronic pain, nodular tendon, may be asymptomatic | Load management, heavy slow resistance, surgery if failed conservative |

**Key principle**: Tendon responds to load. Under-load = degeneration. Over-load = reactive tendinopathy. The goal is optimal loading.

## 4. RETURN TO PLAY — StARRT Framework

### Step 1: Assess Tissue Health
- Symptom severity (pain, swelling)
- Clinical signs (ROM, strength, stability)
- Diagnostic tests (imaging, functional tests)
- Tissue healing stage

### Step 2: Assess Tissue Stresses
- Planned activity type, intensity, frequency, duration (FITT)
- Sport-specific biomechanical demands
- Position played / movement patterns required
- Functional capacity (endurance, proprioception, ROM)
- Psychological state (fear-avoidance, confidence)

### Step 3: Risk Tolerance Modifiers
- Competitive level (playoffs vs pre-season)
- Athlete's desire to compete
- Financial implications
- Long-term health consequences
- Pressure from external parties (coach, agent, family)
- Litigation risk

**Decision rule**: If Risk Assessment (Step 1 + 2) > Risk Tolerance (Step 3) → Do NOT RTP. If Risk Assessment ≤ Risk Tolerance → Allow RTP.

## 5. PAIN CLASSIFICATION FRAMEWORK

| Pain Type | Mechanism | Clinical Features |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Input-dominated — Nociceptive** | Tissue injury activating nociceptors | Clear mechanical pattern, proportionate to load, resolves with healing |
| **Input-dominated — Neuropathic** | Nerve injury or disease | Burning, shooting, electric, dermatomal, allodynia |
| **Input-dominated — Referred** | Pain perceived distant from source | Sclerotomal/myotomal distribution, no local tenderness |
| **Centrally dominated — Central sensitisation** | Amplified CNS processing | Widespread pain, disproportionate, poor localisation, associated fatigue/sleep disturbance |

## 6. LOW BACK PAIN TRIAGE

### Step 1: Rule Out Serious Pathology (Red Flags)
- Age <20 or >55 (new onset)
- History of cancer
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fever / night sweats
- Saddle anaesthesia / bladder or bowel dysfunction (cauda equina — emergency)
- Progressive neurological deficit
- Thoracic pain
- Non-mechanical pain (constant, worse at night)
- IV drug use, immunosuppression
- Significant trauma

### Step 2: Identify Specific Pathoanatomical Diagnoses
- Lumbar spondylolysis / stress fracture of pars interarticularis
- Spondylolisthesis
- Acute radiculopathy ± nerve root compression
- Vertebral endplate oedema (Modic type 1)

### Step 3: Non-Specific LBP — Clinical Sub-grouping
- **Flexion pattern**: pain with bending forward, sitting. Address hip mobility, motor control of flexion
- **Extension pattern**: pain with standing, walking, extension. Address extension control, core stability
- **Movement control impairment**: pain with uncontrolled movement. Motor control training
- **Multidimensional**: significant psychosocial factors. Combined physical + psychological approach

## 7. CONCUSSION — RED FLAGS AND RTP

### On-Field Red Flags (immediate removal, hospital if present)
- Neck pain or tenderness
- Double vision
- Weakness or tingling/burning in arms or legs
- Severe or increasing headache
- Seizure or convulsion
- Loss of consciousness
- Vomiting
- Increasingly restless, agitated, or combative
- Deteriorating conscious state
- Visible deformity of the skull

### Indications for Urgent CT Brain
- GCS < 15 at 2 hours post-injury
- Suspected open or depressed skull fracture
- Any sign of basal skull fracture (raccoon eyes, Battle's sign, CSF leak)
- Vomiting ≥ 2 episodes
- Age > 65 years
- Focal neurological deficit
- Seizure
- Coagulopathy

### Graduated Return to Sport Protocol (minimum 24 hours per stage)
| Stage | Activity | Goal |
|-------|----------|------|
| 1 | Symptom-limited daily activities | Gradual reintroduction of activities |
| 2 | Light aerobic exercise (walking, stationary cycling) | Increase HR |
| 3 | Sport-specific exercise (running drills) | Add movement |
| 4 | Non-contact training drills | Exercise, coordination, cognitive load |
| 5 | Full-contact practice (after medical clearance) | Restore confidence, functional assessment |
| 6 | Return to sport | |

**Rule**: If symptoms recur at any stage, drop back to the previous asymptomatic stage for 24 hours.

## 8. CLINICAL DECISION RULES — WHEN TO IMAGE

### Ottawa Ankle Rules — X-ray indicated if:
- Bone tenderness at posterior edge or tip of **medial malleolus**, OR
- Bone tenderness at posterior edge or tip of **lateral malleolus**, OR
- Inability to **bear weight** (4 steps) both immediately AND in emergency department

### Ottawa Knee Rules — X-ray indicated if:
- Age ≥ 55 years, OR
- Isolated tenderness of the **patella** (no other bony tenderness), OR
- Tenderness at **head of fibula**, OR
- Inability to **flex knee to 90°**, OR
- Inability to **bear weight** (4 steps) immediately AND in emergency department

## 9. IMAGING GUIDELINES — Modality Selection

| Modality | Best For | Limitations |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **X-ray** | Fractures, dislocations, OA, bony alignment | Poor soft tissue detail |
| **Ultrasound** | Tendons, ligaments, dynamic assessment, guided injections | Operator-dependent, limited deep structures |
| **MRI** | Soft tissue (menisci, labrum, cartilage, marrow oedema), occult fractures | Cost, availability, may over-detect asymptomatic findings |
| **CT** | Complex fractures, bone detail, surgical planning | Radiation dose, poor soft tissue |
| **Bone Scan** | Stress fractures, infection, tumour (now largely replaced by MRI) | Radiation, non-specific |

**Principle**: Imaging should confirm a clinical suspicion, not generate one. Treat the patient, not the scan.

## 10. MASQUERADING CONDITIONS — Systematic Screen

### Red Flags Suggesting Non-MSK Pathology
- Systemic symptoms: fever, night sweats, weight loss, malaise
- Pain that is constant, unrelenting, and not relieved by rest
- Night pain that disturbs sleep (particularly bone pain)
- Bilateral symptoms with no mechanical explanation
- Symptoms out of proportion to findings
- Multiple joint involvement without trauma
- Associated rash, bruising, or skin changes
- Past history of cancer

### Key Masquerading Categories
| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Rheumatological** | Spondyloarthropathies, RA, SLE, gout, pseudogout |
| **Infectious** | Osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, discitis |
| **Vascular** | DVT, popliteal artery entrapment, arterial insufficiency |
| **Neurological** | Peripheral neuropathy, radiculopathy, complex regional pain syndrome |
| **Neoplastic** | Primary bone tumours (osteosarcoma, Ewing's), metastases |
| **Endocrine/Metabolic** | Osteomalacia, Paget's disease, hyperparathyroidism |
| **Visceral referred** | Gynaecological, GI, renal pathology |

## 11. MUSCLE STRAIN GRADING

| Grade | Description | Function |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Grade I** | Small tear, few fibres | Minimal loss of function, some pain, no palpable defect |
| **Grade II** | Partial tear, significant fibres | Moderate loss of function, pain, possible palpable defect |
| **Grade III** | Complete tear / rupture | Complete loss of function, palpable gap, significant haematoma |

## 12. LIGAMENT SPRAIN GRADING

| Grade | Description | Laxity |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| **Grade I** | Stretch injury, microtears | No laxity, stable |
| **Grade II** | Partial tear | Some laxity, firm endpoint |
| **Grade III** | Complete rupture | Gross laxity, no endpoint |

## 13. REHABILITATION PHASES

| Phase | Name | Focus |
|-------|------|-------|
| 1 | Acute | Protect, control pain/swelling, maintain non-injured areas |
| 2 | Restore ADLs | Regain ROM, basic strength, normalise gait/movement |
| 3 | Return to Sport | Sport-specific strength, power, endurance, agility, proprioception |
| 4 | Prevention of Re-injury | Ongoing maintenance program, address risk factors, educate |

## 14. KEY PHARMACOLOGICAL NOTES

- **NSAIDs**: Effective for acute inflammatory pain but may impair tendon, bone, and cartilage healing in the long term. Use judiciously, short courses only.
- **Corticosteroid injections**: Short-term pain relief but may increase long-term recurrence risk in tendinopathy. Not recommended as first-line for tendinopathy.
- **PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma)**: Evidence mixed. May benefit some tendinopathies. Not recommended for acute muscle strains.
- **Paracetamol**: First-line for mild-moderate pain. Limited evidence for acute MSK injury.
- **Vitamin D**: Screen and supplement if deficient — important for bone stress injury prevention.

## 15. INJURY PREVENTION PROGRAM — Key Components

1. **Injury surveillance** — track injuries systematically
2. **Risk identification** — intrinsic (previous injury, strength deficits, biomechanics) + extrinsic (training load, equipment, environment)
3. **Season analysis** — identify high-risk periods in competition calendar
4. **Periodic medical assessment** — pre-season screening
5. **Targeted prevention program** — sport-specific interventions (e.g., FIFA 11+, Copenhagen adductor exercise, Nordic hamstring)
6. **Monitoring and feedback** — compliance tracking, adjust program
