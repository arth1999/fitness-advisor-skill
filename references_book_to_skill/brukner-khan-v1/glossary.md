# Glossary — Brukner & Khan Clinical Sports Medicine Vol 1

## A

**Acute injury**: Sudden onset injury resulting from a specific, identifiable traumatic event (e.g., fracture, ligament sprain, muscle strain, dislocation).

**Acute compartment syndrome**: Rapidly progressive increase in intracompartmental pressure causing muscle ischaemia and necrosis. Surgical emergency. Distinguished from chronic exertional compartment syndrome.

**Adhesive capsulitis** (frozen shoulder): Progressive painful loss of shoulder ROM due to capsular inflammation and fibrosis. More common in diabetics and women aged 40–60.

**AIOS** (Acquired Instability due to OverStress): Sport-specific acquired shoulder instability without a single traumatic event. Common in overhead athletes from repetitive microtrauma.

**AMBRI** (Atraumatic, Multidirectional, Bilateral, Rehabilitation-appropriate, Inferior capsular shift): Classification of atraumatic multidirectional shoulder instability. Treated primarily with rehabilitation.

**Apophysitis**: Traction injury at the apophysis (growth plate attachment of tendon to bone) in skeletally immature athletes. Examples: Osgood-Schlatter (tibial tuberosity), Sever's disease (calcaneus), Sinding-Larsen-Johansson (inferior patella).

**Avulsion fracture**: Fracture where a tendon or ligament pulls off a fragment of bone at its attachment site.

## B

**Bone stress injury (BSI)**: Spectrum from stress reaction (bone marrow oedema without fracture line) to stress fracture (visible fracture line). Result of repetitive loading exceeding bone's remodelling capacity.

**Bursa**: Fluid-filled sac that reduces friction between tissues (tendon-bone, skin-bone).

**Bursitis**: Inflammation of a bursa — can be traumatic (acute haemorrhagic) or overuse-related (chronic). Common sites: subacromial, olecranon, trochanteric, prepatellar, infrapatellar, retrocalcaneal.

## C

**Central sensitisation**: Increased responsiveness of nociceptive neurons in the CNS to normal or subthreshold afferent input. Results in hyperalgesia, allodynia, and widespread pain disproportionate to tissue findings.

**Cervicogenic headache**: Headache arising from dysfunction of cervical spine structures, particularly upper cervical (C1–C3). Referred pain via trigeminocervical nucleus.

**Chronic exertional compartment syndrome (CECS)**: Exercise-induced increase in intracompartmental pressure causing pain, tightness, and sometimes neurological symptoms. Resolves with rest. Confirmed by compartment pressure testing.

**Corticosteroid injection**: Local injection of synthetic glucocorticoid. Reduces inflammation and pain short-term but may impair tissue healing. Used sparingly in sports medicine.

**Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) type 1**: Chronic pain condition (previously reflex sympathetic dystrophy) characterised by pain disproportionate to inciting event, with autonomic changes (colour, temperature, swelling). Requires early recognition and multidisciplinary management.

**Concussion**: Traumatic brain injury induced by biomechanical forces, resulting in rapid onset of short-lived neurological impairment that resolves spontaneously. May or may not involve loss of consciousness.

**Core stability**: The capacity of the trunk muscles (including diaphragm, pelvic floor, transversus abdominis, multifidus) to control the position and motion of the trunk over the pelvis, allowing optimal production, transfer and control of force and motion to the terminal segment in integrated kinetic chain activities.

## D

**Differential diagnosis**: The systematic process of distinguishing a particular disease or condition from others that present with similar clinical features. A three-step process: (1) create a list of possible diagnoses, (2) rank them by probability, (3) test through history, examination, and investigations.

## E

**Eccentric exercise**: Muscle contraction during lengthening. Key component of tendinopathy rehabilitation.

**Enthesopathy**: Pathology at the enthesis — the site of tendon, ligament, or joint capsule insertion into bone. Can be inflammatory (enthesitis) or degenerative.

**Enthesis organ**: The functional unit of the enthesis including adjacent bursa, fat pad, and periosteum. Concept particularly relevant to insertional Achilles tendinopathy and retrocalcaneal bursitis.

**Extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ESWT)**: Delivery of high-energy acoustic waves to injured tissue. Evidence for chronic tendinopathies (plantar fasciopathy, Achilles, patellar) and bone non-union.

## F

**Fat pad**: Specialised adipose tissue with rich innervation that acts as a shock absorber. When irritated/impinged (e.g., Hoffa's fat pad in knee), can be a potent source of pain.

**FITT principle**: Frequency, Intensity, Time (duration), Type — used for training prescription and for modifying tissue stress in rehabilitation.

**Fredericson classification**: MRI-based grading system for bone stress injuries: Grade 1 (periosteal oedema on STIR only), Grade 2 (marrow oedema on T2/STIR), Grade 3 (marrow oedema on T1+T2), Grade 4 (fracture line visible).

## G

**GIRD** (Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit): Pathological loss of internal rotation in the throwing shoulder (>20° deficit compared to non-throwing side). Associated with increased risk of shoulder and elbow injury in overhead athletes.

## H

**Heavy slow resistance (HSR) training**: Resistance training using high loads (15RM to 6RM progression) with slow, controlled movements. Evidence-based treatment for lower limb tendinopathies as an alternative to eccentric-only programs.

**Hyphaema**: Blood in the anterior chamber of the eye. Requires immediate ophthalmology referral — risk of re-bleeding, glaucoma, corneal staining.

## I

**Impingement**: Compression of soft tissues between bony structures. Internal (e.g., subacromial impingement — rotator cuff against coracoacromial arch) or external (e.g., ankle anterior impingement from osteophytes).

**Isometric exercise**: Muscle contraction without change in length. Used in early tendinopathy rehabilitation for analgesic effect without excessive tendon load.

## K

**Kinetic chain**: The linked system of body segments where movement at one joint affects movement at adjacent joints. Dysfunction in one link (e.g., hip weakness) often causes symptoms in another (e.g., knee pain).

## L

**Likelihood ratio (LR)**: Measure of diagnostic test accuracy. LR+ = sensitivity / (1 - specificity). LR+ > 10 indicates a good diagnostic test; LR- < 0.1 indicates a good rule-out test.

**Load management**: The central principle in overuse injury prevention and treatment — balancing training load (volume × intensity) with recovery to avoid exceeding tissue capacity.

## M

**Mechanotherapy**: The use of therapeutic exercise to stimulate repair and remodelling of musculoskeletal tissues through mechanical loading.

**Motor control training**: Rehabilitation approach that retrains the coordination and timing of muscle activation, not just strength. Used for core stability, scapular dyskinesis, and movement control impairments.

**Modic changes**: MRI signal changes in vertebral endplates: Type 1 (oedema/inflammation), Type 2 (fatty replacement), Type 3 (sclerosis). Type 1 changes can be a source of LBP.

**Myositis ossificans**: Heterotopic bone formation within muscle following a contusion (often quadriceps). Presents as a hard mass with restricted ROM. Avoid aggressive massage — can worsen.

## N

**Neural mechanosensitivity**: Sensitivity of neural tissue to mechanical stimuli (movement, stretch, compression). Assessed via neurodynamic tests (e.g., SLR, ULTT).

**Neurodynamic techniques**: Manual therapy techniques that mobilise neural tissue to reduce mechanosensitivity and improve function.

**Neuropraxia**: Temporary conduction block in a nerve due to compression or stretch, without axonal damage. Full recovery expected.

**Nociception**: The neural process of encoding noxious stimuli. NOT synonymous with pain — nociception is input; pain is an output of the brain's evaluation of threat.

## O

**Optimal loading**: Replacing complete rest with controlled, progressive mechanical loading tailored to the specific tissue and stage of healing. A key shift from traditional RICE protocols.

**Osgood-Schlatter lesion**: Traction apophysitis of the tibial tuberosity at the patellar tendon insertion in adolescents. Self-limiting; managed with activity modification and graduated loading.

**Ottawa rules**: Validated clinical decision rules: Ottawa ankle rules and Ottawa knee rules guide when radiographs are indicated after acute injury.

**Overuse injury**: Injury resulting from repetitive submaximal loading of the musculoskeletal system without adequate recovery time for tissue adaptation. Represents the accumulation of microtrauma exceeding tissue capacity.

## P

**Patient-reported outcome measure (PROM)**: Standardised questionnaire completed by the patient to assess symptoms, function, and quality of life. Key attributes: validity, reliability, responsiveness. Examples: KOOS (knee), DASH (upper limb), FAAM (foot and ankle), iHOT (hip).

**Periodisation**: Systematic planning of training into cycles (macrocycle, mesocycle, microcycle) to optimise performance and reduce injury risk through planned variation in volume and intensity.

**Peripheral sensitisation**: Increased responsiveness and reduced threshold of peripheral nociceptive neurons (primary nociceptors) following tissue injury or inflammation.

**Piriformis syndrome**: Controversial diagnosis — compression of the sciatic nerve by the piriformis muscle causing buttock pain and sciatica. Diagnosis of exclusion.

**POLICE**: Protection, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation — the updated acute injury management protocol replacing RICE.

**Prolotherapy**: Injection of an irritant solution (usually hypertonic dextrose) to stimulate a healing response in chronic ligament and tendon injuries. Also called "regenerative injection therapy" or "sclerotherapy".

## R

**Reactive tendinopathy**: The first stage in the Cook-Purdam tendinopathy continuum. Acute, non-inflammatory proliferative response of tenocytes to overload. Thickened tendon on imaging; potentially reversible with load reduction.

**Red flag**: A clinical feature suggesting serious underlying pathology that requires further investigation or referral. Examples: constitutional symptoms, night pain, neurological deficit, history of cancer.

**Referred pain**: Pain perceived at a site distant from its source. In MSK medicine, typically sclerotomal (deep, aching, poorly localised) from spine, joints, or myofascial trigger points.

## S

**Scapular dyskinesis**: Altered scapular position and motion during arm movement. Associated with shoulder pain in overhead athletes. Classified by Kibler system (types I, II, III). Managed with scapular rehabilitation algorithm.

**Sinding-Larsen-Johansson lesion**: Traction apophysitis at the inferior pole of the patella in adolescents (analogous to Osgood-Schlatter but at a different patellar tendon attachment).

**SLAP lesion** (Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior): Tear of the superior glenoid labrum at the biceps anchor. Common in overhead athletes. Diagnosis requires high clinical suspicion + MR arthrogram.

**Spondylolysis**: Stress fracture of the pars interarticularis of the vertebra. Common in young athletes in extension-loading sports (gymnastics, cricket fast bowling, diving). May progress to spondylolisthesis.

**Spondylolisthesis**: Forward slippage of one vertebra on another, usually secondary to bilateral pars defects (isthmic type) or degenerative changes.

**StARRT framework** (Strategic Assessment of Risk and Risk Tolerance): The structured framework for return-to-play decisions. Three steps: (1) tissue health, (2) tissue stresses, (3) risk tolerance modifiers.

**Stress fracture**: The endpoint of the bone stress injury continuum — a visible fracture line through bone due to repetitive loading. See also Bone Stress Injury.

## T

**Tendinopathy**: Clinical syndrome of tendon pain, swelling, and impaired function. The preferred term over "tendinitis" as most painful tendons show degenerative change (tendinosis), not inflammation.

**Tendon dysrepair**: The intermediate stage in the Cook-Purdam tendinopathy continuum. Attempted tendon healing with collagen disorganisation, increased ground substance, and neovascularisation.

**Trigger point (myofascial)**: A hyperirritable spot within a taut band of skeletal muscle that is painful on compression and may produce referred pain. Evidence for their existence as distinct pathological entities is debated.

**TUBS** (Traumatic, Unidirectional, Bankart lesion, Surgery often required): Classification of traumatic shoulder instability — usually anteroinferior labral (Bankart) lesion from a dislocation event.

## U

**Unhappy triad** (O'Donoghue's triad): Combined injury to the ACL, MCL, and medial meniscus. Classic severe knee injury from valgus force with external rotation.

## V

**Visceral referred pain**: Pain arising from internal organs perceived in musculoskeletal structures sharing the same segmental innervation. Examples: shoulder tip pain from splenic rupture (C3-4), groin pain from renal colic (T10-L1), back pain from pancreatic pathology.
