# NASM CPT 7th Edition — Glossary

## A
- **Acute disease**: A medical condition that occurs suddenly and can be treated and healed in a short period of time
- **Acute variables**: Components that specify how each exercise is to be performed; also known as exercise training variables. Include: repetitions, sets, training intensity, repetition tempo, rest intervals, training volume, training frequency, training duration, exercise selection, and exercise order
- **Adherence**: The level of commitment to a behavior or plan of action
- **Agility**: The ability to accelerate, decelerate, stabilize, and change direction quickly while maintaining proper posture
- **Alarm reaction stage**: The initial reaction to a stressor; first stage of GAS
- **Ambivalence**: A person's state of mixed feelings about a situation
- **Amortization phase**: The transition from eccentric loading to concentric unloading during the stretch-shortening cycle
- **Anatomic position**: The position with the body erect, arms at the sides, and palms forward; used as a reference for anatomic nomenclature
- **Anterior (ventral)**: Toward the front of the body
- **Atherosclerosis**: Plaque buildup in arteries narrowing blood flow
- **ATP (adenosine triphosphate)**: High-energy molecule that serves as the main form of energy in the human body
- **Autogenic inhibition**: GTO stimulation causing the stretched muscle to relax
- **Autonomy**: Acting in accordance with how one wants to behave

## B
- **Balance**: The ability to maintain the body's center of gravity within its base of support
- **Behavior change techniques (BCTs)**: Client interventions used to change some determinant of behavior
- **Bilateral**: Relating to two sides of the body
- **Bioenergetics**: The study of energy in the human body
- **Biomechanics**: The science concerned with the internal and external forces acting on the human body and the effects produced by these forces

## C
- **Cardiac muscle**: Involuntary, striated muscle of the heart
- **Cardiorespiratory fitness**: The ability of the circulatory and respiratory systems to provide the body with oxygen during activity
- **Cardiorespiratory system**: A system composed of the heart, blood, blood vessels, lungs, and airways
- **Cardiovascular system**: A system of the body (also known as the circulatory system) that transports blood to tissues
- **Central nervous system (CNS)**: Division of the nervous system that includes the brain and spinal cord
- **Chronic disease**: A medical condition that persists for a long duration (3+ months) or cannot be cured altogether
- **Contraindication**: A specific situation where a medication, procedure, or exercise should be avoided because it may prove harmful
- **Contralateral**: Opposite side of the body
- **Core**: The structures that make up the LPHC, including the lumbar spine, pelvic girdle, abdomen, and hip joint
- **Core endurance**: The ability to control the motion of the spine over a longer duration
- **Core stability**: The ability of an individual to maintain a given position, adequately stabilizing the spine while the extremities are moving
- **Core strength**: The ability to control the motion of the spine (force production)

## D
- **Deconditioned**: A state of lost physical fitness, which may include muscle imbalances, decreased flexibility, and a lack of core and joint stability
- **Delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS)**: Pain or discomfort often felt 24 to 72 hours after intense exercise or unaccustomed physical activity
- **Dietary supplement**: A product (other than tobacco) intended to supplement the diet that bears or contains one or more dietary ingredients (vitamins, minerals, herbs, amino acids, etc.)
- **Distal**: Farther from the trunk of the body
- **Dynamic posture**: Alignment of the body while in motion

## E
- **Eccentric**: Muscle action in which the muscle lengthens under tension
- **Effector sites**: A part of the body, such as a muscle or organ, that receives a signal from a neuron to produce a physiological response
- **Empathy**: The ability to understand and share the feelings of another
- **Essential amino acids**: Amino acids that cannot be synthesized by the body and must be consumed in the diet (9 total)
- **Evidence-based practice**: A practice that relies on evidence for guidance and decision-making, including developing individual expertise, staying current on best external evidence, and prioritizing client values and expectations
- **Extensibility**: Capability to be elongated or stretched
- **Extrinsic motivation**: Engaging in an activity for external rewards

## F
- **First law of thermodynamics**: Energy cannot be created or destroyed but merely converted from one form to another
- **FITTE-VP**: Frequency, Intensity, Type, Time, Enjoyment, Volume, Progression — principles for cardiorespiratory program design
- **Flexibility**: The normal extensibility of soft tissues that allows for full range of motion of a joint
- **Free fatty acids**: The form of fat used in aerobic metabolism; derived from triglycerides
- **Frequency**: The number of training sessions in a given timeframe

## G
- **General adaptation syndrome (GAS)**: Describes the way in which the body responds and adapts to stress. Three stages: alarm reaction, resistance development, exhaustion
- **Glucose**: The simplest form of carbohydrate used by the body for energy
- **Glycogen**: Glucose that is deposited and stored in bodily tissues; the storage form of carbohydrate
- **Ground reaction force**: Reaction force imparted by the ground on the body when a body segment makes contact with it

## H
- **Health risk assessment (HRA)**: A screening tool used to evaluate the benefits and risks associated with starting any type of strenuous exercise
- **Homeostasis**: The process by which the human body strives to maintain a relatively stable equilibrium
- **Human movement system (HMS)**: The collective components and structures that work together to move the body: muscular, skeletal, and nervous systems
- **Hypertrophy**: Enlargement of an organ or tissue; often used to describe the enlargement of skeletal muscle

## I
- **Inferior**: Below; toward the feet
- **Integrated performance paradigm**: To move with efficiency, forces must be dampened (eccentrically), stabilized (isometrically), and then accelerated (concentrically)
- **Integrated training**: A training concept that applies all forms of exercise — flexibility, cardiorespiratory, core, balance, plyometric, SAQ, and resistance training — into one system
- **Intensity**: The level of demand placed on the body by a given activity
- **Intrinsic motivation**: When people engage in an activity because they feel a sense of satisfaction
- **Ipsilateral**: Same side of the body

## K
- **Kinetic chain**: A concept that describes the human body as a chain of interdependent links that work together to perform movement
- **Kinesiology**: Study of movement as it relates to anatomy and physiology

## L
- **Lateral**: Away from the midline of the body
- **Linear periodization**: A traditional method of program design that aims to gradually increase the intensity of the training load while simultaneously decreasing volume over a set period of time
- **LPHC (Lumbo-pelvic-hip complex)**: The core region; includes lumbar spine, pelvic girdle, abdomen, and hip joint

## M
- **Macrocycle**: Annual training plan; long-term progression from month to month
- **Macronutrients**: Food substances required in large amounts to supply energy; includes protein, carbohydrate, and fat
- **Medial**: Toward the midline of the body
- **Mediastinum**: The space in the chest between the lungs that contains the heart and other internal organs
- **Mesocycle**: Monthly training plan; typically 4 weeks
- **Metabolism**: All of the chemical reactions that occur in the body to maintain itself
- **Microcycle**: Weekly training plan; usually 7 days
- **Mitochondria**: The parts of the cell that use nutrients to create energy; the powerhouses of the cell
- **Mobility**: Optimal flexibility and joint range of motion; the ability to move freely
- **Morbidity**: The state of having a disease
- **Mortality**: A state or a risk of death or dying
- **Motor unit**: A motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates
- **Movement assessment**: An assessment of a client's movement patterns and postural alignment during movement; also known as a dynamic postural assessment
- **Muscle imbalance**: When muscles on each side of a joint have altered length-tension relationships
- **Muscle spindle**: Sensory receptor within muscle that detects stretch and rate of stretch
- **Musculoskeletal system**: The combined, interworking system of all muscles and bones in the body
- **Myofascial**: The body's connective tissue that includes muscles and fascia

## N
- **Neuron**: Specialized cell that is the functional unit of the nervous system

## O
- **Obesity**: A complex disease involving an excessive amount of body fat; classified by a BMI of 30 or greater
- **OPT model**: Optimum Performance Training model; NASM's evidence-based five-phase training system
- **Overactive**: When elevated neural drive causes a muscle to be held in a chronic state of contraction
- **Overload**: Increasing the intensity or volume of exercise programs using a systematic and gradual approach
- **Overtraining**: Excessive frequency, volume, or intensity of training resulting in reduction of performance, caused by a lack of proper rest and recovery
- **Overweight**: A body weight greater than what is considered within normal standards; BMI of 25.0 to 29.9

## P
- **PAR-Q+ (Physical Activity Readiness Questionnaire)**: A detailed questionnaire designed to assess an individual's physical readiness to engage in structured exercise
- **Performance assessments**: Assessments used to measure overall strength, muscular endurance, power, and agility
- **Periodization**: Macromanagement or planned manipulation of training variables in an organized fashion to improve performance over time
- **Pes planus**: Collapsed arch of the foot; also known as flat feet
- **Physical activity**: Bodily movement that results in energy expenditure and encompasses many modes and intensities
- **Posterior (dorsal)**: Toward the back of the body
- **Process goals**: Goals focused on behaviors rather than outcomes
- **Progressive overload**: Increasing the intensity or volume of exercise programs using a systematic and gradual approach
- **Proprioception**: The body's ability to sense its position in space
- **Proximal**: Closer to the trunk of the body

## R
- **Range of motion (ROM)**: The degree to which specific joints or body segments can move; often measured in degrees
- **Rate of force production**: Ability of muscles to exert maximal force output in a minimal amount of time
- **Rate of progression**: The process and speed from which frequency, intensity, time, and type are increased
- **Reciprocal inhibition**: Agonist contraction causing relaxation of the antagonist
- **Regional interdependence model**: The concept describing the integrated functioning of multiple body systems or regions of the body
- **Relative flexibility**: The process in which the body seeks the path of least resistance during functional movements
- **Repetition**: One complete movement of an exercise
- **Rest interval**: The time taken to recuperate between sets
- **Resting heart rate (RHR)**: Heart rate measured while fully at rest

## S
- **SAID principle**: Specific Adaptations to Imposed Demands; the body adapts specifically to the type of demand placed on it
- **Self-efficacy**: One's belief that they can complete a task, goal, or performance; also known as self-confidence
- **Set**: A group of consecutive repetitions
- **Skeletal muscle**: Voluntary muscle tissue that connects to bones and generates the forces that create movement
- **Soft tissue**: Tissue connecting, supporting, and surrounding bodily structures and organs
- **Static posture**: The positioning of the musculoskeletal system while the body is motionless
- **Stretch-shortening cycle**: Loading of a muscle eccentrically to prepare it for a rapid concentric contraction
- **Stride length**: Distance covered with each stride
- **Stride rate**: Number of strides per unit of time
- **Substrates**: Intermediate forms of nutrients used in metabolic reactions to create ATP
- **Superior**: Above; toward the head

## T
- **Tempo**: The speed of each repetition, expressed as eccentric/isometric/concentric timing (e.g., 4/2/1)
- **Third space**: A communal space, separate from home or work, where the client experiences their own sense of identity and relationship to others
- **Training intensity**: An individual's level of effort compared with his or her maximal effort, usually expressed as a percentage
- **Training plan**: The specific outline created by a fitness professional that details the form of training, length of time, future changes, and specific exercises to be performed
- **Training volume**: The sum of the repetitions performed in a given set during each training session, multiplied by the resistance used
- **Type I muscle fibers**: Slow-twitch; smaller, lower force, more resistant to fatigue
- **Type II muscle fibers**: Fast-twitch; larger, higher force, fatigue more quickly

## U
- **Underactive**: When a muscle is experiencing neural inhibition and limited neuromuscular recruitment
- **Undulating periodization**: Varies intensity and volume within a training cycle
- **Unilateral**: Relating to one side of the body

## V
- **Ventilatory threshold 1 (VT1)**: The point at which the body uses an equal mix of carbohydrate and fat as fuel sources; where ventilation first increases disproportionately
- **Ventilatory threshold 2 (VT2)**: The point where carbohydrate provides virtually all energy for activity; second disproportionate increase in ventilation; highest sustainable intensity
- **Volume**: Total amount of work performed in a training session (total reps × resistance)
