# Chapter 14: Flexibility Training Concepts

## Learning Objectives
- Summarize the importance of flexibility training for clients with differing goals
- Explain the scientific rationale for flexibility training
- Apply proper methods for effective stretching in client exercise programs
- Execute, instruct, and cue self-myofascial technique, active stretching, and dynamic stretching

## What Is Flexibility?
**Flexibility**: Normal extensibility of all soft tissues that allows full range of motion (ROM) of a joint.
**Mobility**: Optimal flexibility + joint ROM; ability to move freely.

Factors influencing flexibility: genetics, myofascial tissue elasticity, joint structure, opposing muscle strength, body composition, sex, age, activity level, previous injuries, repetitive movements.

### Relative Flexibility
The process in which the body seeks the path of least resistance during functional movements. Examples:
- Squat with excessively externally rotated feet to compensate for limited ankle dorsiflexion
- Overhead press with excessive lumbar extension to compensate for limited latissimus dorsi extensibility

## The Flexibility Continuum
NASM recommends a systematic progression:

### 1. Self-Myofascial Techniques (SMR/Foam Rolling)
- Apply gentle pressure to adhesions (knots) in muscle/fascia
- Mechanism: Autogenic inhibition -- Golgi tendon organ stimulation causes muscle relaxation
- Hold tender areas for 30–60 seconds
- Apply to: calves, IT band, TFL, adductors, latissimus dorsi, thoracic spine, etc.
- Best used: Beginning of warm-up or throughout the day as needed

### 2. Static Stretching
- Passively taking a muscle to the point of tension and holding
- Hold: 30 seconds minimum
- Mechanism: Autogenic inhibition plus creep (tissue elongation over time)
- Best for: Cool-down and corrective flexibility (Phase 1)
- NOT recommended pre-activity if it reduces power output

### 3. Active Stretching
- Using agonist muscle contraction to stretch the antagonist via reciprocal inhibition
- Hold: 1–2 seconds per repetition; 5–10 reps
- Example: Contract quadriceps to extend knee, stretching hamstrings
- Mechanism: Reciprocal inhibition
- Best for: Warm-up (Phases 2–4)

### 4. Dynamic Stretching
- Moving a joint through full ROM with control, using momentum without bouncing
- Reps: 10–15 repetitions per movement
- Examples: Leg swings, arm circles, walking lunges, high knees
- Best for: Warm-up (Phases 2–5); sport-specific preparation
- Mechanism: Reciprocal inhibition, increased tissue temperature, improved neuromuscular activation

## Flexiblity by OPT Phase

| Phase | Technique |
|-------|-----------|
| Phase 1: Stabilization Endurance | SMR → Static → (Active-Isolated optional) |
| Phase 2: Strength Endurance | SMR → Active → (Dynamic optional) |
| Phase 3: Muscular Development | SMR → Active → Dynamic |
| Phase 4: Maximal Strength | SMR → Active → Dynamic |
| Phase 5: Power | SMR → Dynamic → (Active optional) |

## Key Neurophysiological Principles
- **Autogenic inhibition**: GTO stimulation causes the muscle to relax (basis for SMR and static stretching)
- **Reciprocal inhibition**: Agonist contraction causes antagonist relaxation (basis for active stretching)
- **Muscle spindle**: Detects stretch and rate of stretch; causes contraction (stretch reflex). Stretching must be slow and controlled to avoid triggering the spindle

## Corrective Exercise and Flexibility
The **Corrective Exercise Continuum**:
1. **Inhibit**: SMR on overactive tissues
2. **Lengthen**: Static/active stretching of overactive (short) muscles
3. **Activate**: Strengthening underactive muscles
4. **Integrate**: Reintroduce movement patterns with proper form

## Key Definitions
- **Extensibility**: Capability to be elongated or stretched
- **Myofascial**: The body's connective tissue including muscles and fascia
- **Soft tissue**: Tissue connecting, supporting, and surrounding bodily structures
- **Relative flexibility**: The body seeking the path of least resistance during functional movement
