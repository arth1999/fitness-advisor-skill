# Chapter 10: Supplementation

## Learning Objectives
- Define supplementation and the clients' dietary need for taking supplements
- Summarize supplements used for optimal health and body composition
- Explain the role of supplementation within the personal training profession

## What Are Dietary Supplements?
The **Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994 (DSHEA)** defines dietary supplements as products (other than tobacco) intended to supplement the diet containing: vitamins, minerals, herbs/botanicals, amino acids, or concentrates/metabolites/extracts.

### Key Regulatory Facts
- Supplements do NOT need FDA approval before being sold or marketed (in the U.S.)
- The FDA does enforce guidelines through the Code of Federal Regulations
- Quality, purity, and strength are the responsibility of the manufacturer
- 75% of U.S. adults use at least one dietary supplement
- Global supplement revenue: $115+ billion (2018)

### Scope of Practice Warning
CPTs must stay within scope: inform about supplements, do not prescribe specific supplement regimens, especially for treating disease. Always refer clients to licensed healthcare providers (physicians, RDs) for medical supplement recommendations.

## Common Supplement Categories

### Health and Wellness
- **Multivitamins**: Most commonly used supplement; fill nutrient gaps
- **Omega-3 fatty acids**: Anti-inflammatory, heart and brain health
- **Vitamin D**: Bone health, immune function; common deficiency
- **Probiotics**: Gut health; digestive support
- **Calcium**: Bone density, especially for women

### Performance and Body Composition
- **Protein powders**: Convenient protein source; whey, casein, plant-based
  - Whey: fast-digesting; casein: slow-digesting
- **Creatine monohydrate**: Most researched supplement; improves strength, power, and muscle mass
- **Beta-alanine**: Increases muscle carnosine; buffers acid; improves high-intensity endurance
- **BCAAs (branched-chain amino acids)**: Leucine, isoleucine, valine; may reduce exercise-induced muscle damage
- **Caffeine**: CNS stimulant; improves alertness, endurance, power output
- **Pre-workout supplements**: Typically contain caffeine, beta-alanine, citrulline, and other ergogenic aids

### Supplement Safety Guidelines
1. Look for third-party testing (NSF Certified for Sport, Informed-Sport)
2. Research the manufacturer's reputation
3. Be wary of proprietary blends that don't disclose individual ingredient amounts
4. Start with lowest effective dose
5. Consider potential drug-nutrient interactions
6. Supplements are NOT substitutes for whole foods
7. Consult healthcare provider before starting any supplement

## Labels and Regulation
- **Supplement Facts panel**: Required labeling
- **Structure/function claims**: "Supports healthy joints" — no FDA approval needed
- **Health claims**: "May reduce the risk of heart disease" — requires FDA review
- Supplements cannot claim to treat, cure, or prevent diseases (that is reserved for drugs)

## Rationale for Supplement Use
- Fill nutrient gaps in diet
- Support specific health conditions (bone, heart, joint)
- Enhance exercise performance and recovery
- Support body composition goals
- Manage dietary restrictions (vegans: B12, iron; lactose intolerant: calcium)

## Key Definitions
- **Dietary supplement**: A product taken orally that contains a dietary ingredient intended to supplement the diet
- **Toxicity**: When too much of a vitamin/mineral accumulates, causing illness
- **Essential nutrient**: Must be obtained from diet; body cannot synthesize
- **Ergogenic aid**: Substance or technique that enhances performance
