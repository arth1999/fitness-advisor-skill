# Chapter 18: Plyometric (Reactive) Training Concepts

## Learning Objectives
- Explain plyometric training and its three phases to a fitness client
- Summarize the benefits of plyometric training for different client types
- Identify proper progressions of plyometric training exercises
- Employ methods to effectively execute, instruct, and cue plyometric training exercises

## What Is Plyometric Training?
**Plyometric (reactive) training**: Exercises generating quick, powerful movements involving an explosive concentric muscle contraction preceded by an eccentric muscle action. Also called jump or reactive training.

**Rate of force production**: Ability of muscles to exert maximal force output in minimal time -- this is what plyometrics develops.

### Prerequisites
Clients must possess:
- Adequate core strength
- Joint stability and range of motion
- Ability to balance efficiently
- Proper eccentric strength (deceleration)

## The Stretch-Shortening Cycle
The mechanism enabling plyometric power. Three phases:

### 1. Eccentric Phase (Loading/Deceleration)
- Agonist muscle is stretched/preloaded
- Elastic energy is stored in the muscle's elastic components
- Muscle spindles are stimulated; signals sent to spinal cord
- Three variables: magnitude of stretch, rate of stretch, duration of stretch

### 2. Amortization Phase (Transition)
- Time between eccentric phase and concentric contraction initiation
- Also called the electromechanical delay
- **Critical factor**: The shorter this phase, the more powerful the movement
- If amortization is prolonged, stored elastic energy dissipates as heat
- Nerve signals synapse in spinal cord and are sent back to stretched muscle

### 3. Concentric Phase (Unloading/Acceleration)
- Stored elastic energy is released
- Enhanced muscle force production
- Shortening of agonist muscle

### The Integrated Performance Paradigm
To move with efficiency, forces must be:
1. Dampened (eccentrically)
2. Stabilized (isometrically)
3. Accelerated (concentrically)

## Plyometric Training Progressions (OPT Model)

### Plyometric Stabilization (Phase 1)
- **Focus**: Little-to-no joint motion; landing mechanics; deceleration control
- **Exercises**:
  - Squat jump with stabilization (jump → hold landing 3–5 sec)
  - Box jump-up with stabilization
  - Box jump-down with stabilization
  - Multiplanar jump with stabilization
- **Key cue**: "Stick" the landing; soft, quiet landing; knees aligned

### Plyometric Strength (Phases 2, 3, 4)
- **Focus**: Dynamic movement through full ROM; short, repetitive movements
- **Exercises**:
  - Squat jump (continuous)
  - Tuck jump
  - Butt kicks
  - Power step-up
  - Ice skaters
  - Single-leg power step-up
- **Key cue**: Minimize ground contact time

### Plyometric Power (Phase 5)
- **Focus**: Maximal force output; full stretch-shortening cycle; minimal ground contact
- **Exercises**:
  - Depth jump
  - Single-leg hop progression
  - Box-to-box jump
  - Plyometric push-up
  - Medicine ball scoop toss
  - Medicine ball overhead throw
- **Key cue**: Maximal effort; explosive; as fast as possible

## Safety and Programming
- Adequate footwear and landing surface
- Proper warm-up (dynamic stretching)
- Start with low volume; progress gradually
- Emphasize quality over quantity
- Avoid with: osteoporosis, severe joint issues, acute inflammation, certain chronic conditions
- Progress from simple to complex, bilateral to unilateral

## Key Definitions
- **Rate of force production**: Ability of muscles to exert maximal force output in minimal time
- **Stretch-shortening cycle**: Loading of a muscle eccentrically to prepare for a rapid concentric contraction
- **Amortization phase**: The transition from eccentric loading to concentric unloading
- **Ground reaction force**: Reaction force imparted by the ground when a body segment makes contact with it
- **Integrated performance paradigm**: Eccentric → Isometric → Concentric progression
