# Chapter 5: The Nervous, Muscular, and Skeletal Systems

## Learning Objectives
- Outline the basic structure and function of the nervous system
- Classify bone and joint types and their associated functions
- Summarize the structure and function of the muscular system
- Identify the effects exercise has on each component of the human movement system

## The Human Movement System (HMS)
The HMS is the collective components that work together to move the body: **nervous**, **skeletal**, and **muscular** systems. These form a **kinetic chain** — interdependent links working together to produce movement. Dysfunction in one link affects the entire chain.

## Nervous System
A network of specialized cells called **neurons** that transmit and coordinate signals throughout the body. It functions as the body's communication network.

### Divisions
- **Central Nervous System (CNS)**: Brain and spinal cord. Integrative and control center.
- **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)**: Nerves outside the CNS. Further divided into:
  - **Somatic**: Voluntary control of skeletal muscle
  - **Autonomic**: Involuntary (heart, organs). Includes sympathetic (fight/flight) and parasympathetic (rest/digest)

### Key Structures of the Neuron
- **Cell body (soma)** — contains nucleus and organelles
- **Dendrites** — receive signals from other neurons
- **Axon** — transmits signals away from cell body
- **Axon terminal** — releases neurotransmitters to effector sites

### Sensory and Motor Functions
- **Sensory (afferent)** neurons: Carry information from body to CNS
- **Motor (efferent)** neurons: Carry commands from CNS to muscles/organs
- **Mechanoreceptors**: Detect mechanical forces (touch, pressure, stretch). Key sensor: muscle spindle (detects stretch and rate of stretch), Golgi tendon organ (detects tension)

## Skeletal System
Provides structural support, protects organs, enables movement, stores minerals, and produces blood cells.

### Bone Types
- **Long bones**: Femur, humerus (levers for movement)
- **Short bones**: Carpals, tarsals (shock absorption)
- **Flat bones**: Scapula, sternum (protection, muscle attachment)
- **Irregular bones**: Vertebrae (varied functions)
- **Sesamoid bones**: Patella (embedded in tendons)

### Joint Classifications
- **Synarthrosis (immovable)**: Sutures of the skull
- **Amphiarthrosis (slightly movable)**: Pubic symphysis
- **Diarthrosis (freely movable/synovial)**: Most common. Types:
  - Hinge (elbow, knee) — sagittal plane
  - Ball-and-socket (shoulder, hip) — all 3 planes
  - Gliding (carpals) — sliding motions
  - Pivot (atlantoaxial) — rotation
  - Saddle (thumb) — two planes plus opposition
  - Condyloid (wrist) — two planes

### Key Concepts
- **Ligaments**: Connect bone to bone; provide joint stability
- **Tendons**: Connect muscle to bone; transmit force
- **Axial skeleton**: Skull, vertebral column, ribs, sternum
- **Appendicular skeleton**: Limbs and girdles

## Muscular System
### Muscle Types
1. **Skeletal muscle**: Voluntary, striated, attached to bones via tendons. Produces movement.
2. **Cardiac muscle**: Involuntary, striated, found only in the heart.
3. **Smooth muscle**: Involuntary, non-striated, found in organs/blood vessels.

### Muscle Structure (macro to micro)
- Muscle → Fascicle → Muscle fiber (cell) → Myofibril → Sarcomere → Myofilaments (actin and myosin)

### Muscle Actions
- **Agonist (prime mover)**: Primary muscle producing movement
- **Synergist**: Assists the agonist
- **Antagonist**: Opposes the agonist; must relax for movement to occur
- **Stabilizer**: Stabilizes a body part while another moves

### Muscle Contraction Types
- **Concentric**: Muscle shortens under tension (lifting phase)
- **Eccentric**: Muscle lengthens under tension (lowering phase)
- **Isometric**: Muscle generates tension without changing length

### Muscle Fiber Types
- **Type I (slow-twitch)**: Smaller, lower force, fatigue-resistant, used in endurance
- **Type II (fast-twitch)**: Larger, higher force, fatigues quickly, used in strength/power

## Effects of Exercise on the HMS
- **Nervous**: Improved neuromuscular efficiency, motor unit recruitment, coordination
- **Skeletal**: Increased bone density, stronger connective tissue
- **Muscular**: Increased strength, endurance, hypertrophy; improved flexibility

## Key Definitions
- **Kinetic chain**: Interdependent links working together to perform movement
- **Mitochondria**: Cellular powerhouses that produce ATP
- **Neuromuscular junction**: Connection point between neuron and muscle fiber
- **Motor unit**: A motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates
