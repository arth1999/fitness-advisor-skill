# Chapter 20: Resistance Training Concepts

## Learning Objectives
- Summarize how resistance training affects the human body
- Define resistance training systems
- Identify the acute variables of training
- Categorize resistance exercises for different client types, goals, and adaptations
- Employ methods used to safely execute, instruct, and cue resistance exercises

## Principle of Adaptation
The human body adapts to stress. Resistance training delivers a stimulus that triggers adaptations. Benefits include:
- Improved cardiovascular efficiency
- Increased muscular hypertrophy, strength, endurance, and power
- Increased bone density and connective tissue strength
- Improved metabolism and body composition (decreased body fat)
- Enhanced neuromuscular control and coordination
- Improved endocrine and cholesterol profiles

## General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)
Three stages of response to stress:
1. **Alarm reaction**: Initial response to the stressor (weight). Delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS) may occur 24–72 hours after.
2. **Resistance development**: The body adapts, becomes stronger, and can handle more stress.
3. **Exhaustion**: If stress is excessive and recovery insufficient, overtraining and performance decline occur.

## Key Training Principles
- **Principle of specificity (SAID)**: Specific Adaptations to Imposed Demands. The body adapts specifically to the type of demand placed on it (e.g., heavy weights build max strength, high reps build endurance).
- **Progressive overload**: Systematic and gradual increase of intensity or volume. Without progressive overload, adaptation plateaus.
- **Periodization**: Planned manipulation of training variables in organized fashion to improve performance over time.

## Acute Variables of Resistance Training
The building blocks of program design:

| Variable | Stabilization (Phase 1) | Strength Endurance (Phase 2) | Hypertrophy (Phase 3) | Max Strength (Phase 4) | Power (Phase 5) |
|----------|------------------------|------------------------------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Reps** | 12–20 | 8–12 | 6–12 | 1–5 | 1–5 + 8–10 |
| **Sets** | 1–3 | 2–4 | 3–5 | 4–6 | 3–5 |
| **Intensity** | 50–70% 1RM | 70–80% 1RM | 75–85% 1RM | 85–100% 1RM | 85–100% / 30–45% |
| **Rest** | 0–90 sec | 0–60 sec | 0–60 sec | 3–5 min | 3–5 min |
| **Tempo** | 4/2/1 | 2/0/2 / 4/2/1 | 2/0/2 | X/X/X | X/X/X |
| **Volume** | Low-moderate | Moderate | High | Low | Low-moderate |

### Repetition Tempo Format
Three numbers indicating: Eccentric / Isometric / Concentric (e.g., 4/2/1 = 4 sec lowering, 2 sec pause, 1 sec lifting)
- **X** = explosive, as fast as possible

### Exercise Order
1. Power exercises (if applicable)
2. Compound/core lifts (multi-joint)
3. Assistance exercises (single-joint)
4. Stabilization exercises

Alternative: Upper/lower body alternating, push/pull pairing, circuit training.

## Resistance Training Systems
- **Single-set**: One set per exercise. Effective for beginners.
- **Multiple-set**: 2+ sets per exercise. Superior for progressive overload and adaptation.
- **Superset**: Two exercises back-to-back with minimal rest. Options: same muscle group, opposing muscle groups, or strength + stabilization (Phase 2 hallmark).
- **Circuit training**: Series of exercises performed sequentially with minimal rest. Efficient for general fitness and fat loss.
- **Drop sets**: Perform set to failure, reduce weight, continue. Advanced intensity technique.
- **Pyramid**: Increase weight, decrease reps across sets (or vice versa).
- **Complex training**: Strength exercise + power exercise superset (Phase 5 hallmark).
- **Split routine**: Training different body parts on different days (e.g., upper/lower split, push/pull, body part split).

## Exercise Selection
- **Compound/multi-joint**: Engage multiple joints and muscle groups (squat, deadlift, bench press, pull-up). Foundation of most programs.
- **Single-joint/isolation**: Target one muscle group (biceps curl, leg extension). Used for specific muscle development.
- **Stabilization**: Proprioceptively challenging (single-leg, BOSU, stability ball). Build neuromuscular control.

## Key Definitions
- **General adaptation syndrome (GAS)**: Describes how the body responds and adapts to stress
- **DOMS**: Delayed-onset muscle soreness; pain 24–72 hours after unaccustomed exercise
- **Progressive overload**: Systematic and gradual increase of exercise intensity or volume
- **SAID principle**: Specific Adaptations to Imposed Demands
- **Periodization**: Planned manipulation of training variables to improve performance over time
