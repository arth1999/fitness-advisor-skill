# Chapter 16: Core Training Concepts

## Learning Objectives
- Identify the physical benefits of core training
- Differentiate between the core stabilization and movement systems
- Describe the mechanisms of the drawing-in maneuver and abdominal bracing
- Identify proper progressions of core training exercises
- Employ methods to effectively execute, instruct, and cue core training exercises

## The Core (Lumbo-Pelvic-Hip Complex / LPHC)
The core is the body's center of gravity. Boundaries:
- **Superiorly**: Diaphragm
- **Anteriorly/laterally**: Abdominal muscles
- **Posteriorly**: Lumbar spine and gluteal muscles
- **Inferiorly**: Pelvic floor and hip musculature

### Local Muscles (Stabilization System)
Attach on or near vertebrae; short attachments (1–2 segments). Provide dynamic control of spinal segments. Primarily **Type I (slow-twitch)** fibers. High density of muscle spindles for proprioception.

**Key local muscles**: Transverse abdominis, multifidus, internal obliques, diaphragm, pelvic floor, rotatores, quadratus lumborum.

### Global Muscles (Movement System)
Larger, superficial muscles that produce movement. Connect pelvis to spine and extremities.

**Key global muscles**: Rectus abdominis, external obliques, erector spinae, latissimus dorsi, iliopsoas.

## Core Training Objectives
1. **Core stability**: Ability to maintain proper spinal/hip posture while extremities move
2. **Core endurance**: Ability to control spinal motion over a longer duration
3. **Core strength**: Ability to contract the torso in all directions
4. **Core power**: Explosive movements of the core musculature

## Key Mechanisms

### Drawing-In Maneuver
- Pull the navel toward the spine
- Activates transverse abdominis (local stabilization)
- Should not involve pelvic tilt or rib cage depression
- First step before any core exercise

### Abdominal Bracing
- Co-contraction of all abdominal muscles
- Creates intra-abdominal pressure for spinal stability
- More global activation than drawing-in alone
- Used during higher-load exercises

## Core Training Progressions (OPT Model)

### Core Stabilization (Phase 1)
- **Focus**: Little-to-no spinal movement; high proprioceptive demand
- **Exercises**: Marching, floor bridge, plank, cobra, cable push/pull in standing
- **Tempo**: Slow (4/2/1)
- **Key cue**: Drawing-in maneuver throughout

### Core Strength (Phases 2, 3, 4)
- **Focus**: Dynamic movement through full ROM with more load/speed
- **Exercises**: Ball crunch, back extension, cable rotation, reverse crunch, supine ball hip extension
- **Tempo**: Medium (2/0/2 or 3/2/1)
- **Key cue**: Bracing; maintain spinal alignment

### Core Power (Phase 5)
- **Focus**: Explosive movements; rate of force production
- **Exercises**: Medicine ball rotation chest pass, medicine ball soccer throw, medicine ball woodchop throw, overhead medicine ball throw
- **Tempo**: X/X/X (as fast as possible while controlled)
- **Key cue**: Explosive concentric; controlled repositioning

## Benefits of Core Training
- Improved posture and spinal alignment
- Enhanced force production and transfer (kinetic chain efficiency)
- Reduced risk of low back pain and injury
- Improved balance and stability
- Better performance in ADLs and athletics
- Acceleration of injury rehabilitation

## Key Definitions
- **LPHC**: Lumbo-pelvic-hip complex; the core region
- **Core stability**: Ability to maintain a given position with adequate spinal stabilization while extremities move
- **Core endurance**: Ability to control spinal motion over a longer duration
- **Core strength**: Ability to control spinal motion (force production)
- **Intra-abdominal pressure**: Pressure created within the abdominal cavity that contributes to spinal stability
