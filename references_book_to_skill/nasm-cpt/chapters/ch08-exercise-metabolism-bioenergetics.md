# Chapter 8: Exercise Metabolism and Bioenergetics

## Learning Objectives
- Summarize how the first law of thermodynamics governs energy usage
- Describe the energy systems of the human body
- Identify how each macronutrient can be used as a fuel source
- Integrate the concepts of energy balance in relation to body composition management
- Identify how to efficiently fuel the body in relation to activity intensity

## Bioenergetics and Metabolism
- **Bioenergetics**: Study of how energy is transformed through biochemical reactions
- **Metabolism**: All chemical reactions in the body to maintain itself
- **ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)**: High-energy molecule; the "energy currency" of the body

## The First Law of Thermodynamics
Energy can neither be created nor destroyed — only converted from one form to another. In the human body:
- Energy in (food) = Energy out (metabolism + activity) +/- Changes in body mass
- Surplus energy is stored as body fat (or glycogen)
- Deficit energy comes from stored body fat and glycogen
- ~3,500 calories ≈ 1 lb of body fat

### Carbon-Based Energy
Energy is stored in the molecular bonds of carbon-based organic matter. CO2 is the primary waste product of metabolism — exhaling CO2 is the only way carbon (and therefore energy) is removed from the body.

## The Three Energy Systems

### 1. ATP-PC System (Phosphocreatine)
- **Duration**: 0–10 seconds of maximal effort
- **Fuel source**: Stored ATP and phosphocreatine (PCr)
- **Oxygen required**: No (anaerobic)
- **Example activities**: 100m sprint, heavy 1RM lift, vertical jump
- **Key feature**: Immediate energy but very limited supply

### 2. Glycolytic System (Anaerobic Glycolysis)
- **Duration**: ~10 seconds to 2–3 minutes of high-intensity effort
- **Fuel source**: Glucose/glycogen (carbohydrate)
- **Oxygen required**: No (anaerobic)
- **By-product**: Lactate and hydrogen ions (causes "burn")
- **Example activities**: 400–800m run, high-rep resistance training
- **Net ATP yield**: ~2 ATP per glucose molecule (fast but inefficient)

### 3. Oxidative System (Aerobic)
- **Duration**: Sustained activity beyond ~2–3 minutes
- **Fuel sources**: Carbohydrates, fats, and protein
- **Oxygen required**: Yes (aerobic)
- **Net ATP yield per glucose**: ~36 ATP (efficient but slower)
- **Example activities**: Distance running, cycling, walking
- **Subsystems**:
  - **Krebs cycle (TCA cycle)**: Generates electron carriers
  - **Electron transport chain**: Produces bulk of ATP using oxygen

## Fuel Sources
### Carbohydrates (Glucose/Glycogen)
- Primary fuel for moderate-to-high intensity activity
- Can be metabolized with or without oxygen
- Stored as glycogen in muscle (~300–400g) and liver (~100g)
- At **Ventilatory Threshold 1 (VT1)**: 50% carbohydrate / 50% fat usage
- At **Ventilatory Threshold 2 (VT2)**: Nearly 100% carbohydrate usage
- "Hitting the wall" = glycogen depletion

### Fats (Free Fatty Acids/Triglycerides)
- Primary fuel at rest and during low-to-moderate intensity (below VT1)
- Requires oxygen for metabolism (aerobic only)
- Vast supply even in lean individuals (52,500+ kcal for someone at 10% body fat)
- Triglycerides stored in adipose tissue; broken down to free fatty acids for use

### Protein (Amino Acids)
- Not a primary fuel source under normal conditions
- Provides ~5–10% of energy during prolonged endurance exercise
- Used more when glycogen is depleted
- 20 amino acids total; 9 are essential (must be consumed)
- Muscle tissue is the body's protein reserve

## Energy System Integration
All three energy systems operate simultaneously. The intensity and duration of activity determine which system predominates:
- **Low intensity/long duration** → Oxidative (aerobic, fat-burning)
- **Moderate intensity** → Mix; transitions at VT1 (more carb)
- **High intensity** → Glycolytic (anaerobic, carb-burning)
- **Maximal/short bursts** → ATP-PC

## Key Definitions
- **Substrates**: Intermediate forms of nutrients used in metabolic reactions
- **Glucose**: Simplest carbohydrate form used for energy
- **Glycogen**: Stored form of carbohydrate (liver and muscle)
- **Ventilatory threshold 1 (VT1)**: Intensity where the body uses equal mix of carb and fat
- **Ventilatory threshold 2 (VT2)**: Intensity where carbs provide virtually all energy; highest sustainable intensity
- **Free fatty acids**: The form of fat used in aerobic metabolism
- **Triglyceride**: Storage form of fat in the body and in food
