# Chapter 9: Nutrition

## Learning Objectives
- Summarize the role of proper nutrition for clients' optimal health and performance goals
- Discuss macronutrients and their recommended dietary allowances
- Describe micronutrients and their role in overall health
- Integrate nutrition strategies that support client goals within the CPT scope of practice

## Scope of Practice: CPTs vs. Dietitians
**CPTs CAN:**
- Provide nutritional guidance for healthy populations
- Evaluate eating habits and provide general guidance
- Discuss roles of protein, carbs, and fat
- Explain importance of fiber and micronutrients
- Discuss dangers of excess calories, salt, alcohol, added sugar
- Offer basic hydration strategies
- Dispel nutrition myths
- Refer to healthcare providers when necessary
- Offer basic nutritional guidelines for typical athletic events
- Demonstrate healthy portion sizes

**CPTs CANNOT:**
- Write specific meal plans and daily menus
- Prescribe specific supplements or treat chronic disease with vitamins/minerals
- Prescribe drastic caloric restriction
- Prescribe fasting, detoxification, or extreme nutritional practices
- Prescribe diets that severely restrict food groups
- Advise against healthcare professionals' recommendations
- Provide detailed nutrition plans for extreme athletic events (Ironman, ultra-marathon)

## Macronutrients

### Carbohydrates
- **Function**: Primary energy source, especially for brain and high-intensity exercise
- **Energy**: 4 kcal/gram
- **Recommendation**: 45–65% of total calories
- **Types**:
  - **Simple**: Mono/disaccharides (glucose, fructose, sucrose). Quick energy; blood sugar spikes
  - **Complex**: Polysaccharides (starch, glycogen, fiber). Sustained energy
- **Fiber**: Non-digestible carbohydrate; 25g/day (women), 38g/day (men)
- **Glycemic index**: Measure of how quickly a food raises blood glucose

### Protein
- **Function**: Tissue building/repair, enzymes, hormones, immune function
- **Energy**: 4 kcal/gram
- **Recommendation**: 10–35% of total calories; 0.8 g/kg (sedentary); 1.2–2.2 g/kg (active)
- **Amino acids**: 20 total; 9 essential (cannot be synthesized)
- **Complete vs. incomplete**: Complete = all essential amino acids (animal sources, soy, quinoa)

### Fats (Lipids)
- **Function**: Energy storage, hormone production, cell membrane structure, vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K)
- **Energy**: 9 kcal/gram
- **Recommendation**: 20–35% of total calories
- **Types**:
  - **Saturated**: Solid at room temp; animal sources; limit to <10% of calories
  - **Unsaturated**: Liquid at room temp; plant oils, fish; monounsaturated + polyunsaturated
  - **Trans fats**: Hydrogenated oils; avoid; linked to heart disease
  - **Essential fatty acids**: Omega-3 (alpha-linolenic) and Omega-6 (linoleic)

## Micronutrients
### Vitamins
- **Fat-soluble**: A, D, E, K. Stored in body; toxicity risk
- **Water-soluble**: B-complex, C. Not stored; daily intake needed

### Minerals
- **Macrominerals**: Calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, sodium, potassium, chloride, sulfur
- **Trace minerals**: Iron, zinc, iodine, selenium, copper, manganese, fluoride, chromium

## Hydration
- Water is the most critical nutrient; ~60% of body weight
- General recommendation: 3.7 L/day (men), 2.7 L/day (women)
- Exercise hydration: 8–12 fl oz 15–30 min before; 3–8 fl oz every 10–20 min during; 16–24 fl oz per pound lost post-exercise

## Nutrition Strategies by Goal
### Fat Loss
- Modest caloric deficit (300–500 kcal/day)
- Higher protein intake to preserve muscle
- Emphasize whole, nutrient-dense foods
- Adequate hydration
- Focus on sustainable habits, not drastic restriction

### Muscle Gain
- Caloric surplus (300–500 kcal/day)
- Protein: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day
- Proper peri-workout nutrition
- Adequate carbohydrate for training energy

### Sports Performance
- Carb loading for endurance events (3–12g/kg depending on intensity/duration)
- Timing matters: pre-, during, post-exercise
- Post-exercise: 3:1 to 4:1 carb-to-protein ratio for recovery
