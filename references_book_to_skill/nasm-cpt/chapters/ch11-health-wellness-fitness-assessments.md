# Chapter 11: Health, Wellness, and Fitness Assessments

## Learning Objectives
- Explain the importance of fitness assessments surrounding clients' goals and optimal health
- Collect and summarize subjective information from clients using health screening assessments
- Identify the steps for conducting physiological and body composition assessments
- Identify the steps for conducting cardiorespiratory assessments

## Purpose of Assessments
Physiological assessments serve to:
1. Identify individuals at increased risk for cardiovascular, pulmonary, or metabolic diseases
2. Identify those with contraindications who should be medically cleared first
3. Determine who should participate in medically supervised programs
4. Reduce unnecessary medical evaluations for apparently healthy adults
5. Provide appropriate exercise recommendations
6. Educate individuals about their current physical condition
7. Track progress with baseline and follow-up data
8. Motivate through realistic goal setting

## Preparticipation Screening
### Physical Activity Readiness Questionnaire (PAR-Q+)
- Developed 2011, updated 2019
- Simplifies preparticipation screening
- Reflects: current physical activity level, presence of signs/symptoms of CVD/metabolic/renal disease, and desired exercise intensity

### Health Risk Assessment (HRA)
Evaluates benefits vs. risks of starting strenuous exercise. Should be valid, simple, cost/time efficient, and appropriate for the target population.

## Subjective Assessments
Information reported by the client:
- Medical history
- Previous injuries
- Current medications
- Exercise history
- Lifestyle factors (sleep, stress, occupation, nutrition)
- Goals and expectations

## Objective Assessments

### Physiological Measurements
- **Resting heart rate (RHR)**: Normal adult ~60–100 bpm; lower generally indicates better fitness
- **Blood pressure**: Normal < 120/< 80 mmHg; hypertension ≥ 130 SBP or ≥ 80 DBP
  - SBP: Pressure during ventricular contraction
  - DBP: Pressure during ventricular relaxation
- **Measure when**: Client is seated, rested for 5+ minutes, feet flat, arm supported at heart level

### Body Composition Assessments
- **BMI**: Weight (kg) / Height² (m). Underweight < 18.5; Normal 18.5–24.9; Overweight 25–29.9; Obese ≥ 30
  - Limitation: Does not distinguish between fat and lean mass
- **Skinfold measurements**: Jackson-Pollock formulas (3-site or 7-site). Use Durnin-Womersley or Jackson-Pollock equations. Sites include: chest, midaxillary, triceps, subscapular, abdomen, suprailiac, thigh
- **Bioelectrical impedance**: Sends low electrical current through body; lean tissue conducts better than fat
- **Circumference measurements**: Waist, hip, thigh, arm, calf. Waist-to-hip ratio: health risk if > 0.95 (men) or > 0.80 (women)
- **Underwater weighing**: Gold standard; impractical for most settings

## Cardiorespiratory Assessments
### Submaximal Tests
Predict VO2max from heart rate response to submaximal work:
- **YMCA 3-minute step test**: Step at 96 bpm for 3 minutes; measure recovery HR
- **Rockport walk test**: 1-mile walk as fast as possible; measure time and HR
- **Cycle ergometer tests**: YMCA or Astrand-Rhyming protocols

### Maximal Tests
- **1.5-mile run test**: Time to completion
- **12-minute run/Cooper test**: Distance covered in 12 minutes
- Requires medical supervision for at-risk individuals

## Contraindications to Assessment
- Acute or uncontrolled chronic conditions
- Recent surgery
- Unusual shortness of breath
- Chest pain or angina
- Dizziness during physical activity
- Physician-directed restrictions

## Key Definitions
- **Contraindication**: A specific situation where exercise should be avoided due to harm risk
- **Health risk assessment (HRA)**: Screening tool evaluating the benefits and risks associated with starting strenuous exercise
- **Deconditioned**: State of lost physical fitness including muscle imbalances, decreased flexibility, and lack of core/joint stability
- **HIIT**: High-intensity interval training; intervals of near-maximal intensity with short rest periods
