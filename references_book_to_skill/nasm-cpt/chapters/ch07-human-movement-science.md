# Chapter 7: Human Movement Science

## Learning Objectives
- Summarize the scientific terminology that governs exercise and movement
- Identify key biomechanical concepts that define how the neuromuscular system functions
- Describe motor behavior and how the neuromuscular system adapts to changes in the environment

## Biomechanics and Kinesiology
- **Biomechanics**: Study of mechanical laws relating to movement; physics applied to human motion
- **Kinesiology**: Study of movement as it relates to anatomy and physiology
- **Regional Interdependence Model**: The concept that dysfunction in one body region can affect function in other regions

## Planes of Motion
All human movement occurs in three planes:
1. **Sagittal plane**: Divides body into left/right halves. Movements: flexion/extension (e.g., biceps curl, running, squat)
2. **Frontal (coronal) plane**: Divides body into front/back. Movements: abduction/adduction, lateral flexion, eversion/inversion (e.g., lateral raise, side shuffle)
3. **Transverse plane**: Divides body into top/bottom. Movements: rotation, horizontal abduction/adduction (e.g., cable rotation, golf swing)

## Joint Motions
### Sagittal Plane
- **Flexion**: Decrease joint angle
- **Extension**: Increase joint angle
- **Dorsiflexion**: Foot toward shin
- **Plantarflexion**: Foot away from shin

### Frontal Plane
- **Abduction**: Away from midline
- **Adduction**: Toward midline
- **Lateral flexion**: Side bending of spine
- **Eversion**: Sole of foot outward
- **Inversion**: Sole of foot inward

### Transverse Plane
- **Internal rotation**: Toward midline
- **External rotation**: Away from midline
- **Horizontal abduction**: Arm/leg moves away from midline in horizontal plane
- **Horizontal adduction**: Arm/leg moves toward midline in horizontal plane

## Muscle Actions
- **Isotonic**: Muscle changes length — includes concentric (shortening) and eccentric (lengthening)
- **Isometric**: Muscle tension without length change
- **Isokinetic**: Constant speed throughout ROM; requires specialized equipment

### Force-Velocity Relationship
- **Concentric**: As force increases, velocity decreases (inverse relationship)
- **Eccentric**: Force can be higher than concentric; force increases with velocity up to a point

### Length-Tension Relationship
Optimal muscle length produces maximum force. Too short or too stretched = less force.

## Force-Couple Relationships
Muscles working together in synergistic groups to produce movement. A **force-couple** is a combination of muscles that produce a specific joint action together. For example:
- Upward rotation of scapula: Upper trapezius + lower trapezius + serratus anterior
- Pelvic stability: Transverse abdominis + multifidus + pelvic floor + diaphragm (inner unit core)

## Levers
1. **First-class lever**: Fulcrum between effort and load (e.g., see-saw, nodding head; neck extensors)
2. **Second-class lever**: Load between fulcrum and effort (e.g., wheelbarrow, calf raise)
3. **Third-class lever**: Effort between fulcrum and load (most common in body; e.g., biceps curl)

## Motor Behavior
### Motor Control
How the CNS integrates sensory information to control movement.
- **Closed-loop control**: Uses sensory feedback during movement
- **Open-loop control**: Pre-programmed; no feedback needed during rapid movements

### Motor Learning
- **Feedback**:
  - Internal (knowledge of performance) — how it felt
  - External (knowledge of results) — outcome
- **Stages of learning**:
  1. Cognitive: Understanding the task; many errors
  2. Associative: Refining movement; fewer errors
  3. Autonomous: Automatic; minimal conscious effort

### Motor Development
The progression of movement ability across the lifespan — from reflexive infant movements to age-related decline.

## Key Biomechanical Terminology
- **Anatomic position**: Standing erect, arms at sides, palms forward
- **Anterior (ventral)**: Toward the front
- **Posterior (dorsal)**: Toward the back
- **Medial**: Toward the midline
- **Lateral**: Away from the midline
- **Proximal**: Closer to the trunk
- **Distal**: Farther from the trunk
- **Superior**: Above; toward the head
- **Inferior**: Below; toward the feet
- **Contralateral**: Opposite side of the body
- **Ipsilateral**: Same side of the body
- **Bilateral**: Both sides
- **Unilateral**: One side
