# Chapter 21: The Optimum Performance Training Model

## Learning Objectives
- Identify the differences between linear and undulating periodization
- Identify the differences between macrocycles, mesocycles, and microcycles
- Evaluate exercise protocols used within all five phases of the OPT model
- Use the OPT model to design fitness programs for various client goals

## Program Design Fundamentals
**Program design**: An organizational structure of exercises with appropriate volume and intensity to attain a specific fitness or performance goal. The fitness professional provides the road map.

### The Iceberg Effect
Exercise programming involves much more than surface goals (fat loss, muscle gain, strength). The hidden foundation includes: teaching proper movement patterns, improving mobility/stability, enhancing endurance, reducing injury risk, and building neuromuscular efficiency.

## Periodization
**Periodization**: Macromanagement of training variables in an organized fashion to improve performance over time. Two primary objectives:
1. Divide training into distinct periods (phases)
2. Train different forms of strength in each period to control volume and prevent injury

### Time Cycles
- **Macrocycle**: Annual training plan; bird's-eye view of the entire year. Typically includes preparatory (preseason), competitive (in-season), and transition (off-season) periods.
- **Mesocycle**: Monthly training plan; typically 4 weeks. Details specific days and training styles.
- **Microcycle**: Weekly training plan; usually 7 days. Details specific workouts for each day.

### Linear vs. Undulating Periodization
- **Linear periodization**: Gradually increases intensity while simultaneously decreasing volume. Traditional approach (e.g., Phase 1 → 2 → 3 → 4 → 5 sequentially).
- **Undulating (nonlinear) periodization**: Varies intensity and volume within the week or microcycle. May alternate between phases. Example: Heavy day, light day, moderate day repeating weekly. Allows for more variation and may be better for non-competitive clients.

## OPT Model: Five Phases Deep Dive

### Phase 1: Stabilization Endurance
- **Primary adaptation**: Stabilization and muscular endurance
- **Goal**: Improve neuromuscular efficiency, correct muscle imbalances, build foundational strength
- **Resistance protocol**: 12–20 reps, 1–3 sets, 50–70% 1RM, 4/2/1 tempo, 0–90s rest
- **Cardio**: Zone 1, Stage I
- **Core**: Stabilization exercises (marching, plank, floor bridge)
- **Balance**: Stabilization (single-leg balance on floor)
- **Plyo**: Stabilization (jump with hold)
- **SAQ**: Stabilization (slow, controlled ladder drills)
- **Duration**: 2–6 weeks

### Phase 2: Strength Endurance
- **Primary adaptation**: Strength endurance
- **Goal**: Enhance stabilization endurance while increasing prime mover strength
- **Resistance protocol**: Superset — strength exercise (2/0/2 tempo) + stabilization exercise (4/2/1 tempo). 8–12 reps each, 2–4 sets, 70–80% 1RM, 0–60s rest
- **Cardio**: Zone 1 progressing to Zone 2 intervals, Stage II
- **Core**: Stabilization progressing to strength
- **Balance**: Stabilization progressing to strength
- **Plyo**: Stabilization progressing to strength
- **SAQ**: Stabilization progressing to strength
- **Key technique**: Bench press (strength) → Stability ball push-up (stabilization)

### Phase 3: Muscular Development (Hypertrophy)
- **Primary adaptation**: Muscle hypertrophy
- **Goal**: Maximize muscle growth
- **Resistance protocol**: 6–12 reps, 3–5 sets, 75–85% 1RM, 2/0/2 tempo, 0–60s rest
- **Volume**: High (total training volume is the key driver)
- **Cardio**: Zone 2, Stage II–III
- **Core, Balance, Plyo, SAQ**: Strength level
- **Optional for**: Clients with muscle growth or body composition goals

### Phase 4: Maximal Strength
- **Primary adaptation**: Maximal strength
- **Goal**: Increase motor unit recruitment, frequency of motor unit activation, and synchronization
- **Resistance protocol**: 1–5 reps, 4–6 sets, 85–100% 1RM, X/X/X tempo, 3–5 min rest
- **Cardio**: Zone 2–3, Stage II–III
- **Core, Balance, Plyo, SAQ**: Strength progressing to power
- **Only for**: Clients with significant strength training experience

### Phase 5: Power
- **Primary adaptation**: Power (rate of force production)
- **Goal**: Enhance neuromuscular efficiency via the stretch-shortening cycle
- **Resistance protocol**: Superset — heavy strength (1–5 reps, 85–100%) + explosive power (8–10 reps, 30–45% 1RM). 3–5 sets, 3–5 min rest
- **Cardio**: Zone 2–3, Stage III
- **Core, Balance, Plyo, SAQ**: Power level
- **Key technique**: Barbell squat (strength) → Jump squat (power)
- **Only for**: Advanced clients; those with sport/performance goals

## Program Design for Common Goals

### General Fitness / Health
- Phases 1 → 2; cycle between them
- Primary focus: movement quality, function, adherence

### Fat Loss
- Phase 1 (2–4 weeks) → Phase 2 (4–8 weeks) → Optionally Phase 3
- Combine with Zone 1–2 cardio (progressive) and nutrition guidance

### Muscle Gain (Hypertrophy)
- Phase 1 (2–4 weeks foundation) → Phase 3 (primary)
- Cycle between Phases 3 and 2 for variety

### Strength
- Phase 1 (2–4 weeks) → Phase 2 (4 weeks) → Phase 4 (primary)
- Must build stabilization and endurance first

### Athletic Performance / Power
- Phase 1 → Phase 2 → Phase 4 → Phase 5
- Periodize around competitive season
- Use undulating periodization for in-season

## Key Definitions
- **Training plan**: Specific outline detailing form of training, length, changes, and specific exercises
- **Periodization**: Planned manipulation of training variables organized to improve performance over time
- **Linear periodization**: Gradually increases intensity while decreasing volume
- **Undulating periodization**: Varies intensity and volume within a cycle
- **Overtraining**: Excessive frequency, volume, or intensity causing performance decline due to lack of proper rest/recovery
