# Glossary of Sports Supplement Terms

## A

**Adenosine**: A neuromodulator in the central nervous system that promotes sleep and relaxation. Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors, producing stimulant effects.

**Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP)**: The primary energy currency of cells. ATP is hydrolyzed to ADP + Pi to release free energy for muscle contraction and metabolic processes.

**Amino Acid**: Organic compounds that serve as the building blocks of proteins. Twenty amino acids are used in human protein synthesis.

**Anabolic**: Processes that build larger molecules from smaller ones; in muscle context, refers to net protein synthesis and tissue growth.

**Anaerobic Exercise**: High-intensity exercise where ATP demand exceeds oxidative capacity, relying heavily on glycolysis and phosphagen systems. Typically lasts 0-120 seconds.

**Antioxidant**: A molecule that inhibits oxidation of other molecules, reducing free radical damage. Examples include carnosine and vitamin C.

**Area Under the Curve (AUC)**: A pharmacokinetic measure representing total drug/supplement exposure over time after administration.

## B

**Beta-Alanine**: A non-proteogenic amino acid that combines with L-histidine to form carnosine, an intracellular pH buffer in skeletal muscle.

**Bioavailability**: The proportion of an ingested substance that reaches systemic circulation and is available at the target tissue.

**Branched-Chain Amino Acids (BCAAs)**: Leucine, isoleucine, and valine. These amino acids are unique for their roles in protein metabolism and neural function.

**Buffering Capacity**: The ability of a solution or tissue to resist changes in pH when acid is produced. In muscle, carnosine, bicarbonate, and phosphate contribute to buffering.

## C

**Carnosine (beta-alanyl-L-histidine)**: A dipeptide synthesized from beta-alanine and L-histidine. Acts as an intracellular pH buffer, antioxidant, and anti-glycation agent in skeletal muscle.

**Carnosinase**: The enzyme that breaks down carnosine in serum and various tissues. Notably absent in human skeletal muscle.

**Casein**: A slow-digesting milk protein that coagulates in the stomach, providing sustained amino acid release. Rich in leucine (~9.3%).

**Catabolic**: Metabolic processes that break down larger molecules; in muscle context, refers to net protein breakdown.

**Creatine**: A guanidine-phosphagen compound synthesized from arginine, glycine, and methionine. Primarily stored in skeletal muscle as phosphocreatine.

**Creatine Kinase (CK)**: Enzyme that catalyzes the reversible transfer of a phosphoryl group between creatine and ATP. Exists in mitochondrial and cytosolic isoforms.

**Creatine Monohydrate**: The most studied form of creatine (87.9% creatine by weight). White crystalline powder with one water molecule per creatine molecule.

**Creatinine**: The non-enzymatic degradation product of creatine and phosphocreatine. Excreted in urine; used clinically as a marker of kidney function.

**CYP1A2**: The cytochrome P450 enzyme responsible for metabolizing over 95% of caffeine. Genetic variants (-163A>C, rs762551) determine fast vs. slow metabolizer status.

## D

**Dihydrotestosterone (DHT)**: A potent androgen metabolite of testosterone formed by 5-alpha-reductase. Linked to androgenic hair loss in genetically susceptible individuals.

**Dose-Response**: The relationship between the amount of a substance administered and the magnitude of its effect.

## E

**Ergogenic Aid**: Any substance, device, or practice that enhances physical performance, recovery, or training adaptations.

**Essential Amino Acids (EAAs)**: Amino acids that cannot be synthesized by the body and must be obtained from the diet. Includes histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, and valine.

**Excitation-Contraction Coupling**: The process by which an electrical signal (action potential) is converted to a mechanical response (muscle contraction), involving calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum.

## F

**Fat-Free Mass (FFM)**: Total body mass minus fat mass. Includes muscle, bone, organs, and body water.

**Fenton Reaction**: A reaction in which iron or copper catalyzes the production of highly reactive hydroxyl radicals from hydrogen peroxide.

## G

**Guanidinoacetate (GAA)**: The immediate precursor to creatine in the endogenous synthesis pathway, formed from arginine and glycine.

**Glycogen**: The storage form of carbohydrate in muscle and liver. Muscle glycogen is the primary fuel source during moderate-to-high intensity exercise.

## H

**Half-Life (t1/2)**: The time required for the plasma concentration of a substance to decrease by 50%. Caffeine half-life: 4-6 hours (range 1.5-10 hours).

**HMB (Beta-Hydroxy-Beta-Methylbutyrate)**: A metabolite of the amino acid leucine, approximately 5% of leucine is converted to HMB. Exists as calcium salt (HMB-Ca) or free acid (HMB-FA).

**Hyperaminoacidemia**: Elevated concentration of amino acids in the blood, typically following protein ingestion. A key signal for muscle protein synthesis.

**Hyperhydration**: A state of increased total body water above normal levels, used as a strategy for exercise in hot environments.

**Hypertrophy**: An increase in the size of skeletal muscle fibers, primarily through the addition of contractile proteins (myofibrillar hypertrophy).

## I

**Intramuscular**: Within the muscle tissue.

**Ischemia**: Restriction of blood supply to tissues, causing oxygen and nutrient deficiency.

## L

**Leucine**: A branched-chain amino acid that serves as a key anabolic signal for activating muscle protein synthesis via the mTOR pathway. Found in highest concentration in dairy proteins (9-11%).

**Lean Body Mass (LBM)**: Body mass minus fat mass. Often used interchangeably with fat-free mass in supplement research.

**Loading Phase**: An initial period of higher supplement dosing designed to rapidly saturate tissue stores. Common for creatine: 20-25 g/day for 5-7 days.

## M

**Maintenance Dose**: The daily dose required to maintain elevated tissue levels after a loading phase. For creatine: 3-5 g/day.

**Mechanism of Action (MOA)**: The specific biochemical interaction through which a substance produces its effect.

**Meta-Analysis**: A statistical technique that combines results from multiple independent studies to estimate an overall effect size.

**mTOR (Mammalian Target of Rapamycin)**: A protein kinase that acts as a central regulator of cell growth and protein synthesis in response to nutrients, growth factors, and mechanical stimuli.

**Muscle Protein Breakdown (MPB)**: The rate at which muscle proteins are degraded. Balance between MPS and MPB determines net protein balance.

**Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS)**: The rate at which new muscle proteins are synthesized. Measured through stable isotope tracer incorporation techniques.

## N

**Neuroprotection**: Mechanisms and strategies used to protect the nervous system from injury or degeneration.

**Nitrogen Balance**: The difference between nitrogen intake (primarily from protein) and nitrogen excretion. Positive balance indicates net protein accretion.

## P

**Paraesthesia**: A tingling or prickling sensation on the skin. The most common side effect of beta-alanine supplementation, typically experienced with doses >800 mg.

**Phosphocreatine (PCr)**: The phosphorylated form of creatine, serving as a rapid energy buffer to resynthesize ATP during high-intensity exercise.

**Placebo Effect**: A beneficial outcome attributable to the belief in receiving an active treatment rather than the treatment's pharmacological action.

**Position Stand**: An official statement by an organization (e.g., ISSN) representing the consensus interpretation of scientific evidence on a specific topic.

**Post-Prandial**: The period following a meal, during which nutrients are being digested, absorbed, and metabolized.

**Protein Digestibility-Corrected Amino Acid Score (PDCAAS)**: A method of evaluating protein quality based on amino acid composition and digestibility. Milk proteins score highest (1.0).

**Proteolysis**: The enzymatic breakdown of proteins into peptides and amino acids. In muscle, refers to protein degradation pathways including ubiquitin-proteasome and caspase systems.

## R

**Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE)**: A subjective scale (typically 6-20 or 1-10) used to quantify an individual's perception of exercise intensity.

**Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA)**: The average daily dietary intake level sufficient to meet the nutrient requirements of nearly all (97-98%) healthy individuals. For protein: 0.8 g/kg/day.

**Relative Effect (RE)**: A calculated metric comparing pre-to-post changes in treatment groups versus placebo groups. RE > 100 indicates improvement for open-ended tasks; RE < 100 indicates improvement for time trials.

**Respiratory Exchange Ratio (RER)**: The ratio of carbon dioxide produced to oxygen consumed, used to estimate substrate utilization during exercise.

## S

**Sarcopenia**: Age-related progressive loss of skeletal muscle mass, strength, and function.

**Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP)**: A variation at a single position in a DNA sequence among individuals. SNPs in CYP1A2 and ADORA2A influence caffeine response.

**Splanchnic Uptake**: The extraction of amino acids by the gut and liver before they reach systemic circulation.

**Synergistic Effect**: An interaction where the combined effect of two substances exceeds the sum of their individual effects.

## T

**Time to Exhaustion (TTE)**: An open end-point performance test measuring how long an individual can maintain a given exercise intensity until volitional fatigue.

**Time Trial (TT)**: A fixed end-point performance test measuring the time to complete a predetermined distance or amount of work. More ecologically valid than TTE for competitive athletes.

**Training Status**: Classification of an individual's exercise experience (untrained, recreationally trained, or highly trained/elite). Influences supplement responsiveness.

## U

**Ubiquitin-Proteasome System**: The primary cellular pathway for targeted protein degradation, involving ubiquitin tagging and proteasome digestion.

## V

**VO2max**: Maximal oxygen uptake; the maximum rate at which the body can consume oxygen during exhaustive exercise. A key measure of aerobic capacity.

## W

**Washout Period**: The time required for a supplemented substance to return to baseline levels after cessation. For muscle carnosine: 6-15 weeks after beta-alanine cessation.

**Whey Protein**: A fast-digesting milk protein rich in EAAs (~50% by weight) and leucine (~11%). Exists as concentrate, isolate, and hydrolysate forms.
