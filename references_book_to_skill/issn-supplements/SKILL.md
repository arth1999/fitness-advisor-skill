# ISSN Sports Supplements — Combined Agent Skill

## Skill Purpose

This skill provides evidence-based guidance on sports supplements drawing from eight International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) position stands. It enables systematic evaluation of supplement efficacy, safety, dosing, and applicability across athlete populations, sport types, and individual characteristics.

## When to Use This Skill

Invoke this skill when:
- Evaluating whether a specific supplement is evidence-supported for a given athlete and sport
- Determining optimal dosing, timing, and duration for sports supplements
- Assessing safety, side effects, and contraindications
- Comparing supplement options for a specific performance or body composition goal
- Explaining mechanisms of action behind major sports supplements
- Providing ISSN consensus-based recommendations to athletes, coaches, or practitioners

## Core Framework: The Five-Question Supplement Evaluation

For any supplement question, answer these five questions in sequence:

### Q1: Does the Supplement Achieve Its Intended Physiological Effect?

**Creatine**: Yes. Increases intramuscular creatine and phosphocreatine by 20-40%. Upper storage limit ~160 mmol/kg dry muscle mass. Nearly 100% oral bioavailability as monohydrate. Non-responders exist (~20-30%), typically those with high baseline stores.

**Caffeine**: Yes. Rapidly absorbed (Tmax 30-120 min). Near 100% bioavailability. Antagonizes adenosine receptors in CNS. Metabolized by CYP1A2; half-life 4-6 hours (range 1.5-10 h).

**Beta-Alanine**: Yes. 4-6 g/day for 4 weeks increases muscle carnosine by 40-60%; 10 weeks by up to 80%. Carnosinase absent in human skeletal muscle, enabling long-term carnosine retention.

**Protein**: Yes. Increases plasma amino acid concentrations (aminoacidemia). 20-40 g dose contains sufficient EAAs (10-12 g) and leucine (1-3 g) to maximally stimulate MPS for 2-3 hours.

**HMB**: Yes. Plasma HMB peaks at 60-120 min (HMB-Ca) or 30 min (HMB-FA). HMB-FA demonstrates 91-97% greater AUC than equivalent HMB-Ca. Approximately 5% of dietary leucine is converted to HMB endogenously.

### Q2: What Is the Evidence Level for Performance/Health Benefit?

**Level A (Strong)**:
- Creatine monohydrate: Increases high-intensity exercise capacity 10-20%; lean body mass +0.69 kg; lower-body strength +13.5 kg (meta-analytical pooled data)
- Caffeine (endurance): Improves time-trial performance 2-4%; mean power output +2.9%
- Beta-Alanine: Improves exercise capacity in 60-240 s tasks; median performance improvement 2.85%
- Protein supplementation + resistance training: Augments lean mass and strength adaptations

**Level B (Moderate)**:
- HMB: Attenuates muscle damage (CK reduction 20-60%); increases lean mass and strength in untrained populations
- Caffeine (strength/power): Effect sizes 0.16-0.20 for strength; 0.28-0.38 for muscular endurance
- Sodium bicarbonate: Improves high-intensity performance with metabolic acidosis

**Level C (Preliminary)**:
- Creatine for neuroprotection post-concussion (animal models strong; human data emerging)
- Beta-Alanine for tactical/military performance
- HMB for aerobic performance and mitochondrial biogenesis

### Q3: What Is the Optimal Dosing Protocol?

| Supplement | Loading | Maintenance | Duration for Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Creatine | 20-25 g/day (5 g × 4) for 5-7 days | 3-5 g/day | Loading: 5-7 days for saturation; Gradual: 28 days for 3 g/day |
| Caffeine | Not applicable | 3-6 mg/kg per dose | Acute: 30-60 min pre-exercise |
| Beta-Alanine | 4-6 g/day (divided ≤1.6 g doses) for 4+ weeks | Not established | Minimum 2 weeks; optimal 4+ weeks |
| Protein | Not applicable | 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day; 20-40 g/meal; every 3-4 h | Acute and chronic benefits |
| HMB | 3 g/day for 14 days prior to novel exercise | 3 g/day | 2 weeks for recovery; 6+ weeks for trained performance gains |

**Dose scaling by body mass**: Creatine 0.3 g/kg/day loading; Caffeine 3-6 mg/kg; Protein 0.25 g/kg/meal; HMB 38 mg/kg/day; Beta-Alanine absolute dose (4-6 g) rather than per-kg.

### Q4: Is the Supplement Safe at Recommended Doses?

**Creatine**: The most extensively safety-studied supplement. No adverse effects on renal function, liver enzymes, muscle enzymes, electrolytes, or blood markers in studies up to 5 years (up to 30 g/day). Does NOT cause dehydration, cramping, kidney damage, hair loss, or increased fat mass. Only consistent side effect: weight gain (1-3 kg). FDA GRAS status (2020).

**Caffeine**: Safe at 3-6 mg/kg. Side effects increase with dose: anxiety, tachycardia, insomnia, GI distress. Avoid near bedtime (sleep disruption). Genetic variation affects anxiety sensitivity (ADORA2A) and metabolism (CYP1A2). Monitored but not banned by WADA (limit 12 ug/mL urine).

**Beta-Alanine**: Safe at recommended doses. Paraesthesia (tingling) is the only side effect — harmless, dose-dependent, managed by divided or sustained-release dosing. Long-term data (>1 year) limited.

**Protein**: Safe in healthy populations at 1.4-3.0 g/kg/day. Caution only in pre-existing kidney disease. High protein diets combined with energy restriction preserve lean mass while reducing fat.

**HMB**: Safe at 3-6 g/day for up to 1 year. No adverse hematological, hepatic, or renal effects. One rodent study noted increased fasting insulin — not replicated in humans.

## Supplement Mechanism Deep-Dive

### Creatine: The CK/PCr Energy System

```
ATP hydrolysis: ATP → ADP + Pi + Energy
PCr buffer:     PCr + ADP → ATP + Cr (catalyzed by creatine kinase)

Key functions:
1. Rapid ATP resynthesis during high-intensity exercise (anaerobic)
2. Energy shuttle: Mitochondrial ATP → PCr → Cytosolic ATP utilization sites
3. Reduce reliance on glycolysis → less lactate/H+ accumulation
4. Neuroprotection via maintenance of brain bioenergetics
5. Antioxidant effects via reduced ROS formation at mitochondria
```

**Co-factors for optimal effect**: Carbohydrate (47-97 g) or carbohydrate + protein (50 g) co-ingestion enhances creatine retention. Carbohydrate stimulates insulin, which increases creatine transport into muscle.

### Caffeine: Adenosine Receptor Antagonism

```
Primary mechanism (CNS):
Caffeine blocks adenosine A1 and A2A receptors
→ Increases neurotransmitter release (dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine, acetylcholine)
→ Reduces perception of effort (RPE ↓ ~5.6%)
→ Reduces muscle pain perception (analgesic effect)
→ Increases motor unit firing rates
→ Improves vigilance, alertness, and cognitive function

Peripheral mechanisms (secondary):
→ Enhanced Ca2+ mobilization from sarcoplasmic reticulum
→ Increased Na+/K+ pump activity
→ Improved excitation-contraction coupling
```

**Genetic moderation**: CYP1A2 -163A>C SNP determines metabolic rate. AA homozygotes ("fast metabolizers") show 6.8% time-trial improvement at 4 mg/kg. CC homozygotes ("slow metabolizers") may show performance impairment at equivalent doses in endurance tasks. ADORA2A variation affects anxiety response and sleep disruption sensitivity.

### Beta-Alanine: The Carnosine Buffer System

```
Beta-Alanine → + L-Histidine → Carnosine (via carnosine synthetase)

Carnosine properties:
- pKa 6.83 → effective proton buffer over physiological pH range
- Concentrated in skeletal muscle (10-40 mmol/kg dry weight)
- Higher in Type II (fast-twitch) fibers
- No carnosinase in human skeletal muscle → stable accumulation
- Additional roles: antioxidant (ROS scavenging, metal chelation), anti-glycation
```

**Why not just take carnosine?** Oral carnosine is hydrolyzed by serum carnosinase before reaching muscle. Beta-alanine is the rate-limiting precursor — supplementing beta-alanine bypasses this bottleneck.

**Loading dynamics**: Muscle carnosine increases ~20-30% after 2 weeks, 40-60% after 4 weeks, up to 80% after 10 weeks of 4-6 g/day. Washout period: 6-15 weeks.

### Protein: The MPS Signaling Cascade

```
Protein ingestion → Digestion → Plasma EAA elevation (aminoacidemia)
→ Leucine activates mTORC1
→ mTOR phosphorylates p70S6K and 4E-BP1
→ Initiation of mRNA translation
→ Muscle protein synthesis (MPS)

MPS time course:
- Peak: 45-90 min after protein ingestion
- Duration: ~3 hours elevated
- "Muscle-full effect": MPS returns to baseline despite continued aminoacidemia
- Refractory period between meals: ~3-4 hours needed before next anabolic response

Key determinants of MPS response:
1. EAA content (not NEAA) — 10-12 g EAAs per dose optimal
2. Leucine content — 1-3 g threshold to trigger mTOR
3. Digestion rate — faster proteins produce greater peak MPS
4. Dose — 20-40 g per meal for young adults; ~40 g for elderly
```

**Exercise synergy**: Resistance exercise sensitizes muscle to protein feeding for at least 24 hours. The anabolic effect is additive — protein + exercise > either alone.

### HMB: Dual Anti-Catabolic and Pro-Synthetic Action

```
HMB mechanism:
1. Anti-proteolytic: Inhibits ubiquitin-proteasome pathway ↓ proteasome expression and activity
   → Reduces muscle protein breakdown
2. Pro-synthetic: Stimulates mTOR → p70S6K and 4E-BP1 phosphorylation
   → Increases muscle protein synthesis
3. Anti-apoptotic: Attenuates caspase protease upregulation
   → Preserves myonuclei
4. Satellite cell activation: Increases MyoD, myogenin, MEF2 expression
   → Enhanced muscle regenerative capacity
5. Metabolic: Activates AMPK, Sirt1/Sirt3 → increases mitochondrial biogenesis and fatty acid oxidation
```

**Why HMB over leucine?** Leucine's anti-proteolytic effects require 10-20x the concentration needed to stimulate MPS (5-10 mM vs. 0.5 mM). HMB may be the metabolite that actually mediates leucine's anti-catabolic effects.

## Critical Methodological Principles for Evaluating Supplement Studies

When evaluating supplement research, apply these checks:

1. **Tissue level validation**: Does the study confirm the supplement actually reaches and increases target tissue concentration? (e.g., muscle biopsy for creatine/carnosine; plasma amino acids for protein)

2. **Dose adequacy**: Was the dose sufficient to produce an effect? Many negative studies used suboptimal dosing.

3. **Duration adequacy**: Was the study long enough? Creatine loading takes 5-7 days; beta-alanine requires 2-4 weeks; HMB requires 2 weeks pre-loading; trained athletes need 6+ weeks training studies.

4. **Training stimulus**: In trained populations, was the exercise stimulus sufficient to induce adaptation? Maintenance training will not show supplement effects.

5. **Population matching**: Were subjects matched to the supplement's expected benefit? (e.g., beta-alanine studied in exercise causing acidosis; creatine in high-intensity intermittent sports)

6. **Total protein/energy intake**: In protein timing and HMB studies, was total daily intake controlled? Differences in total protein intake have been shown to confound timing effects.

7. **Placebo effect assessment**: Were subjects adequately blinded? Caffeine studies should report whether blinding was successful, as belief in receiving caffeine can independently improve performance.

8. **Individual response analysis**: Group means can mask substantial inter-individual variability. Supplement-gene interactions (CYP1A2 for caffeine) may explain divergent responses.

## Key ISSN Consensus Statements

### Protein (2017)
1. Protein intake of 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day is sufficient for most exercising individuals
2. Acute protein dose: 0.25 g/kg or 20-40 g absolute, with 700-3000 mg leucine
3. Doses should be distributed every 3-4 hours across the day
4. Pre-sleep casein (30-40 g) increases overnight MPS without inhibiting lipolysis
5. Total daily protein intake is the strongest predictor of muscular hypertrophy
6. Rapidly digested, high-leucine proteins are most effective for MPS stimulation

### Creatine (2017)
1. Creatine monohydrate is the most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement available
2. Safe for short- and long-term use (up to 30 g/day for 5 years)
3. Acceptable for young athletes with proper precautions and supervision
4. Loading: 0.3 g/kg/day for 5-7 days; Maintenance: 3-5 g/day
5. Carbohydrate or carbohydrate + protein co-ingestion enhances muscle uptake
6. Creatine monohydrate is the optimal form; alternative forms are not superior

### Creatine Common Questions (2021)
1. Does NOT always lead to water retention (short-term only; intracellular proportional to muscle mass)
2. Is NOT an anabolic steroid (completely different chemical structure and mechanism)
3. Does NOT cause kidney damage in healthy individuals at recommended doses
4. Does NOT cause hair loss/baldness (single unreplicated study)
5. Does NOT cause dehydration or muscle cramping (actually reduces incidence)
6. Appears safe and potentially beneficial for children and adolescents
7. Does NOT increase fat mass
8. Loading phase is NOT required (3-5 g/day for 4 weeks achieves similar saturation)
9. Benefits older adults when combined with resistance training
10. Benefits extend beyond resistance/power activities to many sport types
11. Provides benefits for females across the lifespan
12. Alternative forms are NOT superior to creatine monohydrate

### Beta-Alanine (2015)
1. 4-6 g/day for 4 weeks significantly augments muscle carnosine
2. Appears safe in healthy populations at recommended doses
3. Only side effect: paraesthesia (tingling); managed by divided dosing or sustained-release
4. Most effective in open end-point tasks lasting 1-4 minutes; modest benefit up to ~25 minutes
5. Attenuates neuromuscular fatigue, especially in older subjects
6. Modest additive effect when combined with sodium bicarbonate
7. Multi-ingredient pre-workout supplements containing beta-alanine are effective if dosing is sufficient

### Caffeine (2021)
1. Small to moderate benefits across endurance, muscular endurance, strength, power, and sport-specific actions
2. Aerobic endurance shows most consistent moderate-to-large benefits
3. Optimal dose: 3-6 mg/kg body mass
4. Most common timing: 60 min pre-exercise (adjust for gum, gels, and other delivery forms)
5. Improves performance in both trained and untrained individuals
6. Genetic variation (CYP1A2, ADORA2A) contributes to inter-individual response differences
7. Ergogenic for cognitive function including attention, vigilance, and reaction time
8. May improve performance under sleep deprivation conditions
9. Supported for use in heat (3-6 mg/kg) and altitude (4-6 mg/kg)

### HMB (2013)
1. Enhances recovery by attenuating exercise-induced muscle damage (trained and untrained)
2. Consume in close proximity to workout
3. Most effective when consumed for 2 weeks prior to damaging exercise bout
4. 38 mg/kg/day (approximately 3 g) enhances hypertrophy, strength, and power
5. Two forms: HMB-Ca (calcium salt) and HMB-FA (free acid); HMB-FA shows faster/greater plasma appearance
6. Increases lean body mass and functionality in elderly, sedentary populations
7. Combined with structured exercise program, may result in greater fat mass declines
8. Mechanisms: inhibits proteolysis (ubiquitin-proteasome, caspase pathways) and stimulates protein synthesis (mTOR)
9. Chronic consumption is safe in young and old populations

## Supplement Interaction Notes

### Creatine + Beta-Alanine
Two studies suggest additive ergogenic effects; others do not. Both independently improve high-intensity performance. Combining is reasonable for athletes training in the 60-240 second domain.

### Creatine + Caffeine
Older studies suggested caffeine might antagonize creatine's ergogenic effects; recent evidence does not support this. Both independently effective.

### Creatine + HMB
Limited data; one study found HMB + creatine increased lean mass and strength additively (Jowko 2001); another found no additional benefit of HMB over creatine alone in trained rugby players.

### Beta-Alanine + Sodium Bicarbonate
Evidence supports a modest additive effect. Beta-alanine buffers intracellularly (carnosine), sodium bicarbonate buffers extracellularly. Probability of additive benefit: 64-78% across studies. Practical limitation: GI distress from sodium bicarbonate.

### Protein + All Supplements
Adequate protein intake (1.4-2.0 g/kg/day) is foundational. All other supplements are adjuncts to, not replacements for, adequate protein and energy intake.
