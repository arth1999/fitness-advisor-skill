# Supplement Evaluation Decision Framework

This framework provides the reasoning patterns and decision logic for evaluating sports supplements based on ISSN consensus methodology. Use these patterns to assess any supplement against evidence-based criteria.

---

## Pattern 1: Supplement Classification Matrix

Classify supplements into one of four categories based on evidence strength and risk profile:

| Evidence Strength | Low Risk | Moderate Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong (A)** | **Class I - Recommended**: creatine monohydrate, caffeine, beta-alanine, protein supplementation, sports drinks/carbohydrate | **Class II - Recommended with Caution**: evaluated on case basis |
| **Moderate (B)** | **Class III - Possibly Effective**: HMB, sodium bicarbonate, beetroot juice/nitrate | **Class IV - Insufficient Evidence**: most novel supplements |
| **Limited/Preliminary (C/D)** | **Class III - Possibly Effective**: many single-ingredient supplements | **Class IV - Insufficient Evidence**: supplements lacking peer-reviewed human trials |

---

## Pattern 2: Evidence Quality Assessment

Apply the following hierarchy when evaluating supplement claims:

### Tier 1: Gold Standard Evidence
- Multiple randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials (RCTs)
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses confirming effects
- Position stands from recognized scientific bodies (ISSN, ACSM, AND)
- Studies with direct measures of tissue concentration (muscle biopsy, MRS)

### Tier 2: Supportive Evidence
- Single well-designed RCTs
- Mechanistic studies establishing biological plausibility
- Dose-response studies identifying optimal dosing
- Studies in target populations (athletes, elderly, clinical)

### Tier 3: Preliminary Evidence
- Animal or in vitro studies only
- Cross-sectional or correlational data
- Small pilot studies without adequate statistical power
- Unpublished data or conference abstracts

### Tier 4: No/Conflicting Evidence
- Anecdotal reports, testimonials
- Marketing claims without peer-reviewed support
- Studies with major methodological flaws
- Conflicts of interest without independent replication

---

## Pattern 3: Supplement Efficacy Decision Tree

```
START: Is there evidence the supplement increases target tissue concentration?
├── NO → Tier 3/4 → Insufficient evidence for recommendation
└── YES → Does this translate to performance/health benefit?
    ├── NO → May have mechanistic rationale but lacks outcome evidence
    └── YES → Is the effect magnitude clinically/practically meaningful?
        ├── NO → Small effect size; marginal benefit
        └── YES → Are safety data adequate?
            ├── NO → Recommend only with caution and monitoring
            └── YES → Are dosing protocols established?
                ├── NO → Tentative recommendation; research needed
                └── YES → STRONG RECOMMENDATION (Class I)
```

## Pattern 4: Athlete-Specific Evaluation Framework

### Step 1: Sport/Activity Analysis
- **Energy system demand**: Phosphagen (0-10 s), glycolytic (10-120 s), oxidative (>120 s)
- **Injury risk profile**: Contact vs. non-contact, concussion risk, heat stress risk
- **Competition structure**: Single event, repeated bouts, tournament, season-long
- **Weight class considerations**: Need for weight management or body composition goals

### Step 2: Athlete Status Assessment
- **Training status**: Untrained (high responder), trained (moderate responder), elite (small margins matter)
- **Age**: Youth (<18), adult (18-50), masters (>50, anabolic resistance, sarcopenia risk)
- **Dietary pattern**: Omnivore vs. vegetarian/vegan (baseline creatine and carnosine stores)
- **Health status**: Pre-existing conditions, medication use, supplement interactions
- **Sex-specific factors**: Menstrual status, pregnancy/lactation, post-menopause, iron status

### Step 3: Supplement Selection Matrix
Map athlete profile to supplement prioritization:

| Profile | Primary Supplements | Secondary Supplements | Minimal Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strength/Power Athlete | Creatine, Protein, Caffeine | Beta-Alanine, HMB | Most single-ingredient supplements |
| Endurance Athlete | Caffeine, Carbohydrate, Protein | Beta-Alanine, Nitrate | HMB, most "fat burners" |
| Team Sport Athlete | Creatine, Caffeine, Protein | Beta-Alanine | Individual amino acids |
| Combat Sport Athlete (weight-making) | Protein (high intake), Creatine, Caffeine | HMB, Beta-Alanine | Fat burners without safety data |
| Masters Athlete (>50 yrs) | Creatine + Resistance Training, Protein | Beta-Alanine, HMB | Unstudied combinations |
| Adolescent Athlete | Protein, Carbohydrate | Creatine* | Most supplements without pediatric data |
| Female Athlete | Protein, Iron (if deficient), Creatine | Caffeine, Beta-Alanine | Supplements without female-specific data |
| Vegetarian/Vegan Athlete | Creatine**, Beta-Alanine**, Protein | Caffeine, Vitamin B12, Iron | Carnitine, Taurine |

*Only for serious/competitive supervised training, with balanced diet and knowledge of appropriate use
**Greater potential benefit due to lower baseline tissue stores

### Step 4: Risk-Benefit Analysis
- Evidence for benefit in the specific population and sport context
- Known side effects and their impact on the specific sport
- Long-term safety data availability
- Cost and practical feasibility
- Anti-doping compliance (WADA, NCAA, etc.)

## Pattern 5: Supplement Timing Decision Logic

### Pre-Exercise Window (0-120 min before)
- **Caffeine**: 60 min before (capsule) or 30 min (gum); dose 3-6 mg/kg
- **HMB-Ca**: 60-120 min before exercise for anti-catabolic effect
- **HMB-FA**: 30-60 min before exercise (faster absorption)
- **Sodium Bicarbonate**: 60-90 min before; risk of GI discomfort
- **Protein**: Can be consumed pre-exercise; anabolic effect of exercise lasts 24+ hours

### During Exercise
- **Carbohydrate**: 30-60 g/h for exercise >60-90 min
- **Protein + Carbohydrate**: 0.25 g/kg/h protein during prolonged endurance exercise to reduce muscle damage
- **Caffeine**: Mid-to-late exercise for longer events; cola, gels, gum
- **Hydration**: Individualized to sweat rate; sodium as needed

### Post-Exercise Window (0-2 hours after)
- **Protein**: 20-40 g within 0-2 h; timing less critical if total daily protein is adequate
- **Carbohydrate**: 1.0-1.2 g/kg immediately post-exercise for glycogen replenishment
- **Creatine**: Co-ingest with carbohydrate/protein for enhanced uptake

### Evening / Pre-Sleep
- **Casein Protein**: 30-40 g, 30 min before sleep for overnight MPS
- **Avoid**: Caffeine within 6 h of sleep (longer for slow metabolizers)

### Chronic Loading (Days to Weeks)
- **Creatine Loading**: 20-25 g/day for 5-7 days (optional; 3-5 g/day for 28 days is alternative)
- **Beta-Alanine Loading**: 4-6 g/day for minimum 2-4 weeks
- **HMB Pre-loading**: 3 g/day for 2 weeks before novel exercise stimulus

## Pattern 6: Evaluating Supplement Combinations

### Synergistic Combinations (Evidence-Supported)
- **Creatine + Carbohydrate/Protein**: Enhances creatine retention (higher muscle uptake)
- **Creatine + Beta-Alanine**: Potential additive effects on high-intensity exercise (mixed evidence)
- **Beta-Alanine + Sodium Bicarbonate**: Modest additive effect on proton buffering (64-78% probability)
- **Protein + Creatine**: Both independently effective; combined for lean mass and strength

### Neutral Combinations (No Expected Interaction)
- **Protein + Caffeine**: Different mechanisms; no known interaction
- **Creatine + Caffeine**: Mixed evidence; older studies suggested antagonism but not confirmed in recent research
- **HMB + Creatine**: Insufficient evidence for superiority over either alone

### Potentially Antagonistic
- Caffeine before sleep may interfere with overnight MPS from pre-sleep protein

### Stacking Principle
When combining supplements, each ingredient should be independently justified for the specific athlete and sport context. Multi-ingredient pre-workout supplements should demonstrate efficacy at the specific doses contained.

## Pattern 7: Interpretation of Effect Magnitudes

| Effect Size | Practical Significance |
|---|---|
| <0.2 | Trivial - Likely not practically meaningful |
| 0.2-0.5 | Small - Meaningful at elite level where marginal gains matter |
| 0.5-0.8 | Moderate - Meaningful for most competitive athletes |
| >0.8 | Large - Meaningful for all athletes |

For performance tests: A 2-3% improvement represents the difference between medaling and not in many Olympic endurance events.

## Pattern 8: Genetic Considerations for Supplement Response

### Caffeine
- **CYP1A2 rs762551**: AA = fast metabolizer (likely to benefit); AC/CC = slow metabolizer (may experience performance impairment at higher doses, especially >4 mg/kg)
- **ADORA2A rs5751876**: TT genotype = greater anxiety sensitivity and sleep disturbance risk from caffeine

### Creatine
- Non-responders exist (~20-30%); related to baseline muscle creatine levels, fiber type, habitual diet
- Vegetarians have lower baseline stores and typically respond more robustly

### Beta-Alanine
- High vs. low responders based on baseline carnosine levels and muscle fiber composition
- Vegetarians have lower baseline carnosine and generally show greater increases

## Pattern 9: Female Athlete Considerations

### Supplement Prioritization for Female Athletes
1. **Iron**: Screen for deficiency (serum ferritin); female athletes at higher risk due to menstruation
2. **Protein**: Same per-kg recommendations as males; ensure adequate energy availability
3. **Creatine**: Females have higher baseline intramuscular creatine (per unit muscle mass); may show smaller but still significant responses. Potential benefits: muscle mass, strength, bone health, cognitive function, mood, pregnancy outcomes
4. **Caffeine**: Oral contraceptive use prolongs caffeine half-life; menstrual phase may affect metabolism
5. **Beta-Alanine**: Effective in females; women may experience greater paraesthesia
6. **Calcium/Vitamin D**: Important for bone health; female athlete triad / RED-S prevention

### Female-Specific Kinetics
- Creatine kinase levels vary across menstrual cycle (highest during menstruation)
- Oestrogen and progesterone influence creatine synthesis enzymes
- Pregnancy increases creatine demand; maternal supplementation shows neuroprotective benefits in animal models
- Post-menopausal athletes may benefit more from creatine + resistance training for bone density preservation
