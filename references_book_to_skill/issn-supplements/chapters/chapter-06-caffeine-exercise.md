# Chapter 6: ISSN Position Stand on Caffeine and Exercise Performance (2021)

**Source**: Guest NS, et al. J Int Soc Sports Nutr. 2021;18:1.

## ISSN Position Statements

1. Caffeine acutely enhances various aspects of exercise performance: muscular endurance, movement velocity, muscular strength, sprinting, jumping, throwing, and aerobic/anaerobic sport-specific actions. Small to moderate benefits.
2. Aerobic endurance shows the most consistent moderate-to-large benefits, though magnitude differs between individuals.
3. Optimal dose: 3-6 mg/kg body mass. Minimal effective dose may be as low as 2 mg/kg. Very high doses (9 mg/kg) associated with high side effects and not required for ergogenic effect.
4. Most common timing: 60 min pre-exercise. Optimal timing depends on caffeine source (e.g., chewing gum requires shorter wait time).
5. Improves performance in both trained and untrained individuals.
6. Inter-individual differences attributed to genetic variation (CYP1A2 for metabolism; ADORA2A for sensitivity) and habitual caffeine intake.
7. Ergogenic for cognitive function including attention and vigilance.
8. May improve cognitive and physical performance under sleep deprivation.
9. Supported for use in heat (3-6 mg/kg) and altitude (4-6 mg/kg) endurance exercise.
10. Alternative sources (chewing gum, mouth rinses, energy gels, chews) improve performance, primarily aerobic.
11. Energy drinks and pre-workout supplements containing caffeine enhance both anaerobic and aerobic performance.

## Caffeine Pharmacokinetics

### Absorption
- Rapid absorption from GI tract (primarily small intestine, also stomach)
- Appears in blood within minutes
- Tmax: 30-120 min after oral ingestion (varies by form)
- Near 100% absolute bioavailability
- No hepatic first-pass effect
- Saliva concentration: 65-85% of plasma levels

### Distribution
- Hydrophobic → crosses all biological membranes including blood-brain barrier
- Distributed throughout total body water

### Metabolism
- >95% metabolized by CYP1A2 enzyme in liver
- Primary metabolites: Paraxanthine (84%), Theobromine (12%), Theophylline (4%)
- Metabolites further demethylated and oxidized to urates
- ~3-5% excreted unchanged in urine

### Half-Life
- Average: 4-6 hours
- Range: 1.5-10 hours (high inter-individual variability)
- Prolonged by: Pregnancy, hormonal contraceptives, liver disease
- Shortened by: Cigarette smoking, heavy caffeine use (enzyme induction)

### Form-Dependent Pharmacokinetics
- **Capsule**: Tmax ~30-67 min (standard research form; easiest blinding)
- **Coffee**: Tmax ~30-42 min (volume, temperature, rate of consumption have small effects)
- **Cola**: Tmax ~90-120 min (delayed absorption)
- **Chewing gum**: Significantly faster absorption (buccal cavity absorption bypasses GI tract); comparable overall bioavailability
- **Energy drinks**: Tmax varies with consumption rate (fast 2 min vs. slow 20 min: minor differences)
- **Aerosols (nasal/mouth)**: Pharmacokinetic profile comparable to beverages; limited research

## Mechanism of Action

### Primary: Central Nervous System (CNS) — Adenosine Receptor Antagonism

Caffeine's molecular structure resembles adenosine → competitively binds to adenosine receptors (primarily A1 and A2A subtypes) → blocks adenosine's inhibitory effects:

**Effects of adenosine blockade**:
1. Increased neurotransmitter release (dopamine, serotonin, acetylcholine, norepinephrine, glutamate)
2. Increased motor unit firing rates
3. Reduced perception of effort (RPE decreased ~5.6% in meta-analysis; explains ~29% of performance variance)
4. Reduced muscle pain perception (analgesic effect via dopamine-mediated pathways)
5. Improved mood, vigilance, focus, alertness
6. Enhanced cognitive function (especially under fatigue/sleep deprivation)

**Key evidence for CNS mechanism**:
- Davis et al. (2003): Caffeine injected into rat brains increased run time 60% (120 vs. 75 min); peripheral injection had no effect; effect abolished when adenosine agonist co-administered
- Low doses (1-3 mg/kg) that do not produce significant metabolic changes (FFA, RER, lactate) still improve performance

### Secondary: Peripheral Effects

1. **Calcium mobilization**: Enhances Ca2+ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum → improved excitation-contraction coupling; attenuates fatigue-related decline in Ca2+ release
2. **Na+/K+ pump activity**: Enhanced activity may improve membrane excitability
3. **Glycogen sparing (historical)**: Earlier theory that caffeine increased FFA oxidation and glycogen sparing is now largely dismissed; inconsistent RER changes; cannot explain benefits in short-duration exercise

### The Placebo Effect
- Belief in having received caffeine can independently improve performance
- Linear dose-response relationship observed even when all doses were placebo (Beedie 2006)
- Correct identification of caffeine improves performance; correct identification of placebo may worsen it
- Blinding efficacy should be assessed and reported in caffeine studies

## Performance Effects by Exercise Type

### Aerobic Endurance — Strongest and Most Consistent Evidence

**Magnitude**:
- Meta-analysis (46 studies): Mean power output +2.9 ± 2.2% (effect size 0.22)
- Time-trial completion: +2.3 ± 2.6% improvement (effect size 0.28)
- Range: -3.0% to +15.9% across individual studies
- A <1% change affects medal rankings in Olympic endurance events (~45 s to 8 min events)

**Supported endurance sports**:
- Cycling (most studied): Consistently improved time-trial performance
- Running: Improved 5 km, 10 km, marathon times
- Cross-country skiing: Reduced time to complete set distance
- Swimming: Improved 1600 m time in competitive swimmers

### Muscular Endurance — Moderate Evidence

**Magnitude**:
- Meta-analyses: Effect size 0.28-0.38; 6-7% improvement
- Repetitions to failure at 60-70% 1RM consistently increased
- Mixed results: Some studies show no effect or even impairments
- CYP1A2 genotype interaction: AA genotype benefited; C-allele carriers did not (30 resistance-trained men study)

### Strength — Small but Significant Effect

**Magnitude**:
- Three meta-analyses: Effect size 0.16-0.20; 2-7% improvement
- Most meaningful for competitive strength athletes (powerlifting, weightlifting)

### Power / Sprint Performance — Mixed Evidence

**Single sprints / Wingate**:
- Meta-analysis: Enhanced mean power (ES 0.18, +3%) and peak power (ES 0.27, +4%)
- Older meta-analysis: No benefit (ES 0.17, p=0.36)
- Substantial individual variability: 28-50% of participants show no improvement

**Repeated sprints**:
- Meta-analysis: Total work, best sprint, and last sprint performance NOT affected overall
- Rest interval matters: 90-s rest between sprints = ergogenic effect; 20-s rest = no effect
- Intermittent sprint protocols more likely to show benefit

### Ballistic Movements (Jumps, Throws) — Small Effect

- Jump height: Effect size 0.17-0.22 (2-4% improvement)
- Shot put distance: Significant improvement in intercollegiate athletes
- Medicine ball throw: Significant improvement with 6 mg/kg
- Limited research on throwing events overall

### Movement Velocity in Resistance Exercise — Moderate to Large Effects

- Mean and peak velocity improved in both upper and lower-body exercises
- Measured via linear position transducers
- Consistent findings but area merits further research

### Sport-Specific Performance

| Sport | Effects | Key References |
|---|---|---|
| Basketball | Increased jump height (AA genotype only), free throws, rebounds; no sprint improvement | Puente 2017; Scanlan 2019 |
| Soccer | Increased total distance, passing accuracy, jump height | Foskett 2009; Lara 2014 |
| Volleyball | Increased successful actions; decreased imprecise actions; no physical performance improvement in professional females | Perez-Lopez 2015; Del Coso 2014 |
| Rugby | Increased body impacts, running pace, muscle power; no agility improvement | Del Coso 2013; Assi 2020 |
| Ice Hockey | Limited impact on skill performance; may enhance physicality | Madden 2019 |
| Combat Sports | Increased offensive actions and throws | Diaz-Lara 2016 |
| Cross-Country Skiing | Reduced time; improved time to task failure | Stadheim 2013 |

## Factors Affecting Response

### Genetics — Major Source of Individual Variation

**CYP1A2 (rs762551) — Caffeine Metabolism**:
- AA genotype ("fast metabolizers"): 6.8% time-trial improvement at 4 mg/kg (Guest 2018)
- AC genotype: No significant effect at either 2 or 4 mg/kg
- CC genotype ("slow metabolizers"): 13.7% performance IMPAIRMENT at 4 mg/kg; no effect at 2 mg/kg
- Consistent with earlier study (Womack 2012): Faster cycling times with caffeine only in AA genotype

**ADORA2A (rs5751876) — Caffeine Sensitivity**:
- TT genotype: All 6 subjects improved 10-min cycling TT with caffeine (pilot study, Loy 2015)
- C allele carriers: Only 1 of 6 improved
- Also implicated in sleep quality: CC/CT genotypes more sensitive to caffeine-induced sleep disturbance
- Anxiety sensitivity: TT genotype associated with greater anxiety increase after caffeine

### Habitual Caffeine Intake — Equivocal

- Short abstinence (12-48 h): May produce performance improvements simply by reversing withdrawal effects
- 4-day abstinence in habitual users: Caffeine still improved performance
- 4-week supplementation in low-habitual users: Tolerance developed; ergogenic effect no longer apparent
- 20-day consecutive supplementation: Ergogenic effect maintained but effect size attenuated
- 40 cyclists study: Caffeine improved performance regardless of habitual intake (low 58 mg, moderate 143 mg, high 351 mg/day)

### Training Status
- Both trained and untrained improve with caffeine (most studies)
- Competitive swimmers improved; recreational did not (but different distances used)
- One study: Trained improved in morning only; untrained improved in evening only
- Generally: Training status does NOT consistently moderate caffeine's ergogenic effect

### Time of Day
- Potential temporal effect: Trained athletes may respond better in morning
- Caffeine in late afternoon (18:00): Sleep disruption in 6/13 participants

## Caffeine and Sleep

- Sleep is essential for recovery from high-intensity training
- Caffeine negatively impacts: Sleep latency, wake after sleep onset (WASO), sleep efficiency, sleep duration
- Caffeine INGESTION TIMING is critical:
  - Morning ingestion: Minimal sleep impact
  - Late afternoon (18:00): Significant insomnia risk
  - Rule of thumb: Avoid within 6 hours of bedtime (longer for slow metabolizers)
- ADORA2A genetic variation influences sleep disruption sensitivity:
  - CC/CT genotypes: Greater sensitivity; higher EEG beta activity in non-REM sleep after caffeine
  - TT genotype: Minimal sleep disruption from caffeine
- Beneficial for cognitive and physical performance during sleep deprivation (military applications documented)

## Side Effects

- Incidence and severity increase linearly with dose
- Common side effects at high doses: Tachycardia, heart palpitations, anxiety, headaches, insomnia, GI distress
- Sport-specific considerations: Jitters/anxiety detrimental in precision sports (tennis, biathlon shooting); may be beneficial in strength/power contact sports (football lineman)
- Anxiety: Elite athletes have 50% mental health issue prevalence; caffeine may exacerbate
- Minimized by using lower effective doses (3 mg/kg vs. 6+ mg/kg)

## Cognitive Performance

- Doses from 32-300 mg enhance attention, vigilance, reaction time
- Caffeine in carbohydrate performance bar: Improved endurance + complex cognitive ability during and after 2.5-h cycling
- 300 mg energy drink: Improved psychomotor vigilance (reaction time)
- Military applications under sleep deprivation:
  - 600-800 mg via chewing gum: Maintained vigilance, improved run times, obstacle course
  - 100-300 mg repeated doses: Better for vigilance than single large bolus
  - 200 mg × 4 over 3 days: Maintained psychomotor speed, event detection, logical reasoning
- Without sleep deprivation: Cognitive effects less pronounced; some studies show no benefit

## Caffeine in Heat and Altitude

**Heat**:
- 3-6 mg/kg supported for endurance exercise in hot environments
- Does not appear to compromise thermoregulation

**Altitude**:
- 4-6 mg/kg supported for exercise at altitude
- May help offset hypoxia-related performance decrements

## Alternative Caffeine Sources

**Chewing Gum**:
- Faster absorption via buccal cavity; comparable bioavailability
- Improved cycling performance when administered immediately pre-exercise (but not 1-2 h before)
- Practical for military and field sports

**Mouth Rinse**:
- Limited but emerging evidence
- May act via CNS receptors in oral cavity without requiring ingestion

**Energy Drinks**:
- Typically contain caffeine + carbohydrate + other ingredients
- Enhance both anaerobic and aerobic performance
- Dose standardization is challenging (caffeine content variable)

**Coffee**:
- Performance effects similar to anhydrous caffeine in most studies
- Natural variability in caffeine content (8-9 fold differences in commercially purchased coffee)
- Other compounds in coffee (chlorogenic acids) may modulate effects

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Dose**: 3-6 mg/kg body mass (e.g., 210-420 mg for a 70 kg athlete)
2. **Timing**: 60 min pre-exercise (capsule/coffee); 30 min pre-exercise (gum); mid/late exercise for events >90 min
3. **Dose minimization**: Start at 2-3 mg/kg; increase only if needed. Higher doses increase side effects without proportional performance benefit.
4. **Individualization**: Test in training before competition; monitor sleep quality and anxiety response
5. **Genetics consideration**: CYP1A2 CC genotype may not benefit and could experience performance impairment at >2 mg/kg in endurance events
6. **Sleep hygiene**: Avoid within 6 hours of bedtime (longer for slow metabolizers or those with ADORA2A sensitivity variants)
7. **Abstinence protocol**: 2-7 days caffeine withdrawal before competition if seeking to maximize acute response (evidence equivocal)
8. **Sport matching**: Most beneficial in endurance sports; beneficial but variable in strength/power; test individually in sport-specific contexts
9. **WADA status**: Monitored but not banned; maintain urinary caffeine <12 ug/mL (corresponds to ~10 mg/kg orally)
