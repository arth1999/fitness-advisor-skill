# Chapter 2: ISSN Position Stand on Protein and Exercise (2017)

**Source**: Jager R, et al. J Int Soc Sports Nutr. 2017;14:20.

## ISSN Position Statements

1. Acute exercise (especially resistance exercise) and protein ingestion synergistically stimulate MPS.
2. Daily protein intake: 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day for most exercising individuals.
3. Higher intakes (>3.0 g/kg/d) may enhance body composition in resistance-trained individuals.
4. Per-meal dose: 0.25 g/kg or 20-40 g absolute, with 700-3000 mg leucine.
5. Doses should be evenly distributed every 3-4 hours across the day.
6. Pre- or post-workout ingestion both effective; exercise anabolic effect lasts at least 24 hours.
7. Whole food is viable; supplementation ensures quality and quantity while controlling calories.
8. Rapidly digested, high-EAA, adequate-leucine proteins most effective for MPS.
9. Protein type and quality affect amino acid bioavailability.
10. Focus on whole food sources containing all EAAs.
11. Endurance athletes: adequate carbohydrate is primary; protein may offset muscle damage.
12. Pre-sleep casein (30-40 g) increases overnight MPS and metabolic rate without inhibiting lipolysis.

## Recommended Intake

### Daily Protein Intake by Population

| Population | g/kg/day | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| RDA (sedentary) | 0.8 | Minimum to prevent deficiency |
| Recreational exerciser | 1.0-1.4 | Support general fitness needs |
| Endurance athlete | 1.2-1.6 | Higher during intensified training |
| Strength/power athlete | 1.4-2.0 | Support MPS and training adaptations |
| Energy-restricted athlete | 2.0-3.0 | Preserve lean mass; 2-3x RDA |
| Elderly | 1.2-2.0 | Counteract anabolic resistance |

The traditional RDA of 0.8 g/kg/day systematically underestimates needs when using the nitrogen balance technique. Athlete requirements are 50-175% greater than sedentary controls.

### Per-Meal Protein Dosing

- **Absolute dose**: 20-40 g high-quality protein (optimal for MPS stimulation)
- **Relative dose**: 0.25 g/kg body weight per meal
- **Elderly**: ~40 g dose may be needed to maximize MPS
- **Very high dose**: 70 g attenuates muscle protein breakdown more than 40 g
- **Upper practical limit**: MPS returns to baseline after ~3 hours despite continued aminoacidemia ("muscle-full effect")

### Leucine Threshold

- 1-3 g leucine per meal is the threshold for activating mTOR-mediated MPS
- Post-exercise leucine requirement may increase to ~3.5 g
- Dairy proteins provide highest leucine density (whey ~11%, casein ~9.3%)
- Leucine alone stimulates MPS but sustained synthesis requires all EAAs

### Protein Distribution Pattern

- Optimal: 4-6 meals of 20-40 g protein, spaced every 3-4 hours
- Intermediate dosing (4 × 20 g) superior to bolus (2 × 40 g) or pulse (8 × 10 g) for 12-hour MPS
- Protein "pacing" strategy: equitable distribution across meals consistently improves body composition

## Protein Timing

### Pre- vs. Post-Exercise

- Muscle is sensitized to protein feeding for at least 24 hours after resistance exercise
- Pre-exercise ingestion increases amino acid delivery during exercise
- Post-exercise ingestion coincides with peak MPS signaling (30-60 min post-exercise)
- Net effect: both pre- and post-workout protein are effective
- Total daily protein intake is a stronger predictor of hypertrophy than timing per se
- Meta-analysis: consuming protein within 1-hour post-exercise has small significant effect on hypertrophy, but effect disappears when controlling for total protein intake

### Practical Timing Strategy
- If pre-exercise meal was consumed <5 hours before workout completion, immediate post-exercise protein is less critical
- No benefit from NOT consuming protein post-exercise → always recommend consuming a protein-containing meal
- Adding carbohydrate to post-exercise protein does not further enhance MPS beyond protein alone; carbohydrate primarily supports glycogen replenishment

### Pre-Sleep Protein

- 30-40 g casein protein 30 minutes before sleep
- Increases overnight MPS in both young and old individuals
- Does NOT blunt overnight lipolysis or fat oxidation (casein specifically; whey may increase RER)
- Morning resting metabolic rate elevated after pre-sleep protein
- 12-week study: pre-sleep casein + resistance training → greater muscle mass and strength gains
- Evening protein may be superior to morning protein for lean mass accretion (non-significant trend)

## Protein Quality and Sources

### Quality Hierarchy (by leucine content and MPS stimulation)

**Tier 1 — Most Effective for MPS**:
- Whey protein (hydrolysate > isolate > concentrate): ~11% leucine, rapid digestion
- Milk (whole, fat-free): Blend of whey + casein, sustained delivery

**Tier 2 — Effective**:
- Egg protein: ~8.6% leucine, complete amino acid profile, moderate-fast digestion
- Beef/meat: ~8% leucine, micronutrient-rich, moderate digestion
- Casein: ~9.3% leucine, slow digestion, ideal for pre-sleep

**Tier 3 — Less Effective (but viable alternatives)**:
- Soy protein: ~8% leucine, intermediate digestion, phytoestrogens may inhibit mTOR
- Rice protein: Moderate leucine, medium-slow absorption; comparable to whey for body composition in one 8-week study
- Wheat protein: ~6.8% leucine, requires ~60 g dose to match MPS of 35 g casein

### Protein Blends
- Whey + Casein blends provide both rapid aminoacidemia and sustained delivery
- Whey + Soy blends extend the MPS response window (2-4 h) compared to whey alone (0-2 h)
- Fat-free milk (natural whey/casein blend) stimulates greater lean mass gains than soy protein

### Criteria for Protein Comparison
1. Leucine content (% of total protein)
2. Digestion rate (fast → slow)
3. EAA content and profile
4. PDCAAS or IAAO score
5. Bioactive peptide content
6. Micronutrient co-delivery (meat: iron, B12, selenium; eggs: choline, lutein)

## Effects on Exercise Performance

### Endurance Performance
- Adding protein to adequate carbohydrate does NOT improve endurance performance
- Higher protein (3.3 g/kg/d) with lower carbohydrate (5.9 g/kg/d) significantly IMPAIRS cycling time-trial performance (20% longer completion time) vs. typical endurance diet
- Protein during endurance exercise (0.25 g/kg/h) + carbohydrate may:
  - Suppress muscle damage markers (CK) 12-24 hours post-exercise
  - Reduce subjective muscle soreness
  - Promote whole body net protein balance (but not further MPS increase)

### Resistance Exercise Performance
- Most individual studies: small to modest strength improvements, often non-significant
- Meta-analysis (22 studies, 680 subjects): protein supplementation → +13.5 kg lower-body strength vs. placebo
- Effect moderated by: training program design, duration, training status, energy intake, protein quality/quantity, co-ingestion of other ergogenic aids

### Body Composition
- Protein supplementation + resistance training: +0.69 kg FFM (meta-analysis)
- Higher protein (2-3x RDA) + energy restriction + exercise: maximizes fat loss while preserving FFM
- 2x RDA group: 70% of weight loss from fat mass; 1x RDA group: lost more FFM

## Mechanisms

1. Exercise sensitizes muscle to protein feeding for 24+ hours
2. Protein ingestion increases plasma EAAs → stimulates MPS via mTOR pathway
3. Leucine acts as the primary anabolic signal (mTOR → p70S6K → 4E-BP1)
4. MPS peaks at 45-90 min post-ingestion, returns to baseline after ~3 hours
5. Muscle-full effect: MPS decline despite continued aminoacidemia
6. ATP/energy status may limit sustained MPS; leucine + carbohydrate between meals can extend protein synthesis
7. Insulin primarily anti-catabolic (reduces MPB) at low-moderate levels; adding carbohydrate to protein provides no additional MPS benefit

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Daily intake**: 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day from high-quality sources
2. **Per-meal**: 20-40 g protein (0.25 g/kg) with 1-3 g leucine
3. **Frequency**: Every 3-4 hours; 4-6 feedings per day
4. **Post-exercise**: Consume protein within 2 hours (timing less critical with adequate total intake)
5. **Pre-sleep**: 30-40 g casein, 30 min before sleep
6. **During endurance exercise**: 0.25 g/kg/h protein added to carbohydrate may reduce muscle damage
7. **Protein sources**: Prioritize whey, milk, egg, meat; plant proteins are viable in adequate doses
8. **Safety**: High protein intakes (up to 3.0+ g/kg/d) are safe in healthy individuals
9. **Energy restriction**: Increase protein to 2.0-3.0 g/kg/d to preserve lean mass
10. **Elderly**: Higher per-meal doses (~40 g) and total intake (1.2-2.0 g/kg/d) to overcome anabolic resistance
