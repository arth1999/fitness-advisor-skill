# Chapter 4: Common Questions and Misconceptions About Creatine Supplementation (2021)

**Source**: Antonio J, et al. J Int Soc Sports Nutr. 2021;18:13.

## Overview

Despite over 500 peer-reviewed publications on creatine supplementation and the comprehensive 2017 ISSN position stand, misconceptions persist. This paper assembled an international team of research experts to provide evidence-based answers to the 12 most common questions.

## Q1: Does Creatine Lead to Water Retention?

**Short answer**: Only in the short term (first several days); long-term studies show no disproportionate water retention relative to muscle mass gains.

**Evidence**:
- Short-term (3-7 days): Increases in total body water (TBW) and intracellular water (ICW) have been observed
- Long-term studies (5-10 weeks): No significant change in ICW, extracellular water (ECW), or TBW
- One study found TBW +7.0% and ICW +9.2% after 8 weeks, but the ratio of skeletal muscle mass to ICW remained similar — water increased proportionally with muscle mass
- ICW increase is an important cellular signal for protein synthesis, driving muscle mass gains

## Q2: Is Creatine an Anabolic Steroid?

**Short answer**: No. Creatine has a completely different chemical structure, mechanism of action, and legal classification.

**Key distinctions**:
- Anabolic steroids: Synthetic testosterone derivatives; bind androgen receptors; increase gene expression of muscle-specific proteins; Schedule III controlled substances (illegal without prescription)
- Creatine: Methylguanidine-acetic acid; increases PCr stores to enhance ATP production during exercise; dietary supplement regulated under DSHEA (1994); no legal restrictions

## Q3: Does Creatine Cause Kidney Damage / Renal Dysfunction?

**Short answer**: No. At recommended dosages in healthy individuals, there is no evidence of kidney damage.

**Evidence**:
- Multiple controlled studies show no adverse effects on kidney function at doses up to 30 g/day for up to 5 years
- Blood creatinine may transiently increase but this reflects increased creatine intake, not impaired kidney function (creatinine is the normal degradation product of creatine)
- Clinical populations (type 2 diabetes, elderly) show no renal impairment with creatine
- The 1998 case study that sparked concern involved a patient with pre-existing kidney disease on immunosuppressants
- After ~30 years of post-marketing surveillance with thousands of exposures, no evidence of kidney harm exists
- **Caution**: Individuals with pre-existing kidney disease should consult a physician

## Q4: Does Creatine Cause Hair Loss / Baldness?

**Short answer**: No. The claim stems from one unreplicated study.

**The single study** (van der Merwe 2009):
- College rugby players, 25 g/day for 7 days + 5 g/day for 14 days
- DHT increased 56% after loading, remained 40% above baseline at maintenance
- However: Baseline DHT was 23% lower in creatine group vs. placebo; DHT changes remained within normal clinical limits; no increase in total testosterone
- This result has NEVER been replicated

**What other studies show**:
- 12 other studies measured testosterone response to creatine (3-25 g/day, 6 days to 12 weeks)
- 10 of 12 studies: no change in testosterone
- 5 studies measured free testosterone: no increases found
- No study has ever reported actual hair loss/baldness in humans from creatine

## Q5: Does Creatine Lead to Dehydration and Muscle Cramping?

**Short answer**: No. Evidence shows creatine REDUCES cramping and dehydration incidence.

**Evidence**:
- NCAA Division I football players (38 creatine users vs. 34 non-users, 4-month season): Creatine users had significantly LESS cramping, heat illness/dehydration, muscle tightness, muscle strains, and total injuries
- Hemodialysis patients: Creatine (12 g pre-dialysis) reduced symptomatic muscle cramping by 60%
- Creatine may improve fluid distribution and electrolyte balance; its osmotic properties enhance hyperhydration
- ACSM's early 2000s caution against creatine in hot environments was based on speculation, not evidence
- Subsequent studies: Creatine with or without glycerol is an effective hyperhydration strategy for hot/humid exercise

## Q6: Is Creatine Harmful for Children and Adolescents?

**Short answer**: Evidence suggests creatine is safe and potentially beneficial for children and adolescents.

**Evidence**:
- Clinical studies in pediatric lupus, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, and TBI show no adverse effects
- Children with TBI (0.4 g/kg/day for 6 months): improved outcomes with no negative lab markers
- FDA classified creatine as GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) in 2020
- High school survey data: ~21% of boys and 3% of girls already use creatine
- ISSN recommendation: Acceptable for young athletes in serious/competitive supervised training who consume a balanced diet, know appropriate use, and don't exceed recommended dosages

## Q7: Does Creatine Increase Fat Mass?

**Short answer**: No. Creatine does not increase fat mass.

**Evidence**:
- Short-term studies (1-8 weeks): No effect on fat mass
- Long-term studies (12 weeks - 2 years): No increase in fat mass; some show fat mass reduction
- Meta-analysis (19 studies, 609 older adults): Creatine group had greater reduction in body fat percentage and lost ~0.5 kg more fat mass than placebo
- Children with acute lymphoblastic leukemia on creatine: significant reduction in fat mass vs. fat gain in placebo
- Postmenopausal women on creatine for 2 years: no change in absolute or relative body fat

## Q8: Is a Creatine 'Loading Phase' Required?

**Short answer**: No. A loading phase is not required; 3-5 g/day achieves similar muscle saturation over 4 weeks.

**Evidence**:
- Classic study (Hultman 1996): 3 g/day for 28 days achieved similar ~20% muscle creatine increase as 20 g/day for 6 days
- Loading advantages: Faster saturation (5-7 days), quicker performance effects
- Gradual advantages: Avoids potential GI distress, avoids rapid 1-3 kg weight gain
- Recommendation: Loading for athletes needing immediate benefits (<30 days); gradual for those planning long-term use (>30 days)
- Divide loading doses: less than or equal to 10 g per serving to avoid diarrhea

## Q9: Is Creatine Beneficial for Older Adults?

**Short answer**: Yes. Creatine combined with resistance training provides musculoskeletal and performance benefits in older adults.

**Evidence**:
- Creatine alone (without training): Unlikely to produce substantial gains in strength or functional performance
- Creatine + resistance training: Three meta-analyses confirm augmented lean mass and strength gains
- Mechanisms: Creatine enhances training volume and/or intensity → greater muscle adaptations
- Bone: Attenuated hip bone mineral loss in postmenopausal women (52 weeks); reduced bone resorption in older men
- Functionality: Improved sit-to-stand, activities of daily living, fatigue resistance
- Emerging benefits for cachexia, myopathies, post-surgery, bed rest, brain health

## Q10: Is Creatine Only Useful for Resistance/Power Type Activities?

**Short answer**: No. Creatine benefits extend to many sport types beyond strength/power.

**Beyond resistance/power, creatine supports**:
1. **Glycogen replenishment**: Enhanced with creatine + carbohydrate/protein co-ingestion
2. **Muscle damage recovery**: Reduced CK, inflammation, and soreness after 30 km run
3. **Injury prevention**: Fewer injuries; accelerated recovery after immobilization
4. **Heat tolerance**: Hyperhydration effect for hot/humid competition
5. **Neuroprotection**: Reduced severity of concussion, TBI, SCI (animal models; human evidence emerging)
6. **Endurance sports**: Enhanced interval training capacity; glycogen supercompensation

## Q11: Is Creatine Only Effective for Males?

**Short answer**: No. Creatine provides benefits for females across the lifespan.

**Female-specific considerations**:
- Females may have higher baseline intramuscular creatine (per unit muscle mass), which may explain some diminished responsiveness
- Creatine kinetics fluctuate across menstrual cycle, pregnancy, postpartum, perimenopause, and postmenopause
- CK levels: highest during menstruation; lowest during early pregnancy
- **Pregnancy**: Animal studies show protective effects against fetal death and organ damage from intrapartum hypoxia; maternal supplementation enhances neuronal cell creatine uptake
- **Brain health**: Females have lower brain creatine (frontal lobe); creatine may reduce depression symptoms (adolescent females resistant to SSRIs); depression rates 2x higher in females during reproductive years
- **Postmenopause + resistance training**: Improved muscle mass, strength, and functionality; attenuated bone mineral loss at femoral neck
- **Performance studies**: 10 weeks creatine + training significantly increased intramuscular creatine, muscle mass, and strength in females (19-22 yrs); improved sprint/agility in elite female soccer players

## Q12: Are Other Forms of Creatine Similar or Superior to Monohydrate? Is Creatine Stable in Solutions?

**Short answer**: No other form is superior to creatine monohydrate. Creatine is NOT stable in solution.

**Forms comparison**:
- Creatine monohydrate: 87.9% creatine by weight; most studied; established safety and efficacy
- All other forms (creatine salts, ethyl ester, buffered, nitrate, dipeptides, serum): Either less effective or no superior effect demonstrated
- Most alternative forms contain less creatine per gram than monohydrate
- German-sourced Creapure: 99.9% pure; recommended source for research and supplementation

**Stability**:
- Creatine monohydrate powder is very stable over years (solid form)
- Unstable in solution: degrades to creatinine at rates depending on pH and temperature
- At pH 5.5: ~4% degradation after 3 days at 25°C; at pH 3.5: ~21% degradation
- During digestion: <1% degrades to creatinine (GI environment prevents cyclization)
- Solubility: 14 g/L at 20°C, neutral pH; better solubility in warm liquids
- This is why creatine is sold as powder, not in pre-mixed beverages
