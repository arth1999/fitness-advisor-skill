# Chapter 8: Female Athlete Nutrition — ISSN Consensus and Evidence Synthesis

**Sources**: Synthesized from ISSN Exercise & Sports Nutrition Review (2018), ISSN Creatine (2017, 2021), ISSN Caffeine (2021), ISSN Protein (2017), ISSN Beta-Alanine (2015), ISSN HMB (2013), and supporting literature.

## Overview

While no standalone ISSN position stand on female athlete nutrition existed at the time of this skill's compilation, substantial female-specific content is distributed across all ISSN position stands. This chapter synthesizes that content into a unified reference for the female athlete.

## Foundational Nutritional Considerations

### Energy Availability

The primary nutritional concern for female athletes is **low energy availability (LEA)**, defined as energy intake minus exercise energy expenditure <30 kcal/kg fat-free mass/day.

**Consequences of LEA**:
- Menstrual dysfunction (functional hypothalamic amenorrhea)
- Impaired bone health (low BMD, increased stress fracture risk)
- Reduced metabolic rate
- Impaired protein synthesis
- Increased injury risk
- Decreased performance

**At-risk sports**: Aesthetic (gymnastics, figure skating, diving), weight-class (combat sports, lightweight rowing), endurance (distance running, cycling)

**RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)**:
- Broader concept replacing the "Female Athlete Triad"
- Affects: menstrual function, bone health, metabolic rate, immunity, protein synthesis, cardiovascular health, psychological well-being
- Male athletes also affected (broader than triad)

### Iron Status

Female athletes are at elevated risk for iron deficiency due to:
- Menstrual blood losses
- Hemolysis from foot-strike (runners)
- GI bleeding (endurance athletes)
- Inadequate dietary intake (especially vegetarian/vegan athletes)

**Screening**: Serum ferritin recommended; supplementation when ferritin <35 ug/L
**Iron types**: Heme iron (meat, seafood) has ~2-3x greater bioavailability than non-heme iron (plants)
**Enhancers**: Vitamin C co-consumption; avoid tea/coffee with iron-rich meals (tannins inhibit absorption)
**RDA**: 18 mg/day for premenopausal women (vs. 8 mg for men)

### Calcium and Vitamin D

**Calcium**: 1000-1300 mg/day depending on age and menstrual status. Critical for bone health, especially in athletes with history of amenorrhea or LEA.
**Vitamin D**: 1500-2000 IU/day recommended. Screen for deficiency (serum 25-OH D <30 ng/mL). Affects: bone health, immune function, muscle function, injury recovery.

### Macronutrient Guidelines for Female Athletes

**Protein**: Same 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day as male athletes. Critical to increase intake during energy restriction (2.0-3.0 g/kg/d) to preserve lean mass.

**Carbohydrate**: Periodize to training demands; avoid chronic low-carbohydrate intake which may exacerbate LEA. Female endurance athletes on high-fiber diets may be at particular risk for inadequate energy intake.

**Fat**: Minimum 20% of total energy intake; essential for hormone production (estrogen, progesterone). Very low-fat diets (<15%) associated with menstrual dysfunction.

## Supplement-Specific Considerations for Female Athletes

### Creatine

**Baseline differences**:
- Females have higher intramuscular creatine concentrations per unit muscle mass (due to lower total muscle mass)
- This may partially explain some studies showing diminished responsiveness

**Hormonal influences on creatine metabolism**:
- Creatine kinase (CK) levels: Highest during menstruation, lowest during early pregnancy (~50% of peak)
- Enzymes associated with creatine synthesis influenced by estrogen and progesterone
- Creatine kinetics fluctuate across reproductive life stages: menses, pregnancy, postpartum, perimenopause, postmenopause

**Performance evidence in females**:
- Vandenberghe 1996 (10 weeks, 20 g/day loading + 5 g/day maintenance): Significantly increased intramuscular creatine, muscle mass, and strength in females (19-22 yrs)
- Elite female soccer players: Improved sprint and agility after 6-day loading
- College-aged females: Improved knee extension muscle performance after 5-day loading
- Not all studies show benefit: Some report no significant performance effects in young females
- Benefit-to-risk ratio favors creatine: Minimal risks vs. potential benefits across multiple domains

**Pregnancy and fetal development**:
- Maternal creatine supplementation in animal models: Protective against fetal death and organ damage from intrapartum hypoxia
- Reduced creatine levels in late pregnancy associated with low fetal growth
- Placental metabolic demand further lowers maternal creatine pool
- Creatine supplementation during pregnancy: Enhanced neuronal cell creatine uptake; supported mitochondrial integrity in offspring; reduced brain injury from birth asphyxia (animal studies)
- Human studies during pregnancy: Not yet conducted but proposed as safe, low-cost intervention for reducing intra/post-partum complications
- May be more important for vegetarian women or those unable to consume meat due to nausea/taste preferences during pregnancy

**Postmenopause**:
- Creatine + resistance training: Improved muscle mass, upper- and lower-body strength, functional tasks (30-s chair stand, lying prone-to-stand test, arm curl test)
- Bone health: 52 weeks creatine + supervised resistance training → attenuated rate of bone mineral loss at femoral neck vs. placebo
- Acute creatine alone (0.3 g/kg/day for 7 days, females 67 yrs): Improved lower-extremity physical performance, FFM, upper- and lower-body strength
- 1 year low-dose creatine: No change in absolute or relative body fat
- 2 years low-dose creatine: No effect on bone mass or geometry — suggests creatine must be combined with resistance training for bone benefits

**Brain health and mood**:
- Females have lower brain creatine levels (frontal lobe) compared to males
- Creatine supplementation increases cerebral PCr, particularly in females
- Increasing brain creatine may reduce depression symptoms: Adolescent females resistant to SSRIs showed inverse relationship between cerebral PCr increases and depression scores
- Depression rates ~2x higher in females during reproductive years; accelerates around pubertal hormonal changes
- Altered brain bioenergetics and mitochondrial dysfunction linked with depression (CK, ATP, Pi involvement)

### Caffeine

**Pharmacokinetic differences**:
- Oral contraceptive use: Prolongs caffeine half-life (estrogen component inhibits CYP1A2)
- Pregnancy: Significantly prolonged caffeine half-life; metabolism may decrease to 1/3 of normal rate
- Menstrual phase: May affect caffeine metabolism (limited data)

**Genetic considerations**:
- CYP1A2 genotype effects apply to both sexes but may interact with hormonal status
- ADORA2A variation: TT genotype more sensitive to anxiety induction by caffeine — potentially relevant as females have higher baseline anxiety rates
- Female-specific research on caffeine genetics is limited

**Performance**:
- Most caffeine studies use predominantly male subjects (~74%)
- Available evidence suggests females experience similar ergogenic benefits at equivalent doses
- Some studies showing no effect in female athletes (e.g., professional volleyball players, specific tasks) warrant consideration
- Individual response testing is particularly important given limited female-specific data

**Pregnancy and lactation**:
- Caffeine crosses placenta; fetus has limited CYP1A2 activity
- Typical guidance: Limit caffeine to <200-300 mg/day during pregnancy
- Lactation: Caffeine appears in breast milk; infant metabolism is slow (half-life ~80-100 hours in newborns)
- Competitive athletes who are pregnant/breastfeeding should consult medical professionals regarding caffeine use

### Beta-Alanine

- Effective in females at standard doses (4-6 g/day)
- Females may experience greater paraesthesia (Macphee 2013: Asian females > Asian males)
- Muscle carnosine concentrations tend to be lower in females compared to males at baseline
- Stout 2007: Female participants showed 14.4% improvement in PWCFT after 28 days beta-alanine
- Smith-Ryan 2012: Female runners showed large improvements in TTE at 90% and 100% vVO2max with beta-alanine
- No female-specific safety concerns beyond standard paraesthesia management

### Protein

- Same per-kg recommendations as males
- Female athletes in aesthetic/weight-class sports at risk for inadequate protein intake due to energy restriction
- During energy restriction: Increase protein to 2.0-3.0 g/kg/day to preserve lean mass
- Protein supplementation may be particularly useful for female athletes struggling to meet elevated needs through whole foods while managing energy intake
- No evidence for sex-based differences in MPS response to protein feeding at equivalent relative doses

### HMB

- Very limited female-specific research
- Portal 2011: Included female adolescent volleyball players; HMB-Ca increased FFM, decreased FM, improved strength and power
- Panton 2000: Both men and women (20-40 yrs) showed reduced CK and improved body composition with HMB-Ca
- Flakoll 2004: Elderly women (n=50) supplemented with HMB + arginine + lysine for 12 weeks → increased LBM, strength, and functionality
- Female judo athletes during 3-day caloric restriction: HMB preserved peak power and enhanced fat loss
- No female-specific adverse effects reported

## Bone Health Across the Female Athlete Lifespan

### Risk Factors for Low BMD in Female Athletes
1. History of amenorrhea/oligomenorrhea (cumulative effect; independent of current menstrual status)
2. Low energy availability
3. Inadequate calcium and vitamin D intake
4. Low body weight / BMI
5. High training volume with inadequate nutrition

### Supplement Strategies for Bone Health

| Supplement | Evidence | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Calcium | Strong for bone health generally | 1000-1300 mg/day from diet + supplements as needed |
| Vitamin D | Strong | 1500-2000 IU/day; maintain serum 25-OH D >30 ng/mL |
| Creatine | Emerging: preserves femoral neck BMD with training | 3-5 g/day + resistance training in post-menopausal women |
| Protein | Supportive: adequate protein for bone matrix | 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day |
| Magnesium | Bone mineralization co-factor | Ensure adequate intake through diet |

## Practical Recommendations for Female Athletes

### Screening Priorities
1. Energy availability assessment (dietary intake vs. estimated expenditure)
2. Menstrual status and history
3. Serum ferritin and hemoglobin (at least annually)
4. Serum 25-OH vitamin D
5. Bone mineral density (DXA) if history of amenorrhea >6 months or stress fracture
6. RED-S screening questionnaire

### Supplement Prioritization Matrix for Female Athletes

| Priority | Supplement | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Protein | Meet elevated needs; critical during energy restriction |
| 2 | Iron | If clinically deficient (ferritin <35 ug/L) |
| 3 | Vitamin D | Bone health, immune function; nearly universal insufficiency risk |
| 4 | Creatine | Muscle, bone, brain, and potential pregnancy benefits across lifespan |
| 5 | Calcium | If dietary intake inadequate to meet 1000-1300 mg/day |
| 6 | Caffeine | Ergonomic aid; individualize based on genetics, anxiety risk, menstrual phase, contraceptive use |
| 7 | Beta-Alanine | High-intensity sport performance; same dosing as males |
| 8 | HMB | Recovery, lean mass preservation during energy restriction (limited female-specific data) |

### Special Considerations by Life Stage

**Adolescence**: 
- Establish healthy relationship with food; avoid restrictive eating
- Adequate energy and calcium for peak bone mass accrual
- Iron needs elevated due to growth + menstruation onset

**Reproductive years**:
- Periodize nutrition to menstrual cycle where appropriate (limited evidence for performance benefit)
- Monitor iron status regularly
- Adjust caffeine timing based on contraceptive use

**Pregnancy**:
- Increased creatine demand; consider supplementation (consult physician)
- Limit caffeine to <200-300 mg/day
- Maintain adequate protein intake for fetal development
- Iron supplementation as prescribed

**Postpartum/Lactation**:
- Elevated protein and energy needs for milk production
- Gradual return to training; prioritize recovery nutrition
- Continue iron if depleted from pregnancy/delivery

**Perimenopause/Postmenopause**:
- Increased protein needs (anabolic resistance)
- Creatine + resistance training for muscle and bone preservation
- Calcium and vitamin D for bone health
- Body composition changes: prioritize lean mass preservation over scale weight
