# Chapter 3: ISSN Position Stand on Creatine Safety and Efficacy (2017)

**Source**: Kreider RB, et al. J Int Soc Sports Nutr. 2017;14:18.

## ISSN Position Statements

1. Creatine monohydrate is the **most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement** currently available for increasing high-intensity exercise capacity and lean body mass during training.
2. Creatine monohydrate is not only safe but has therapeutic benefits in healthy and diseased populations from infants to the elderly. No compelling evidence of detrimental effects from short- or long-term use (up to 30 g/day for 5 years).
3. With proper precautions and supervision, creatine is acceptable for children and adolescent athletes who: (a) are in serious/competitive supervised training, (b) consume a well-balanced diet, (c) are knowledgeable about appropriate use, (d) do not exceed recommended dosages.
4. Label advisories against use under 18 are legal precautions, not science-based.
5. Creatine monohydrate is the most extensively studied and clinically effective form.
6. Carbohydrate or carbohydrate + protein co-ingestion increases muscular creatine uptake.
7. Quickest loading: ~0.3 g/kg/day for 5-7 days, then 3-5 g/day maintenance. Gradual: 3-5 g/day for 3-4 weeks (less supported for initial performance effects).
8. Clinical populations have safely used 0.3-0.8 g/kg/day (21-56 g/day) for years.
9. Further research warranted on medical benefits of creatine and precursors like GAA.

## Metabolic Role

### Creatine Kinase / Phosphocreatine (CK/PCr) System

Creatine (Cr) + ATP ↔ PCr + ADP (catalyzed by Creatine Kinase)

Functions:
1. **Rapid ATP buffer**: PCr donates phosphoryl group to ADP during high-intensity exercise
2. **Energy shuttle**: Transports ATP from mitochondria to cytosol utilization sites
3. **Metabolic regulator**: Maintains ATP/ADP ratio; reduces reliance on glycolysis
4. **Antioxidant**: Reduces ROS formation at mitochondria
5. **Neuroprotectant**: Maintains brain bioenergetics

### Tissue Distribution
- ~95% in skeletal muscle (2/3 as PCr, 1/3 as free Cr)
- ~5% in brain and testes
- Total pool: ~120 mmol/kg dry muscle (vegetarians 90-110; upper limit ~160)
- Daily degradation: 1-2% to creatinine, requiring 1-3 g/day replacement

### Endogenous Synthesis
- Arginine + Glycine → Guanidinoacetate (AGAT enzyme) → + SAM → Creatine (GAMT enzyme)
- Synthesized primarily in liver and kidneys
- Vegetarians: lower intramuscular stores (90-110 mmol/kg), greater response to supplementation

## Supplementation Protocols

### Loading Protocol (Rapid Saturation)
- 20-25 g/day (0.3 g/kg/day), divided into 4-5 doses of 5 g
- Duration: 5-7 days
- Increases muscle creatine by 20-40%
- Co-ingestion with carbohydrate (47-97 g) or carbohydrate + protein (50 g) enhances retention

### Gradual Protocol (Slow Saturation)
- 3-5 g/day for 28 days
- Similar final muscle creatine levels as loading, but delayed
- Less supported for initial performance effects

### Maintenance
- 3-5 g/day for most individuals
- Larger athletes (e.g., American football linemen) may need 5-10 g/day
- After cessation, muscle creatine returns to baseline in 4-6 weeks
- No evidence of long-term suppression of endogenous synthesis

## Bioavailability

- Creatine monohydrate: 87.9% creatine by weight (anhydrous: 100%)
- Absorption: Nearly 100% from GI tract; plasma peaks at ~60 min post-ingestion
- Degradation: Minimal in digestive tract; stable in solid form; degrades to creatinine in solution (accelerated by low pH, high temperature)
- German-sourced (Creapure): 99.9% pure; Chinese sources may contain contaminants (dicyandiamide, dihydrotriazine, heavy metals)

**Alternative forms ALL inferior to monohydrate**:
- Creatine ethyl ester: Less effective; degrades to creatinine faster
- Buffered creatine (Kre-Alkalyn): No advantage over monohydrate
- Creatine nitrate: No greater creatine retention
- Creatine serum: Contains micro amounts; ineffective
- Effervescent creatine: No advantage

## Ergogenic Benefits

Table 1 from the paper lists these ergogenic benefits:
- Increased single and repetitive sprint performance
- Increased work during maximal effort muscle contractions
- Increased muscle mass and strength adaptations during training
- Enhanced glycogen synthesis
- Increased anaerobic threshold
- Possible aerobic capacity enhancement via mitochondrial ATP shuttling
- Increased work capacity
- Enhanced recovery
- Greater training tolerance

### Performance Magnitude
- High-intensity/repetitive exercise: 10-20% increase after loading
- Training adaptations: Strength +13.5 kg (meta-analysis lower-body); Lean mass +0.69 kg (meta-analysis)

### Sport-Specific Benefits

**Increased PCr**:
- Track sprints: 60-200 m
- Swim sprints: 50 m
- Pursuit cycling

**Increased PCr Resynthesis**:
- Basketball, Field hockey, American Football, Ice hockey, Lacrosse, Volleyball

**Reduced Muscle Acidosis**:
- Downhill skiing, Water sports (rowing, canoe, kayak), Swim events (100-200 m), Track events (400-800 m), Combat sports (MMA, wrestling, boxing)

**Oxidative Metabolism Benefit**:
- Basketball, Soccer, Team handball, Tennis, Volleyball, Interval training in endurance athletes

**Increased Body Mass/Muscle Mass**:
- American Football, Bodybuilding, Combat sports, Powerlifting, Rugby, Track/Field throwing events, Olympic Weightlifting

### Prevalence of Use
- 15-40% of athletes and military personnel
- NCAA male athletes: baseball 28%, football 27.5%, ice hockey 29%, wrestling 28.5%
- Female athletes: 0.2-3.8%

## Other Applications in Sport

### Enhanced Recovery
- Co-ingestion with carbohydrate enhances glycogen storage
- Creatine loading + glycogen loading promotes greater glycogen restoration
- Reduces muscle damage markers (CK -84% after 7 days recovery)
- Attenuates inflammatory markers (TNF-alpha, C-reactive protein, prostaglandin E2)
- Maintains performance during intensified overreaching periods

### Injury Prevention
- Multiple studies show creatine users experience FEWER:
  - Muscle cramping (p = 0.021)
  - Heat illness/dehydration (p = 0.043)
  - Muscle tightness (p = 0.020)
  - Muscle strains (p = 0.021)
  - Total injuries (p < 0.001)

### Heat Tolerance / Hyperhydration
- Creatine has osmotic properties; short-term fluid retention ~0.5-1.0 L
- Augments repeated sprint performance in the heat without altering thermoregulation
- Combined with glycerol: highly effective hyperhydrating strategy for hot/humid environments
- Reduces heart rate, rectal temperature, and sweat rate during prolonged exercise in heat

### Rehabilitation from Injury
- Reduced muscle atrophy during 2-week cast immobilization
- Greater muscle fiber CSA (+10%) and strength (+25%) during 10-week post-cast rehabilitation
- Offset decline in muscle GLUT4 protein during immobilization
- Not all studies consistent; ACL surgery study found no significant effect

### Brain and Spinal Cord Neuroprotection
- Ameliorates cortical damage by 36-50% in animal TBI models
- Improves locomotor function and reduces scar tissue after SCI in rats
- Reduces brain infarct size by 40% after ischemic events
- Reduces gray matter loss after SCI
- ISSN recommends creatine for athletes in sports with concussion/SCI risk

## Clinical Applications

### Creatine Synthesis Deficiencies
- AGAT, GAMT, CRTR deficiencies: inborn errors treated with 0.3-0.8 g/kg/day
- Early treatment (<9 months) leads to normal or near-normal developmental outcomes
- Long-term safety demonstrated in pediatric populations including infants

### Neurodegenerative Diseases
- Studied in: Muscular dystrophy, Huntington's disease, Parkinson's disease, ALS, mitochondrial diseases
- Large clinical trials (1,687 patients, 5,480 patient-years, average 9.5 g/day):
  - No clinical benefit in Parkinson's or ALS
  - Slowed brain atrophy progression in Huntington's disease
  - Safe at up to 30 g/day for 5 years

### Aging / Sarcopenia
- Meta-analysis (357 elderly, 12.6 weeks): Greater gains in muscle mass, strength, and functional capacity with creatine + resistance training vs. training alone
- Another meta-analysis (405 elderly): Greater muscle mass and upper body strength
- Combined with protein supplementation: Increased lean mass, decreased bone resorption
- Postmenopausal women: 12 months creatine + training preserved femoral neck BMD, increased femoral shaft subperiosteal width

### Other Clinical Areas
- **Diabetes**: Improved HbA1c, glycemic control, GLUT-4 translocation with 5 g/day + exercise
- **Cognitive function**: Increases brain creatine 5-15%; reduces mental fatigue; improves working memory, intelligence, processing speed, and cognitive performance under sleep deprivation
- **Ischemic heart disease**: Prophylactic creatine may protect during ischemic events
- **Pregnancy**: Maternal creatine improves neonatal survival and organ function after birth asphyxia in animal models; human studies emerging
- **Depression**: Some evidence for antidepressant effects (adolescent depression)

## Safety (Comprehensive Review)

### The Evidence

Over 1,000 studies and billions of servings consumed. The only consistent side effect: weight gain (1-3 kg).

**What creatine does NOT cause** (each claim specifically refuted by controlled studies):
1. Kidney damage / renal dysfunction at recommended doses
2. Dehydration
3. Muscle cramping (actually reduces incidence)
4. Gastrointestinal upset
5. Musculoskeletal injuries (actually reduces incidence)
6. Increased fat mass
7. Long-term detrimental effects
8. Hair loss / baldness

**Kidney Function**: Multiple studies show no effect on creatinine, creatinine clearance, GFR, or tubular resorption at doses of 5-20 g/day for 1 week to 21 months to 5 years. The 1998 case study that sparked concern was confounded by pre-existing kidney disease and immunosuppressant medication.

**Adolescents**: No evidence of adverse effects in performance studies. Clinical studies in pediatric lupus, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, and TBI show safety with health benefits. FDA GRAS status (2020).

## Practical Recommendations

1. Use **creatine monohydrate** (preferably German-sourced Creapure)
2. Loading: 0.3 g/kg/day for 5-7 days (optional; can use 3-5 g/day gradual)
3. Maintenance: 3-5 g/day (5-10 g/day for larger athletes)
4. Co-ingest with carbohydrate or carbohydrate + protein for enhanced uptake
5. Daily supplementation (any time of day; consistency matters)
6. No need to cycle; safe for long-term continuous use
7. For neuroprotection: 3 g/day recommended for general health across lifespan
8. Athletes in contact sports with concussion risk should consider prophylactic use
