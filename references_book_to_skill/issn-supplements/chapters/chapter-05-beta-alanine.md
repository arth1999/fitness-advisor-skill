# Chapter 5: ISSN Position Stand on Beta-Alanine (2015)

**Source**: Trexler ET, et al. J Int Soc Sports Nutr. 2015;12:30.

## ISSN Position Statements

1. Four weeks of beta-alanine supplementation (4-6 g daily) significantly augments muscle carnosine concentrations, acting as an intracellular pH buffer.
2. Beta-alanine currently appears safe in healthy populations at recommended doses.
3. The only reported side effect is paraesthesia (tingling); attenuated by divided lower doses (1.6 g) or sustained-release formula.
4. Daily supplementation with 4-6 g for at least 2-4 weeks improves exercise performance, with more pronounced effects in open end-point tasks/time trials lasting 1-4 minutes.
5. Beta-alanine attenuates neuromuscular fatigue, particularly in older subjects. Preliminary evidence indicates benefits for tactical performance.
6. Combining beta-alanine with other supplements may be advantageous when beta-alanine dosing is sufficient (4-6 g daily) and duration is adequate (minimum 4 weeks).
7. More research is needed on: strength effects, endurance beyond 25 minutes, and other health benefits of carnosine.

## Mechanism of Action

### Carnosine Synthesis
```
Beta-Alanine + L-Histidine → Carnosine (via carnosine synthetase)
```
- Beta-alanine is the **rate-limiting precursor** to carnosine synthesis
- Oral carnosine is ineffective: hydrolyzed by serum carnosinase before reaching muscle
- Carnosinase is ABSENT in human skeletal muscle → carnosine is stable once formed

### Physiological Functions of Carnosine

1. **Intracellular pH buffering** (primary ergogenic mechanism):
   - pKa = 6.83, ideally positioned for physiological pH range
   - Nitrogen atoms on imidazole ring accept protons
   - More effective than bicarbonate (pKa 6.3) or inorganic phosphate (pKa 7.2)
   - Carnosine buffering precedes bicarbonate buffering during exercise
   - Estimated contribution: 7-40% of total muscle buffering capacity

2. **Antioxidant**:
   - Scavenges free radicals and singlet oxygen
   - Chelates transition metals (copper, iron) → prevents Fenton reaction ROS production
   - Reduces lipid peroxidation when combined with aerobic exercise

3. **Anti-glycation**:
   - Prevents advanced glycation end-product (AGE) formation
   - Acts as a "sacrificial peptide" reacting with carbonyl groups to protect proteins
   - Implicated in anti-aging mechanisms

4. **Calcium sensitivity** (proposed, not confirmed in humans):
   - May improve calcium sensitivity in muscle fibers
   - Evidence mixed; Hannah et al. (2015) suggests calcium kinetics are not the primary mechanism

## Supplementation Strategy

### Dosing Protocol
- **Daily dose**: 4-6 g/day
- **Divided doses**: ≤1.6 g per dose to minimize paraesthesia
- **Sustained-release**: Reduces paraesthesia; allows higher single doses
- **With meals**: Enhances muscle carnosine loading (insulin-mediated?)
- **Duration**: Minimum 2 weeks (20-30% increase); 4 weeks (40-60% increase); 10 weeks (up to 80% increase)

### Loading Dynamics
- 2 weeks: ~20-30% increase in muscle carnosine
- 4 weeks: ~40-60% increase
- 10 weeks: Up to 80% increase
- No known upper limit for muscle carnosine concentration
- Total dose threshold: Median 179 g total across studies for 2.85% performance improvement

### Individual Response Variability
- **High responders**: +55% increase in muscle carnosine (5-6 weeks, 4.8 g/day)
- **Low responders**: +15% increase
- Variability related to: baseline carnosine content, muscle fiber composition, dietary habits
- Vegetarians have lower baseline → greater relative increases
- Trained athletes may have slightly higher increases than non-athletes (Bex 2014)

### Washout
- 6-15 weeks to return to baseline after cessation
- Slower washout in high responders

## Safety

### Paraesthesia (Tingling)
- Most common side effect; experienced with doses >800 mg (non-sustained release)
- Mechanism: Beta-alanine activates MrgD receptors (sensory neuron G-protein coupled receptors in skin)
- Location: Face, neck, back of hands
- Duration: Disappears within 60-90 minutes post-ingestion
- NOT harmful; no evidence of tissue damage
- Mitigation: Divided doses (≤1.6 g), sustained-release formulations
- Variability: Asian males may experience reduced effect; Asian females greater

### Taurine Interaction
- Beta-alanine and taurine share the Tau-T transporter into skeletal muscle
- Animal models: Beta-alanine decreases circulating taurine by ~50%
- Human data (Harris 2006): 4 weeks of beta-alanine (10-40 mg/kg) actually INCREASED plasma taurine; no significant decrease in muscle taurine
- Taurine decrease, if any, not of physiological significance in humans

### Long-Term Safety
- No safety data >1 year currently
- Due to endogenous production (non-essential), safety concerns considered low
- No adverse effects in studies up to 90 days in elderly populations

## Performance Effects

### Anaerobic Exercise (60-240 seconds) — Strongest Evidence

**Open End-Point (Time to Exhaustion)**:
- Cycling TTE at 110% max power: 13-14% improvement (Hill 2007; Sale 2011; Danaher 2014)
- Running TTE at 90-100% vVO2max: Large improvements for females; inconsistent for males (Smith-Ryan 2012)
- Greatest effects in tasks under 270 seconds (4.5 minutes)
- Meta-analysis (Hobson 2012): Exercise capacity improved to greater extent than fixed end-point performance

**Fixed End-Point (Time Trials)**:
- More modest effects compared to open end-point tasks
- Relative effects near 100 (small improvements)
- Largest relative effects in bouts lasting 63-141 seconds
- 2000-m rowing: 2.9-4.3 seconds faster (Baguet 2010; Ducker 2013)
- 400 m swim: Not significant; 100 m swim: 71.8% probability of benefit when combined with sodium bicarbonate

### Aerobic Exercise (>4 minutes)
- Modest benefits for exercise up to ~25 minutes
- Graded exercise tests: TTE improved 2.6-3.1% with beta-alanine vs. placebo (Smith 2009; Stout 2007)
- Beyond 25 minutes: Limited and inconsistent evidence
  - 1-hour cycling TT: No improvement despite doubled muscle carnosine (Chung 2014)
  - GXT ~50 min: No effect (Van Thienen 2009)

### Neuromuscular Fatigue — Strong Evidence in Elderly

**Physical Working Capacity at Fatigue Threshold (PWCFT)**:
- Young men: +16.9% improvement (Stout 2006)
- Young women: +14.4% improvement (Stout 2007)
- Elderly (70.7 yrs) + nutritional supplement fortified with beta-alanine: +23.0% (McCormack 2013)
- Elderly (72.8 yrs) + 90 days: +37.3% improvement (Stout 2008)
- Concomitant high-intensity interval training augments improvements

### Strength Outcomes — Mixed Evidence
- Short-term (30 days): No significant strength improvement; increased training volume and reduced subjective fatigue
- Isometric endurance: Significant improvement (Sale 2012)
- 10-week training: Creatine + beta-alanine showed greater improvements than creatine alone in body composition and training volume, but not significantly greater than creatine alone for strength
- Overall: Beta-alanine may improve training volume and fatigue; direct strength gains not consistently demonstrated

### Tactical Performance — Preliminary Evidence
- 28 days in soldiers: Moderate improvements in peak power, marksmanship, target engagement speed (Hoffman 2014)
- Follow-up study: Increased muscle carnosine; improved cognitive function and 50-m casualty carry; did NOT improve 2.5 km run, 1-min sprint, repeated sprints, or marksmanship
- 2014 expert panel review: Insufficient evidence to recommend for military personnel at that time

## Combination with Other Supplements

### Beta-Alanine + Sodium Bicarbonate

**Rationale**: Beta-alanine buffers intracellularly (carnosine); sodium bicarbonate buffers extracellularly (HCO3-)

**Evidence**:
- Sale 2011: Beta-alanine alone improved cycling TTE; 70% probability of additive effect with SB
- Tobias 2013: Both independently improved repeated Wingate mean power; Beta-alanine + SB superior but not significantly
- Hobson 2013 (2000-m rowing): Beta-alanine 96% chance of positive effect; SB 87% chance; combination 64% probability of additive benefit
- De Salles Painelli 2013 (swimming): 71.8% (100 m) and 78.5% (200 m) probability of additive effect
- Practical limitation: SB can cause GI distress; individual tolerance testing recommended

**Dosing for combination**:
- Beta-alanine: 4.8-6.4 g/day for at least 28 days
- Sodium bicarbonate: 0.3-0.5 g/kg acutely pre-exercise

### Beta-Alanine + Creatine

- Two studies showed additive ergogenic effects (Harris 2003 abstract; Hoffman 2006) but did NOT include beta-alanine-only treatment arm
- Studies WITH beta-alanine-only arm: No significant synergistic effect (Zoeller 2007; Stout 2006; Kresta 2014)
- Hoffman 2006: Creatine + beta-alanine > creatine alone > placebo for body composition and training volume
- Most likely beneficial for high-intensity exercise bouts where both phosphagen and glycolytic systems are stressed

### Beta-Alanine as Part of Multi-Ingredient Pre-Workout Supplements

- Typically contain 2-4 g beta-alanine per serving plus creatine, caffeine, BCAAs, whey, NO precursors
- Acute ingestion: Improves muscular endurance, running TTE, and power output (but beta-alanine unlikely to be the primary acute ingredient — requires chronic loading)
- Chronic ingestion (4-8 weeks): Increases strength, power, lean mass
- Beta-alanine contributes to effects when supplementation duration is sufficient (≥4 weeks)

## Health Benefits

- **Antioxidant**: Reduces lipid peroxidation; mitigates free radical accumulation with aerobic exercise
- **Anti-aging**: Reduces AGE formation; positive effects in elderly (neuromuscular fatigue, functionality)
- **Immune function**: Theoretical based on carnosine properties
- **Neurotransmitter**: Carnosine in brain, heart, eye tissue; neuronal effects emerging

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Dose**: 4-6 g/day in divided doses of ≤1.6 g
2. **Duration**: Minimum 2 weeks; optimal 4+ weeks
3. **Timing**: With meals to enhance carnosine loading; sustained-release formulations reduce paraesthesia
4. **Target sports**: High-intensity exercise lasting 60-240 seconds; sports with repeated high-intensity efforts and metabolic acidosis
5. **Special populations**: Elderly (neuromuscular fatigue benefit); tactical athletes (preliminary evidence)
6. **Combinations**: Additive benefit with sodium bicarbonate (modest); possible additive benefit with creatine
7. **Limitations**: Not effective for exercise <60 seconds (ATP-PCr dominant) or >25 minutes
8. **Paraesthesia management**: Divided doses or sustained-release formula; not harmful
