# Chapter 1: ISSN Exercise & Sports Nutrition Review Update (2018)

**Source**: Kerksick CM, et al. J Int Soc Sports Nutr. 2018;15:38.

## Overview

This comprehensive review serves as the ISSN's umbrella document for sports nutrition, covering supplement efficacy, dietary strategies, and nutrient timing across energy systems and sport types. It provides the evidence hierarchy for evaluating sports supplements and establishes the framework used by subsequent position stands.

## Supplement Categories and Evidence Levels

### Apparently Effective (Strong Evidence)
Supplements with multiple RCTs and meta-analyses confirming efficacy:
- **Creatine monohydrate**: Muscle PCr stores, high-intensity performance, training adaptations, lean mass
- **Caffeine**: Endurance performance, cognitive function, reduced RPE
- **Beta-alanine**: Muscle carnosine, high-intensity exercise buffering, neuromuscular fatigue
- **Protein / EAAs**: MPS stimulation, lean mass accretion, recovery
- **Sports drinks / Carbohydrate**: Endurance performance, glycogen replenishment, hydration
- **Sodium bicarbonate**: Extracellular buffering, high-intensity performance
- **Nitrate / Beetroot juice**: Endurance performance, vasodilation, oxygen efficiency

### Possibly Effective (Moderate Evidence)
- HMB: Recovery, lean mass preservation in untrained
- Glycerol: Hyperhydration for heat stress
- BCAAs: MPS signaling (less effective than whole protein)
- Carnitine: Mitochondrial function (limited evidence)
- Phosphate: Aerobic capacity (mixed results)

### Too Early to Tell / Insufficient Evidence
- Most single-ingredient supplements
- Novel delivery forms without independent verification
- Proprietary blends lacking ingredient-specific data

### Apparently Ineffective
- Glutamine: No performance or body composition benefit in healthy, fed athletes
- Individual BCAA supplements without other EAAs: Inferior to whole protein
- Many "testosterone boosters" and "fat burners"

## Sport-Specific Nutrition Principles

### Phosphagen System Sports (0-10 seconds)
Sports: Olympic weightlifting, shot put, 40-100 m sprints, powerlifting singles
- Primary supplements: Creatine, Caffeine
- Carbohydrate: Minimal intra-event; focus on daily glycogen maintenance
- Protein: Standard recommendations

### Anaerobic Glycolysis Sports (10-120 seconds)
Sports: 200-400 m track, 50-100 m swim, combat sports rounds, repeated sprints
- Primary supplements: Creatine, Beta-Alanine, Sodium Bicarbonate, Caffeine
- Carbohydrate: Modest intra-event for repeated bout sports
- Protein: Recovery-focused

### Oxidative Sports (>2 minutes continuous)
Sports: Distance running, cycling, triathlon, rowing, cross-country skiing
- Primary supplements: Caffeine, Carbohydrate, Nitrate, Protein (for recovery/damage mitigation)
- Carbohydrate: 30-90 g/h during prolonged events
- Protein + CHO during endurance: Muscle damage attenuation, not performance enhancement per se

### Intermittent/Team Sports
Sports: Soccer, basketball, football, tennis, hockey, lacrosse
- Primary supplements: Creatine, Caffeine, Protein, Beta-Alanine
- Carbohydrate: During activity for events >60 min; half-time re-fueling
- Recovery: Protein + CHO post-competition

## Female Athlete Nutrition Considerations

### Energy Availability
- Low energy availability (LEA) is the primary nutritional concern for female athletes
- LEA defined as <30 kcal/kg FFM/day; contributes to menstrual dysfunction and bone loss
- Female athletes in aesthetic, weight-class, and endurance sports at highest risk
- Screening for RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport) is recommended

### Iron Status
- Female athletes at elevated risk for iron deficiency due to menstrual losses
- Serum ferritin screening recommended; supplementation when ferritin <35 ug/L
- Heme iron (meat sources) has ~2-3x greater bioavailability than non-heme iron
- Vitamin C co-consumption enhances non-heme iron absorption

### Macronutrient Recommendations
- Protein: Same 1.4-2.0 g/kg/day as males; critical during energy restriction
- Carbohydrate: Periodize to training demands; avoid chronic low carbohydrate intake
- Fat: Minimum 20% of total energy; essential for hormone production

### Bone Health
- Calcium: 1000-1300 mg/day depending on age and menstrual status
- Vitamin D: 1500-2000 IU/day; screen for deficiency (serum 25-OH D <30 ng/mL)
- Weight-bearing exercise combined with adequate nutrition is essential
- History of amenorrhea increases risk of low BMD, independent of current status

### Supplement Recommendations
- Same evidence-based supplements as male athletes at equivalent per-kg doses
- Iron supplementation when clinically indicated
- Creatine may be particularly beneficial for bone density in post-menopausal athletes
- Protein supplementation to meet elevated needs during energy restriction

## Practical Recommendations from ISSN

1. Athletes should prioritize whole food sources before considering supplementation
2. Supplement decisions should be evidence-based, not marketing-driven
3. Individualized protocols accounting for sport, training phase, goals, and tolerance
4. Regular monitoring of iron, vitamin D, and energy availability status in at-risk athletes
5. Anti-doping compliance: third-party tested supplements only (Informed-Sport, NSF Certified for Sport)
6. Supplements are adjuncts to — not replacements for — sound training and nutrition programs
