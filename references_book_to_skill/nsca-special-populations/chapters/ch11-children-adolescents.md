# Chapter 11: Children and Adolescents

## 11.1 Trends in Youth Physical Activity

### Current Status
- Significant decline in youth physical activity over past decades
- Reduced school physical education; decreased outdoor/active play
- Increased screen time (TV, computers, mobile devices)
- Only ~25% of youth meet current physical activity recommendations
- Obesity prevalence: ~17% of children aged 2-19

## 11.2 Benefits of Physical Activity for Youth

- Improved cardiorespiratory fitness, muscular strength, bone density
- Healthy body composition; reduced obesity risk
- Improved academic performance, concentration, memory
- Reduced symptoms of anxiety and depression
- Development of motor skills and physical literacy
- Establishment of lifelong healthy habits
- Enhanced self-esteem and social skills
- Reduced risk of chronic disease later in life

## 11.3 Growth, Maturation, and Physical Activity

### Key Considerations
- Growth cartilage (epiphyseal plates) vulnerable to injury; avoid maximal loads until skeletal maturity
- Peak height velocity (PHV): rapid growth period; coordination may temporarily decline
- Bone mineral accrual: critical window during adolescence; weight-bearing exercise crucial
- Neuromuscular development: skill acquisition optimal during childhood
- Thermoregulation: less efficient than adults; higher surface area-to-body mass ratio
- Maturation timing varies widely (early vs. late maturers)

## 11.4 Exercise Recommendations for Children and Adolescents

### FITT-VP Summary
| Component | Children (6-12 years) | Adolescents (13-18 years) |
|-----------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Aerobic Frequency | Daily; >=60 min/day moderate-vigorous | Daily; >=60 min/day moderate-vigorous |
| Aerobic Intensity | Moderate-vigorous; >=3 d/wk vigorous | Moderate-vigorous; >=3 d/wk vigorous |
| Aerobic Mode | Play-based; varied; fun | Structured + recreational; sport |
| Resistance Frequency | >=3 d/wk (as part of 60 min) | >=3 d/wk |
| Resistance Intensity | Bodyweight; light resistance; focus on technique | Progressive; 8-15 RM; technique priority |
| Bone Strengthening | >=3 d/wk (running, jumping, sports) | >=3 d/wk |
| Flexibility | Daily | Daily |

### Key Programming Principles for Youth

1. **Fun and variety**: primary motivation; diverse activities prevent boredom
2. **Technique before load**: master movement patterns before adding resistance
3. **Qualified supervision**: trained professional oversight essential
4. **Age-appropriate equipment**: sized for children; not adult equipment
5. **RPE/OMNI scales**: appropriate for youth to self-regulate intensity
6. **Progressive overload**: gradual increases in volume before intensity
7. **Avoid specialization**: encourage diverse movement experiences
8. **Limit screen time**: <2 hours/day

### Modifications, Precautions, and Contraindications

**Growth Plate Safety:**
- Avoid maximal/1RM lifts until skeletally mature (Tanner stage 5)
- Submaximal loads with proper technique are safe and effective
- Avoid repetitive high-impact specialization (overuse injuries)

**Heat Management:**
- Children less efficient thermoregulators
- Acclimatize gradually to heat
- Hydration: scheduled drink breaks; children may not self-regulate
- Avoid exercise in extreme heat/humidity

**Overuse and Burnout:**
- Limit year-round single-sport specialization
- Encourage at least 1-2 days off per week from organized sport
- Monitor for signs of overtraining (performance decline, mood changes, sleep issues, recurrent injury)

**Hydration:**
- Encourage pre-exercise hydration
- Scheduled drinking during activity (children often don't voluntarily drink enough)
- Water generally sufficient for <60 min; sports drinks for prolonged vigorous activity

**Medical Clearance:**
- Generally not required for apparently healthy youth starting moderate activity
- Required for youth with known medical conditions
- Preparticipation physical evaluation recommended before organized sports

### Strength Training for Youth: Specific Guidelines
- Begin with bodyweight exercises; progress to light resistance
- 1-3 sets of 8-15 reps at moderate loads
- 2-3 non-consecutive days per week
- Include all major muscle groups
- Proper breathing: avoid Valsalva maneuver
- Full ROM through each exercise
- Qualified instruction on proper technique mandatory
- Resistance training is SAFE and EFFECTIVE for children when properly supervised
- Strength gains primarily neural (not hypertrophy) in prepubertal children
