# Chapter 9: Cognitive Conditions and Disorders

## 9.1 General Exercise Considerations for Cognitive Disorders

### Common Challenges
- Difficulty following complex instructions
- Reduced attention span and motivation
- Sensory processing differences
- Behavioral challenges
- Communication limitations
- Need for structured, predictable routines

### General Programming Strategies
- Simple, clear instructions; one cue at a time
- Visual demonstrations and visual schedules
- Consistent, predictable routine
- Positive reinforcement; immediate feedback
- Minimize environmental distractions (noise, visual clutter)
- Adapted communication methods
- One-on-one supervision may be needed initially

## 9.2 Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

### Characteristics
- Neurodevelopmental disorder; social communication deficits, restricted/repetitive behaviors
- Sensory processing differences: hypo- or hypersensitive to sound, light, touch, movement
- Motor skill delays common; coordination challenges
- Often prefer routine and predictability

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; moderate intensity; 30+ min
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; moderate; structured format
- **Flexibility**: Daily
- **Effective modalities**: Water-based exercise, martial arts (judo, karate), structured fitness programs

### Key Modifications & Precautions
- **Structured, predictable routine**: same order, same equipment, same instructor when possible
- **Visual supports**: picture schedules, visual timers, video modeling
- **Sensory considerations**: reduce noise, bright lights; some prefer deep pressure input
- **Motor skill development**: break complex movements into smaller components
- Minimize transitions; prepare client for changes
- Individual interests as motivation
- Aquatic exercise: water pressure provides calming proprioceptive input
- Martial arts: structured environment, repetitive practice, individual focus

## 9.3 Down Syndrome

### Characteristics
- Genetic condition (trisomy 21); most common chromosomal disorder
- Intellectual disability (mild to moderate range typically)
- Hypotonia (low muscle tone); joint laxity
- Increased risk: congenital heart disease, atlantoaxial instability, thyroid disorders, obesity, early-onset Alzheimer's

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: Most days; moderate; >=150 min/wk
- **Resistance**: >=2 d/wk; progressive; focus on postural muscles
- **Balance**: Essential component

### Key Precautions & Contraindications
- **Atlantoaxial instability** (AAI): screen via cervical spine x-ray before participation; if present -> avoid contact sports, forward rolls, diving, activities with extreme neck flexion
- **Cardiac evaluation** pre-exercise (congenital heart defects common)
- **Hypotonia**: may affect exercise form; provide postural support; emphasize core stability
- Simple instructions; demonstration; consistent routine
- Positive reinforcement crucial
- Social engagement enhances adherence
- Obesity prevention important (higher prevalence)

### Medications
- May include thyroid hormone replacement, cardiac medications; review all medications for exercise interactions

## 9.4 Intellectual Disability (ID)

### Characteristics
- Limitations in intellectual functioning and adaptive behavior
- Onset during developmental period
- Ranges from mild to profound
- Often accompanied by physical limitations, motor delays

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: Most days; moderate; >=150 min/wk
- **Resistance**: >=2 d/wk; moderate; progress slowly
- **Flexibility**: Daily
- **Functional exercises**: Emphasize ADL-related movements

### Key Modifications
- Very simple, concrete instructions; one step at a time
- Extensive demonstration; physical guidance if needed
- Consistent routine; same time, place, sequence
- Task variation to maintain engagement
- Social support and group settings
- Supervised environment for safety
- Positive, encouraging approach; celebrate all progress
- May need longer adaptation periods

## 9.5 Dementia and Alzheimer's Disease

### Characteristics
- Progressive neurodegenerative disease
- Cognitive decline: memory, language, problem-solving, judgment
- Behavioral and psychological symptoms
- Physical function decline in later stages
- Alzheimer's most common type (60-80%)

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: Most days; moderate; >=150 min/wk
- **Resistance**: >=2 d/wk; moderate; maintain functional strength
- **Balance training**: Essential for fall prevention
- **Flexibility**: Daily

### Key Modifications & Precautions
- **Safe, familiar environment**: minimize fall risks; consistent location
- **Dual-task training**: combine walking with cognitive tasks (e.g., counting, naming objects)
- **Structured, predictable plan**: may need 1:1 supervision
- **Behavioral management**: patience, redirection, avoid confrontation
- **Music and rhythm**: often preserved and motivating even in advanced stages
- **Wandering risk**: secure exercise area
- **Fall prevention**: balance exercises; remove trip hazards
- **Hydration**: may forget to drink; prompt fluid intake
- Exercise may slow cognitive decline; improves mood, sleep, behavior

## Philosophy of Exercise Programming for Clients with Special Needs

### Core Principles
1. **Individualization**: Programs must be tailored to each person's unique abilities, limitations, preferences, and goals
2. **Function over form**: Emphasize functional capacity and quality of life improvements over aesthetic or performance goals
3. **Inclusive approach**: Train in the least restrictive, most integrated setting possible
4. **Positive reinforcement**: Celebrate every achievement; build self-efficacy
5. **Patience and flexibility**: Progress at the client's pace; adapt daily based on status
6. **Collaboration**: Work with family, caregivers, and healthcare team
7. **Safety first**: Maintain appropriate supervision, modify environment, know emergency procedures
