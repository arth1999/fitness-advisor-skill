# Chapter 13: Female-Specific Conditions

## 13.1 Female Athlete Triad

### Definition
Interrelationship of three components:
1. **Low Energy Availability** (with or without disordered eating)
2. **Menstrual Dysfunction** (amenorrhea, oligomenorrhea)
3. **Low Bone Mineral Density** (osteopenia/osteoporosis)

### Pathophysiology
- Energy deficiency (intake insufficient for exercise expenditure + basic physiological functions)
- Hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis suppression -> reduced estrogen
- Low estrogen + energy deficiency + nutritional deficits -> impaired bone formation, increased bone resorption
- Can occur at any level of sport/activity; not limited to elite athletes

### Screening and Identification
- Female athlete presenting with: recurrent stress fractures, amenorrhea/oligomenorrhea, restrictive eating patterns, excessive exercise, significant weight changes, preoccupation with body weight/food
- Requires multidisciplinary team: physician, dietitian, mental health professional, exercise professional

### Exercise Recommendations
- **PRIMARY FOCUS**: Address energy availability before intensifying exercise
- Reduce training volume/intensity if energy deficiency present
- Weight-bearing exercise important for BMD (when medically appropriate)
- Resistance training 2-3 d/wk; moderate intensity; avoid excessive volume
- Avoid using exercise as compensatory behavior ("earning" food)
- Emphasis on health and function, not performance or aesthetics
- Return-to-play decisions made by medical team (not athlete or coach alone)

### Key Points
- NOT simply about eating more or exercising less; complex psychological and physiological condition
- Early intervention critical for bone health (lost BMD may not be fully recoverable)
- Scope of practice: exercise professionals do NOT treat eating disorders or provide psychological counseling; refer to appropriate licensed professionals
- Monitor for signs: fatigue, recurrent injury, performance decline, mood changes, social withdrawal, ritualistic eating/exercise behaviors

## 13.2 Pregnancy and Postpartum

### Physiological Changes During Pregnancy
- Increased blood volume (40-50%), heart rate, cardiac output
- Decreased systemic vascular resistance
- Ligamentous laxity (relaxin hormone) -> increased joint mobility, potential instability
- Shift in center of gravity; lordosis; balance changes
- Increased metabolic demands
- Thermoregulatory changes
- Supine hypotensive syndrome (vena cava compression after ~16 weeks)

### Benefits of Exercise During Pregnancy
- Reduced risk of excessive gestational weight gain
- Reduced risk of gestational diabetes, preeclampsia
- Reduced back pain; improved posture
- Improved psychological well-being
- Maintained/preserved fitness
- Possible shorter labor; reduced complications
- Faster postpartum recovery

### Exercise Recommendations for Pregnancy

| Parameter | Guideline |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Frequency** | >=3 d/wk (daily encouraged) |
| **Intensity** | Moderate; RPE 13-14 (Borg 6-20); "talk test" applicable |
| **Time** | >=150 min/wk; 20-30+ min/session |
| **Type** | Walking, swimming, stationary cycling, low-impact aerobics, prenatal yoga, Pilates (modified) |
| **Resistance** | Habitual exercisers may continue with modifications; discuss with physician; moderate loads; avoid Valsalva |
| **Pelvic Floor** | Kegel exercises daily |
| **Progression** | Previously sedentary: start 15 min/day, 3 d/wk, progress to 30 min, 4+ d/wk |

### Absolute Contraindications to Exercise During Pregnancy
- Hemodynamically significant heart disease
- Restrictive lung disease
- Incompetent cervix or cerclage
- Multiple gestation at risk for premature labor
- Persistent second- or third-trimester bleeding
- Placenta previa after 26 weeks gestation
- Premature labor during current pregnancy
- Ruptured membranes
- Preeclampsia or pregnancy-induced hypertension
- Severe anemia

### Relative Contraindications (Require Medical Evaluation)
- Severe anemia
- Unevaluated maternal cardiac arrhythmia
- Chronic bronchitis
- Poorly controlled type 1 diabetes
- Extreme morbid obesity
- Extreme underweight (BMI <12)
- History of extremely sedentary lifestyle
- Intrauterine growth restriction
- Poorly controlled hypertension
- Orthopedic limitations
- Poorly controlled seizure disorder
- Poorly controlled hyperthyroidism
- Heavy smoking

### Warning Signs to Stop Exercise and Seek Medical Attention
- Vaginal bleeding
- Regular painful contractions
- Amniotic fluid leakage
- Dyspnea before exertion
- Dizziness or feeling faint
- Headache
- Chest pain
- Muscle weakness affecting balance
- Calf pain or swelling (possible DVT)
- Decreased fetal movement

### Activities to AVOID During Pregnancy
- Contact sports (ice hockey, soccer, basketball)
- Activities with high fall risk (gymnastics, horseback riding, downhill skiing)
- Scuba diving
- Hot yoga or hot Pilates (hyperthermia risk)
- Supine position exercise after first trimester (may compress vena cava)
- Activities at altitude >6,000 ft (1,829 m) if not acclimatized
- Prolonged stationary standing
- Valsalva maneuver during resistance training
- Exercise in extreme heat/humidity
- Abdominal trauma risk activities

### Postpartum Exercise

#### Early Postpartum (First 6 Weeks)
- Gradual return to activity; focus on recovery
- Walking: start 10-15 min; progress gradually
- Pelvic floor exercises (Kegel): critical; daily
- Abdominal breathing exercises; gentle core activation
- Assess for diastasis recti BEFORE abdominal exercises
- Avoid heavy lifting; respect cesarean or perineal healing

#### After 6 Weeks (With Medical Clearance)
- Progressive return to pre-pregnancy exercise levels
- Aerobic: 150+ min/wk moderate
- Resistance: gradual return; pelvic floor and core priority
- Flexibility: especially hip flexors, low back (altered posture during pregnancy)
- Breastfeeding: exercise before feeding or pump before exercise (breast comfort); ensure adequate hydration and caloric intake
- Diastasis recti: modify abdominal exercises; avoid crunches/forward flexion if separation present
- Return to high-impact exercise: gradual; monitor for pelvic floor symptoms (incontinence, heaviness)

## 13.3 Menopause and Postmenopause

### Physiological Changes
- Decline in estrogen production (ovarian function cessation)
- Average age of menopause: 51 years (range 40-58)
- Perimenopause: transitional period (can last several years)
- Postmenopause: 12+ months after final menstrual period

### Key Changes Affecting Exercise
- Accelerated bone loss (estrogen deficiency): 2-3% BMD loss/year in early menopause
- Body composition changes: increased central adiposity; decreased lean mass
- Altered lipid profile: increased total cholesterol, LDL; decreased HDL
- Vasomotor symptoms: hot flashes, night sweats (may affect exercise comfort)
- Mood changes: increased risk of depression, anxiety
- Sleep disturbances
- Joint pain and stiffness
- Cardiovascular risk increases post-menopause
- Thermoregulatory alterations

### Exercise Recommendations for Menopause/Postmenopause

| Component | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Aerobic Frequency** | >=5 d/wk; weight-bearing emphasis |
| **Aerobic Intensity** | Moderate-vigorous |
| **Aerobic Time** | 150-300 min/wk; weight management: 250+ min/wk |
| **Resistance Frequency** | >=2 d/wk |
| **Resistance Intensity** | 60-80% 1RM; 8-12 reps; 2-3 sets |
| **Balance** | >=2-3 d/wk; critical for fall/fracture prevention |
| **Flexibility** | Daily; joint health maintenance |
| **Impact Activities** | Include weight-bearing impact (jogging, jumping) if bone-health appropriate |

### Key Programming Principles
- **Bone health priority**: weight-bearing + resistance + impact (when tolerated)
- **Fall prevention**: balance training; reduce fracture risk
- **Cardiovascular risk**: comprehensive exercise program + lifestyle management
- **Body composition**: resistance training preserves lean mass; aerobic for weight management
- **Thermoregulation**: layer clothing; cool environment; timing around hot flashes
- **Pelvic floor**: maintain pelvic floor muscle training (Kegel)
- **Joint health**: adequate warm-up; ROM exercises; address stiffness
- **Sleep**: morning exercise may improve sleep quality; avoid vigorous exercise close to bedtime if sleep-disturbing

### Medications (Menopause)
- **Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT)**: preserves BMD; may reduce hot flashes; risks/benefits individualized
- **Bisphosphonates**: for osteoporosis; GI side effects; no major exercise interactions
- **SERMs** (raloxifene): BMD maintenance; no major exercise effects
- **Calcium + Vitamin D**: supplementation for bone health
- **No routine polypharmacy specific to menopause**; but age-related medications may be present
