# Chapter 7: Immunologic and Hematologic Disorders

## 7.1 Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

### Pathophysiology
- Autoimmune disease; antibodies attack cartilage and synovial lining (synovitis)
- Begins in wrists, fingers, hands; spreads to other systems (heart, lungs, kidneys)
- Progressive joint damage over 10-20 years
- No cure; treatment focuses on controlling inflammation

### Exercise Recommendations
| Type | Frequency | Intensity |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| Resistance | 2-3 d/wk (vary based on post-exertion symptoms) | 40-80% 1RM; 8-10 exercises; 2-3 sets of 10-15 reps |
| Aerobic | 2-5 sessions/wk -> most days | 55-85% MHR; 3-10 min bouts -> 20-60 min continuous |
| Flexibility | 1-3 d/wk | 8-10 static stretches; 5-10s -> 20s holds |

### Key Precautions & Contraindications
- Adjust intensity/frequency based on daily pain and flare status
- Low-impact to no-impact during inflammation (walking, swimming, cycling, water exercise)
- Water-based activities highly recommended
- Avoid ballistic movements; use smooth, controlled motions
- Vary training modes to avoid repetitive-motion injuries
- Longer adaptation time needed when starting new activities
- Joint pain may reflect muscle stiffness -> gentle flexibility helps
- Monitor pain levels and adjust exercise accordingly
- Moderate aerobic exercise (65-<75% MHR) provides cardiovascular and functional benefits

### Medications (RA)
- **NSAIDs**: anti-inflammatory; may reduce exercise-induced fatigue
- **DMARDs** (methotrexate): slow disease progression; immunosuppression
- **Biologics** (TNF-alpha inhibitors): block immune response; infection risk
- **Corticosteroids**: reduce inflammation; long-term: weight gain, muscle weakness, osteoporosis
- **Intra-articular corticosteroid injections**: improve ROM; temporary effect

## 7.2 Lupus (Systemic Lupus Erythematosus - SLE)

### Pathophysiology
- Multisystem idiopathic autoimmune disease; acute and chronic inflammation
- 10:1 female-to-male ratio; onset typically 15-45 years
- ~1.5 million Americans affected
- Triggers: UV rays, sulfa drugs, antibiotics, infections, exhaustion, injury, emotional stress

### Exercise Recommendations
| Type | Frequency | Intensity |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| Resistance | 2-3 d/wk (vary based on symptoms) | 40-60% -> 65-75% 1RM; 8-10 exercises; 2-3 sets of 10-12 reps |
| Aerobic | 3-4 d/wk (start conservatively) | 60% VO2peak or 70-80% MHR; 3x10 min -> 30 min continuous |
| Flexibility | Daily or as tolerated | 8-10 static stretches; 5-10s -> 20s holds |

### Key Precautions & Contraindications
- **Photosensitivity**: avoid sun/UV exposure; exercise indoors or with protection
- Avoid overexertion (can trigger flare-ups)
- Allow extra adaptation time for new training modes
- Start conservatively; individualized approach essential
- High-intensity aerobic training tolerated in mild-moderate SLE (no organ damage in one study)
- Not exceeding RPE 10-12 on Borg scale
- Low-impact modes: biking, walking, swimming, tai chi, yoga
- Joint pain may reflect muscle stiffness -> gentle flexibility exercises

### Medications (Lupus)
- **NSAIDs**: similar to RA
- **Corticosteroids**: may cause weight gain, hypertension, increased cholesterol; long-term: muscle weakness
- **Antimalarials** (hydroxychloroquine): minor side effects (skin rash, appetite loss)
- **Immunosuppressants**: increased infection susceptibility
- **DHEA** (unapproved): may enhance muscle mass/strength with resistance training

## 7.3 Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)

### Pathophysiology
- Autoimmune disorder of unknown origin; persistent fatigue >=6 months, unrelieved by rest
- Prevalence: 0.007-2.8% of adults; 83% female
- Post-exertional malaise (PEM) is hallmark: up to 75% experience worsening symptoms after strenuous activity
- Often comorbid with fibromyalgia (35-70% overlap)

### Exercise Recommendations
| Type | Frequency | Intensity |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| Resistance | 1-2/wk -> 2-3/wk; not to exceed 2-3x/wk | Bodyweight -> light loads; 1 set -> 2-3 sets of 10-15 reps; avoid volitional fatigue |
| Aerobic | 1-2/wk -> most days; multiple light sessions/day | 5-10 min -> progressive; light-mod RPE |
| Flexibility | Daily or as tolerated | Dynamic drills (5-10m, low amplitude, proximal-to-distal) + static stretches (15-60s) |

### Key Principles
- **Graded Exercise Therapy (GET)**: start very low, progress very slowly
- **Symptom-contingent approach**: adjust daily based on symptoms
- **Time-contingent approach**: based on available energy stores
- NEVER "make up" missed sessions on good days (leads to PEM)
- End each session feeling "could have done more"
- On bad days: reduce intensity and duration accordingly
- Progression: increase frequency/duration BEFORE intensity
- Start with bodyweight multijoint exercises; gravity as resistance
- Select intensity where client feels could do 2-3 more reps at end of set
- For severely deconditioned: warm-up alone may be sufficient stimulus initially

### Key Precautions & Contraindications
- Avoid ballistic motions; smooth, controlled movements
- Consider time-based work intervals
- Vary activity types between sessions
- Focus on ADL-related movements
- Monitor 12-48h post-exercise symptom response

### Medications (CFS)
- No medications specifically approved for CFS
- Symptom management: sleep medications, pain relievers, antidepressants, stimulants
- Stimulants: may decrease peak HR; chronotropic incompetence risk
- Alternative: vitamins, minerals, essential fatty acids, CoQ10, amino acids

## 7.4 Fibromyalgia (FM)

### Pathophysiology
- Chronic widespread musculoskeletal pain with fatigue
- 2-4% of population affected; 90% female; onset typically 20-40 years
- Central sensitivity syndrome: lower threshold for pain, heat, noise, strong odors
- Elevated substance P (pain sensitization neurotransmitter)

### Exercise Recommendations
| Type | Frequency | Intensity |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| Resistance | 1-2/wk -> 3-4/wk (split routine) | 40-60% 1RM; 1 set -> 3 sets of 10-15 reps; avoid volitional fatigue |
| Aerobic | 1-2/d -> 3-4 d/wk | 30-<60% HRR or 55-<75% MHR; RPE 9-13; 5-10 min bouts -> 30-40 min |
| Flexibility | Between sets/intervals or as separate session | 8-10 dynamic low-amplitude stretches; 10-15s -> 20-30s; to point of tautness, never pain |

### Key Precautions & Contraindications
- Daily symptom-contingent approach essential
- Progression takes much longer than apparently healthy clients
- Avoid high-impact movements
- Intersperse low-level static stretching between sets/intervals
- Water exercise/meditation/qigong helpful adjuncts
- Repetitive motion may aggravate symptoms -> shorter intervals throughout day
- Flexibility: stretch more frequently rather than for longer durations
- Test for gluten sensitivity (celiac disease common in FM)
- 5,000+ steps/day may produce clinically significant pain reduction
- Balance training: positive effect on static balance; depression related to fall risk

### Medications (FM)
- Only 3 FDA-approved: pregabalin (Lyrica), duloxetine (Cymbalta), milnacipran (Savella)
- NSAIDs, antidepressants, muscle relaxants also commonly used

## 7.5 HIV/AIDS

### Pathophysiology
- Retrovirus attacking CD4 helper T cells; progressive immune deterioration
- Stage 1: acute infection, may be asymptomatic for years
- Stage 2: AIDS-related complex (ARC); weight loss, muscle wasting, weakness, fatigue
- Stage 3: AIDS; severe CD4 depletion; opportunistic infections and cancers

### Exercise Recommendations
| Type | Frequency | Intensity |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| Resistance | 2-3 d/wk | Mod-vigorous (stage-dependent) |
| Aerobic | 3-5 d/wk | 40-<60% or >=60% HRR (client preference) |
| Duration | 20-60 min | Stage-dependent |

### Key Precautions
- Exercise recommendations primarily stage-dependent
- Stage 1 (asymptomatic): near-normal exercise programming
- Progressive disease: exercise capacity diminishes; fatigue/wasting greater concern
- Resistance training beneficial for muscle wasting (affordable, few side effects)
- Aerobic training improves CV fitness, fatigue, central adiposity
- Universal precautions for infection control mandatory
- ART (antiretroviral therapy) metabolic effects:
  - Lipodystrophy (central adiposity, peripheral lipoatrophy)
  - Insulin resistance, diabetes risk
  - Dyslipidemia, hypertriglyceridemia
  - CVD risk elevated
  - Osteoporosis, osteopenia
- Exercise + nutrition counter metabolic effects of ART
- Avoid exercise during acute opportunistic infections

## 7.6 Sickle Cell Disease

### Key Points
- Genetic blood disorder; abnormal hemoglobin causes RBC sickling under stress
- Sickling triggers: dehydration, acidosis, extreme temperatures, high-intensity exercise

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; low-mod intensity; adequate rest periods
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; light-mod; avoid maximal efforts
- **Key Precautions**: AVOID dehydration; avoid extreme temperatures; avoid high-intensity exercise causing significant acidosis; adequate rest between sets

## 7.7 Hemophilia

### Key Points
- Genetic bleeding disorder; deficient clotting factors
- Risk of spontaneous or trauma-induced bleeding into joints/muscles

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; low-mod; non-contact activities
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; light-mod; controlled movements
- **Contraindications**: contact/collision sports; ballistic/high-velocity movements
- Coordinate exercise timing with factor replacement therapy
- Monitor for joint bleeding; swimming excellent (non-weight-bearing, low joint stress)
