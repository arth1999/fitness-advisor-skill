# Chapter 8: Neuromuscular Conditions and Disorders

## 8.1 Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

### Pathophysiology
- Demyelinating autoimmune disease of the central nervous system
- Variable presentation: relapsing-remitting, primary progressive, secondary progressive
- Symptoms: fatigue (most common), muscle weakness, spasticity, balance/coordination impairment, heat sensitivity, cognitive changes

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; moderate (40-<60% HRR); 20-30 min (interval approach if needed)
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; 50-70% 1RM; progressive
- **Balance/flexibility**: Essential components

### Key Precautions & Modifications
- **Temperature sensitivity**: avoid overheating; exercise in cool environment; pre-cooling strategies; aquatic exercise in cool water (80-84°F / 27-29°C)
- **Fatigue management**: morning exercise often better; intermittent bouts; energy conservation
- **Balance support**: use stable equipment, wall support; seated exercise options
- Aquatic exercise beneficial (buoyancy, cooling, support)

## 8.2 Parkinson's Disease

### Pathophysiology
- Progressive neurodegenerative disorder; dopamine deficiency in basal ganglia
- Cardinal signs: tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia (slow movement), postural instability
- Gait disturbances: shuffling, freezing, reduced arm swing

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; moderate-vigorous; 30-60 min
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; 60-80% 1RM; full-body
- **Balance training**: Essential; tai chi, dance, specific balance exercises
- **Flexibility**: Daily; address rigidity

### Key Modifications
- **Rhythmic/auditory cues**: metronome, music for gait; visual cues (lines on floor) for freezing
- **Large amplitude movements**: emphasis on exaggerated motions (LSVT BIG principles)
- **Schedule during "on" medication periods** when mobility is best
- Fall prevention strategies essential
- Structured, predictable environment

## 8.3 Muscular Dystrophy

### Pathophysiology
- Genetic disorders causing progressive muscle degeneration and weakness
- Types: Duchenne, Becker, limb-girdle, facioscapulohumeral, myotonic
- Progressive loss of functional muscle tissue

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: As tolerated; low-mod intensity; short bouts; avoid fatigue
- **Resistance**: As tolerated; low-mod loads; submaximal effort ONLY
- **Respiratory muscle training**: Specific inspiratory/expiratory exercises

### Key Precautions & Contraindications
- **ABSOLUTELY AVOID** eccentric-emphasis exercise (causes excessive muscle damage)
- **AVOID** exercise to exhaustion (may accelerate muscle degeneration)
- Contracture prevention: ROM exercises critical
- Monitor for excessive fatigue/soreness 24-48h post-exercise
- Respiratory function monitoring during exercise
- Focus on maintaining function rather than building strength
- Aquatic exercise excellent (support, resistance)

## 8.4 Cerebral Palsy (CP)

### Pathophysiology
- Non-progressive brain injury occurring before, during, or shortly after birth
- Affects movement, posture, coordination
- Types: spastic (most common), dyskinetic, ataxic, mixed

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; moderate; 20-30 min; adapted to ability level
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; 50-70% 1RM; progressive as able
- **Flexibility**: Daily; address spasticity-related contractures
- **Aquatic exercise** and partial body-weight supported treadmill training highly effective

### Key Modifications
- Proper positioning/seating essential during exercise
- ROM exercises for spasticity management
- Adapt equipment for grip/positioning limitations
- Functional task-specific training
- Communication adaptations if needed

## 8.5 Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

### Pathophysiology
- Acquired brain damage from external force; severity ranges mild (concussion) to severe
- Physical, cognitive, behavioral, emotional consequences
- Balance deficits, coordination problems, muscle weakness common

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; low-mod start; gradual progression
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; light-mod start; progressive
- **Balance**: Essential component
- **Flexibility**: Daily

### Key Precautions
- Medical clearance essential (seizure risk, autonomic dysfunction)
- Cognitive fatigue: shorter sessions, structured environment
- Balance safety: close supervision initially
- May have behavioral/cognitive challenges requiring adapted communication

## 8.6 Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident)

### Pathophysiology
- Ischemic (87%) or hemorrhagic (13%) brain injury
- Contralateral motor/sensory deficits; hemiparesis/hemiplegia common
- Often accompanied by CV risk factors (HTN, diabetes, dyslipidemia)

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; 40-70% HRR; 20-60 min (interval if needed)
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; 50-70% 1RM; affected limb ROM and assisted movements
- **Balance training**: Critical; task-specific
- **Flexibility**: Daily; especially affected side

### Key Modifications
- Affected limb: ROM exercises; assisted/active-assisted movements; constraint-induced therapy principles
- Task-specific training for functional recovery
- Fall risk high: safety supervision; appropriate support
- Secondary CV prevention: manage risk factors
- Communication adaptations if aphasia present

## 8.7 Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)

### Pathophysiology
- Damage to spinal cord; sensory and motor loss below injury level
- Classified by level (cervical = tetraplegia; thoracic/lumbar = paraplegia) and completeness
- Level T6 and above: risk of autonomic dysreflexia

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; 40-<60% HRR; 20-60 min; arm ergometry if needed
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; progressive as able; adapt to functional capacity
- **Flexibility**: Daily; maintain ROM above and below injury

### Key Precautions
- **Autonomic dysreflexia** (T6+): sudden dangerous BP elevation; triggered by noxious stimuli below injury (full bladder, bowel distension, skin irritation); MEDICAL EMERGENCY
- **Thermoregulation**: impaired sweating below injury; avoid extreme temperatures
- **Pressure sores**: regular skin checks; proper seating/cushioning
- **Shoulder overuse**: balanced upper body program; rotator cuff protection
- **Wheelchair-accessible equipment**; transfer safety
- Monitor for orthostatic hypotension

## 8.8 Epilepsy

### Pathophysiology
- Neurological disorder characterized by recurrent seizures
- Various types: generalized (tonic-clonic, absence) and focal
- Triggers can include: extreme fatigue, hyperventilation, hypoglycemia, dehydration, hyperthermia

### Exercise Recommendations
- **Aerobic**: 3-5 d/wk; moderate; 20-60 min; avoid extreme fatigue
- **Resistance**: 2-3 d/wk; moderate; avoid maximal loads
- **Flexibility**: Daily

### Key Precautions & Contraindications
- **Seizure safety**: avoid unsupervised swimming, climbing, high-risk activities where sudden LOC would be extremely dangerous
- Exercise generally safe and beneficial for seizure control
- Avoid known triggers: hypoglycemia, hyperthermia, dehydration, extreme fatigue
- Medication side effects: fatigue, dizziness, cognitive changes (antiepileptic drugs)
- Ensure adequate rest and hydration
- Buddy system or supervised environment recommended
