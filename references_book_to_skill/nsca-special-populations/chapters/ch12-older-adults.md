# Chapter 12: Older Adults

## 12.1 Physiology of Aging

### Key Age-Related Changes
- **Cardiovascular**: reduced VO2max (~10% per decade after 30); decreased maximal HR; reduced stroke volume; arterial stiffening
- **Muscular**: sarcopenia (loss of muscle mass ~3-8% per decade after 30, accelerates after 50); reduced strength and power; type II fiber atrophy
- **Skeletal**: reduced BMD (osteopenia/osteoporosis); joint cartilage thinning; reduced ROM
- **Neural**: slower reaction time; reduced balance/proprioception; cognitive processing changes
- **Metabolic**: reduced BMR; altered glucose tolerance; body composition changes (increased fat mass, decreased lean mass)
- **Thermoregulatory**: impaired heat/cold tolerance; reduced thirst sensation

### Consequences of Inactivity in Aging
- Accelerated functional decline
- Loss of independence (ADL difficulty)
- Increased fall and fracture risk
- Frailty development
- Social isolation
- Reduced quality of life
- Increased mortality

## 12.2 Benefits of Exercise in Older Adults

- Improved/maintained functional capacity and independence
- Reduced fall risk (balance training)
- Preserved muscle mass and strength
- Improved bone density (weight-bearing + resistance)
- Enhanced cardiovascular health
- Better glucose control and metabolic health
- Cognitive benefits; reduced dementia risk
- Improved mood; reduced depression/anxiety
- Enhanced social engagement
- Extended active life expectancy

## 12.3 Exercise Recommendations for Older Adults

### FITT-VP Summary
| Component | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Aerobic Frequency** | >=5 d/wk moderate OR >=3 d/wk vigorous |
| **Aerobic Intensity** | RPE 5-6 (0-10 scale) moderate; RPE 7-8 vigorous |
| **Aerobic Time** | 30-60 min/day moderate; 20-30 min/day vigorous; >=150 min/wk |
| **Aerobic Mode** | Walking, cycling, swimming, water exercise; weight-bearing preferred |
| **Resistance Frequency** | >=2 d/wk (non-consecutive) |
| **Resistance Intensity** | Start 40-50% 1RM; progress to 60-80% 1RM; power training 30-60% 1RM with high velocity |
| **Resistance Volume** | 8-10 exercises; 1-3 sets; 8-15 reps |
| **Flexibility** | >=2 d/wk; hold 30-60s; 2-4 reps per stretch |
| **Balance** | >=2-3 d/wk; progressive difficulty (narrowed base, unstable surfaces, dynamic movements) |
| **Progression** | "Start low, go slow"; increase duration before intensity |

### Key Programming Principles

1. **Comprehensive approach**: aerobic + resistance + balance + flexibility = optimal
2. **Functional exercises**: mimic ADLs; sit-to-stand, step-ups, carrying, reaching
3. **Power training**: high-velocity movements at light-moderate loads (30-60% 1RM); crucial for fall prevention and functional mobility
4. **Balance progression**: static -> dynamic; firm surface -> foam; eyes open -> closed; single task -> dual task
5. **Fall risk assessment**: TUG test, single-leg stance, functional reach; assess before programming
6. **Individualize**: wide range of functional capacity in older adults (robust -> frail)
7. **Social support**: group classes enhance adherence; partner exercises
8. **Education**: teach benefits and expected outcomes; build confidence to be active

## 12.4 Common Medications in Older Adults

| Drug Class | Exercise Considerations |
|------------|------------------------|
| Beta-blockers | Blunted HR response; use RPE for intensity |
| Diuretics | Dehydration risk; electrolyte imbalance; orthostatic hypotension |
| ACE inhibitors/ARBs | Postural hypotension; monitor BP |
| Calcium channel blockers | Dizziness; postural hypotension |
| Statins | Myalgia risk; start low intensity |
| Anticoagulants/Antiplatelets | Bleeding risk with falls; avoid high-risk activities |
| NSAIDs | GI bleeding risk; renal effects; may mask pain |
| Bisphosphonates | GI side effects; take on empty stomach |
| Antidepressants | May cause drowsiness, dizziness; fall risk |
| Sedatives/Hypnotics | Dizziness, drowsiness; avoid exercise during peak effect |
| Antidiabetic agents | Hypoglycemia risk; BG monitoring |

### Polypharmacy
- Average older adult takes 4-5 prescription medications
- Drug-drug interactions common
- Cumulative side effects may affect exercise tolerance
- Regular medication review recommended

## 12.5 Recommended Strategies for Instructing Older Adults

1. **Clear communication**: speak clearly; face client; check for hearing/vision limitations
2. **Demonstration**: show exercises; use mirror if helpful; hands-on guidance
3. **Positive feedback**: emphasize improvements and abilities, not limitations
4. **Respect and dignity**: acknowledge life experience and autonomy
5. **Goal setting**: meaningful functional goals (e.g., "play with grandchildren" rather than "increase knee extension torque")
6. **Safety emphasis**: proper footwear, non-slip surfaces, adequate lighting, hydration
7. **Pacing**: allow more time for transitions, explanations, and rest
8. **Social engagement**: encourage group participation; exercise partners
9. **Music**: appropriate tempo and volume; motivating for many
10. **Temperature awareness**: older adults more susceptible to heat/cold stress; maintain comfortable environment
