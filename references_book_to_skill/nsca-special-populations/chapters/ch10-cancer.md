# Chapter 10: Cancer

## 10.1 Pathology and Pathophysiology

### Overview
- Uncontrolled cell growth and spread to other parts of the body
- >100 different types; named for tissue of origin
- Carcinomas (epithelial tissue) most common
- Sarcomas (connective tissue), leukemias (blood-forming), lymphomas (immune system)

### Cancer Treatments and Their Effects
| Treatment | Mechanism | Common Side Effects Affecting Exercise |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------------------------|
| Surgery | Tumor removal | Pain, reduced ROM, lymphedema, deconditioning |
| Chemotherapy | Cytotoxic drugs kill rapidly dividing cells | Fatigue, anemia, immunosuppression, peripheral neuropathy, cardiotoxicity, nausea/vomiting |
| Radiation | High-energy rays damage cancer cell DNA | Fatigue, skin irritation, fibrosis, reduced ROM in irradiated area |
| Hormone Therapy | Blocks hormone-driven cancer growth | Bone loss, muscle loss, weight gain, fatigue, joint pain |
| Immunotherapy | Stimulates immune system to attack cancer | Fatigue, inflammation, autoimmune reactions |
| Targeted Therapy | Drugs targeting specific cancer cell mechanisms | Variable; generally fewer side effects than chemo |

## 10.2 Common Medications and Exercise Effects

| Drug Class | Examples | Exercise Considerations |
|------------|----------|------------------------|
| Anthracyclines | doxorubicin (Adriamycin) | Cardiotoxicity: reduced cardiac function; monitor HR/BP; may limit exercise capacity |
| HER2 inhibitors | trastuzumab (Herceptin) | Cardiotoxicity risk; echocardiogram monitoring |
| Aromatase Inhibitors | anastrozole, letrozole | Joint pain, bone loss; resistance training + weight-bearing exercise important |
| Taxanes | paclitaxel, docetaxel | Peripheral neuropathy: balance issues; avoid unstable surfaces |
| Platinum-based | cisplatin, carboplatin | Peripheral neuropathy; fatigue |
| Corticosteroids | prednisone, dexamethasone | Muscle weakness, weight gain, bone loss with long use |
| Anti-androgens | enzalutamide, abiraterone | Muscle loss, fatigue, bone loss |

## 10.3 Effects of Exercise in Cancer

### Benefits During and After Treatment
- Reduced cancer-related fatigue (strongest evidence)
- Improved physical function and QOL
- Maintained/preserved muscle mass and strength
- Reduced treatment side effects
- Improved body composition
- Reduced anxiety and depression
- Improved sleep quality
- Possible reduced recurrence risk (breast, colon, prostate cancer)
- Improved survival rates (observational studies)

### Lymphedema
- Exercise (including resistance training) is SAFE for breast cancer survivors with and at risk of lymphedema
- Precautions: wear compression sleeves, lift arm above head after exercise, active light recovery to facilitate lymph return
- Reduce resistance or stop specific exercises according to symptom response
- Vigorous upper body exercise: perform with caution; progressive approach

## 10.4 Exercise Recommendations

### General Guidelines
| Parameter | Guideline |
|-----------|-----------|
| Aerobic Frequency | 3-5 d/wk |
| Aerobic Intensity | 40-<60% HRR start; progress as tolerated |
| Aerobic Duration | 20-30 min start; 150+ min/wk goal |
| Resistance Frequency | 2-3 d/wk |
| Resistance Intensity | Light-mod start (40-60% 1RM); progress slowly |
| Flexibility | Daily; ROM exercises |

### Precautions for Exercise Prescription

| Condition/Scenario | Precaution |
|-------------------|------------|
| Bone metastases | Avoid high-impact, high-load exercises; focus on low-impact; avoid twisting at affected sites |
| Thrombocytopenia (platelets <50,000) | Avoid resistance training, contact, high-risk activities |
| Anemia (Hb <10 g/dL) | Reduce intensity; monitor for unusual dyspnea/fatigue |
| Neutropenia (WBC <3,000) | Avoid public facilities (infection risk); home-based or private exercise |
| Cardiotoxicity | Cardiac monitoring; echocardiogram; reduce intensity |
| Peripheral neuropathy | Avoid unstable surfaces; seated exercises; balance training |
| Lymphedema | Compression garments; gradual progression; monitor for symptoms |
| PICC line/port | Avoid water immersion; protect site from trauma |
| Radiation treatment | Avoid chlorine pools for irradiated skin; gentle ROM for irradiated area |
| Severe fatigue | Reduce intensity/duration; multiple short bouts; exercise during highest energy periods |
| Nausea/vomiting | Adjust timing; light activity; avoid eating immediately before exercise |
| Ataxia/dizziness | Seated or supported exercises only; no free weights |

### Contraindications to Exercise (During Cancer Treatment)
- Platelets <50,000/mm3
- Hemoglobin <10 g/dL
- White blood cell count <3,000/mm3
- Severe pain
- Acute infection or fever
- Severe nausea and vomiting
- Ataxia, dizziness, or severe peripheral neuropathy
- Bone pain (possible metastasis)
- Unusual or severe fatigue
- Recent surgery (until surgeon clearance)

## 10.5 Key Points for Exercise Professionals

1. Medical clearance and current lab values essential before programming
2. Adjust program based on treatment cycle (chemo week vs. recovery week)
3. Exercise during treatment is safe and beneficial for most cancer types
4. ACSM Roundtable on Exercise Guidelines for Cancer Survivors recommends avoiding inactivity
5. "Start low, go slow" principle applies
6. Communication with oncology team recommended
7. Psychological benefits equal to or exceeding physical benefits
8. Individualized approach critical: cancer type, stage, treatment, side effects, fitness level
